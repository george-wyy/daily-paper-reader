## 摘要
预期姿势调节（APAs）随接近物体的速度而缩放，缩放幅度取决于移动物体是被主动注视并追踪、通过注视的外周视觉处理，还是通过注视的中央视觉处理。衰老优先降解了外周运动处理背后的巨细胞通路，同时保留了平滑追踪期间可用的视网膜外信号。因此，我们探究了衰老对速度依赖性 APA 缩放的影响在上述三种视觉通路中是否存在差异。18 名年轻人和 18 名老年人在三种注视条件下（通过平滑追踪的主动注视、中央固定和外周固定）拦截以四种速度（15 至 33 cm/s）接近的虚拟物体。我们测量了峰值预期力、力产生率以及力开始时的接触时间。尽管老年人的平滑追踪增益降低，但在主动注视期间，两组的速度依赖性缩放相当，而在中央固定期间，两组的缩放均极小。关键在于，年轻人在外周固定期间的力率缩放几乎与主动注视期间一样陡峭，而老年人的斜率显著较低，这种差异在其他注视条件下并未观察到。老年人通过更早地启动反应达到了相当的峰值力。这些结果表明，与年龄相关的预期运动控制下降具有通路特异性：衰老选择性地损害了用于 APA 的外周运动处理，而视网膜外机制仍能维持速度依赖性缩放。更广泛地说，外周运动处理成为年龄相关姿势脆弱性的候选生理位点，这提出了针对巨细胞通路的训练是否能恢复老年人 APA 缩放的问题。

## Abstract
Anticipatory postural adjustments (APAs) scale with velocity of approaching objects, with scaling magnitude depending on whether the moving object is actively foveated and tracked, processed through fixated peripheral vision, or processed through fixated central vision. Aging preferentially degrades the magnocellular pathway underlying peripheral motion processing while sparing the extraretinal signals available during smooth pursuit. We therefore asked whether the effect of aging on velocity-dependent APA scaling differs across these three visual pathways. Eighteen young and eighteen older adults stopped a virtual object approaching at four velocities (15 to 33 cm per s) under three gaze conditions: active foveation via smooth pursuit, central fixation, and peripheral fixation. We measured peak anticipatory force, rate of force development, and time to contact at force onset. Despite reduced smooth pursuit gain in older adults, velocity-dependent scaling was equivalent between age groups during active foveation, and minimal in both groups during central fixation. Critically, young adults scaled force rate during peripheral fixation nearly as steeply as during active foveation, whereas older adults' slope was significantly lower, a difference not observed during the other gaze conditions. Older adults achieved comparable peak force by initiating responses earlier. These results establish that age-related decline in anticipatory motor control is pathway-specific: aging selectively impairs peripheral motion processing for APAs, while extraretinal mechanisms remain capable of sustaining velocity-dependent scaling. More broadly, peripheral motion processing emerges as a candidate physiological locus of age-related postural vulnerability, raising the question of whether magnocellular-targeted training can restore APA scaling in older adults.
