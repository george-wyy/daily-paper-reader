## 摘要
感觉运动决策需要在外部奖励与内在行动成本之间取得平衡。在快速眼动选择过程中，这些信号如何整合尚不清楚。我们表明，人类的眼跳选择遵循与神经经济决策一致的成本-效益计算。在两个实验中，参与者最大化奖励并最小化努力成本。奖励滴定显示，更费力的眼跳需要更高的金钱奖励才能被同等频率地选择。虽然努力成本线性地影响选择，但小的奖励差异比同等大小的较大差异更能改变选择。此外，努力成本（而非奖励）差异调节决策参与：只有当成本差异很大时，思虑才会延长。总之，成本和奖励在眼跳选择中扮演可分离的角色，将眼动定位为一个易于处理的经济决策模型系统。

意义声明
感觉运动决策被认为是在奖励与内在行动成本之间取得平衡，然而在快速眼动选择下一个注视点的过程中，这些信号如何整合仍不清楚。通过两个实验，我们表明眼跳选择满足经济决策的递增标准：成本和奖励在共同效用尺度上权衡，通过可分离的计算对选择做出贡献，并根据效用差异调整思虑程度。仅努力成本差异就调节决策参与。这些发现将注意选择重新定位为一种神经经济过程，以及一个易于处理的经济决策模型系统。

## Abstract
Sensorimotor decisions require balancing extrinsic rewards against intrinsic action costs. How these signals are integrated during rapid eye-movement choices remains unclear. We show that human saccade selection follows cost-benefit computations consistent with neuroeconomic decision-making. Across two experiments, participants maximized rewards and minimized effort-costs. Reward titration revealed that more effortful saccades required higher monetary rewards to be chosen equally often. While effort-costs affected choices linearly, small reward differences shifted choices more than equally-sized larger ones. Furthermore, effort-cost (but not reward) differences regulated decision engagement: Deliberation was extended only when cost differences were large. Together, costs and rewards play dissociable roles in saccade selection, positioning eye movements as a tractable model system for economic decisionmaking.

Significance statementSensorimotor decisions are thought to balance rewards against intrinsic action costs, yet how these signals are integrated during rapid eye movement choices on where to look next has remained unclear. Across two experiments, we show that saccade selection satisfies escalating criteria of economic decision-making: costs and rewards trade off on a common utility scale, contribute to choices through dissociable computations, and scale deliberation with utility differences. Effort-cost differences alone regulate decision engagement. These findings reposition attentional selection as a neuroeconomic process and a tractable model system for economic decision-making.
