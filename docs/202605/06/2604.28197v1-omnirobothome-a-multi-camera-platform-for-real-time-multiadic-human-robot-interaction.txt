# 1 Introduction 

Enabling robots to assist with or replace human labor in everyday living en-vironments remains a fundamental objective in robotics. Unlike structured in-dustrial workspaces or constrained tabletop scenarios, household environments are inherently unstructured and dynamic. Humans move freely, interact with diverse objects, and continuously reconfigure their surroundings. A system that observes, understands, and anticipates such behavior can enable far more in-telligent robotic assistance. Yet practical home scenarios go beyond a single robot assisting a single person: many tasks require multiple robots and humans to share the same workspace, acting concurrently on interleaved subtasks with tight spatial and temporal coupling—a regime we refer to as multiadic collabo-ration. Studying this regime requires distributed real-time perception that spans the full living space, tightly coupled with robotic hardware so that assistance is conditioned on live human and object state. The central bottleneck is reliable multi-agent 3D tracking: close-proximity interaction between humans, robots, and objects creates persistent occlusion and rapid state changes, making real-time, occlusion-robust, room-scale perception the critical missing capability. Most prior robotics research assumes constrained environments with localized sensing [3, 21], particularly tabletop settings where perception is simplified and surrounding human motion is not explicitly modeled [42, 58]. More recent work explores mobile robots in larger spaces [31, 52], but sensing remains designed for navigation, not continuous human monitoring. On the other hand, computer vision research has advanced large-scale human sensing through multi-camera 3D motion capture [19, 25], but most such systems target offline processing or dataset construction and rarely support real-time operation [13, 33]. Plat-forms that provide the real-time, occlusion-robust perception needed to study multiadic collaboration under close-proximity interaction remain largely unex-plored. Without such a platform, the core research questions of this regime— how to maintain safety under concurrent operation, how to infer human intent for timely handovers, and how to coordinate simultaneous actions with shared dependencies—have remained beyond experimental reach. OmniRobotHome 3

We present OmniRobotHome , a room-scale physical AI testbed that re-moves this bottleneck and enables systematic study of multiadic human-robot collaboration in a natural home environment. The system continuously perceives human activity and object state across the full living space, and provides coor-dinated multi-robot assistance conditioned on the shared, real-time 3D scene state, through four key capabilities. 

(1) Large sensing coverage in a unified world frame. The system tracks human location and full-body pose across a wide living space as the person moves naturally, supporting analysis of trajectories, behavioral patterns, and activity intent. A single consistent world frame shared by all cameras and robot arms provides the spatial foundation for multi-agent coordination and safety policies. 

(2) Real-time markerless multi-human and multi-object 3D percep-tion. Unlike conventional multi-camera systems designed for offline processing, our system performs real-time markerless multi-human tracking with 6D ob-ject pose estimation. This allows robot actions to be conditioned on live human and object state rather than replayed data, enabling reactive safety and timing-sensitive interaction. 

(3) Multi-robot actuation. Two robot arms are deployed within the envi-ronment, temporally aligned with the shared perception stream. This enables multiadic interaction scenarios where multiple robots act concurrently with task-coupled dependencies on the human and on each other, and broadens the avail-able workspace. 

(4) Human behavior memory. Continuous markerless capture within a con-sistent world frame enables long-term accumulation of behavioral data from residents, supporting modeling of habitual routines. Accumulated trajectories become priors that collaboration policies consume directly, enabling anticipa-tory assistance that improves with observation time. Together, these four capabilities make OmniRobotHome the first platform that simultaneously provides room-scale real-time human and object sensing in 3D with coordinated multi-arm robotic assistance in a unified residential envi-ronment. Building such a system introduces substantial engineering challenges in calibration, synchronization, networking, and distributed processing; this paper presents practical solutions to each. More importantly, the platform makes mul-tiadic collaboration experimentally tractable, enabling controlled studies that isolate each capability’s contribution. We focus on two central problems: (1) 

safety in shared human-robot environments : we demonstrate that real-time sens-ing and predictive modeling of human motion significantly improve safety when humans and robots coexist; and (2) human-anticipatory robotic assistance : we evaluate whether behavioral observation enables intent inference for anticipatory assistance, from placement prediction to pose-conditioned handovers. Our contributions are: (1) a room-scale residential platform with 48 hardware-synchronized cameras and two robot arms; (2) an end-to-end real-time sensing pipeline for perception, data acquisition, and human motion prediction; and (3) systematic human-robot interaction studies in multiadic residential environ-ments demonstrating that wide-area sensing, real-time perception, and long-term 4 Lee et al. 

behavioral modeling each contribute measurable gains to safety and assistive performance. All code and data will be publicly released. 

# 2 Related Work 

2.1 Room-Scale Human and Object Sensing 

Dense multi-view coverage is required to recover 3D human and object state under occlusion. Panoptic Studio [19] scaled markerless capture to social inter-action, and subsequent systems extended coverage to hand-object grasping [44], whole-body interaction in furnished rooms [25], and joint human-object trajec-tories [15, 32]. All operate offline and none produces real-time 3D scene state for closed-loop robotic control. 

2.2 Robotic Manipulation Platforms 

Platforms for robot learning have scaled in task diversity and data volume. ALOHA [11, 60] demonstrates bimanual skills via teleoperation, SayCan [1] grounds language in affordances, and large datasets [9, 23, 36] aggregate tra-jectories across embodiments. Multi-robot coordination has progressed from in-dustrial assembly [54] to collision-free multi-arm planning [20,28] and multi-user evaluation [56]. These platforms treat human state as external to the control loop; none integrates room-scale real-time human and object sensing with coor-dinated multi-robot actuation. 

2.3 Human-Robot Interaction and Safety 

Collaboration in shared workspaces requires intention understanding, real-time adaptation, and proximity-aware safety. Handover methods condition on pre-dicted contact [48], receiver motion [24], and gaze [35]. Cooperative planners model human motion [6], learn interaction primitives [8], predict intention from skeleton cues [43], and learn strategies via imitation [46]. For safety, reactive [2, 38] and learning-based [10, 55] controllers handle collision avoidance in dynamic settings. Existing work overwhelmingly studies dyadic interaction with onboard or single-viewpoint sensing; multiadic collaboration with task-coupled dependen-cies remains largely unexplored. 

2.4 Human Motion Prediction and Behavior Memory 

Proactive assistance requires anticipating human actions. Short-horizon pre-diction forecasts body trajectory seconds ahead, enabling preemptive collision avoidance and timely handovers. Trajectory forecasting [34, 39] has advanced substantially, with InteRACT [22] modeling how predicted intent shifts in re-sponse to robot actions. # 3 System Design 

3.1 Overview 

Exploring multiadic human-robot collaboration requires real-time, occlusion-robust, room-scale 3D perception tightly coupled with robotic actuation. Om-niRobotHome provides this through a synchronized sensing and control archi-tecture (Fig. 2). We instrument a 23.1 m 2 residential space with 48 hardware-synchronized RGB cameras operating at 30 Hz. The cameras are distributed across 12 edge nodes that run real-time person detection and 2D pose estimation in parallel. A central server triangulates 3D whole-body keypoints and estimates object 6D poses, producing a unified scene state in a shared world frame. This scene state is temporally aligned with two Franka Research 3 arms, enabling multi-robot actuation conditioned on live human and object state. Continuous capture within this consistent frame further accumulates behavioral data over time, providing trajectory priors that improve both safety responses and antici-patory assistance.

Robot Setup. Our system deploys two Franka Research 3 arms, each equipped with a parallel-jaw gripper at the end-effector. 

Hardware Synchronization. All RGB cameras are synchronized via an exter-nal hardware trigger. We extract timestamps from a reference camera to trigger the robot’s data reception, ensuring that all detection and reconstruction outputs are temporally aligned. 

3.3 System Calibration 

Placing 48 cameras and two robot arms in a shared world frame requires three calibration stages. First, each camera is intrinsically calibrated with ChArUco boards [12] using Zhang’s method [59]. Second, we recover extrinsic poses by solving PnP [14, 37] across 16 distributed ChArUco boards, unifying all cam-eras into a single world frame via a bipartite pose graph, and refining jointly with bundle adjustment over board poses, camera poses, and optionally intrin-sics. This custom pipeline outperforms COLMAP [41] in our setting because it avoids accuracy degradation in regions without calibration patterns. Third, OmniRobotHome 7

each robot base is registered into the camera world frame via hand-eye cali-bration [45], placing the full kinematic chain in the unified coordinate frame. Detailed formulations are provided in the supplementary material. 

3.4 Real-Time Perception 

Fig. 3 summarizes the two perception pipelines and their throughput as a func-tion of scene complexity. 

Multi-View Human Pose Estimation. The 48 cameras generate approxi-mately 13.5 GB/s of raw data. We handle this with a distributed architecture: each of 12 edge nodes runs TensorRT-optimized YOLO26 [40] (INT8) for detec-tion and RTMPose [17] (FP16) for 2D whole-body keypoint estimation following COCO WholeBody [18], streaming only keypoints and confidence scores to a central server. The server triangulates 3D joints via RANSAC-based multi-view reconstruction in a parallel process and applies a One Euro Filter [4] for temporal smoothing, delivering stable whole-body pose estimates to the robot controller. 

Object 6D Pose Estimation. Our system uses only RGB cameras, as dedi-cated depth sensors lack the hardware synchronization support needed across 48 streams. We adopt a fully learned pipeline on a calibrated stereo pair: Founda-tionStereo [50] (TensorRT-optimized) provides dense metric depth, YOLO-E [40] generates segmentation masks, and FoundationPose [51] performs marker-free 6D tracking from a template mesh alone. For objects without CAD models, we reconstruct meshes offline using MV-SAM3D [5] from our dense camera array and align them to the world frame via multi-view silhouette reprojection. The pipeline runs at approximately 6 Hz, sufficient for the typical velocities of ma-nipulated objects.

Intention-aware transfer. The robot must infer which object a human needs and deliver it with timing conditioned on real-time pose. The pipeline proceeds in three stages: (1) the system detects the human in the workspace via multi-view 3D tracking; (2) a VLM infers the target object from the human’s 3D pose and surrounding scene context (objects and spatial configuration around the human); and (3) the robot executes a pick-handover-place sequence from the shelf to the human’s hand, with the handover target continuously updated from the human’s real-time 3D hand keypoints rather than committed to a fixed pose. The transfer initiates when short-horizon cues indicate readiness and delays when the human disengages. We demonstrate three scenarios (Fig. 6): (a) a human notices a dry plant and the robot delivers a watering kettle, (b) a human prepares to eat a hot dog and the robot hands over a mustard bottle, and (c) a human eating chips gestures for a drink and the robot delivers a soft drink. In all cases, the system successfully identifies the needed object from pose and scene cues and adapts the handover trajectory online as the human’s reach evolves.

View coverage and camera count (Fig. 7). In close-proximity human-robot interaction, humans and objects are frequently occluded by furniture, robot arms, and each other, necessitating dense camera coverage for robust track-ing. To determine how many cameras suffice, we simulate sparser setups by subsampling from the full 48-camera set via farthest-point removal. We mea-sure two metrics: joint visibility (joints seen by ≥2 views) and the triangulation error bound 1/ sin θ. Above 40 cameras, unobserved joints become negligible (Fig. 7 (a)). The triangulation error bound stabilizes even earlier, beyond ap-proximately 35 cameras (Fig. 7 (d)). Per-frame distributions further confirm the trend: 12 or 24 cameras still show large fluctuations, whereas 36 cameras already match the full set (Fig. 7 (e,f)). Beyond joint-level tracking, we evaluate volumet-ric room coverage (Fig. 7 (g,h)). Voxel coverage increases steadily with camera count; at 48 cameras, over 80% of room voxels are observed by four or more views (Fig. 7 (g)), ensuring reliable triangulation throughout the workspace. The top-down visibility maps (Fig. 7 (h)) show this progression spatially: at 12 cameras, large dead zones persist in corners and behind furniture; by 36 cameras, coverage gaps shrink substantially; at 48 cameras, dead zones are eliminated across the full 23.1 m 2 room. These results confirm that 48 cameras provide reliable joint tracking and complete volumetric coverage in our capture environment. For more economical setups, 30 to 35 cameras offer adequate pose accuracy, though some spatial dead zones remain. 

# 5 Conclusion 

We presented OmniRobotHome , a room-scale residential platform that makes multiadic human-robot collaboration experimentally tractable. The system in-struments a 23.1 m 2 home environment with 48 hardware-synchronized RGB cameras for markerless, occlusion-robust 3D tracking of multiple humans and objects in a unified world frame, temporally aligned with two robot arms for multi-robot actuation conditioned on live scene state. Continuous capture within this consistent frame further accumulates behavioral trajectories that the system converts into priors for safer and more anticipatory assistance. Controlled studies on two central problems isolate the contribution of each capability. For safety in shared workspaces, conditioning on real-time 3D human state breaks the safety-throughput tradeoff that static-radius policies cannot re-solve, reducing collisions by 2.6 times at improved cycle time, and accumulated behavior memory further reduces collisions with no throughput penalty by an-ticipating human motion before it occurs. For human-anticipatory assistance, partial observation of a resident’s behavioral history already disambiguates in-OmniRobotHome 15 

tent: the system reaches full placement accuracy from half the demonstration se-quence, while a copy baseline requires the complete sequence. Behavior memory reveals complementary dynamics across the two problems: safety benefits from a compact early observation window that captures dominant movement patterns, while intent prediction requires demonstrations spanning both object categories before converging. Qualitative demonstrations of intention-aware transfer extend the coupling further: the system infers the target object from real-time 3D human pose and scene context and adapts the handover trajectory online as the human’s reach evolves. System ablations confirm that the infrastructure supports these results, with calibration achieving 1.21 px mean reprojection error versus 1.42 px for COLMAP and 48 cameras providing over 80% volumetric coverage across the full room. Across both problems, the common enabling factor is continu-ous room-scale perception in a unified world frame: real-time 3D state drives reactive safety and timing-sensitive interaction, while accumulated behavioral priors improve anticipatory performance, together making the multiadic regime observable and actionable for the first time.

Our evaluation uses a single instrumented room with two fixed-base arms and task-specific controllers; generalization across lay-outs, embodiments, and control interfaces remains open. Future work includes integrating mobile robots, expanding to diverse room configurations, and re-leasing synchronized multiadic sequences with calibrated 3D trajectories, object poses, robot states, and task annotations. # A System Details 

A.1 Hardware Details Camera configuration. The perception system comprises a synchronized net-work of 48 industrial RGB cameras (FLIR Blackfly S BFLY-PGE-31S4C-C) capturing 2048 × 1536 images at 30 Hz. We employ a hybrid lens configuration to balance environmental context with fine-grained detail: 24 wide-angle lenses (f =3 mm) provide broad spatial coverage for human pose estimation, while 24 telephoto lenses ( f =6 mm) enable detailed capture—of which 8 are arranged as 4 stereo pairs for real-time 6D object pose estimation. Exposure is globally fixed at 2.5 ms to minimize motion blur while maintaining an adequate signal-to-noise ratio. 

Synchronization and data acquisition. Frame-level synchronization is en-forced via a hardware trigger (10 Vpp square wave, 50% duty cycle) generated by a UNI-T UTG962 unit and distributed through a hierarchical chain of 8-port GPIO signal distributors. Data acquisition is distributed across 12 capture nodes connected via GigE Vision interfaces, each managing four cameras. Ten nodes are dedicated to pose estimation (40 cameras), while two nodes each host two calibrated stereo pairs for 6D object pose estimation (8 cameras). A dedicated server aggregates all data streams in real-time. Each capture node is equipped with dual local SSDs (500 GB + 1 TB) for high-bandwidth buffering, and 15 LED panels (4500 lm each) provide uniform illumination throughout the workspace. 

A.2 Extrinsic Calibration Pipeline 

Placing 48 cameras in a shared world frame requires a robust extrinsic calibration pipeline. A common choice is COLMAP [41], but it requires pattern placement throughout the scene and its accuracy degrades when tracking objects in regions where patterns were missing during calibration. A.5 Multi-View Human Pose Estimation Pipeline 

Our human detection pipeline operates on a distributed client-server architec-ture, where each capture node independently performs 2D pose estimation and transmits results to a central server for multi-view 3D triangulation. 

2D Pose Estimation. Each capture node processes frames from its designated cameras through a two-stage detection pipeline. First, a TensorRT-optimized YOLO26 [40] detector performs batch inference on full-resolution frames ( 2048 ×

1536 ) to localize persons with a confidence threshold of 0.5. To mitigate momen-tary occlusions, we employ a temporal carry-over mechanism that propagates the last valid bounding box for up to 3 frames with a confidence decay factor of 0.85. Detected person regions are cropped, affine-warped to 384 × 288 , and fed into an RTMPose [17] model (also TensorRT-accelerated). The model out-puts 133 keypoints per person via SimCC [30] decoding, covering the body (17), feet (6), face (68), and hands (42). Finally, 2D keypoints are undistorted using calibrated lens parameters before transmission. 

Data Aggregation. Clients transmit keypoint data to the server via ZMQ PUSH/PULL sockets using msgpack serialization. The server tracks the liveness of each camera and promotes a frame for triangulation only when at least 60% of the active pose cameras (minimum 4) report data within a 60-frame window, ensuring sufficient multi-view coverage. OmniRobotHome 27 

Cross-Camera Person Association. When multiple persons are present, we adopt a two-stage association strategy inspired by [7]: the first stage tracks confirmed persons via projection matching, while the second stage initializes newly appearing persons through geometric consensus. In Stage 1, for each tracked person p with known 3D joint positions Jp, we project the four torso keypoints (hips and shoulders) into every camera view using the calibrated projection matrices. To handle brief occlusions, we apply a velocity-corrected prediction ˆJp = Jp + vp · nmiss , where vp is an exponentially weighted moving average of per-frame displacement with momentum α = 0 .7 and displacement clamped at 0.5 m/frame, and nmiss counts consecutive unmatched frames. For each camera, we construct a cost matrix of Euclidean distances be-tween the projected and detected 2D positions, using a depth-adaptive threshold 

τ = τbase · (dref /d cam ) (τbase =150 px, dref =3 .0 m) to compensate for perspective foreshortening. The Hungarian algorithm [27] is then applied per camera to ob-tain optimal assignments. In Stage 2, unmatched 2D detections across cameras are considered as can-didates for new person initialization. We extract the hip midpoint from each unmatched detection, enumerate all pairwise camera combinations, and triangu-late via DLT. Each triangulated candidate is reprojected into all cameras, and we count inlier views whose reprojection error falls below 20 pixels. Candidates are greedily accepted in order of decreasing inlier count, requiring agreement from at least 3 cameras; accepting a candidate suppresses all 2D detections it claims, preventing duplicate initializations. We manage track lifecycles with three mechanisms. First, a confirmation gate 

requires newly detected persons to be consistently matched for 3 consecutive frames before their keypoints enter the triangulation pool. Second, persons that receive no match for 60 frames are pruned from the active set. Third, a re-identification cache retains the last known 3D position of pruned persons for 90 frames ( ∼3 s); if a newly initialized person appears within 1 m of a cached position, the original identity is restored, preserving continuity across temporary disappearances. 

3D Triangulation. Of the 133 detected keypoints, we triangulate only the 65 non-face keypoints (body, feet, and hands), as face landmarks are too fine-grained for room-scale 3D reconstruction. For each confirmed person in a synchronized frame, the associated 2D keypoint observations from matched camera views are triangulated using the Direct Linear Transform (DLT). Each keypoint is solved independently by constructing an overdetermined system from the 2D observa-tions and corresponding projection matrices, followed by an SVD-based solution. Triangulation is restricted to keypoints observed in at least two camera views with a confidence score exceeding 3.0, where the score is defined as √sx · sy from the SimCC [30] logit maxima along each spatial axis. To suppress outlier detec-tions, we enforce RANSAC-based rejection with a 3-view minimal sample set, running up to 30 iterations, and a reprojection error threshold of 2 pixels. 

Temporal Smoothing. Triangulated 3D joints are processed by per-joint One Euro Filters [4] ( fmin = 1 .0 Hz, β = 0 .007 ) to adaptively balance jitter reduction 28 Lee et al. 

during stationarity and responsiveness during fast motion. To prevent drift dur-ing long occlusions, filter states are re-initialized if a joint remains undetected for more than 10 consecutive frames.

B.4 Simulation Environment 

We evaluate all safety policies in a custom 3D simulation that replays recorded human trajectories alongside robot task execution, enabling reproducible and high-throughput comparison under identical conditions. 

Simulation architecture. The simulator runs an internal physics loop at 1 000 Hz, matching the Franka Research 3 control frequency, while the 3D vi-sualization [57] is rendered at 60 Hz. Human trajectories are replayed from 30 Hz skeleton recordings captured from four subjects; each frame is linearly interpolated to the internal rate. The robot simultaneously executes pipelined pick-handover-place task trajectories, with the safety-trigger policy under test governing pause and fallback decisions at every control step. 

Collision geometry. We approximate the robot tool-center-point (TCP) as a sphere with radius rtcp = 0 .08 m and the human body as a vertical cylinder with radius rh = 0 .3 m and height Hh = 1 .8 m centered at the hip. At each collision-check step, the signed distance between the sphere and cylinder is computed analytically: the horizontal distance between centers is reduced by both radii, and the vertical extent is clamped to the cylinder height. A collision is registered when the signed distance falls below zero.

B.5 Behavior Learning and Memory Learning mechanism The Dynamic + Behavior Learning policy extends the dynamic cylinder baseline (Sec. B.6 Intention-Aware Transfer Pipeline 

The preceding sections address robot-to-robot object transfer; this section tack-les the complementary problem of delivering an object to a human based on inferred intention. Because the multi-camera infrastructure provides both real-time 3D human body tracking and 6D object pose estimation concurrently, the robot can reason jointly about what the person needs and where to deliver it. We describe a fully automated pipeline: once the user issues a trigger, multi-view perception, intention inference, object selection, and physical delivery proceed without further human intervention. Upon receiving the trigger signal, the system executes the following sequence: (1) multi-view image capture, (2) 3D human body pose extraction via triangu-lation, (3) vision-language model (VLM) based intention inference, (4) object identification, and (5) Pick-Handover-Place (PHP) execution with the handover target set to the human’s hand. 

Multi-view scene representation Inferring human intention from a single viewpoint is inherently ambiguous: occlusion, oblique perspectives, and limited spatial context can all lead to incorrect readings of the person’s gaze, gesture, or surrounding objects. To mitigate these issues, we select six camera views from the 48-camera array and composite them into a single 2×3 grid image. The resulting mosaic provides comprehensive spatial coverage of the person’s posture, OmniRobotHome 37 

gaze direction, and environmental context from multiple complementary angles. Among the six views, a single view specifically captures the tabletop region where graspable candidate objects are arranged, allowing the VLM to observe both the person’s ongoing activity and the available objects within a single input frame. 

3D human body keypoints In addition to the visual grid, we supply the VLM with quantitative geometric cues derived from 3D human body tracking. Our multi-view system reconstructs 16 body keypoints—from pelvis to wrists— in world coordinates via multi-view triangulation. These keypoints compactly encode body pose, facing direction, hand extension, and spatial location, com-plementing the qualitative visual information with structured numerical input. 

VLM-based chain-of-thought inference We employ GPT-5.2 with high-effort reasoning to perform structured intention inference. The model receives three inputs: the six-view grid image, the 3D body keypoints, and a prede-fined candidate list of 23 everyday items spanning food, kitchenware, household tools, and personal belongings. Each candidate has a corresponding 3D mesh generated through an automated reconstruction pipeline using MV-SAM3D [5]. Rather than directly predicting the target object, we instruct the model to rea-son in five explicit steps: (i) identify which candidates are physically present on the tabletop and thus reachable by the robot; (ii) describe the person’s current activity by examining all six viewpoints holistically; (iii) infer the person’s under-lying intention from gaze, gesture, body pose, and situational context; (iv) rank the top five most plausible objects from the reachable subset; and (v) select the single best object with contrastive reasoning explaining why each runner-up is less suitable. This chain-of-thought formulation grounds the selection in observable evidence rather than generic priors, and the intermediate outputs— reachable list, activity description, and intention analysis—provide interpretable justification for the final decision. 

Handover execution Once the target object is identified, the PHP pipeline grasps it from the tabletop using 6D pose estimation and delivers it to the human. The handover target pose is continuously updated using the person’s real-time 3D hand keypoints from the multi-view tracking system. Because full-body tracking runs concurrently, the delivery destination naturally adapts as the person moves, enabling fluid handover without predefined waypoints or static target positions. The full VLM prompt is reproduced in Sec. B.8. 

B.7 Intent-Aware Placement Task 

This section details the intent-aware placement prediction task reported in Tab. 2 of the main paper. The robot must infer the correct placement location (sink or shelf) for each object after observing a variable number of human demonstra-tions. 38 Lee et al. 

Object set. We use 12 everyday objects divided into two semantic categories:

Lookup baseline. This non-learned baseline maintains a lookup table mapping each observed object category to its demonstrated placement. For test objects whose category has been seen in the demonstrations, it copies the observed placement; for unseen categories it resorts to a uniform random guess. Lookup is purely analytical and does not invoke any language model. 

LLM baseline. We query Claude Sonnet 4.5 (temperature = 0.0) with a domain-neutral prompt that enumerates the demonstrations as text and asks the model to predict placements for the remaining objects. The prompt explic-itly instructs: “Do NOT use prior knowledge about these objects; base your an-swer solely on the provided examples.” This tests the model’s ability to perform pattern extraction without chain-of-thought scaffolding. 

+ Reasoning baseline. The same model and temperature are used, but the prompt is augmented with a chain-of-thought instruction: “First, figure out the underlying pattern from the demonstrations. Then, apply the pattern to predict the placement for each remaining object.” The output is requested in a structured JSON format to enable automated parsing. No category names or semantic hints are provided. 

Query volume. Across all 13 percentages and 15 combinations each, the LLM and + Reasoning baselines require approximately 1,170 LLM queries in total. We use prompt caching to reduce latency and cost.
