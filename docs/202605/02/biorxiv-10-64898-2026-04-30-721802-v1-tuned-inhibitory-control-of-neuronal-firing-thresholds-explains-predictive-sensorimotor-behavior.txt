Abstract 15 

Prior expectations guide sensorimotor behavior when sensory information is uncertain, yet the cellular 16 

mechanisms underlying this integration remain elusive. Here, we investigate how priors  in sensory 17 

motion direction  shape neural population dynamics by combining recurrent neural network (RNN) 18 

modeling with macaque smooth pursuit behavior and  middle temporal area ( area MT ) electrophysiology. 19 

Our RNN model reveals that prior expectations are implemented by elevating firing thresholds in 20 

neurons tuned away from the expected direction. This selective inhibition sharpens population tuning 21 

and reduces behavioral variability under weak sensory information conditions. We validated this 22 

prediction  in vivo : delta -band local field potentials in area MT, wh ere  the  phase  reflects  neural 23 

excitability  states, exhibited direction -specific phase shifts that scaled with the deviation from the 24 

expected direction. These findings demonstrate that prior expectations enhance behavioral reliability 25 

through tuned inhibitory control of neuronal excitability, providing a mechanistic link between 26 

Bayesian inference and cortical circuit dynamics. 27

Introduction 35 

Perception and action are tightly coupled processes that enable organisms to  adapt  efficiently to 36 

dynamically  changing  environments.  Efficient sensorimotor transformation often  relies on  integrating 37 

multiple sources of information —including sensory inputs —to guide motor actions, with each source 38 

weighted according to its reliability 1-5. This  process, consistent with  Bayesian in ference , provides a 39 

principled computational framework for how  predictions  derived from prior knowledge are  dynamically 40 

combined with incoming sensory information. When sensory information is highly reliable,  behavior is 41 

dominated by  current sensory inputs; when it is uncertain or noisy, prior knowledge exerts a stronger 42 

influence. 43 

Previous studies have demonstrated neural representations of prior knowledge across multiple brain 44 

regions, including the prefrontal cortex, parietal cortex, and even sensory cortices 6-10 . Several studies 45 

have further revealed neural mechanisms underlying reliability -based integration,  whereby  sensory 46 

representations are refined by prior expectation. Such expectation -induced enhancement of sensory 47 

information can be explained by changes in neural tuning functions for specific stimulus features. In 48 

particular , shape changes in population tuning functions —such as sharpening of orientation or motion -49 

direction tuning —have been proposed as key neural substrates for prior -induced perceptual biases or 50 

increases in perceptual reliability 5,11 -13 . Sharpening of population tuning functions naturally explains 51 

enhanced sensory representations and thus provides a population -level mechanism for how prior 52 

expectations improve sensory processing. However, the  cellular -level mechanisms  by which prior 53 

expectations modulate population tuning functions remain poorly understood. 54 

At the cellular level, s harpening of population tuning  may arise from  several mechanisms , including 55 

tuned modulation of  neuronal  firing threshold s, regulation of  input or response gain , or  combinations 56 

of the se processes . However,  directly testing the causal roles of these mechanisms remains 57 

experimentally challenging,  as current techniques do not allow selective and targeted control of firing 58 

thresholds or gains across specific subsets of neurons within a population.  Computational modeling —59 

particularly recurrent neural network (RNN) modeling —offers a powerful complementary approach to 60 

overcome  these limitations 14 -16 . RNNs have become increasingly important in neuroscience because 61 

they provide full access to network dynamics, connectivity, and unit -level activity, enabling causal tests 62 

of mechanistic hypotheses that are difficult or impossible to perform experimentally. Leveraging these 63 

advantages, we used RNNs  not merely as task -performing models  but  as controlled testbeds for 64 

hypothesis -driven investigation of  cellular -level  mechanisms  underlying prior -dependent modulation 65 

of  population  tuning.  Specifically , we focused on the possibility that prior expectations modulate the 66 

input –output function s of individual neurons within a  population —a form  of population -specific gain 67

In this study, we investigated these cellular -level mechanisms by constraining RNN models with 70 

behavioral and neural data  recorded  from monkeys.  Building on prior work that demonstrated 71 

expectation -dependent changes in behavior and population tuning —such as tuning sharpening under 72 

low sensory  reliability 13 , we  leveraged  these experimental  observation  to  both  constrain and evaluate 73 

our  computational models.  We  focused on  monkeys performing a smooth pursuit eye movement task —74 

a well -established paradigm for probing real -time sensorimotor transformation. By independently 75 

manipulating sensory and prior information, we examined how prior knowledge shapes pursuit behavior 76 

across different levels of sensory reliability. Using these behavioral data together with neural population 77

model identifies  tuned inhibitory control of neuronal firing thresholds  as  a plausible mechanism for 79 

prior -induced behavioral and neural modulation . We  further examined  this mechanism  in a spiking 80

analyzing local field potentials (LFPs) recorded from area MT. These recordings allowed us to test 82 

whether sensory cortical activity exhibits signatures of prior -induced inhibitory modulation consistent 83 

with the model’s predictions. Together, this integrative approach links behavioral signatures of 84 

reliability -weighted Bayesian inference to plausible cellular -level mechanisms in sensory cortex. 85

Prior expectations sharpen sensory cortical population responses and improve pursuit reliability 87 

under sensory uncertainty 88 

We trained two monkeys to perform a smooth pursuit eye movement task. Each trial began with fixation 89 

on a central dot , followed by the appearance of a moving dot patch. The monkeys were required to track 90 

the moving target with their eyes (Fig. 1A). We manipulated two factors: the strength of sensory 91 

information and the  strength of prior expectation. Sensory information was controlled by stimulus 92 

contrast , with  high contrast (100%)  providing  strong sensory evidence and low contrast ( 10% )93 

providing  weak sensory evidence  (Fig. 1B) . Prior expectation  was manipulated  through  block struc ture: 94 

on a given day, we selected a motion direction as the ‘prior’ direction. I n the  no prior  bl ock,  motion 95 

directions were spaced 120 ° apart  around the prior direction , resulting in high directional uncertainty, 96 

whereas  in the  prior  block , directions were clustered within 15 ° of the prior direction , allowing monkeys 97 

to develop a strong direction al  expectation  (Fig. 1C) . For example, both blocks included  the  common 98 

prior  motion direction ( e.g.,  60 °), but its contextual meaning differed: in the  no prior  block , this 99 

direction  was one of three widely separated possibilities, while in the  prior  block , it  was strongly 100 

expected. This design yielded four experimental conditions (2 sensory  × 2 prior). 101 

From a Bayesian perspective, combining sensory evidence with prior expectation leads to two key 102 

behavioral  predictions. First, when sensory information is unreliable, the  estimated motion direction 103 

should be biased toward the prior, whereas this bias should be minimal when sensory evidence is strong. 104 

Second, prior expectations should reduce uncertainty in the estimated direction, resulting in lower  trial -105 

to -trial variability,  especially under weak sensory  conditions . Thus, Bayesian integration predicts both 106 

a systematic directional bias and a reduction in behavioral variability when priors are available, with 107 

both effects being strongest under low sensory reliability. 108 

Consistent with these predictions, we observed  a clear interaction  between  sensory reliability and prior 109 

expectation.  In the  no prior  block , both high - and low -contrast stimuli induced  similar eye trajector ies 110 

across the three directions separated by  120 ° (Fig. 1D) . However, in the  prior  block , low -contrast 111 

stimuli produced biased trajectories toward the expected 60 ° direction,  especially  for nearby stimulus 112 

directions of  45 ° and 75 °. Statistically, prior  expectation  induced a stronger directional bias toward the 113 

prior direction under low  contrast  compared with high contrast (paired  t-test,  t(125) = 21.599,  P =114 

5.154 ×10 -44 ; Fig. 1E , Supplementary Fig ure  1A).  This indicates that when sensory evidence was weak, 115 

prior expectations guided pursuit behavior. In addition, prior expectations reduced trial -to -trial 116 

variability in  pursuit . This effect was  evident under  low -contrast  conditions , where  variability was high 117 

without priors but substantially reduced with  priors  across trials  (ANOVA,  F(3, 500 ) = 103 .634 , P =118

responses in  the  middle temporal area (area MT)  of monkeys , a key region for motion processing. 125 

Previous studies have  shown  that behavioral improvements  associated with  prior expectations  are 126 

accompanied by  sharpening of neural population tuning, which enhances the precision of  sensory 127 

representations 11,13 . Consistent with our prior report , and using the same MT recordings, we  confirmed 128 

this sharpening by reconstructing  population neural responses  from  actual neural recordings  (see 129 

Methods for detail) 13 . Specifically,  prior expectations sharpened  population direction tuning in area MT 130

these population -level effects using mechanistic RNN and LFP analyses. 138

by both biasing estimated motion direction and reducing behavioral variability when sensory  inputs  are 140 

ambiguous . At the neural level, these effects are supported by prior -dependent sharpening of population 141 

neural tuning  in area MT,  consistent with Bayesian integration of sensory evidence and prior 142 

knowledge 3,17,18 .143

Recurrent neural network  modeling 188 

Emergence of  area  MT -like direction selectivity in a  neural network  trained  on the  smooth pursuit  eye 189 

movement  task 190 

To investigate  the  potential  cellular  mechanisms underlying the behavioral effects of prior expectation, 191 

we  first  constructed a n RNN model  that was  designed to reproduce smooth pursuit eye movements  (Fig. 192 

2A) . The RNN was trained  using  the  target motion  of  a moving dot patch as  the  input and  the monkey ’s193 

act ual eye movements  as  the  output.  Eye  movement  traces were obtained from two monkeys across 194 

multiple  recording  days . For training  RNN , we used daily averaged eye traces exclusively from the  no 195 

prior  condition. This training strategy was adopted to first establish a baseline network capable of 196 

generating accurate pursuit behavior and  area  MT -like direction selectivity  in RNN units , independent 197 

of prior  expectations . This approach allowed us to first  isolat e the core sensorimotor transformation 198 

required for pursuit  and  then  examine  how prior -related mechanisms could emerge or be implemented 199 

on top of an already functional pursuit network.  During training, the model ’s performance  improved, 200 

and  the  difference  between  the  predicted and  actual  pursuit traces decreased  steadily  (Fig. 2B). 201 

Following  training, the RNN  successfully generated pursuit trajectories that closely matched the 202 

monkey ’s eye movements  in  all  directions of  motion ( Fig.  2C , D). The  individual  RNN  units  comprising 203 

the  neural network  showed  robust  response s to  a specific  motion direction  (Fig . 2E) , and  exhibited 204 

tuning curves  that  closely resembled those observed in  area MT  (Fig . 2F, G) . When  the neural 205 

population activity was aligned  with  the  actual motion direction at  0°, the  neural network  produced a 206 

typical  population  direction  tuning curve (Fig. 2 H), similar  to  the previous study that mimicked the 207 

motor cortical responses to arm reaching behavior 19 . This confirmed that the model  not only  captured 208 

pursuit  dynamics  but  also developed neural representations that mirrored empirical observations at both 209 

the  single -cell and population levels. 210

Emergent threshold adjustments reproduce prior -dependent effects on pursuit behavior and population 232 

neural responses 233 

After establishing a neural network that reproduced both smooth pursuit behavior and MT -like direction 234 

selectivity, we next asked how prior expectations could be incorporated into this circuit. For any 235 

candidate mechanism to be plausible, it should account not only for behavioral effects  on  pursuit eye 236 

movements but also for the sharpening of neural population tuning observed under prior expectation 237 

(Fig. 1H, I).  Such modulation could plausibly arise from changes in  neuronal excitability, sensitivity to 238 

input, or response dynamic range.  Accordingly , we hypothesized that prior -dependent effects could be 239 

implemented through modulation of neuronal input -output functions, specifically by adjusting the 240 

spiking  nonlinearities of individual units. To test this  hypothesis , we introduced parametric modulation 241 

of each unit's activation function and  optimized  these parameters to reproduce the observed behavioral 242 

bias. Three candidate modulatory parameters were considered (Supplementary Figure 2): (i) threshold, 243 

which controls the input level required for activation (horizontal shift); (ii) input gain, which  scales 244 

sensitivity (slope); and (iii) response gain, which determines the maximum firing rate (amplitude). 245

This approach was motivated by prior work  showing  that modulatory signals  in motor cortical circuits 255 

can shape complex motor dynamics by training the gain parameter s of  individual neurons’  input -output 256 

functions 21 . More broadly,  our approach builds on  extensive  previous studies showing that neuron -257 

specific threshold or gain modulations can shape population dynamics 22 -25 . Crucially, to isolate the 258 

effect of prior expectation, synaptic weights were  held  fixed after the initial training phase  (Fig. 2) ; only 259 

the modulatory parameters were subsequently optimized using the monkey’s pursuit bias as the  sole 260 

objective . As a result , while the reproduction of pursuit bias was a  direct  outcome  of  optimization , the 261 

reduction in trial -to -trial pursuit variability and the sharpening of neural population tuning were not 262 

explicitly constrained . These  emergent properties  therefore  serve as  independent  model validation s,263 

arising  only if the  identified modulatory mechanism  provides a plausible account of the experimental 264 

data .265 

To identify  which  parameters  drove  these effects, we optimized each parameter individually while 266 

holding the others fixed.  Threshold  modulation alone was sufficient to reproduce both the  behavioral 267 

and  neural  signatures  of the experimental observations.  At the population level, threshold modulation 268 

sharpened direction tuning by selectively suppressing neurons whose preferred directions were distant 269 

from the prior (Fig.  3A).  Under high -contrast conditions,  population  tuning curves were similar 270 

regardless of whether  the  threshold modulation was applied (paired  t-test,  t(99) = 1.591,  P = 0.115; Fig. 271 

3B).  Under low -contrast conditions,  however,  threshold modulation  produced a pronounced  sharpening 272 

of the tuning curve (ANOVA,  F(3, 396 ) = 9670 .24 9, P < 0.001; paired  t-test,  t(99) = 62.735,  P =273 

1.603 × 10 -81 ).  This selective sharpening reproduced the tuning changes observed in area MT, even 274 

though  preferred directions of individual units  were not explicitly  constrained  during RNN training.  A275 

similar pattern  emerged  for pursuit variability. Trial -to -trial variability did not differ between  prior  and 276 

no  prior  conditions under high -contrast (paired  t-test,  t(99) = 1.929,  P = 0.057; Fig.  3D,  E), but was 277 

significantly reduced by prior  expectation  under low -contrast (ANOVA,  F(3, 396) = 6796.226,  P <278 

0.001; paired  t-test,  t(99) = 58.420,  P = 1.545 × 10⁻ ⁷⁸ ), consistent with behavioral observations in 279 

monkeys (Fig. 1G). In the behavioral data, pursuit variability (mean ± SD) decreased from 14.283 ± 280 

3.988 in the low -contrast,  no  prior  condition to 11.649 ± 3.139 in the low -contrast,  prior  condition. The 281

Predicted neural and behavioral changes during  spontaneous activity  emerg ing  from  the  threshold 382 

modulation 383 

Prior knowledge , acquired through repeated experience , can  become embedded within the neural 384 

network.  As shown in area MT neurons previously 13  (Supplementary figure 6A), the neural 385 

representation of this prior persists even in the absence of external sensory stimuli (the spontaneous 386 

activity period).  To simulate this, we injected  Gaussian noise into the trained  RNN during the period 387 

corresponding to the fixation window. This noisy input allows the network to operate in a baseline state, 388 

revealing how intrinsic prior information generates directional biases. 389 

We found that the threshold modulation of the  input -output  function  showed the neural and behavioral 390 

signatures of the prior expectation while other parameter modulations (input gain, response gain) did 391 

not.  When  direction -selective threshold modulation was applied,  noise injection  revealed  that neurons 392 

with preferred directions farther from the prior direction (0°) showed a greater reduction in spontaneous 393 

activity, resulting in direction -selective tuning centered on the prior direction (Fig.  4A).  These neural 394 

changes appeared to produce  spontaneous eye drifts toward the prior direction even in the absence of 395 

sensory input  (Fig.  4B) . These  model predictions were consistent with  experimental observ ations in 396 

monkeys : spontaneous activity in the  prior  condition was tuned to the  prior  direction when compared 397 

with the  no prior  condition  (Supplementary f igure  6A, B ).398 

In contrast, modulation of input gain  or  response gain  failed to  reproduce these  neural and behavioral 399 

effects.  Under these conditions, n oise  injections  did not generate  direction -selective tuning in 400 

spontaneous neural activity , nor did it produce  systematic behavioral biases toward the prior direction .401 

These results  indicat e that  input gain and response gain modulation are insufficient to  intrinsically 402 

encode prior expectations  in spontaneous  activity .403 

In summary, our RNN modeling demonstrates that prior expectations can be implemented through 404 

increased thresholds in neuronal  input -output  function s. This mechanism operates by reducing the 405 

baseline excitability of neurons misaligned with the prior direction, thereby sharpening neural 406 

population activity, suppressing behavioral variability, and biasing pursuit behavior toward the expected 407 

direction . Consequently, it enhances behavioral  accuracy and reliability under uncertain sensory 408 

conditions. 409

Our modeling approach has focused on modulation of  neuronal  input -output functions, rather than 430 

changes  in synaptic connectivity,  as a mechanism for  implement ing  the effects of prior expectation. To 431 

directly compare this approach with more  conventional  frameworks, we next tested a standard weight -432 

based RNN model in which prior information was provided explicitly as an additional input signal , and 433 

its effects were learned through synaptic weight optimization  (Fig.  5A).  Using  the same pursuit bias 434 

measurements  as in the  input -output function parameter  optimization , we optimized  the input, recurrent, 435 

and readout weights  to reproduce  the monkey ’s pursuit bias behavior,  while  keeping  the  input -output 436 

function  parameters  fixed  across  prior  conditions . This  approach  follows  standard RNN frameworks in 437 

which contextual modulation is  implemented  through weight -based adjustments  rather than changes in 438 

neuronal input -output properties 15,26 .439 

The weight -optimized model  successful ly reproduced  the behavioral pursuit bias, indicating that 440 

synaptic weight changes alone can account for the prior -induced shift in pursuit behavior ( paired  t-test, 441 

t(99) = 18.363,  P = 1.195 × 10⁻³³ ; Supplementary figure  7). In addition, the model  reproduced  a442

5D, E ).446

objective. Instead, introducing the prior input led to  a broaden ing of population tuning curves 450

Tuned inhibitory input to membrane potential accounts for prior -dependent modulation in spiking 491 

neural network. 492 

The threshold modulation identified in the RNN corresponds, at the biophysical level, to systematic 493 

shifts in membrane potential driven by inhibitory input. To directly test this interpretation, we 494 

constructed a spiking neural network model in which membrane potential was explicitly modulated by 495 

direction -selective inhibitory input. The network consisted of 500 leaky integrate -and -fire (LIF) 496 

neurons distributed along a circular axis of preferred motion directions, organized with structured 497 

recurrent connectivity following a bump attractor architecture 30,31 . When a directional stimulus was 498 

presented, neurons responded with spike trains determined by the match between the stimulus direction 499 

and their preferred directions, giving rise to a population activity "bump" centered on the stimulus. The 500 

represented motion direction was decoded from spiking responses using population vector decoding, 501 

allowing us to directly link neural population dynamics to predicted pursuit behavior (Fig. 6A). 502 

We introduced a direction -dependent decrease in membrane potential, such that neurons whose 503 

preferred directions were farther from the prior received stronger inhibitory input, reducing their 504 

excitability (Fig. 6A, B ; see Methods ). We then examined whether this biophysical implementation 505 

could reproduce the prior -dependent changes in neural and behavioral responses observed 506 

experimentally.  In the model,  prior  and  no prior  conditions were defined by the presence or absence of 507 

this membrane potential modulation, while high - and low -contrast conditions were determined by the 508 

strength of directional input to the network. 509 

Population neural activity showed no significant difference between the  prior  (expecting 0°) and  no 510 

prior  conditions when the target, moving toward 0°, was presented as a high -contrast motion stimulus 511 

(paired  t-test,  t(99) =  0.907 , P = 0. 367 ; Fig.  6C , D).  Under the low -contrast stimulus condition,  however, 512 

overall population activity was reduced and was further suppressed in the presence of prior  expectation 513 

compared with the  no prior  condition.  Specifically, neurons whose preferred directions  differ ed 514 

substantially from the prior direction exhibited the strongest response reduction. This suppression 515 

sharpened the population tuning curves,  indicating  that prior  expectation  enhances the precision of 516 

population coding when sensory evidence is weak (ANOVA,  F(3, 396) =  166363 .934,  P < 0.001; paired 517 

t-test,  t(99) =  221 .546 , P = 2.774 ×10 -135 ; Fig.  6C , D). 518

when prior  expectation  was present  (paired  t-test,  t(99) = 1 5.751 , P = 1.015 ×10 -28; Fig.  6E), consistent 522 

with the monkey s’ eye movements (Fig. 1D).  Second, pursuit variability did not differ between 523

Neural evidence of tuned synaptic inhibition observed in lo w-frequency local field potential activity 555 

Our modeling results suggest that prior expectations modulate sensorimotor behavior and population 556 

tuning by adjusting neuronal firing thresholds, implicating tuned synaptic inhibition as the underlying 557 

mechanism. To seek empirical evidence for this modulation  in vivo , we examined baseline pre -stimulus 558 

neural activity recorded from area MT in two monkeys performing the smooth pursuit task. Because 559 

direct measurement of intracellular membrane potentials in behaving primates remains technically 560 

challenging, we utilized the local field potential (LFP) as a proxy for local network excitability and 561 

subthreshold synaptic activity 32 .562 

While classical studies under anesthesia map the phase of low -frequency LFPs to discrete UP and 563 

DOWN membrane potential states 33 , low -frequency LFP signals are  also  known to continuously track 564 

network excitability in the awake brain. Okun et al. (2010) provided direct evidence for this link by 565 

showing, through simultaneous intracellular and extracellular recordings, that the LFP tracks 566 

subthreshold membrane potential fluctuations in unanesthetized animals 34 . Crucially, this relationship 567 

has been confirmed in behaving non -human primates: using  in vivo  whole -cell patch -clamp recordings 568 

in alert macaques, Tan et al. (2014) demonstrated that spontaneous subthreshold membrane potential 569 

excursions during visual fixation are tightly correlated with concurrent LFP deflections 35 . On the basis 570 

of these findings, we used the phase and power of low -frequency LFP signals as a continuous index of 571 

local network excitability and synaptic input, and examined whether pre -stimulus low -frequency LFP 572 

dynamics covaried with prior expectation in a direction -selective manner —serving as an empirical test 573 

of the tuned inhibition predicted by our model. 574

Discussion 673 

In this study, we investigated how prior expectations influence smooth pursuit eye movements and 674 

neural population tuning  using monkey behavioral experiments, recurrent neural network (RNN)  and 675 

biophysical spiking  neural  network model s, and local field potential (LFP) recordings from area MT. 676 

Our findings provide converging computational and physiological evidence  that prior expectations 677 

sharpen population responses and improve behavioral precision under sensory uncertainty,  and point to 678 

a cellular -level mechanism  through which  Bayesian inference  may be implemented  in  cortical circuits .679 

Monkeys’ pursuit eye movements  showed systematic biases toward the expected direction,  especially 680 

under low -contrast conditions where sensory reliability was reduced (Fig. 1 D, E ). Trial -to -trial 681 

variability was also reduced  in the presence of priors  (Fig. 1F, G), consistent with Bayesian frameworks 682 

in which priors exert greater influence when sensory evidence is weak 17,18 . These  results  align with 683 

previous studies showing that expectations  improve  perceptual estimates of motion , orientation,  and 684 

object location  under  ambiguous conditions 9,13,39 .685 

At the neural level,  earlier works have  shown  that  such  behavioral  effects  are accompanied by 686 

sharpening of population tuning 3,11,13,17,18 . Consistent with  these  findings, we observed that prior 687 

expectations sharpe n direction tuning in area MT  by  selectively  suppressi ng  responses  of neurons 688 

preferring  direction s inconsistent with the prior,  with the strongest effects under  noisy sensory 689 

conditions  (replicated in  Fig. 1H, I) .690 

Such  selective  suppression is thought to reduce noise in  population  activity, thereby  increas ing 691 

perceptual accuracy.  Importantly, tuned inhibitory mechanisms  are well established as a means of 692 

sharpen ing  feature selectivity  across  cortical circuits 40 -42 , supporting the plausibility that expectation -693 

related modulation in  area  MT operate s through similar principles.  For example, optogenetic activation 694 

of  parvalbumin -positive  interneuron  in mouse  primary visual cortex  sharpen s orientation tuning and 695 

improve s perceptual discrimination 41 . Similarly , in the rat somatosensory cortex, GABA A-mediated 696 

inhibition preferentially suppressed responses to non -principal whiskers,  whereas blocking  inhibition 697 

broaden s tuning 40 . Together, t hese  findings  across sensory modalities highlight  a general principle 698 

whereby  structured inhibitory inputs refine  population  tuning by selectively suppressing non -preferred 699 

features. 700

Threshold modulation (prediction) vs. Gain modulation (attention) 701 

A key contribution of our  modeling is th e identification of threshold modulation as the mechanism by 702 

which pr ior expectations  influence sensory processing. A ttentional  modulation is commonly described 703 

in terms of gain control:  feature attention is typically implemented via input gain, whereas spatial 704 

attention is associated with  response gain that  multiplicative ly  scal es neuronal output 43 -45 . However, 705 

systematically manipulat ing  either  input gain or response gain  in our RNNs  failed to reproduce the 706 

tuning sharpening and  variability  reduction  observed in the behavioral and neural data  (Supplementary 707 

figure  3, 4).708 

In contrast, threshold modulation —implemented as elevated firing thresholds for neurons tuned away 709 

from the expected direction —captured all major empirical effects. By selectively suppressing weakly 710 

driven, feature -irrelevant neurons without globally amplifying activity, this mechanism sharpened 711 

population tuning and reduced trial -to -trial variability under low sensory reliability (Fig. 3). These 712 

results generalized to a biophysical spiking network, in which threshold modulation was implemented 713 

as direction -dependent inhibitory shifts in membrane potential, reproducing the same pattern of tuning 714 

sharpening, behavioral bias, and variability reduction (Fig. 6). 715 

An important question raised by these results is why threshold modulation, but not gain modulation, 716 

can account for both reduc ed  pursuit variability and sharpen ed  population tuning under low -contrast 717 

conditions.  Input gain scales sensory  signals  and  response gain scales neuronal outputs ; however,  both 718 

act  largely proportional ly  across the population and therefore preserve the relative  tuning  structure 719 

across neurons.  As a result,  gain modulation lacks the selectivity  required  to preferentially suppress 720 

neurons whose preferred directions are inconsistent with the prior.  In contrast, threshold modulation 721 

introduces a nonlinear, selective effect: neurons receiving weak sensory drive —particularly those tuned 722 

far from the expected direction —are  preferentially silenced, whereas neurons aligned with the prior 723 

remain active.  This  selective suppression sharpens  population  tuning and reduces variability by limiting 724 

contributions from noisy, weakly driven neurons.  Because threshold shifts have minimal  effects  when 725 

sensory drive is strong, this mechanism  also  explains why prior -induced modulation  emerge s under low 726 

sensory reliability but  is  negligible under high -contrast conditions .727 

These results indicate that prediction  operates  through  a computational process  distinct from attention .728 

Rather than  selectively  facilitating relevant signals, priors act primarily by inhibiting irrelevant ones 729 

through  targeted  changes in  neuronal excitability. 730

Flexible tuning through neuronal nonlinearities 731 

A second central issue concerns how the brain flexibly incorporates expectations on  short timescale s,732 

even  on  a trial -by -trial basis . While prior information could be  implemented through changes in synaptic 733 

connectivity , rapidly varying expectations would make this strategy  computationally inefficient as a fast 734 

control mechanism. Our results instead highlight modulation of neuronal nonlinearities —such as firing 735 

thresholds , which  largely  depend on the overall  excitability state of the neuron —as a n effective  means 736 

of  flexibly reshaping functional responses without  altering  core sensory connectivity .737 

This does not imply that  synaptic mechanisms are irrelevant. Rather, prior expectations may  be 738 

implemented through s ynaptic  and circuit -level processes that manifest functionally as changes in 739 

neuronal input -output nonlinearities.  Neuronal physiology  provides  strong support for this view. 740 

Whereas  AMPA receptor -mediated currents  scale approximately  linearly with input  strength 46,47 ,741 

NMDA receptors exhibit  pronounced nonlinearity: channel s remain largely inactive until membrane 742 

depolarization reaches a  critical  lev el,  at which point Mg²⁺  block is relieved and  calcium influx 743 

occurs 48,49 . Such  threshold -dependent gating  enables  state -dependent  computations  without  large -scale 744 

synaptic  rewiring  and  support flexible  operations  including  memory consolidation , coincidence 745 

detection , and  flexible state transitions 50 -52 .746 

By analogy,  modulation of neuronal  input –output  nonlinearities  in area  MT  provides a  computationally 747 

efficient way to reshape population responses according to prior expectations while preserving stable 748 

feature selectivity (Fig. 3A). In our RNN analys is,  a standard approach —introducing prior information 749 

as an additional input and  updating synaptic weights  across conditions —led to  broad ened  tuning and 750 

elevated activity levels that were inconsistent with experimental observations  (Fig.  5). In contrast, 751 

implementing priors  through threshold  modulati on reproduced  both  the sharpen ing of  population  tuning 752 

and  the  reduc tion of trial -to -trial  variability observed  in  neural  activity  and behavior  (Fig.  3), with 753 

comparable effects obtained in the spiking network model through directionally tuned inhibitory 754 

modulation  of membrane potential  (Fig. 6). 755 

At the biological level, the tuned threshold modulation identified here could  be  realized  through 756 

multiple neural substrates , including  direction -selective inhibitory circuits,  shifts  in  excitation -757 

inhibition  balance mediated by  specific  interneuron  populations , or  neuromodulatory  influences  on 758 

baseline  excitability 53 -55 . Rather than specifying a single cellular mechanism, our results  point to  a759 

functional principle : direction -specific modulation of neuronal nonlinearity  shapes sensory responses 760 

under prior expectation .761

Finally,  th e distinction  between  incorporating contextual information through  synaptic weight -based 762 

integration  and through modulation of  neuronal  input -output nonlinearities may reflect broader 763 

architectural di fferences across cortical hierarchies.  Higher -order areas  such as  the  prefrontal cortex 764 

(PFC)  often  exhibit mixed selectivity and integrate  contextual variables  directly alongside sensory 765 

inputs 15,26 . For example, Mante et al. (2013)  modeled  sensory and contextual signals as inputs to  a766 

recurrent neural network,  capturing how  PFC neurons  flexibly combine sensory and cognitive 767 

variables 26 . In contrast, s ensory areas  such as  area  MT maintain stable, feature -specific  representations 768 

and may  incorporate contextual information through modulatory signals that reshape response 769 

nonlinearities  rather than  altering  core sensory connectivity.  From this perspective,  modulation of 770 

neuronal nonlinearity offers a  biologically plausible  an d computationally efficient  strategy for 771 

incorporating prior expectations into sensory processing. 772 

An important clarification concerns the scope of our modeling approach. The RNN was not trained to 773 

acquire prior expectations from experience; rather, it was first trained under the  no prior  condition to 774 

establish a baseline sensorimotor mapping, and prior effects were subsequently introduced as structured 775 

modulations of unit input –output nonlinearities through parameter optimization. The model therefore 776 

addresses how an already -formed prior can be implemented within a neural population, not how such 777 

priors are learned. 778 

Within this framework, direction -specific modulation of firing thresholds provides a parsimonious 779 

mechanism capable of reproducing key empirical observations, including tuning sharpening and 780 

reduced trial -to -trial variability under low sensory reliability. However, this should be interpreted as a 781 

functional account rather than a specific biological implementation; multiple circuit -level 782 

mechanisms —including learned feedback projections, recurrent inhibitory connectivity, or distributed 783 

modulatory signals —could give rise to similar effects. Future work will be needed to bridge this 784 

functional description with models of how priors are acquired, updated, and represented across cortical 785 

circuits. 786

Low -frequency top -down modulation 787 

A third major theme concerns the source of the modulatory signal implement ing  threshold -like 788 

inhibition in  area  MT.  Although  local synaptic  circuity can support  rapid  sensory encoding and precise 789 

feature selectivity  (Fig. 2) , dynamically updating prior expectations solely through synaptic 790 

rec onfiguration within  area  MT  would be inefficient given  the rapid  timescale  of expectation changes .791 

Instead, our behavioral, physiological, and modeling results  point to  low -frequency top -down signals 792 

from  higher cortical regions —such as PFC,  frontal  eye  field  smooth  eye  movement  region  (FEFsem ),793 

or  supplementary  eye  field  (SEF )—as a source of  direction -selective modulat ion that  adjusts  neuronal 794 

excitability  in  area  MT. 795 

Consistent with this interpretation, prior  expectations reduced pre -stimulus delta -band power and 796 

induced direction -specific  phase  modulation  in  area  MT  (Fig. 7 , Supplementary figure  11 ). Delta -band 797 

oscillations have been  strongly  linked to top -down predictive signaling and are known to  convey 798 

information from frontal regions to sensory cortices 56,57 . Th ese  observations  align with hierarchical 799 

communication framework s in which b ottom -up sensory  signals are  carried by higher -frequency 800 

activity , such as  gamma 58 -60 , wh ereas  top -down contextual signals, including priors,  are transmitted 801 

through  low -frequency bands 61 -63 . Within this framework, delta -band modulation in  area  MT likely 802 

reflects  top -down in fluences that  selectively  alter  neuronal  excitability , preferentially suppressing 803 

responses that  deviate from the  expected motion direction. Notably, this pattern closely matches  the 804 

direction -specific threshold modulation predicted by our RNN model (Fig.  4), and its biophysical 805 

implementation as tuned inhibitory modulation in the spiking network (Fig. 6). 806 

These findings motivate a computational architecture in which prior information is  encoded in higher -807 

order regions and transmitted to sensory cortex as frequency specific  modulatory  signals.  Multi -region 808 

RNN s comprising a  cognitive module  that represents  prior  information and a  sensory module  that 809 

processes incoming input s provide a promising framework 64,65 . If top -down influences are inhibitory, 810 

training such networks on tasks requir ing  integration of  prio rs  with sensory evidence should naturally 811 

give rise to structured  inhibitory projections from cognitive to sensory module s.812 

Complementing the se  modeling approach es , future experiments  combining  simultaneou s record ings 813 

from  frontal regions  that  convey expectation signals  and  from  area MT  will  allow  di rect tests of these 814 

predictions.  Such recordings will enable  precise  characterization of  the timing, directionality, and 815 

frequency specificity of top -down signals  during  prediction -driven sensor imotor processing 66,67 .816

Conclusion 817 

In summary, this study demonstrates that prior expectations enhance motor behavior under sensory 818 

uncertainty through a threshold -based inhibitory mechanism . Our findings suggest that prior 819 

information can be incorporated through  rapid  and  reversible modulation of neuronal  input -output 820 

nonlinearities,  potentially mediated by  low -frequency top -down signals from higher cortical regions. 821

Task design 838 

Visual stimuli were presented on a gamma -corrected 24″ Cathode -Ray Tube (CRT) monitor (HP1230, 839 

1600 × 1200 pixels, 85 -Hz vertical refresh rate). The monitor was placed 570 mm from the animal, 840 

subtending  38.67° by 29.49° of the horizontal and vertical visual  field, respectively. The background 841 

on which the visual stimuli were  presented was gray with a luminance level of 36.8 cd/m 2 (luminance 842

The two monkeys were trained in a smooth pursuit eye movement task (Fig. 1A). The pursuit target was 846 

a random dot kinematogram, and its  size in each day’s experiment was determined as one of  4° × 4°, 8° 847 

× 8°, or 12° × 12° depending on the location and size of  the receptive fields of the recorded MT  area .848 

Each trial began  when the animals fixated their eyes on a small dot (fixation spot)  at the center of the 849 

monitor screen. After a randomized fixation duration (800, 1300, or 1800 ms), the dot patch appeared 850 

at the center  of the screen or 1 to 2° displaced from the center to the opposite  direction of the target 851 

direction.  This positional manipulation was used to facilitate the initiation of smooth pursuit and did 852 

not qualitatively affect the measured behavioral outcomes; dat a from different initial positions were 853 

pooled for analysis.  Subsequently, a local motion,  whereby all the dots inside the invisible circular 854 

window moved  in the target direction at a given speed but the invisible window  did not move, was 855 

implemented. Following the local motion, both  the dots and windows moved together at the same speed 856

Behavior  analysis 887 

The horizontal and vertical eye positions were separately recorded at  a sampling rate of 1 kHz using an 888 

infrared video tracking system  (EyeLink 1000 Plus, SR Research Ltd.). A low -pass Butterworth  filter 889 

with an order of two and a cutoff frequency of 20 Hz was  applied on the horizontal and vertical 890 

components of the eye position. Subsequently, the filtered eye positions were differentiated to  obtain 891 

the horizontal and vertical eye velocities. To focus on the initiation of the smooth pursuit eye movements 892 

and remove the influence of the saccadic eye movements (saccades) on the behavioral  responses, the 893 

trials with saccades occurring within a time window  between −100 and 250 ms from the onset of the 894 

pursuit target were  removed. Next, the eye velocity traces were  rotated such that their average direction 895 

was 45°. The rotated eye  velocity during the open -loop period [1 50 ms from pursuit onset 68 ] was 896 

decomposed by horizontal and vertical templates 69 , which were the average of each rotated eye velocity 897 

component  between −20 and 100 ms from the average pursuit latency (mean  pursuit onset for high - and 898 

low -contrast stimuli: 76 and 123 ms  in monkey A and 57 and 105 ms in monkey B). The optimal  pu rsuit 899 

latency and scaling factors were estimated through the  sliding and scaling of the two templates using 900 

the least -squares  method and the nonlinear optimization with mesh adaptive direct  search (NOMAD) 901 

algorithm 70 . We included trials for further analysis only if  the fitted function accounted for more than 902 

70% of the data  variance. After this procedure was carried out, we used the results  for subsequent 903 

analysis only if the number of remaining trials  of the prior direction in each block for the day’s 904 

experiment  was more than 70.  The e ye velocities were normalized by dividing them by the target speed 905 

used in the motion stimulus. This was possible because there was no significant difference in speed 906 

between the  prior  and  no  prior  condi tions 13 . This normalization allows direction to be analyzed 907 

independently. Importantly, it enables the use of a large number of  eye  velocity datasets for training 908 

RNN models, thereby improving the efficiency and speed of the training process. 909
