# Abstract 28 

29 

Spatial attention is critical for solving the cocktail party problem, a longstanding problem 30 in neuroscience and artificial speech recognition. The ability to decode where humans 31 are attending in a cocktail party like scene would empower applications in brain computer 32 interfaces and assistive devices such as hearing aids. Here we demonstrate that, in an 33 overt attention task, the attended spatial location can be decoded robustly from single 34 trial hemodynamic responses, using a wearable whole head high density fNIRS system. 35 We also identify critical brain regions that make the highest contribution to decoding 36 accuracy. Specifically, we find that decoding based on a small fraction of channels within 37 the left and right inferior parietal lobule (IPL), achieve maximal decoding accuracy 38

# Introduction 42 

43 

The cocktail party problem (CPP), the problem of following a target speaker in a cluttered 44 scene amongst other competing speakers (Cherry, 1953; McDermott, 2009), is a45 challenging problem for many humans, e.g., those with hearing loss (Arlinger, 2003; 46 Podury et al., 2023), ADHD (Fu et al., 2022; Mihali et al., 2018) and autism (Marco et al., 47 2011). Normal hearing listeners can solve this problem with relative ease, indicating the 48 existence of a solution in the brain. However, human brain regions that enable the solution 49 remain poorly understood. 50 

51 

Spatial attention is thought to play a critical role in processing and analyzing complex 52 cluttered scenes with competing audiovisual objects (Shinn-Cunningham, 2008), as in the 53 CPP. For example, at a crowded gathering, a listener can look at and focus their attention 54 on a target speaker at a spatial location to follow what they are saying. Relatively little 55 remains known about human brain networks underlying spatial attention in the CPP. 56 

57 

Many studies have employed purely visual or auditory paradigms to investigate spatial 58 attention. However, in complex everyday scenes, visual, auditory, and multimodal regions 59 work in concert to select and process audiovisual objects. Indeed, a previous study found 60 an effect of gaze direction on improving cocktail party listening (Best et al., 2023). In 61 everyday scenes, we naturally orient towards an object by moving our eyes and then 62 selectively process that target (overt attention). Here, we investigate the CPP using 63 audiovisual stimuli, i.e., movies of competing speakers from different spatial locations, in 64 an overt attention task. We also compare this with the processing of sounds of competing 65 speakers, when the subject attends to a spatial location without visually orienting and 66 looking at that location (covert attention). 67 

68 

Previous studies in humans have investigated the cocktail party problem using various 69 methods, e.g., ECoG, EEG and MEG. fNIRS offers a number of advantages relative to 70 other methods for investigating cortical networks in humans (Boas et al., 2014; Ehlis et 71 al., 2014; Yucel et al., 2017). First, fNIRS is non-invasive and allows for studies in broader 72 human populations compared to ECoG. Second, fNIRS is portable allowing for 73 experiments in more naturalistic, ecologically valid settings compared to fMRI and MEG 74 (Pinti et al., 2018; von Lühmann et al., 2021). Third, fNIRS has better spatial resolution 75 compared to EEG, providing the potential to reveal specific cortical regions. A previous 76 study provided a preliminary fNIRS dataset in a cocktail party-like setting (Ning et al., 77 2024). However, this study did not employ a high density system or monitor eye 78

We then decode the 81 locus of spatial attention from the measured cortical responses using classification 82 techniques. Such a system has potential for applications in brain computer interfaces for 83 “steering” assistive devices, e.g., hearing aids, to the attended location to assist users 84 with communication in noisy multi-speaker scenes. Additionally, such a system could 85 enable attention-guided resource allocation for audio-visual rendering in AR/VR 86 environments. Our results provide a glimpse into the human brain engaged in solving the 87 cocktail party problem in more naturalistic conditions and reveal specific cortical regions 88 that make an important contribution to decoding attended spatial location using fNIRS. 89

# Discussion 245 

246 

In this study, we attempted to decode the attended spatial location in a cocktail party like 247 scene using whole head high density fNIRS measurements and classification methods. 248 In the overt attention condition, we obtained robust classification performance based on 249 single trials in most subjects. These results demonstrate the viability of a fNIRS based 250 system for decoding attended location in a scene and opens the way to potential 251 applications in brain computer interfaces for steering assistive devices to the attended 252 location. We found that the average latency to peak classification accuracy in the overt 253 condition was ~2.5 seconds, which is within behaviorally relevant timescales in natural 254 conversations. 255 

256 

Our results also shed light on specific brain regions important for decoding attended 257 location, which is difficult to obtain using EEG which has poor spatial resolution compared 258 to fNIRS. Specifically, we found that the classification accuracy obtained using only the 259 channels from the inferior parietal lobule (IPL) region, was not significantly different from 260 all channel classification accuracy. This result is consistent with the attention literature 261 suggesting an important role of IPL as part of the fronto-parietal network in attention. Our 262 results demonstrate that this area is accessible to fNIRS and plays a critical role in 263 decoding attended location. It also opens the possibility of designing compact fNIRS 264 devices specifically targeting the IPL for decoding spatial attention. 265 

266 

We observed significant variation in classification accuracy across subjects. Classification 267 accuracy was significantly correlated with within-subject variability of the single-trial HbO 268 

response, quantified as the coefficient of variation (CV = σ/|μ|) computed across trials per 269 channel, both when averaged across all channels and when restricted to channels 270 overlying the Angular Gyrus (Supplementary Figure 6). Subjects whose single-trial 271 responses were more variable relative to their mean amplitude tended to achieve lower 272 classification accuracy. Thus, lower classification performance in some subjects may 273 have resulted from less consistent trial-to-trial activation in key regions for decoding such 274 as the IPL. Optimizing the collection of signals in key regions, in combination with 275 individual subject scans, may further improve decoding performance in future studies. 276 

277

279 

This study revealed key contributing brain regions to decoding performance, which would 280 be challenging in other modalities such as EEG. The top ROIs included Brodmann 281 regions of left and right angular gyrus (AG), visuomotor cortex / Superior Parietal Lobule 282 (SPL), premotor and supplementary motor cortex, superior temporal gyrus (STG), and 283 supramarginal gyrus (SMG). The SPL (BA 7), a core node of the dorsal attention network, 284

Oculomotor component in IPL responses 304 

305 

In natural scenes, we visually orient by moving our eyes to attend to an object of interest. 306 In such scenarios, both the oculomotor and attention systems are active and intimately 307 linked. An interesting and challenging question relates to the relative contributions of 308 these two systems under such conditions. Studies on attention have carefully considered 309 whether these systems are independent, identical or overlapping(Corbetta, 1998; 310 Corbetta & Shulman, 1998). The evidence is consistent with identical or overlapping 311 systems, but sufficient to rule out the independent systems (Corbetta, 1998). Our analysis 312 comparing overt vs. covert classification revealed that visual stimuli and/or visual 313 orientation makes a strong contribution to decoding performance. Given the leading 314 contribution of IPL to decoding performance, we were specifically interested in whether 315 eye-movement alone could account for decoding performance in IPL. To further decouple 316 eye-movement signals from evoked responses, we designed an additional set of controls, 317 where we introduced a longer delay between the visual orientation and the movie onset. 318 This design allowed us to examine evoked responses long after eye movements were 319 completed. Data from these additional control experiments revealed that responses in 320 IPL are difficult to account for based on eye movement only (see supplementary methods 321 and results and supplementary figure 1). Rather such responses may represent attended 322 location and/or eye position (which are identical in the overt condition). Such a323

Limitations and Future Directions 327 

328 

While we took steps towards a more naturalistic design for investigating the cocktail party 329 problem by incorporating audiovisual stimuli and allowing for visual orientation using eye 330 movements, additional factors arising in natural conditions remain to be explored. For 331 example, future studies should investigate designs that allow for head movements which 332 could occur under natural conditions. 333 

334 

Second, although the decoding of overt attention was robust, we did not obtain robust 335 decoding for the covert attention condition. There are several possible explanations for 336 this. In everyday natural scenes, we visually orient and attend to particular objects in the 337 scene to analyze them in detail. In the overt condition, the visual orientation component 338 and the visual component of stimuli may have generated a stronger signal, which in turn 339 improves decoding. These components were absent in the covert condition. It is also 340 possible that our fNIRS cap did not optimally target auditory cortical regions near the ear 341 which could be sensitive to auditory only stimuli from different spatial locations. Future 342 studies should investigate whether improved coverage of auditory cortical regions 343 improves decoding performance, especially in the covert condition. 344 

345 

Third, this study used decoding based solely on fNIRS. Existing literature suggests that 346 EEG-fNIRS sensor fusion techniques could potentially improve single trial decoding 347 accuracy than fNIRS alone  (Codina et al., 2025) . We are currently exploring whether 348 simultaneous measurement of fNIRS and EEG can further improve decoding 349 performance in both overt and covert conditions. 350 

351 

Finally, this study was limited to binary classification between left vs. right. It will be 352 interesting to expand this paradigm to a larger number of spatial locations and investigate 353 the spatial resolution of decoding. 354 

355 

Potential applications to BCI systems and assistive devices 356 

357 

Recently, we have proposed a brain inspired algorithm for sound segregation (BOSSA) 358 (K. F. Chou et al., 2022). BOSSA significantly improves speech recognition in cocktail 359 party-like settings for both listeners with normal hearing and hearing loss (Boyd et al., 360 2025; X. L. Chou et al., 2020). BOSSA requires as input the spatial location a user wants 361 to attend to. The decoded attended spatial location from fNIRS could be used to steer 362 BOSSA to the attended location to better isolate the target sound from competing sounds. 363

Stimuli 400 

401 

The stimuli used in this study are extracted from an online open repository by Ephrat et. 402 al (Ephrat et al., 2018). The videos were mined using a custom automated downloading 403 script. Downloaded videos were automatically screened for presence of a single talker in 404 the video, English as a spoken language, context of the language being not offensive, 405 and sentences with at least 4 words that make semantic sense. Videos were further 406 required to show the talker positioned approximately at the center of the frame, defined 407 as cases in which the automatically detected face never entered the leftmost or rightmost 408 third of the screen. All the custom scripts were written in Python with semantic and 409 sentiment analysis done using OpenAI’s GPT-4 API and language detection (speech 410 detection) using OpenAI’s whisper model. A total of 360 unique videos were shortlisted 411 after manual inspection and verification of all the above-mentioned automated criteria. 412 The audios from the shortlisted videos were spatialized -30 to the left and +30 degrees to 413 the right using HRTF filters based on KEMAR head model using MATLAB. 414

Experimental Design 416 

417 Our experimental design mimics an ecologically valid cocktail party scenario in both overt 418 and covert contexts as shown in figure 1. In the overt scenario, 3-second audiovisual 419 movie clips are presented simultaneously at 30 degrees to the left and right. Prior to each 420 clip, a 2-second spatialized white noise cue is paired with a white crosshair on the 421 corresponding screen, guiding subjects on which direction to focus, with eye movements 422 allowed. In the covert scenario, subjects are exposed solely to spatialized audio from the 423 same set of movies. Here, the 2-second spatialized white noise serves as the cue, 424 directing their attention, while they maintain a gaze on a central screen displaying a static 425 white crosshair on a light gray background. A total of 60 trials (30 trials for attend-left and 426 30 trials for attend-right conditions) for both overt and covert conditions were collected. 427 Additionally, 20 trials (10 trials for look-left and 10 trials for look-right) were collected as 428 eye movement control trials where the subjects move their eyes to fixate at the center of 429 left or right screen in absence of any audio or video stimuli. Experiment Execution 443 

444 

The experiment was conducted using custom scripts written in Python using Psychopy 445 (code available with link on code availability section). Upon the participants’ arrival, they 446 filled out a demographics questionnaire and signed an informed consent form. A quiet 447 room was used where the participants sat down in a chair in front of 3 monitor setup. The 448 subjects were instructed to put their head on chin rest and instructed to not move their 449 head or any body parts during the task period. The subjects were allowed to do 2-4 450 practice trials of each task to ensure task comprehension. fNIRS caps with appropriate 451 sizes were fitted to their heads. Cap size was adjusted by changing the length of extender 452 clips bridging the two hemispheres at the midline. Tobii pro (Tobii, stockholm, Sweden) 453 eye tracking glasses 3 (first 16 subjects) or neon (pupil labs, Berlin, Germany) eye 454 trackers (rest of the subjects) were put on the subjects to track their eye gaze on the 455 monitors. Insert earphones (3M eartones, USA) with earplugs were placed in subjects’ 456 ear to deliver the audio and reduce the ambient auditory noise. Bright overhead room 457 lights were turned off and warm-tone halogen lamps were used to minimize ambient 458 optical interference and improve fNIRS signal-to-noise ratio during optimization and 459 recording. In addition, black shower cap was used to cover the entire fNIRS cap to reduce 460 ambient light leakage into the fNIRS recording. Manual maneuvering of the hair was done 461 to improve signal calibration. Subjective decisions were made by the experimenters until 462 satisfactory signals (around 60% channels being of good quality) were achieved during 463 the calibration stage. 464

fNIRS data preprocessing 499 

500 

fNIRS preprocessing was carried out in MATLAB programming language using Homer3 501 software package (Huppert et al., 2009) and in Python programming language using 502 Cedalion toolbox (Middell et al., 2026). Raw intensity at both wavelengths was quality-503 screened for low signal and saturation; channels with amplitudes below 0.001 a.u. or 504 above 0.84 a.u. were pruned. The remaining intensity data were converted to optical 505 density (OD) and motion-corrected using Temporal Derivative Distribution Repair (TDDR) 506 (Fishburn et al., 2019). The corrected OD was band-pass filtered between 0.01 and 0.5 507 Hz using a zero-phase Butterworth filter (order 4). Filtered OD was then converted to 508 hemoglobin concentration changes using the modified Beer-Lambert law, without 509 differential pathlength correction. 510 

511 

For group level average, the General Linear model (GLM) was used to estimate single 512 trials by regressing out the systemic physiological noise using mean of all channels less 513 than 20 mm source-detector separation as global signal regressor. The hemodynamic 514 response was modeled using a sequence of Gaussian basis functions spaced 1 s apart 515 

with a standard deviation of 1 s, spanning −2 to +15 s relative to stimulus onset (Gagnon 516 et al., 2011). The mean pre-stimulus baseline (−2 to 0 s) was subtracted from each trial. 517 For each channel, all of the trials were averaged for each condition (left and right). For 518 each subject, all of the channels were averaged within a defined region of interest (ROI). 519 All of the ROIs were averaged across the subjects to get group average results. 520

Group level Average 604 

605 

Group level average was calculated by averaging all single trials across the channel 606 space and across the subjects in each defined region of interest (ROI). Standard error of 607 the mean was calculated across the subjects to report on hemodynamic response 608 functions (HRF). 609 

610 

Statistical analysis for evoked responses 611 

612 

Statistical significance was determined using a t-test on the 2 seconds baseline (t=-2 to 613 t=0) segment compared to the 2 seconds window starting cue (t=n to t=n+2, starting t=0) 614 for both oxy and deoxy hemoglobin concentration within each condition (e.g., left HbO). 615 If any of the segments were statistically significant (p<0.05 after Bonferroni correction), 616 the corresponding evoked response was marked as a statistically significant evoked 617 response. 618 

619 

Classification scheme 620 

621 

To avoid information leakage, a cross-validation scheme was employed where OD data 622 was partitioned into training and testing datasets. The training dataset was filtered 623 between 0.01 Hz and 0.5 Hz and converted into concentration. GLM was performed with 624 global signal regression on only the training set. Estimated global signal regressor as 625 systemic physiological contributor was used to regress out physiological noise from the 626 training set on a single trial level. Identical to the group-level analysis, the HRF was 627 modeled using a sequence of Gaussian basis functions spaced 1 s apart with a standard 628 

deviation of 1 s, spanning −2 to +15 s relative to stimulus onset (Gagnon et al., 2011). 629 Single trials on the test dataset were estimated by subtracting global signal regressor 630 weighted trials from the original single test trials (Ning et al., 2024). Baseline (-2 to 0 s) 631 was subtracted from each individual trial. 632

Permutation test 658 

659 

Due to the small number of data points in the dataset (n = 60 trials total), the true chance 660 level is not at 50 %(Combrisson & Jerbi, 2015). Permutation test was carried out by 661 shuffling the labels 1000 times (with 5-fold cross validation) to estimate the true chance 662 level. Lower bound was estimated to be 40.7% and upper bound was estimated to be at 663 62.3% in one tailed test. Accuracy above 62.3% was marked as statistically above chance 664 level accuracy. 665
