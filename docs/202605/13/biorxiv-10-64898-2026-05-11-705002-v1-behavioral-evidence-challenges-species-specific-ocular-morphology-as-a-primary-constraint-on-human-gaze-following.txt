Abstract 28 Previous research suggests that humans are extremely sensitive to object-directed eye gaze, 29 which effectively guides their attention toward objects of shared interest. This contrasts with non-30 human primates, who typically require much more salient eye-gaze cues to achieve comparable 31 attentional orienting. However, it remains unclear whether cross-species differences in ocular 32 morphology account for this performance gap. To address this question, we examined humans’ 33 covert shifts of spatial attention in response to eye-gaze cues provided by either realistic human 34 or rhesus monkey head avatars. Target detection was reliably enhanced on gaze-congruent 35 compared to gaze-incongruent trials, with comparable gaze-cueing effects for both avatar types, 36 despite the fact that monkey eyes lack many of the conspicuous features characteristic of human 37 eyes. Hence, eye morphology alone does not substantially modulate gaze-driven attentional 38 orienting in humans, whereas humans’ reliable use of monkey eye-gaze cues highlights a clear 39 species difference in perceptual sensitivity to eye gaze signals. 40 

Significance Statement 41 Eye-gaze–mediated attentional orienting is a conserved ability across primates, yet sensitivity to 42 subtle eye-gaze cues may differ between species. Here, we provide empirical evidence that 43 humans exhibit a quantitatively greater capacity than non-human primates to follow subtle eye-44 gaze cues. Importantly, we showed that this difference cannot be attributed to species-specific 45 ocular morphology as human participants showed robust and comparable reflexive attentional 46 orienting to both human and rhesus monkey eye-gaze cues. This is striking given the pronounced 47 differences in ocular morphology and coloration/contrast between the two species. These findings 48 suggest that cross-species diversity in extracting spatial information from eye-gaze cues likely 49 reflects differences in perceptual sensitivity rather than bottom-up constraints imposed by 50 species-specific ocular morphology. 51 52

Introduction 53 

54 Human communication relies heavily on language, yet a substantial portion of socially relevant 55 information is conveyed through nonverbal signals that engage visual and motor systems. Among 56 these signals, gaze direction—defined by the orientation of the eyes and influenced by the 57 orientation of the face or body—plays a particularly prominent role by indicating where another 58 individual’s attention is directed. The ability to co-orient oneself with another’s gaze direction, 59 known as gaze-following (1), enables observers to detect behaviorally relevant aspects of the 60 environment, such as threats or resources, that might otherwise go unnoticed. Beyond these 61 immediate perceptual benefits, gaze-following also supports higher-level social cognition by 62 enabling individuals to infer what others see as well as their goals, intentions, and mental states 63 shaped by their objects of interest—a route to the development of a theory of (the other´s) mind 64 (2, 3). 65 Examining how gaze-following operates across species is critical for determining which aspects 66 of this ability reflect shared, evolutionarily conserved mechanisms and which may depend on 67 species-specific adaptations. Non-human primates (NHPs) are of particular relevance to this 68 question given their close evolutionary relationship with humans and the homology in visuo-69 oculomotor system organization (4, 5) which could in principle support perceptual sensitivity to 70 fine visual details comparable to that of humans. Until recently, the dominant view held that 71 humans possess a categorically superior gaze-following capacity (6), often attributed to the 72 exceptional conspicuity of their eyes. According to this view, eye-gaze following was considered 73 uniquely human and rooted in distinctive ocular morphology (7, 8). However, more recent 74 comparative morphological analyses have undermined this latter claim, showing that features 75 putatively allowing the perception of gaze direction are also present in several non-human 76 primate species and, moreover, that substantial intra-species variability exists in scleral 77 pigmentation and eye contrast within humans (see Perea-García et al.(9) for a review). While 78 these findings substantially weaken a strictly human-specific morphological account, it remains 79 unclear whether these anatomical differences translate into variation in gaze-following behavior. 80

4To move beyond morphological description and directly assess functional relevance, we designed 81 and conducted the present study. We asked whether gaze-following in humans is modulated by 82 species-typical ocular morphology. To this end, we employed a gaze-cueing paradigm in which 83 non-predictive gaze cues, presented by either ultra-realistic human or monkey avatars, preceded 84 a brief, near-threshold luminance change at one of two peripheral target locations that 85 participants were required to detect. Crucially, gaze cue were non-predictive within the task 86 structure and participants were explicitly instructed that gaze did not signal target location, 87 following gaze conferred no net strategic advantage for target selection. This design therefore 88 allowed us to isolate and assess the spontaneous influence of gaze cues, independent of their 89 instrumental value. Eye-gaze shifts demonstrated by the human and monkey avatars were 90 matched in their trajectory and dynamics, thereby isolating the impact of species-typical ocular 91 morphology to gaze-following. As expected, we observed significantly higher detection accuracy 92 for targets appearing at gaze-congruent locations relative to incongruent locations, consistent 93 with a robust gaze-cueing effect. Importantly, the magnitude, direction, and temporal dynamics of 94 this effect were comparable for human and monkey avatars. These findings indicate that species-95 typical differences in ocular morphology do not substantially influence gaze cueing in humans 96 under the present conditions. Instead, they suggest that humans’ robust ability to utilize eye-gaze 97 cues reflects a species-specific advantage in perceptual sensitivity to gaze signals. 98

Discussion 193 194 Previous research has shown that the ability to use conspecifics’ object-directed eye-gaze— 195 independent of accompanying head movements—to orient one’s own attention toward an 196 external object is a perceptual capacity shared by humans and non-human primates (see Shafiei 197 et al., for a comprehensive review of NHP research, and Chac  on -Candia et al., 2023 for a meta-198 analytic review of the human studies). Although these findings argue against a categorical, 199 species-level dissociation, previous work has not been able to determine whether potentially 200 relevant quantitative differences in this ability exist between humans and nonhuman primates 201 (NHPs) and, if so, what mechanisms might underlie them. In the present study, we directly 202 addressed this issue by comparing human gaze following induced covert shifts of visual spatial 203 attention which were guided by either human or monkey eye gaze cues. Critically, we employed 204 the same monkey gaze stimuli, behavioral paradigm, and even experimental setup we had 205 previously used to study gaze-following in rhesus monkeys, thereby enabling a direct comparison 206 of performance between monkey and human observers. More specifically, human participants 207 viewed a realistic rhesus monkey head avatar – the same that had been used in the preceding 208 study of rhesus monkeys - or, alternatively, a human head avatar, and were required to detect 209 near-threshold luminance changes at one of two peripheral targets, with the demonstrator’s gaze 210 directed toward the target location on 50% of trials, i.e. a non-predictive gaze cue. Target 211 detection was significantly enhanced for gaze-congruent trials relative to incongruent trials, no 212 matter if prompted by the human or the monkey avatar. In contrast, our prior work demonstrated 213 that the identical monkey gaze stimulus was unable to induce gaze-following behavior in rhesus 214 monkeys, who exhibited reliable eye gaze following only when prompted by a more salient cue 215 characterized by increased eye-gaze deviation amplitude and reduced viewing distance. Taken 216 together, these results support the existence of a clear quantitative species difference with 217 humans exhibiting greater perceptual sensitivity to object-directed eye-gaze cues, allowing even 218 low-quality visual input to elicit robust gaze-following effects. Moreover, the comparable efficacy 219 of monkey and human gaze cues in eliciting human gaze following also challenges the 220 widespread belief that differences in eye morphology explain previous observations of poorer eye 221 gaze following in monkeys. This conclusion is allowed given the fact that the monkey and human 222 avatar eyes reproduced the well-known differences in eye features. As shown in figure 2, the 223 morphological distinctions are significant: the human palpebral fissure is characterized by a 224 mediolateral elongation (almond-shaped), whereas the rhesus monkey aperture is markedly more 225 circular. Furthermore, humans possess significantly greater scleral exposure across both primary 226 and eccentric gaze positions. Regarding luminance-contrast relationships, brown-eyed humans 227 exhibit a primary contrast boundary between the depigmented conjunctiva and the pigmented 228 iris—rendering the pupil-iris transition less distinct—whereas rhesus monkeys (possessing hazel-229 brown irises and minimal visible sclera) present a dominant contrast between the relatively 230 reflective iris and the dark pupil. Despite these profound configurational disparities, the 231 magnitude, direction, and temporal dynamics of gaze-cueing effects exhibited by our human 232 observers—as measured across stimulus-onset asynchronies (SOAs)—were nearly identical. 233 These findings indicate that, for an average human observer, gaze cues derived from human and 234

This conclusion is in line with previous results from comparative studies of eye 236 morphology across various non-human primate species and human populations (10–13), 237 which have called into question the long-held belief that the seemingly poorer eye-following 238 abilities of nonhuman primates might be a direct consequence of less conspicuous eyes as 239 originally proposed by Kobayashi and Koshima (7, 8). In fact, collectively, more recent 240 morphological work suggests that while clear differences in eye appearance exist, human eyes 241 are not uniquely conspicuous (see Perea-García et al. (9) for a review). 242 We quantified gaze following using the cueing effect, defined as higher accuracy in detecting a 243 luminance change when the target was preceded by a target-congruent gaze cue compared with 244 a target-incongruent cue. This effect is interpreted as reflecting a covert shift of visual spatial 245 attention guided by the avatar’s gaze. Covert orienting was ensured by task constraints: 246 throughout gaze-cue presentation and until the go signal, participants were required to maintain 247 fixation on a central fixation point, and any trial in which fixation was broken prior to the go signal 248 was aborted and excluded from analysis. In incongruent trials, luminance changes were therefore 249 more likely to be missed due to a misallocation of the attentional spotlight toward the distractor 250 location cued by the gaze stimulus. Importantly, given the structure of the task, performance 251 could not exceed 50% accuracy if participants relied exclusively on the gaze cue while ignoring 252 target luminance; however, overall accuracy was approximately 75%, indicating that participants 253 followed task instructions and reported luminance changes rather than simply following gaze. 254 Moreover, gaze following was not explicitly reinforced, as auditory feedback signaling success 255 was contingent solely on correct target detection, irrespective of cue congruency. Taken together, 256 these considerations indicate that enhanced performance on gaze-congruent trials reflects 257 spontaneous covert gaze-following tendencies, expressed despite the absence of explicit 258 incentives or strategic benefits for the observer. 259 In the present experiment, the cue–target interval, or stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA), varied 260 randomly across trials, with each SOA drawn with equal probability from a discrete set of five 261 values (50, 100, 200, 300, and 500 ms). We observed that the gaze-mediated attentional effect 262 emerged as early as 50 ms following cue onset. Such rapid modulation of performance is 263 consistent with a reflexive orienting mechanism driven by exogenous properties of the cue, which 264 can be processed without extensive, and thus time-consuming, top–down control (14–16). The 265 early onset of the cueing effect is also consistent with neurophysiological evidence demonstrating 266 that neural signatures of covert attentional shifts can be detected in the thalamus and early visual 267 cortices as early as ~50 ms post-cue in non-human primates performing Posner-like paradigms 268 (14, 17, 18). Converging evidence from human gaze-cueing studies further supports this 269 interpretation in showing reliable cueing effects for non-predictive eye-gaze cues at short SOAs 270 (see McKay et al. (19) and Chacón-Candia (20), for meta-analytic reviews). Although in most 271 prior studies the shortest SOAs tested ranged between 100 and 150 ms (e.g. 21–27), at least one 272 study explicitly testing a 50 ms SOA reported a robust gaze-cueing effect (28). Comparable early 273 effects have also been documented in rhesus monkeys (Marciniak et al., 2015; Shafiei et al., in 274 press). Perhaps the strongest evidence for a reflexive attentional mechanism comes from studies 275 in which human participants were explicitly informed that the target was more likely to appear at 276 the location opposite to the location cued by the gaze stimulus. Despite this counter predictive 277 instruction, reliable gaze-cueing effects persisted at short SOAs (<200 ms), indicating that early 278 gaze-following cannot be fully suppressed by top–down expectations (29, 30). A similar temporal 279 pattern has been observed in rhesus monkeys: gaze cues exerted a robust influence on attention 280 at short SOAs (50–200 ms), whereas at longer SOAs ( ≥300 ms) monkeys were able to suppress 281 gaze-following and localize targets in accordance with learned task contingencies (31). Together, 282 these findings support the view that eye-gaze cues engage an early, reflexive attentional orienting 283 mechanism that operates prior to, and largely independently of, voluntary cognitive control. 284 A limitation of the present study is that we examined only human and not also monkey observers 285 on their ability to follow the gaze of representatives of the respective other species. As we found 286 that humans are much more proficient at eye gaze-following compared to rhesus monkeys, it may 287

Assuming that there 289 is a direct link between sensitivity to eye information and the ability to cross the species 290 boundary, one might speculate that rhesus monkeys may be less able to generalize across 291 heterospecific eye configurations. Addressing this issue will require systematic behavioral 292 evidence from NHPs tested with heterospecific eye-gaze cues, which remains sparse, even 293 though a growing body of comparative morphological work suggests that—despite clear absolute 294 differences in ocular morphology across species—key features relevant for gaze cue visibility 295 (e.g., relative contrast) may not differ substantially. Moreover, it remains poorly understood that 296 how facial context modulates gaze-following, as gaze perception likely reflects an integration of 297 eye cues with surrounding facial information rather than the eyes alone. In other words, even 298 identical eye morphologies may carry different behavioral relevance depending on the 299 surrounding facial embedding and species-specific configural cues. An important next step 300 therefore is to test how gaze-following changes when eye morphology and facial context are 301 dissociated—for example, by embedding human eyes within a rhesus monkey face (and vice 302 versa). For this purpose, computer-generated avatars —such as those used in the present 303 study—will be critical, as they allow controlled manipulation of eye and face properties while 304 preserving realism and minimizing low-level confounds. 305 A particularly intriguing question arising from the present findings is why humans are markedly 306 more proficient than non-human primates (NHPs) at extracting spatial cueing information from 307 very subtle eye-gaze signals. Given the broad similarities in the organization of the visual and 308 oculomotor systems of diurnal old world primates including rhesus monkeys and humans (32– 309 34), it is unlikely that this difference can be attributed to fundamental neurobiological advantages 310 in humans. Instead, learning through experience may account for humans’ higher sensitivity. 311 Humans are exposed to substantially greater opportunities for eye-gaze interaction throughout 312 development than most non-human primates. Arguably humans experience substantially greater 313 opportunities for mutual eye-gaze contact across development than most non-human primates 314 (35–37). From birth onward, human caregivers actively seek eye contact with infants to engage 315 attention (38), and common caregiving practices—such as face-to-face holding (39), 316 breastfeeding (40), and infant carrying—provide frequent opportunities for sustained mutual gaze. 317 In contrast, in many non-human primate species, infants are typically carried on the mother’s 318 back or ventrum during quadrupedal locomotion (41), a posture that likely limits opportunities for 319 sustained face-to-face interaction and prolonged eye contact (42, 43). Later in development, 320 humans routinely exchange eye contact in everyday social interactions as an affiliative signal, a 321 cue for turn-taking, and an indicator of communicative intent, and brief eye contact is generally 322 tolerated even in the absence of prior social bonding (44, 45). By contrast, in most non-human 323 primate species, direct eye contact outside affiliative contexts such as grooming is often avoided, 324 as it can be perceived as threatening and is frequently associated with aggression, dominance, or 325 challenge displays, particularly when prolonged (2, 46, 47). Consequently, exposure to eye-gaze 326 cues is likely far more constrained in NHPs than in humans throughout development and 327 adulthood. Such differences in social experience may provide a developmental basis for the 328 enhanced sensitivity and flexibility of human gaze processing, enabling humans to reliably extract 329 spatial information from subtle eye-gaze cues. Nevertheless, we cannot exclude the possibility 330 that humans’ greater perceptual sensitivity to eyes is already available from an early 331 developmental stage (potentially driven by currently unknown differences in low-level visual 332 processing), which in turn could strengthen the contribution of eye information to subsequent 333 social-cognitive development. 334 In conclusion our results demonstrate a clear quantitative difference in gaze-following capabilities 335 of humans and rhesus monkeys: humans can reliably extract gaze direction from small-amplitude 336 cues that fail to drive gaze cuing in monkeys, despite both species showing qualitatively similar 337 gaze-following capabilities under optimal conditions. Moreover, the near-equivalence of gaze-338 cuing effects elicited by human and monkey avatars in humans argues against the relevance of 339 species-specific differences in eye morphology and coloration/contrast. Materials and Methods 345 

346 All procedures complied with the Declaration of Helsinki and were approved by the Ethics 347 Committee of the University Hospital Tübingen. Participants provided written informed consent 348 after receiving written and oral study information and received monetary compensation for their 349 participation. 350 

Participants 351 A total of 28 neurotypical individuals were initially recruited through round-invitation emails sent to 352 students and employees of the University of Tübingen. The following criteria were used for the 353 initial recruitment of eligible participants: (1) availability to attend three experimental sessions, 354 each lasting approximately 90 minutes; (2) no history of attention-deficit or attention-related 355 disorders (e.g., ADHD); (3) no known color-vision deficiencies; (4) no sensitivity to flashing or 356 flickering lights; and (5) absence of claustrophobia. During the first experimental session, 357 participants’ ability to comply with the task requirements was evaluated which led to the exclusion 358 of five participants (17.9%): three (10.7%) due to poor eye-tracking data quality caused by 359 frequent blinking or difficulty maintaining their head stable, one (3.6%) due to inconsistent 360 perception of luminance during the initial perceptual threshold estimation phase—likely related to 361 uncorrected ametropia—and one due to an incomplete dataset following discontinuation of 362 participation after the first session for personal reasons. None of the participants who completed 363 all three experimental sessions were excluded from the final dataset. The final sample therefore 364 comprised 23 participants (age 28.7 ± 9.81 years; 14 female). All participants reported normal or 365 corrected-to-normal vision using prescribed glasses. 366 

Experimental procedure 367 The experiment consisted of three distinct phases: training, perceptual threshold estimation, and 368 gaze-cueing paradigm. These were completed in three 90-min sessions, conducted on separate 369 days, on average 3.23 days (SD = 4.05, range = 17 days) apart. The first session began with 370 training, followed by the perceptual threshold estimation. The gaze-cuing task was mainly 371 conducted in the 2 nd and 3 rd sessions. All experiments were conducted in a sound-attenuated 372 booth which was dimly lit, with ambient lighting adjusted and controlled to maintain low 373 illumination throughout the session. As shown in figure 3a, participants were seated in a height-374 adjustable chair with their head partially stabilized using an adjustable chin and forehead rest to 375 enhance eye-tracking accuracy. Participants’ arms rested comfortably on a desk positioned in 376 front of them. Visual stimuli were presented on a 24-inch LCD monitor (BenQ XL2411-B; 1920 × 377 1080 pixels, refresh rate 60 Hz) mounted at a viewing distance of 60 cm, with participants’ neutral 378 line of sight aligned to the center of the display. A transparent plexiglass panel was positioned 379 parallel to the screen at 14 cm in front of it (figure 3a). Two LEDs were affixed to the panel, 380 positioned symmetrically at ±9.3 ° from the panel center along the horizontal meridian, aligned 381 with the center of the screen. Eye movements were recorded using a desktop-mounted, video-382 based eye-tracking system (EyeLink® 1000 Plus, SR Research Ltd., Canada) with a sampling 383 rate of 2 KHz. Stimulus presentation and eye-tracking data acquisition were controlled using the 384 Neurological Recording and Experiment Control (NREC), an in-house developed software suite 385 (for more details visit: https://nrec.neurologie.uni-tuebingen.de). 386

10 The first experimental session began with a training phase consisting of 50 practice trials, 388 preceded by a verbal explanation of the task. Prior to training, eye movements were calibrated 389 using a standard nine-point calibration procedure, in which participants fixated nine points 390 displayed sequentially across the screen in a 3×3 grid spanning 15° in width and 10° in height. 391 This procedure allowed the eye tracker to accurately map measured eye positions to known 392 screen locations. Each trial began with the presentation of a central fixation point (CF; white disk, 393 0.2° in diameter) on an otherwise black screen (figure S1a). Participants were instructed to 394 maintain fixation on the CF to initiate the trial. Fixation was monitored using an invisible 5° × 5° 395 window centered on the fixation point; leaving this window before the go signal resulted in 396 immediate trial abortion. After a fixed interval of 500 ms, a salient light flash (duration: 300 ms) 397 was presented by one of the two LEDs, thereby defining the target LED. Following a 500-ms 398 delay after light offset, an auditory “go” signal was delivered. Participants were required to 399 maintain fixation on the CF until the go signal and were instructed to report the target as quickly 400 and accurately as possible by executing a saccade toward the corresponding LED upon hearing 401 the signal. If the selected LED corresponded to the target, a distinct auditory feedback was 402 provided indicating a correct response; incorrect responses were not followed by feedback. After 403 an intertrial interval (ITI) of 700–1500 ms, the next trial began. During the training phase, verbal 404 feedback was provided by the experimenters to facilitate understanding the task elements and 405 performance. Once participants demonstrated consistent correct performance across several 406 consecutive trials, verbal feedback was withheld until the end of training. If the learning criterion 407 was not met after the initial 50 trials, training was extended by up to two additional blocks of 50 408 practice trials. Participants who failed to meet the criterion after a total of 150 training trials were 409 excluded from further participation. Upon successful completion of training, participants 410 proceeded to the subsequent phase, i.e. perceptual threshold estimation. 411 

Phase II: perceptual threshold estimation (PTE) 412 PTE was conducted to determine, for each participant, the LED luminance increase from baseline 413 that enabled a 25% increase in detection accuracy relative to chance performance (50%), defined 414 as the individualized point of subjective equality (PSE). The paradigm used in this phase closely 415 matched the training paradigm, with luminance systematically varied on a trial-by-trial basis. 416 Luminance levels ranged from near-chance detection to near-ceiling performance and were 417 adaptively selected using a parameter estimation by sequential testing (PEST) procedure (48), 418 which adjusted luminance intensity based on participants’ responses to converge on the target 419 accuracy level of 75%. In brief, each trial began with the presentation of a central fixation point 420 (CF) superimposed on a colored monkey-head silhouette against a black background (figure 421 S1b). This background configuration was chosen to match overall screen luminance with that of 422 the subsequent gaze-cueing phase, ensuring comparable light exposure across paradigm which 423 critically impacts luminance perception. After a fixed delay of 675 ms, one of the two LEDs, 424 selected at random, briefly increased in luminance for 300 ms. This delay corresponded to the 425 midpoint of the range of delays (550–900 ms) used in the gaze-cueing task. The go signal 426 followed 500 ms after LED offset, after which participants had up to 1500 ms to initiate an 427 indicative saccadic response. Correct responses were confirmed by an auditory feedback tone 428 followed by an ITI of 700–1500 ms. 429 PSE reliability was evaluated based on the shape of the psychometric function describing 430 detection accuracy (with the lower asymptote fixed at chance level) to luminance intensity. This 431 evaluation was performed immediately after completion of the perceptual threshold estimation 432 (100 trials), while participants took a 5-minute break. An excessively steep transition between the 433 lower and upper asymptotes was interpreted as insufficient resolution of luminance levels, 434 whereas failure of the function to reach an upper asymptote indicated disproportionate task 435 difficulty. To adjust LED visibility, we reduced LED luminance using neutral density (ND) filters 436 placed over the LEDs and the ambient illumination within the testing booth. Pilot testing showed 437

Phase III: gaze-cuing paradigm 447 This paradigm builds on the spatial cueing task of Posner (49), in which a pre-cue shifts attention 448 to a particular location. Here the other´s gaze served as pre-cue that guided attention to one of 449 two LEDs, right and left of the fixation spot with one of the two exhibiting a luminance change to 450 be detected by the experimental subject. The luminance could change at the LED cued by the 451 demonstrator´s gaze or at the other LED. Gaze cues were provided by highly realistic pseudo-3D 452 avatars of human and rhesus monkey heads respectively. The monkey head avatar has 453 previously been shown to evoke behavioral responses in rhesus monkeys that do not differ from 454 those elicited by videos of natural conspecifics (50). In addition, gaze cues offered by the same 455 avatar have been shown to reliably elicit gaze-following behavior in monkeys (51). We used 456 small-amplitude eye-gaze shifts identical to those described in Shafiei et al. (51). For the present 457 study, gaze cues demonstrated by human head avatars were generated using Unreal Engine 5 458 (52) and CapCut (53). We carefully ensured that the gaze cues of the human avatars closely 459 matched those of the monkey avatar. Specifically, saccade duration, speed, and amplitude, as 460 well as eye size (comprising visible sclera and iris) and overall facial brightness, were matched 461 across avatars. The accuracy of these adjustments was further verified using ImageJ (54), a free, 462 open-source image analysis software widely used in science to view, measure, and process 463 images. The avatars executed horizontal eye movements of 7.7° (when viewed from a distance of 464 60 cm), directed toward either the left or right LED (Figure 4). All participants were tested using 465 gaze cues demonstrated by the monkey avatar. In addition, each participant was tested with one 466 of two human avatar variants (male or female), chosen based on the participant’s subjective 467 preference. Each avatar variant was tested in a separate block, with block order counterbalanced 468 across participants. Each block consisted of 10 runs of 100 trials. Participants were allowed to 469 take short breaks between runs. 470 Each trial in the main behavioral paradigm began with the presentation of a forward-facing avatar 471 gazing straight with a central fixation point (CF) located on the bridge of its nose (Figure 3b). The 472 CF was located midway on the horizontal axis connecting the two LEDs which exhibited the same 473 level of baseline illumination. To initiate a trial, participants were required to maintain fixation 474 within an invisible 5° × 5° window centered on the CF for 500 ms. Following successful fixation, 475 the avatar shifted its gaze toward either the left or right LED, selected at random, and maintained 476 this gaze direction until the end of the trial (figure 4). After a variable delay from the gaze shift 477 onset, the luminance of one of the LEDs increased briefly for 300 ms, identifying the LED as the 478 target in the given trial. The interval between the gaze shift onset and the luminance change is 479 referred to as the stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA). The target LED (left or right) was selected at 480 random (50% share each) and independently of the avatar’s gaze direction, ensuring that the 481 gaze cue was uninformative with respect to target location. The LED luminance stepped from its 482 baseline level to the individualized value determined during the perceptual threshold estimation 483 phase that predicted 75% detection accuracy; this increase is referred to as ΔLuminance. Five 484 hundred milliseconds after the offset of the ΔLuminance, the auditory go signal was presented. 485 Participants were instructed to report the target LED by making a saccade toward it. Responses 486 were required within 1500 ms following the go signal, after which the trial was aborted. Correct 487

497 To distinguish between endogenous and exogenous sources of attention, we examined the 498 temporal profile of the cueing effect by including five stimulus onset asynchronies (SOAs): 50, 499 100, 200, 300, and 400 ms. One SOA was randomly assigned to each trial, with equal 500 representation of all SOAs within each run. In total, there were 20 trial types defined by the 501 combination of gaze direction (left, right), target LED location (left, right), and SOA (five levels). 502 Each trial type was repeated five times per run, with trials randomly interleaved. Because our 503 primary interest was luminance detection accuracy in gaze-congruent (i.e., target LED located in 504 the direction of the avatar’s gaze) versus gaze-incongruent trials, the four gaze-direction–by– 505 LED-location combinations were collapsed into two congruency conditions. This resulted in 10 506 trials per congruency condition per SOA per run for each participant. 507 

Statistical Analysis 508 Data preprocessing was conducted using a custom-written script in MATLAB (R2023a)(55). 509 Statistical data analysis and visualization were performed in R (version 4.4.2, 2024-10-31 510 ucrt)(56). The detection of response saccades and computation of their metrics were made using 511 SAL (Saccade Analysis Lab), a custom-made MATLAB-based algorithm (Version: 512 9.10.0.1710957 (R2021a) Update 4)(57) adapted from the method described by Nyström and 513 Holmqvist (58). The full SAL codebase is openly available for reproducibility at: 514 https://github.com/shafieimasih/SAL. 515 Estimation of PSE was performed by modelling hit rate as a function of ΔLuminance level using a 516 logistic psychometric function using psignifit package in MATLAB (59), with the lower asymptote 517 fixed at 50% to reflect chance-level performance. 518 Behavioral performance was analyzed at the trial level using generalized linear mixed-effects 519 models (GLMMs) implemented in R (lme4 package (60)). The dependent variable was hit 520 probability (correct vs. incorrect response), modeled with a binomial error distribution and a logit 521 link function. For the primary analysis, fixed effects included avatar type (human, monkey), 522 congruency (congruent, incongruent), stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA; 50, 100, 200, 300, 400 523 ms), and Congruency-by-SOA and avatar-type-by-congruency interactions. Random effects were 524 specified to account for repeated measurements within participants, including random intercepts 525 for subjects and random slopes for congruency by subject. This random-effects structure allowed 526 baseline performance and the magnitude and direction of the congruency effect to vary across 527 individuals. Model convergence and fit were verified by inspection of residuals and random-528 effects estimates. Statistical significance of fixed effects was further assessed using Wald z tests. 529 Effect sizes are reported as regression coefficients ( β), standard errors ( SE ), z values, and 530 associated p values. To facilitate interpretation of the congruency effect, odds ratios (ORs) were 531 computed from model estimates. Post-hoc comparisons were conducted to examine the 532 congruency effect separately for each avatar type and SOA. Estimated marginal means and 533 contrasts were computed deploying the emmeans package (61), with p values adjusted for 534 multiple comparisons using the Benjamini-Hochberg method. 535

Participant sex (male vs. female) and avatar presentation order (monkey-541 first vs. human-first) were included as between-subject factors, whereas human avatar sex (male 542 vs. female avatar) was included as a within-subject factor in analyses restricted to human avatar 543 type. All models included random intercepts and random slopes for congruency by subject to 544 account for individual differences in baseline performance and gaze-cuing sensitivity.
