Spatial and object-based saccadic selection in memory 2

Abstract 1

Saccades made during memory maintenance prioritize memory for the saccade target, but it 2

is unclear if this benefit is specific to a location or extends across memorized objects. In three 3

experiments, we examined whether saccadic selection spreads to other locations within the 4

same object. In Experiment 1, we asked observers to remember three oriented Gabors 5

presented either within contour-defined objects or without object structure. A subsequent 6

movement cue prompted observers to move their eyes to the indicated location. We then 7

probed memory for stimuli at locations equidistant from the saccade target, in either the same 8

or a different object. Memory was best for stimuli at locations congruent with the saccade 9

target, and consistently weaker for other stimuli presented in the same or a different object 10 

than the saccade target. In Experiment 2, we created more complex objects by adding more 11 

object features to the stimulus. Again, memory performance was best for stimuli congruent 12 

with the saccade target location, whereas memory in incongruent trials was worse and similar 13 

for stimuli in the same and different object as the saccade target. In Experiment 3, we tested 14 

if saccadic selection is present and propagates within the object in a change detection task. 15 

Again, memory performance (i.e., change detection) was best at the saccade target location. 16 

However, this memory benefit also spread to other locations within the same object. Our 17 

results imply that saccadic selection in visual working memory is primarily space-based but 18 

can also spread towards locations within the object where a saccade was directed. 19

Spatial and object-based saccadic selection in memory 3

Introduction 1

Successfully navigating in situations such as a crowded airport terminal requires that visual 2

information can be retained and accessed even after the original sensory input is no longer 3

available. For example, one might need to check whether a bag is still beside them after briefly 4

shifting attention to a flight-information display. We maintain such representations in a 5

capacity-limited visual working memory (Bays & Husain, 2008; Cowan, 2001; Luck & Vogel, 6

1997; Nakayama, 1990; Pashler, 1988; for reviews see Luck & Vogel, 2013; Ma et al., 2014). 7

Because of this limited capacity, cognitive systems must select which information is encoded 8

and preserved. Extensive research shows that attention can be directed to space, features, 9

objects, and time to select information for encoding into visual working memory or to regulate 10 

encoding and prioritize information during memory maintenance (Griffin & Nobre, 2003; 11 

Landman et al., 2003; Wei et al., 2024; for review see Souza & Oberauer, 2016). 12 

More recently, theories of visual working memory have emphasized the role of action 13 

in guiding selection. Rather than relying solely on attentional mechanisms, movements 14 

themselves—and even their preparation—can shape which representations are prioritized 15 

(Heuer, Ohl, & Rolfs, 2020; Myers et al., 2017; Olivers & Roelfsema, 2020; Van der Stigchel 16 

& Hollingworth, 2018; van Ede, 2020; van Ede & Nobre, 2023). Simple actions such as 17 

saccadic eye movements (Hanning et al., 2016; Hanning & Deubel, 2018; Ohl & Rolfs, 2017, 18 

2018, 2020; Ohl, Kroell, & Rolfs, 2024), reach movements (Heuer et al., 2017; Heuer & 19 

Schubö, 2017), or the mere preparation of such actions (Trentin et al., 2023, 2024) prioritize 20 

information in visual working memory that aligns with current action goals. 21 

Saccadic eye movements are among the most frequent actions that humans perform 22 

and thus offer a powerful mechanism for dynamically reorganizing memory contents in 23 

accordance with ongoing behavioral demands (Ohl & Rolfs, 2017; Ohl et al., 2024). Saccadic 24 

selection in visual working memory occurs irrespective of whether participants remember 25 

small versus large arrays of items (Ohl & Rolfs, 2020), and even when the saccade target 26

location is unlikely to be probed in a later memory test, indicating that this prioritization 1

emerges automatically (Ohl & Rolfs, 2017, 2020). 2

Although these findings consistently demonstrate that saccades influence memory, it 3

remains unclear which level of representation is targeted by saccadic selection (for review see 4

Heuer et al., 2020). One possibility is that saccades act as a purely spatial selection 5

mechanism, such that memory benefits arise only for items located at the saccade target 6

location (i.e., space-based selection). Alternatively, saccadic selection may operate at the 7

level of objects. In visual perception, attention can select discrete objects and spreads across 8

their entire surface, yielding the classical same-object advantage (Duncan, 1984; Egly et al., 9

1994; Jeurissen et al., 2016; Malcolm & Shomstein, 2015; for reviews see Scholl, 2001; 10 

Cavanagh et al., 2023). Similar same-object advantages have been observed in visual 11 

working memory (Lin et al., 2021; Peters et al., 2015). 12 

Importantly, presaccadic attention also produces a same-object perceptual advantage, 13 

such that stimuli perceptually grouped with the saccade target via color and motion cues 14 

benefit from the presaccadic shift of attention (Shurygina et al., 2021). Whether this object-15 

level prioritization also occurs for items stored in visual working memory, however, has not 16 

been tested. Demonstrating a same-object advantage in memory would provide strong 17 

evidence that saccade-related prioritization extends beyond spatial coordinates and operates 18 

at the level of object-based representations. Here, we directly tested this hypothesis in three 19 

experiments designed to determine whether saccadic preparation enhances entire objects 20 

maintained in memory, or whether its influence is restricted to a spatial location at the 21 

movement goal. 22

(Figure 1 ). At the end of each trial, participants reported the orientation of one remembered 26

memory benefit is restricted to the saccade target location, and memory performance drops 4

off at neighboring locations (Ohl & Rolfs, 2017, 2018, 2020; Ohl, et al., 2024). 5

Here, we examined whether this spatial specificity is modulated by object structure. 6

Specifically, we investigated whether the memory benefit at the saccade target location could 7

spread to neighboring locations within the same object or whether the memory advantage 8

would remain strictly restricted to the saccade target location. Spreading performance benefits 9

to other locations within the same object is one way to quantify object-based attention (see 10 

reviews by Scholl, 2001, and Cavanagh et al., 2023). We used the Gestalt law of common 11 

region to ensure that the stimuli would be perceived as belonging to a coherent object and 12 

thus support potential object-based selection. By contrasting the spatial specificity of saccadic 13 

selection in conditions with and without contour-defined objects, we can determine how the 14 

presence of objects alters saccadic selection in memory within our established paradigm. 15

Method 17 

Participants. We tested 10 naïve participants (3 males, 7 females; ages 23–30, M = 26.3, 18 

SD = 2.26; all right-handed; 9 right-eye dominant, 1 left-eye dominant) in three 1-hour sessions 19 

with at least one night between consecutive sessions. The first session served as a training 20 

session to verify rapid saccade generation in response to a movement cue. No participant was 21 

excluded from the study. All participants had normal or corrected-to-normal vision and eye 22 

dominance was assessed using a hole-in-a-card test. Informed written consent was obtained 23 

prior to the experiment. Participants were compensated with 8.50 € per session plus a 2 €24 

bonus upon completing all sessions. 25

Materials and procedure. Participants sat in a dark room at a viewing distance of 57 cm with 1

their heads placed in a chin and forehead rest to reduce head movements. We recorded the 2

positions of the observers’ dominant eye with an Eyelink 1000 Desktop Mount (SR Research, 3

Ottawa, ON, Canada). For stimulus presentation we used a gamma-corrected VIEWPixx/3D 4

(VPixx Technologies Inc., Saint Bruno, QC, Canada) in scanning backlight mode (luminance 5

0 to 100 cd/m2; pixel response time ∼1 ms) at a spatial resolution of 1920 × 1080 pixels and 6

120 Hz refresh rate. The experiment ran on a DELL Precision T3600 (Debian GNU Linux 8) 7

and was implemented in MATLAB (The Math Works, Inc., 2020) using the Psychophysics 8

Toolbox 3 (Brainard, 1997; Pelli, 1997) and the Eyelink Toolbox (Cornelissen et al., 2002). 9

Eye and screen coordinates were aligned using a standard nine-point calibration and 10 

validation routine at the beginning of each session, after breaks, and whenever recalibration 11 

was required. Before every trial onset, participants needed to direct their gaze upon a fixation 12 

point (a white disk with a diameter of 0.17 degrees of visual angle (dva) enclosed by a black 13 

counter with a diameter of 0.68 dva and a width of 0.085 dva) directly at the center of the 14 

screen. A trial started as soon as the participants’ eye positions remained within a 1.5-dva 15 

circular region centered on fixation for at least 300 ms. 16 

We presented stimuli on a gray background (luminance 77 cd/ m2). The fixation point 17 

that we used for the fixation control at the beginning of the trial remained on the screen and in 18 

addition eight circular placeholders (black circle outline; 1.95 dva diameter) forming an 19 

imaginary circle at an eccentricity of 6 dva from the screen center appeared. The placeholders 20 

indicated the potential locations where the stimuli of the memory set could be presented. In 21 

half of the trials (object-present trials), the eight placeholders were grouped into two objects 22 

by displaying two additional contours (3 pixels wide; each consisting of two semicircles with 23 

inner borders 4.25 dva and outer borders 7.75 dva from fixation; two black lines connected 24 

the semicircles, resulting in a closed object; contours enclosed 4 placeholders and spanned 25 

18 dva; possible orientations: 45, 135, 225, or 315 dva from vertical). Placeholders and 26 

contours remained on the screen for the remainder of the trial. 27

After 500 ms, a memory array consisting of 3 oriented Gabor patches (+45° or -45° from 1

Observers memorized the Gabor orientations for a later test. Following a 400-ms delay, a 5

movement cue appeared at fixation (0.26 dva black line segment extending beyond the 6

fixation outline) prompting a saccade within 400 ms to the indicated placeholder. After an 7

additional 800 ms delay, the response cue (i.e., thickening of one placeholder’s outline from 8

0.05 dva to 0.085 dva) instructed observers to report the remembered orientation (clockwise 9

or counterclockwise from vertical) using keys N and B. 10 

Importantly, in Experiment 1 the movement cue always pointed to a placeholder that 11 

had contained a Gabor, but the cue was uninformative about which item would be tested. 12 

Thus, the saccade target location matched the memory-probe location in 1/3 of trials; in the 13 

remaining 2/3, a non-target location was probed. 14 

We aborted trials online if a blink occurred, the gaze deviated more than 1.5 dva from 15 

fixation before movement-cue onset, or if the saccade latency exceeded 400 ms. Aborted 16 

trials were repeated in a randomized order at the end of each block. 17 

Each block contained all conditions (saccade target and memory probe congruent vs. 18 

incongruent; objects present vs. absent; same vs. a different object trials). A test session 19 

consisted of 12 blocks of 48 trials, resulting in 1,152 trials across the two test sessions and 20 

576 trials in the training session for each participant. 21

Data analysis. For saccade detection, we first transformed raw eye positions into 2D velocity 1

space offline and classified successive eye-position samples as saccades if velocity exceeded 2

the median by 5 SDs for a at least 8 ms (Engbert & Mergenthaler, 2006). Saccadic events 3

separated by less than 20 ms were merged. The first saccade landing within 3.6° of the 4

saccade-target center (i.e., 60% of the target’s eccentricity) was classified as the response 5

saccade. We excluded trials containing saccades with amplitudes larger than 1 dva prior to 6

the response saccade, as well as trials with blinks, from further analyses. In total, 9,806 trials 7

(85.1 %) entered the final data analysis. 8

We conducted an rmANOVA for statistical inference, followed by post-hoc paired t-9

tests for significant effects. Error bars are ±1 within-subject standard error of the mean (SEM; 10 

Baguley, 2011; Morey, 2008). All data and analysis scripts are available on the Open Science 11 

Framework 12 

(https://osf.io/2e7ay/overview?view_only=f4e78e29fb4e48c68ae9a68b0506654a). 13

The spatial specificity of saccadic selection 4

We compared the spatial specificity of saccadic selection in object-present and absent trials 5

(Figure 2b ). In line with our previous findings, we observed best memory performance at the 6

saccade target location and a sharp drop in memory performance already at the location next 7

There are multiple plausible reasons for this lack of a clear object-based advantage. 3

One possibility is that saccades do not select entire objects in visual memory. Alternatively, 4

aspects of our design may have reduced the likelihood of detecting such an effect. First, our 5

object manipulation might have been too weak to make observers perceive the Gabor 6

locations as strongly grouped or part of an object. Second, randomly interleaving object-7

present and object-absent trials could have allowed participants to suppress the contours and 8

focus only on the oriented Gabors. We designed a second experiment to overcome these 9

limitations. 10

Experiment 2 12 

In Experiment 2 , we addressed the potential concerns raised in the previous experiment. 13 

First, we asked whether the presentation of objects in a blocked design would allow observers 14 

to more readily take advantage of processing the available objects, and therefore reveal a 15

Discussion 4

We showed that the type of visual content previously occupying the saccade target location 5

systematically influenced both saccade parameters and memory performance. Saccades 6

were more accurate—reflected in larger amplitudes—when directed to locations that had 7

previously contained orientation-filtered noise patches compared to locations previously 8

occupied by non-oriented pink noise. Strikingly, however, an object-based advantage in 9

change detection emerged only when saccades were directed toward the former locations of 10 

non-oriented pink noise. No such advantage occurred when the saccade targeted a former 11 

orientation-filtered patch. This dissociation suggests that the visual characteristics of the 12 

saccade target determine not only the oculomotor response but also whether object-based 13 

selection in visual working memory is engaged. 14

General Discussion 16 

In the present study, we contrasted two possible levels at which saccades may select 17 

representations in visual working memory. First, saccadic selection in visual working memory 18 

could be space-based, predicting that the memory advantage is confined strictly to the 19 

saccade target location. Second, saccadic selection could take place at the level of maintained 20 

objects, in which case the memory advantage would extend beyond the saccade target 21 

location, enhancing memory for all items within the object containing the saccade target. Our 22 

findings from Experiments 1 and 2 support strong space-based saccadic selection in visual 23 

working memory, whereas the change detection task in Experiment 3 revealed that saccadic 24 

selection in visual memory can also be object-based, in addition to robust space-based 25 

selection. 26

The finding from Experiment 3 that saccadic selection in memory can in principle be 1

object-based in addition to space-based is in line with other studies reporting object-based 2

spread of attention in both perception and memory. For instance, reaction times are shorter 3

when attending to locations in memory that were parts of attended objects, compared with 4

equidistant locations in different objects. Moreover, brain activation in visual areas V1-V4 and 5

parietal cortex is substantially higher for positions along the attended object, suggesting that 6

attentional selection in working memory activates the entire object (Peters et al., 2015). 7

Memory updates are faster for two equidistant colors on the same object than on different 8

objects, and accuracy in retrieving memorized features is higher for un-cued features in the 9

same object than cued feature in a different object (Lin et al., 2021). 10 

Typically, such studies employ retro-cues in their experimental designs, which can be 11 

used strategically to suppress non-cued items or enhance cued memory representations (for 12 

review see Souza & Oberauer, 2016). In contrast, our experiments relied on saccades that 13 

were uninformative for the memory task, suggesting that, under certain conditions, saccadic 14 

selection can elicit an automatic spread of attention ( Experiment 3 ). This aligns with findings 15 

in the perceptual domain showing that attention can spread automatically to locations grouped 16 

with the saccade target, even when those locations are irrelevant to the task. Such effects 17 

have been demonstrated both in neural activation patterns in primary visual cortex in monkeys 18 

(Wannig et al., 2011) and in increased visual sensitivity in humans (Shurygina et al., 2019). 19 

20 

Factors modulating object-based selection 21 

We next consider the factors that might have hindered or enabled object-based attention in 22 

our three experiments. In the first two experiments, in which participants had to recall whether 23 

an orientation was clockwise or counterclockwise, we observed a strong space-based effect: 24 

Memory performance improved at the saccade target location but did not extend to other parts 25 

of the object containing the target. In contrast, the third experiment, which used a change 26 

detection task, revealed both space-based and object-based advantages. Notably, the object-27

First, the change detection paradigm may have promoted a global encoding of the 4

visual sensitivity did not increase at the saccade target location (Gupta & Sridharan, 2024). 9

Instead, the change detection task revealed a criterion shift leading participants to report more 10

participants to localize changes, aiming to encourage holistic object perception which may 13 

have resulted in overall better performance at the saccade target. Thus, our result is consistent 14 

with the general notion that the unit of selection in visual working memory can depend on task 15 

demands (Cao & Deouell, 2025). 16

perceptual grouping, making the objects more salient and cohesive while simultaneously 19 

reducing the saliency of individual probes. 20

those elements were task-irrelevant (Shurygina et al., 2019). In Experiment 2 , we attempted 25 

to enhance perceptual grouping relative to Experiment 1 by introducing distinctive textures, 26

the target. These results suggest that object-based selection in memory is conditional and 13 

task-dependent. 14

than saccades orientation-filtered probes. This suggests that remembering an oriented item 17

allow memory selection to spread more broadly across the object. Analogous findings in 19 

perception show that performance benefits at a saccade target can extend across textured 20 

regions if it separates the saccade target from the background, but rapidly declines with 21 

distance from when the target is on a uniform background (Ghahghaei & Verghese, 2017). 22

Robustness of space-based selection vs. fragility of object-based selection 24 

Overall, our results suggest that object-based selection in memory is more fragile and task-25 

dependent than the robust space-based selection, which consistently enhances memory at 26 

the saccade target. This aligns with a broader body of literature on object-based attention. 27

First, space-based attention tends to be stronger and steadier than object-based attention 1

(Egly, et al., 1994; Francis & Thunell, 2022). Moreover, object-based effects are dependent 2

on spatial direction and task type, often being stronger along the horizontal axis (Hein et al., 3

2016; Al-Janabi & Greenberg, 2016; Barnas & Greenberg, 2019; Chen & Cave, 2019) and in 4

detection or comparison tasks rather than identification tasks (Pilz et al., 2012; Zheng & 5

Moore, 2021). Spatial cue presence and validity also matter: Lower cue validity reduces the 6

likelihood of observing an object-based effect (Lou et al., 2022), and according to Donovan et 7

al. (2016) in the absence of a spatial (endogenous) cue objects do not guide attention. Finally, 8

while an increase in visual sensitivity is a gold standard for behavioral measures of attention, 9

behavioral measures of object-based attention are often observed in reaction times rather 10 

than accuracy (Egly, et al., 1994; Jeurissen et al., 2016b). We suggest that similar principles 11 

may apply in visual working memory. As evidence, behavioral and electrophysiological studies 12 

show that object integration at early perceptual stages influences the processing at 13 

subsequent stages, facilitating the maintenance of individual objects and their constituent 14 

features, and leading to enhanced precision of representations stored in memory. Thus, unlike 15 

space-based selection, object-based selection in memory depends on an interplay of stimulus 16 

configuration, spatial orientation, and task demands. 17 

18 

Limitations of the current study and future directions 19 

We argued that the emergence of an object-based effect in in Experiment 3 was driven by 20 

changes to both the task and the stimulus. However, these changes introduced a new 21 

challenge: a high drop-out rate among participants. Prior to data collection, we defined 22 

exclusion criteria based on task performance and saccade accuracy. Specifically, participants 23 

who failed to reach above chance accuracy in the first session were excluded from the 24 

experiment, as were participants who failed to execute accurate or timely saccades. In total, 25 

we excluded (and replaced) 15 participants in Experiment 3 .26

the integrity of the design. One straightforward approach is to reduce the delay between the 6

two stimuli which should markedly increase change detection performance. 7

Importantly, several participants who failed to meet performance criteria reported that 8

they could barely distinguish the oriented patches embedded in the noise texture. This 9

suggests that the difficulty was not solely memory-related but may also stem from perceptual 10 

limitations. To address this, a promising follow-up design could incorporate an adaptive 11 

staircase procedure to individually adjust the contrast between oriented patches and the 12 

background texture. This approach can ensure that the items are perceptually discriminable 13 

for each participant (e.g., Kroell & Rolfs, 2022). However, this solution is non-trivial: the 14 

contrast must remain subtle enough that the oriented patches are not perceived as distinct 15 

objects, which could reintroduce item-based encoding strategies. 16 

An alternative, and potentially simpler, solution would be to reduce the size of the 17 

stimulus display, thereby decreasing the distance between fixation and probe locations. This 18 

could enhance both perceptual encoding and saccadic accuracy, improving overall task 19 

feasibility. Another promising direction for future research is to move experimental designs 20 

toward more realistic, real-world scenarios because real, tangible objects elicit different 21 

perceptual, memory, and attentional responses than images (for review see Snow & Culham, 22 

2021) 23
