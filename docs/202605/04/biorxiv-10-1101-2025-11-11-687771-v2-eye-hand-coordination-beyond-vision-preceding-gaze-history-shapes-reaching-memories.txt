Abstract 12 

Skilled reaching behaviors, such as grasping a cup or catching a ball, require adaptive 13 

changes in response to error signals. Our natural reaches are usually coordinated with 14 

preceding eye movements. Despite this behavioral relevance, the potential linkage 15 

between preceding eye movements and memory formation for reaching has been largely 16 

overlooked. We show that two opposing visuomotor maps for reach, which normally 17 

prevent learning due to interference, can be learned when each is associated with a 18 

different immediately preceding eye movement. We further show that the generalization 19 

pattern of reach learning across different gaze conditions can be explained by the gaze 20 

state’s distance computed with temporal weighting. These findings indicate that reaching 21 

memories are encoded in tight association with the recent history of preceding gaze states. 22 

Our results expand the functional role of eye-hand coordination beyond visual guidance, 23 

revealing its importance in orchestrating flexible motor learning. 24 

25 

Significance 26 

In everyday behavior, goal-directed arm movements are typically preceded by eye 27 

movements, and eye-hand coordination has been regarded mainly as a functional 28 

mechanism for visual guidance of action. This study expands that view by showing that 29 

preceding gaze states also shape how the brain learns and retrieves motor memories of 30 

reaches. We demonstrate that conflicting visuomotor maps, which are usually difficult to 31 

learn because of memory interference, can be learned simultaneously when they are 32 

associated with different preceding eye movements. Furthermore, generalization 33 

experiments show that reach learning depends on the recent history of gaze states within a 34 

brief time window before reach onset. These findings indicate that preceding gaze history 35 

plays a significant role in shaping flexible reach memories. 36

Introduction 37 

In an ever-changing world, our skillful arm-reaching movements rely on motor learning, 38 

which involves the formation and updating of sensorimotor maps called internal models 1. A 39 

key issue in motor learning is understanding how the brain represents internal models, 40 

especially regarding their association with various body states 2,3 . Reaches are typically 41 

preceded by eye movements toward the target, hence much research has been conducted 42 

on how the brain spatiotemporally coordinates eye and hand movements, both in daily 43 

activities and laboratory tasks, particularly in relation to target representation 4,5 . In real-44 

world scenarios, reach learning often occurs under coordinated eye-then-reach 45 

movements 6. In tennis, for example, players track a moving ball with their eyes, hitting it 46 

while foveating it. Sometimes, their gaze shifts from the net to the ball just before taking the 47 

shot. Novel reaching skills, such as forehand strokes and backhand volleys, are acquired 48 

through repeated practice under dynamic eye-hand chains. This raises an unexplored 49 

question that spans both fields of eye-hand coordination and motor learning: Does the 50 

preceding dynamic gaze state inherently contribute to the encoding of motor memory for 51 

subsequent reaching movements? 52 

53 

There would be two opposing views on this matter. One view highlights the functional 54 

aspect of eye-hand coordination as target representation and deems it unrelated to the 55 

reach learning. From this view, gaze states serve two primary functions: 1) providing 56 

accurate localization of the reach target via foveation and 2) serving as a coordinates 57 

reference for encoding target location. During reach planning, target locations are initially 58 

represented in a gaze-centered frame and updated through eye movements. 7–9 Such gaze-59 

dependent signals are transformed into gaze-independent signals (e.g., hand-to-target 60 

trajectories), subsequently used with internal models to generate reach motor commands. 61 

Thus, in this sequential processing scheme, preceding gaze states are generally 62 

considered irrelevant to motor learning that primarily occurs downstream of target 63 

representation. 64 

65 

An alternative view focuses on the direct coupling of sensorimotor signals in the interwoven 66 

mechanisms of the eye and hand motor systems. Such motor-coordination mechanisms 67 

enable multiple effectors to work cooperatively, which is observed at various levels of the 68 

control hierarchy (e.g., bimanual coordination and multi-joint movements) 10 . From this view, 69 

motor commands to one effector depend heavily on the state of the other effector. Thus, in 70 

closely linked eye-hand coordination 11–13 , such as a single motor sequence, preceding 71 

gaze states may play a role in representing reach internal models. 72

Consistent with the latter view, motor-learning studies have demonstrated that learning 74 

reaching movements with one arm depends on the state of the other arm 14–17 . Several of 75 

these studies used context-dependent learning tasks in which two opposing perturbations 76 

(e.g., clockwise and counterclockwise velocity-dependent force fields) randomly switched 77 

across trials. Such opposing fields are usually difficult to learn due to memory interference 78 

but can be learned simultaneously when each is uniquely associated with a different 79 

movement pattern in the other arm. In unimanual and bimanual tasks, simultaneous 80 

learning is facilitated even when contextual movements occur before the onset of 81 

movement for adaptation 16–19 .82 

83 

Studies suggest that the prior and current hand states have a contextual effect on the 84 

separation of motor memories, thus are crucial components in the representation of reach 85 

internal models. In particular, the prior contextual effect is consistent with recent theories 86 

that emphasize preparatory neural states in motor-related cortices in motor learning 20–22 .87 

Most studies, however, have been limited to focusing on hand-related states as the learning 88 

context, and it remains untested whether similar parallel conclusions can be drawn for other 89 

body states. Although our recent study has demonstrated that the gaze location relative to 90 

the target (i.e., reaching towards a central or peripheral target) can separate visuomotor 91 

memories, the investigation was confined to specific static gaze conditions 23 . Despite its 92 

relevance for natural and common human behaviors, it is yet unknown whether/how 93 

preceding dynamic gaze states contribute to reach learning. 94 

95 

We address this issue and demonstrate, through a series of experiments, that participants 96 

can learn two opposing visuomotor maps when each is associated with a distinct preceding 97 

dynamic gaze state. This eye-dependent learning effect is sensitive to the time interval 98 

between eye and reaching movements. Our findings provide evidence that memory 99 

encoding for reaching is tightly linked to the recent history of gaze states. Preliminary 100 

findings from Experiment 1 were previously reported in a conference proceeding 24 .101

121 

Given that distinct static gaze conditions (i.e., PER and FOV) serve as effective contextual 122 

cues for simultaneous reach learning under conflicting conditions 23 , we designed three 123 

gaze conditions to test the contextual effect of eye movements. Even in the SACCADE 124 

condition, static gaze phases exist both before and after eye movements. In a two-condition 125 

paradigm (e.g., PER and SACCADE, or FOV and SACCADE), these static phases could 126 

serve as contextual cues, making it difficult to isolate the effect of dynamic eye movements. 127 

In contrast, our three-condition design enables a clear dissociation between static and 128 

dynamic gaze states. 129 

130 

In the current three-condition design, SACCADE trials started in the same way as PER, so 131 

pre-saccade gaze states were identical between the two conditions. Because different 132 

visuomotor rotations were applied to both conditions, pre-saccade static gaze states could 133 

not separate motor memories. Likewise, post-saccade static gaze states under SACCADE 134 

were identical to those under FOV, resulting in memory interference when relying on these 135 

post-saccade static gaze states. Thus, to enable simultaneous learning across the three 136 

gaze conditions, the brain must rely on the contextual differences between static and 137 

dynamic gaze conditions. 138

Control Experiment 1 253 

We further conducted a control experiment to test if the visual cue of fixation jump, rather 254 

than eye movements, could facilitate learning. The paradigm was the same as in 255 

Experiment 1, except that SACCADE was replaced with the fixation jump condition (FIX-256 

JUMP) (Suppl. Fig.1A). Under FIX-JUMP, the fixation cross jumped to the target, but 257 

participants were instructed to maintain their gaze at the original fixation location without 258 

making any eye movements. As in Experiment 1, one direction of visuomotor rotation was 259 

associated with two static conditions (i.e., PER and FOV), and the other direction was 260 

associated with FIX-JUMP. If visual cues are sufficient for simultaneous learning, 261 

comparable learning should be observed across all three conditions, as in the Short group 262 

of Experiment 1. In contrast, if gaze states, rather than visual cues, are crucial, learning 263 

should fail for PER ad FIX-jump, as these conditions share the same gaze states (i.e., 264 

reaching to a peripheral target) while being associated with opposing visuomotor rotations 265 

(CW and CCW; See Suppl. Fig.1A). Meanwhile, learning should progress for FOV, as it has 266 

a unique gaze state (i.e., reaching to a foveal target). Consistent with latter prediction, the 267 

results showed that learning was evident only under FOV, whereas PER and FIX-JUMP 268 

showed minimal improvement, indicating a failure of simultaneous learning (Supplementary 269 

Figure 1B). This pattern was statistically confirmed by quantifying the final learning level 270 

and aftereffects (see Supplementary Figure 1C). These results indicate that visual cues 271 

alone were insufficient, and that static versus dynamic gaze states are crucial for 272 

separating motor memories. 273

Experiment 2 275

simultaneous learning. In Experiment 2, we further investigated whether distinct patterns of 277

two saccade conditions were labeled by initial saccade direction (i.e., from F0 to F1): Left-285 

side and Right-side. 286

Experiment 3 338 

Finally, we investigated which factors in dynamic-gaze contexts contribute to reaching 339 

adaptation, using a generalization paradigm. Participants adapted to a single visuomotor 340 

rotation (20° CW or CCW; counterbalanced across participants). In each learning trial, they 341 

executed a rightward saccade toward the reach target, followed immediately by a reach to 342 

the foveal target (Figure 3A). In the subsequent probe session, we tested learning 343 

generalization to 14 possible gaze conditions (Figure 3B), with no cursor feedback. For the 344 

dynamic probes (probes 1–9 in Fig.3B), reaching movements were preceded by saccades, 345 

the locations and amplitudes of which varied across probes. For the static probes (probes 346 

10-14), reaches were performed while fixation was maintained at a certain location. Reach 347 

target remained fixed at the position used during the learning session. To prevent rapid 348 

decay of memory, each probe trial was preceded by a learning trial identical to those in the 349 

learning session (20° rotation; Figure 3A). For clarity, we plotted typical eye (horizontal 350 

position, solid lines) and hand (forward position, dashed lines) movements from the 351 

learning and selected probe conditions to illustrate the temporal eye-hand relationship 352 

(Figure 3C). 353

Discussion 425 

Reaching movements are typically preceded by coordinated eye movements. Such 426 

coordination has primarily been studied in relation to the roles of retinal and extraretinal 427 

signals in target representation and reaching accuracy 25–29 . Other studies have focused on 428 

the initiation of eye and hand movements (i.e., reaction times) to elucidate the 429

interconnected mechanisms between both motor systems 13,30–33 . Surprisingly little attention 430 

has been paid to how preceding eye movements impact reach-memory encoding, even 431 

though reach learning often occurs under eye-hand coordination in daily life. We used two 432 

learning paradigms—context-dependent learning and generalization learning—and 433 

demonstrated that preceding dynamic gaze states contribute to the formation and updating 434 

of internal models for reaching. 435 

436 

Several studies using sequential reaching tasks have reported improvement in reach 437 

learning when participants were allowed free eye movements compared to when gaze was 438 

fixaed 34,35 . However, these effects may be attributed to the rich visual information, such as 439 

target location and error signals, obtained by eye movements. This view is supported by 440 

findings from a hand-tracking adaptation task 36 . Thus, prior studies could not dissociate 441 

whether the effect was due to the inherent linkage of gaze states to the reach internal 442 

model or to the visual effect on the online reach control. Our previous study showed that 443 

different gaze conditions, reaching toward foveal and peripheral targets, can lead to the 444 

formation of distinct motor memories for reaching 23 . While this suggests a close link 445 

between gaze states and reach learning, gaze remained stationary throughout each trial, 446 

leaving open the possibility that learning was driven by differences in visual inputs (i.e., 447 

foveal vs. peripheral vision) during and/or at the end of the reaching movement. In contrast 448 

to prior studies, we ensured that gaze was directed at the reach target during reach 449 

execution, even under different gaze conditions associated with opposing visuomotor 450 

rotations (i.e., FOV and SACCADE in Experiment 1, Left-side and Right-side in Experiment 451 

2). Thus, the success of simultaneous learning cannot be explained by the difference in the 452 

quality of online visual feedback control. In Experiment 3, learning transfer was limited in 453 

probes 4 and 13, even though gaze was on the reach target during movement execution, 454 

just as under the learning condition (Fig. 3E). These results indicate that the preceding 455 

dynamic gaze states, rather than differences in visual feedback, play a significant role in 456 

the formation and expression of reaching memories. 457 

458 

The contextual encoding of multiple motor memories is a central issue in motor 459 

neuroscience 37,38 . Arbitrary visual cues unrelated to reaches generally fail to facilitate 460 

learning 39,40 , as confirmed in Control Experiment 1 and Control Experiment 2 (but, see Refs. 461 

27 and 28). Previous studies have shown that effective contextual cues are typically related 462 

to the state of the hand 14,39,40,43–45 or hand-associated objects 46–48 . Nozaki et al. 14 

463 

demonstrated that, in a bimanual reaching task, force-field learning in one arm was 464 

impacted by concurrent movements of the other arm but not by ankle flexion, suggesting 465

that contextual states should be somatotopically close to the trained effector. In contrast, 466 

we, along with our previous study 23 , show that gaze states—despite being somatotopically 467 

distant from the arm—can contribute to reach learning. This challenges the conventional 468 

view of somatotopic proximity and instead highlights functional relevance to reach planning 469 

as the critical determinant of context-dependent reach learning. Gaze locations relative to 470 

the reach target provide essential information for target representation, and dynamic gaze 471 

states update such gaze-centered representations in reach planning 28,49–51 , which might 472 

contribute to learning. The importance of functional relevance in contextual learning is also 473 

supported by recent findings showing that decision uncertainty can serve as a learning 474 

context 52 .475 

476 

The impact  of prior movements on motor learning suggests that the adapted motor output 477 

at time t depends not only on the corresponding body state at time t ([x_t]) but also on its 478 

recent history within a certain time window, expressed as [x_t,x_(t-1), ⋯,x_(t-∆t)] 16–19 . This 479 

indicates that the learning effect is not determined by binary information (i.e., presence or 480 

absence of eye movements) but should be sensitive to the time interval between the 481 

contextual and adaptation movements. Supporting this view, in Experiment 1 (Fig.1D), the 482 

contextual learning effect decreased from the Short (~260-ms interval) to the Middle group 483 

(~540 ms) and disappeared in the Long group (~1200 ms). In Experiment 3, the 484 

generalization pattern was well accounted for by the state distance of eye positions across 485 

gaze conditions, computed using a temporally weighting (Fig. 3G). This analysis showed 486 

that learning incorporated gaze history up to approximately 600 ms prior to reach onset (Fig. 487 

3H), a temporal window broadly consistent with the findings of Experiment 1. 488 

489 

Several explanations may account for the temporal decay of prior contextual effects. One 490 

possibility is that the limited time window reflects a common mechanism across different 491 

learning paradigms. In sequential reaching tasks, lead-in movements serve as contextual 492 

learning cues for subsequent movements in force fields 18,19 . Howard et al. 18 found that this 493 

effect decayed progressively as the interval between two sequential movements increased, 494 

disappearing by ~600 ms, a time range consistent with our findings. A more recent study 53 

495 

also showed that associative learning between preceding contextual stimuli (sound or light) 496 

and reaching movements occur when the stimulus-reach interval is around 300 ms but not 497 

beyond 1000 ms. These findings, together with ours, support the hypothesis that prior 498 

context impact memory encoding for subsequent reaching within a constrained temporal 499 

window, regardless of the modality or effector serving as the contextual cue. One potential 500 

mechanism underlying this temporal window is cerebellar processing, as has been 501

Consistent with previous motor learning studies and our findings, optimal conditioning 505 

learning occurs when the conditioned stimulus precedes the unconditioned stimulus by 506 

approximately 500 ms (e.g., Ref. 54 ). 507 

508 

Alternatively, the temporal window in gaze-dependent learning may stem from eye-509 

movement-specific processes. To separate motor memories by preceding gaze states, the 510 

brain needs to retain saccade-related information for a certain period during and after the 511 

saccade, ensuring its availability for reach planning and/or execution. According to this view, 512 

neurophysiological studies have shown that the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and lateral 513 

intraparietal cortex (LIP), both involved in saccade control, exhibit post-saccadic activity 514 

lasting approximately 500 ms after saccade termination 55–60 . Within the first 300 ms, this 515 

post-saccadic activity carries information about the visual location of the saccade target, 516 

saccade direction 55,59 , and movement vector in depth 56 . Eye position and saccade direction 517 

were also accurately decoded by population activities in the LIP after saccade termination 60 .518 

Thus, post-saccadic activity carrying saccade-related information could provide contextual 519 

signals for reaching memories, with its impact decaying as more time elapses after the 520 

saccade. Consistent with our findings, a recent behavioral study suggested that preceding 521 

saccades are used as learning contexts in a sequential saccades task 61 . Together, these 522 

results raise an interesting question for future research: whether the temporal features of 523 

contextual learning are mediated by cue-specific processes linked to gaze or by more 524 

general mechanisms of motor learning such as cerebellum-dependent processes. 525 

526 

Several behavioral studies 16–20 , along with the present study, have highlighted the 527 

importance of contextual cues during reach planning for reach learning. These findings 528 

align with an emerging framework that views motor cortices as a dynamical system 62 .529 

Preparatory neural activities in the premotor and primary motor cortices are tightly linked to 530 

reach learning 21,22 . Human imaging 63 and brain stimulation 64 studies further supported the 531 

critical role of motor cortices in context-dependent learning. According to this framework, 532 

our results suggest that preceding static and dynamic gaze states induce different 533 

preparatory neural states for reaching, enabling the formation of multiple motor memories. 534 

Consistent with this interpretation, gaze-dependent neural signals have been identified 535 

along the dorsal visuomotor pathway, from parietal to motor areas. Premotor neural 536 

activities vary with static and dynamic gaze conditions in the eye-hand movement task 65–71 .537

Future work is needed to clarify how interplays among the parietal cortex, motor areas, and 538 

cerebellum contribute to contextual encoding and the formation of motor memories in 539

541 

In daily situations such as  playing tennis, individuals are often faced with the need to 542 

learn and switch different reaching skills (e.g., forehand and backhand shots) while tracking 543 

a fast-moving ball with their gaze. Given the limitations of external cues in contextual 544 

learning, it is possible that specific patterns of preceding eye movements are linked to 545 

particular internal models of reaching, facilitating efficiency of motor-skill acquisition and 546 

expression, especially during the early stages of skill training. Considering  the highly 547 

variable eye-hand coordination in daily life, gaze-independent learning would of course  be 548 

more appropriate than a  gaze-dependent manner in certain situations. Important questions 549 

remain as to how the brain transitions between gaze-dependent and gaze-independent 550 

learning and how these mechanisms can be effectively applied in real-life settings, including 551 

sports and rehabilitation. 552

Data analysis. 796 

The initiation and termination of reaching movements were detected online during the 797 

experiment and defined as the first time point at which tangential velocity exceeded or fell 798 

below 2 cm/s for at least 30 ms. For offline analysis, the obtained position data were 799 

aligned at the reach onset and low-pass filtered at 20 Hz (4th-order Butterworth). Velocity 800 

profiles were also obtained by three numerical time differentiations. 801 

802 

To assess learning, the initial direction of reaching movements (initial reach direction) was 803 

quantified on each trial as the angular difference between the movement trajectory and a 804 

line connecting the start point to the target. The initial reach direction was computed as the 805 

mean direction of velocity vectors during the 100–120-ms window after reach onset. To 806 

collapse data across different rotation directions (CW and CCW), the sign of the reach 807 

direction was adjusted so that positive values indicated compensatory movements for the 808 

corresponding rotation. 809 

810

direction under each condition in the pre-learning session. In Experiment 3, such biases 812 

were estimated across different gaze and visual conditions (cursor visible or invisible). 813 

These baseline biases were then subtracted from the corresponding trials throughout the 814 

experiment. This process was conducted for each participant. 815
