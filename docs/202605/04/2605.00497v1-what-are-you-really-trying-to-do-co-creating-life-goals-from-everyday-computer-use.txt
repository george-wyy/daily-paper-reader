## Abstract 

Recent advances in user modeling make it feasible to conduct open-ended inference over a person’s everyday computer use. Despite longstanding visions of systems that deeply understand our ac-tions and the purposes they serve in our lives, existing systems only capture what a person is doing in the moment—not why they are doing it—limiting these systems to surface-level support. We introduce striving co-creation, a process for inferring broader life goals from unstructured observations of computer use. Grounded in Activity Theory and Emmons’ personal strivings framework, our

## 1 Introduction 

How might we build systems that understand not just what a per-son is doing, but the broader purposes their actions serve? Consider a person who reads about a city they have never lived in, updates a cost-of-living spreadsheet, and texts their partner about school districts. Each action occurs in a different application and a different moment, yet all three serve a single activity: planning a relocation. This activity itself might serve an even broader personal pursuit, such as trying to build a stable home for a growing family. Human activity draws its meaning from exactly this kind of hierarchical organization, in which low-level actions serve higher-level activi-ties, and activities in turn serve broader, long-term strivings. From Weiser’s vision of ubiquitous computing [ 79 ] to Apple’s Knowl-edge Navigator [ 3], the most enduring visions of human-computer interaction have imagined systems with a deep understanding of our everyday activities. Such an understanding of our broader life goals could enable proactive agents [ 58 ], reflection tools [ 20 , 51 ], adaptive interfaces [ 25 ], or other context-aware systems [ 1, 15 ] that support us not just in momentary productivity, but in making sense of what matters and achieving our long-term goals. Current applications fall short of this vision because existing representations of context capture only the lower levels of the hi-erarchy. The dominant signals available to today’s systems (e.g., location, device state, application focus, and in-app behavior) de-scribe what a person is doing, but not why . For someone planning a relocation, a system limited to such surface-level understand-ing might suggest better apartment filters, flag a cheaper city, or even generate a new interface on the fly to complete a specific task faster. But it would not know to surface family-rated school districts, connect the move to an ongoing effort to reduce financial stress, or recognize that a pause in searching reflects competing priorities rather than lost interest. Recent progress in user model-ing has enabled systems that reason across applications and infer a user’s tasks and state from observed activity [ 48 , 70 , 83 ], but these inferences remain task-level and situational. More broadly, prior approaches to computational goal inference have either remained at the level of immediate actions [ 14 , 48 , 55 , 83 ], required users to articulate their own goals upfront [ 12 , 36 ], or relied on manually de-fined activity structures [ 5, 40 ] rather than inferring the hierarchy from observed behavior. Still, hierarchical inference alone is insufficient because the map-ping from behavior to goals is often ambiguous . The person in our example could be building stability for a growing family or leaving a community where they no longer belong. More data or better models cannot always resolve this ambiguity. If the system infers goals on its own, it is forced to choose among plausible interpreta-tions, imposing its assumptions onto the user. Yet restricting the system to user-articulated goals forgoes the value of surfacing in-ferences the person may not have articulated or even recognized. Instead, we argue that goals must be co-created with the user. On a technical level, user input addresses both the cold-start problem and the disambiguation problem. More fundamentally, co-creation ensures that the person, not the system, has the final say over how they are represented and understood by a system that takes action on their behalf. In this paper, we introduce striving co-creation , a computa-tional process in which a person and the system jointly construct a representation of the long-term goals a person is working toward from unstructured computer use. The system synthesizes actions across applications and time into higher-order goal statements, and the person reshapes those statements to reflect their own under-standing, producing strivings that are recognizable as meaningful and representative of their lives. For instance, the cost-of-living spreadsheet, school-district searches, and partner messages from our earlier example might initially be synthesized as trying to relocate to a more affordable city . The person, recognizing that the move is in service of something broader, might reshape this as trying to build a stable home for a growing family , a constraint the system carries forward into subsequent inductions. We instantiate striving co-creation in Tempo , a system grounded in Activity Theory’s account of hierarchical action [ 41 , 50 ] and Em-mons’ concept of personal strivings [ 18 ]. Tempo organizes observed behavior across applications and time into a hierarchy spanning operations, actions, activities, and strivings. Our system contributes two main modules. An Induce module, seeded with a user-provided self-description, progressively abstracts raw screen observations into a hierarchical representation. An Edit module then surfaces the inferred hierarchy to the person and feeds their corrections back into subsequent induction as constraints, so that each synthesis run reflects both what the system observed and what the person knows about themselves. In this way, Tempo treats strivings not as defini-tive facts to be extracted but as interpretations to be negotiated with the user. We additionally include a contextual integrity [62] Audit 

module, inspired by prior work [ 70 ], which filters observations the user would not expect to be incorporated into the goal model. We evaluate Tempo through a week-long field deployment with 14 participants. We assess whether the system produces strivings that are individually precise, collectively representative of partici-pants’ lives, and useful to negotiate through hierarchical editing. Despite the difficulty of inferring abstract, long-term goals from noisy screenshots of everyday computer use, participants judged strivings generated by the full system as accurate ( 𝑀 = 1.25 on a -3 to +3 scale; 80% rated ≥ 1). Ablation results show that incorporating user-provided context improves individual striving quality, while hierarchical structure is especially important for representativeness. Participants rated the editing experience significantly higher when strivings were represented hierarchically. These findings suggest that modeling what people are working toward long-term requires not only hierarchical inference, but also co-creative mechanisms that let users shape how they are represented. 

## 2 Related Work 

In this section, we situate our work within prior literature on user modeling, human-AI interaction, and reflection on personal data. 

## 2.1 User Modeling from Behavioral Traces 

A longstanding agenda in HCI is to build computational representa-tions of users from observation rather than from explicit specifica-tion alone. Early work in context-aware and ubiquitous computing “What Are You Really Trying to Do?”: Co-Creating Life Goals from Everyday Computer Use 

focused on sensing user state from behavioral signals and adapting system behavior accordingly [ 1 , 15 , 32 , 58 ], while activity-centric computing operationalized Activity Theory to organize work and interactions around higher-level activity structures rather than in-dividual applications [ 5, 40 , 53 ]. A parallel thread established cross-application behavioral observation from screenshots and interac-tion logs [ 64 , 65 ], which multimodal foundation models have since extended to enable user modeling from unstructured observations such as screenshots, interaction logs, and screen recordings [ 33 , 70 ]. While these systems build increasingly rich representations of users, the representations remain at lower levels of the activity hierarchy, capturing what a user is doing or interested in without connecting observations to their long-term pursuits. A related line of work focuses on inducing user goals from behav-ioral traces. Early approaches inferred goals by matching observed actions to pre-specified libraries [ 43 ] or by constructing proba-bilistic models of user intent within individual applications [ 32 ]. Subsequent systems have learned to predict user tasks from desktop interaction logs across applications [ 16 ], while recent work uses LLMs to move beyond closed goal sets to infer open-ended goals from action sequences and anticipate user needs from observa-tion [ 48 , 55 , 83 ] or from extended conversational interactions [ 14 ]. Other systems support long-term or open-ended goals but require users to articulate them upfront rather than inferring them from behavior [ 12 , 36 ]. In both lines of work, the goals are either task-scoped, bounded to a work session or interaction episode, or de-pendent on explicit user specification rather than capturing the enduring cross-domain pursuits that characterize what a person is broadly trying to accomplish in life. To move beyond task-scoped inference, we draw on personal strivings from personality psychology [ 18 ] and the hierarchical decomposition of human activity from Activity Theory [ 26 , 41 , 50 ]. Our work extends this hierarchy one level further, connecting ob-served screen operations, via actions and activities, to the strivings that organize them. 

## 2.2 Human-AI Interaction 

A range of approaches have sought to give users visibility into and control over AI representations of themselves, from scrutable user models that make system inferences inspectable [ 4 , 9, 44 ] to inter-active machine learning techniques that let users correct model behavior through examples and explanatory feedback [ 2, 47 , 72 ]. However, research on algorithmic awareness has shown that visibil-ity alone is insufficient. Users who discover how systems represent them want to reshape those representations, not just accept or reject the output [ 21 , 42 ]. Contestability frameworks argue that users need mechanisms to challenge not just individual decisions but the goals, values, and conceptual relationships encoded in systems [ 30 , 56 , 76 ]. Recent work has further shown that models’ learned conceptual structures can systematically diverge from human-encoded ones [ 7]. Across these works, users can correct what a system infers about them, and in some cases control whose judgments the system re-flects [ 28 ]. But they cannot reshape how the system organizes those inferences into a coherent interpretation of who the user is and what they are trying to accomplish, a concern underscored by growing evidence that AI representations of users’ values and identity can shape how users see themselves, even when those representations are incomplete or sycophantic [34, 35, 71, 82]. Recent work has begun addressing this gap by making AI inter-mediate representations editable at multiple levels, framing memory updates as conflict resolution, transforming reasoning chains into navigable trees, and organizing LLM outputs into hierarchical in-formation spaces [ 23 , 63 , 74 , 77 ]. We draw from these interactive editing mechanisms to build Tempo , where edits at each level of the hierarchy propagate to future inductions as new observations arrive. 

## 2.3 Reflection on Personal Data 

Our work draws on the personal informatics literature, which stud-ies how people collect and reflect on personal data [ 20 , 51 ]. A persistent finding is that while systems make it easy to collect data, the burden of connecting that data to the goals people actually care about still falls on the user [ 6 , 11 , 19 , 24 , 68 ]. One line of recent work addresses this gap through LLM-assisted sensemaking, trans-lating multi-modal sensing streams into higher-level behavioral insights [ 13 , 37 , 52 ]. Another supports goal-directed sensemaking by letting users define wellbeing goals and teach the system what those goals mean in terms of their personal data [ 39 ]. Our work extends this literature by inferring a user’s long-term goals and grounding reflection in the discrepancy between the system’s in-ferences and the user’s self-perception. A related thread has made goals a first-class element in self-tracking [ 17 , 61 , 66 , 67 ], drawing on a goal-setting tradition that supports specific, measurable targets [ 54 ]. As a result, tracked goals are typically self-reported and health-centered rather than being open-ended, long-term pursuits [ 86 ]. Our work inverts the rela-tionship between data and goals. Rather than requiring users to specify goals upfront and track data against them, Tempo starts from observed behavior and proposes candidate strivings, inviting users to reshape the result through co-creation. 

## 3 Tempo: A System for Striving Co-Creation 

We introduce Tempo , a system that operationalizes striving co-creation. Tempo consists of two modules: an Induce module that takes screenshots and lightweight user context as input and pro-gressively abstracts raw observations into a four-level hierarchy; and an Edit module that surfaces the inferred hierarchy to the user and feeds user corrections back into subsequent rounds of induc-tion. Figure 1 shows a snippet of the striving hierarchy produced by Tempo .

## 3.1 The Induce Module 

Tempo ’s Induce module progressively abstracts raw screen obser-vations into a four-level hierarchy of behavior. We use Gemini 3 Flash [ 75 ] for both screenshot transcription and hierarchical decom-position (see §3.3 for details). The resulting hierarchy is stored as a property graph as shown in Figure 2, in which every node carries a natural-language description and structured metadata. Each stage of the pipeline is additionally conditioned on user-provided context, described in §3.1.2. 

3.1.1 The Striving Hierarchy. Tempo draws on Activity Theory’s hierarchical model of human behavior [ 41 , 50 ], which distinguishes Sapkota et al. 

behavior by its purpose. Activity Theory’s original formulation spans three levels: operations (automated responses to conditions), actions (goal-directed behaviors), and activities (motive-driven pat-terns). We extend this hierarchy upward with Emmons’ concept of personal strivings [ 18 ]—ongoing pursuits that organize multiple activities and persist beyond any single project. This extension is necessary because Activity Theory’s highest level (activities) re-mains tied to specific contexts and timescales, whereas strivings capture the enduring goals that unify behavior across life domains. We model Tempo around a means-ends hierarchy in which lower-level behaviors support broader pursuits over time [ 18 , 46 ], allowing the same action to contribute to multiple activities, and the same activity to support multiple strivings. Tempo constructs the hierar-chy through prompt chaining, drawing on the in-context learning capabilities of LLMs [ 8 , 81 ]. Each level’s structured output becomes the input, and the conditioning context, for the level above. We extend prior work on LLM-based concept induction and cluster-ing [49, 78, 84, 85] to a chained hierarchical pipeline [38, 69, 80]. 

Operations. Operations are the lowest level of the hierarchy, de-scribing atomic behavioral interactions such as a click, a scroll, or a keystroke. For instance, a screenshot of the first author browsing YouTube was transcribed as browsing YouTube search results for classical music . Unlike the other levels, operations are descriptive records of screen events, not inferences about purpose. 

Operation Inference. The observer captures screenshots and bun-dles a set of contiguous frames into a single observation. A vision-language model (VLM) transcribes each observation into a struc-tured markdown description of visible screen content, conditioned on user context. From each transcription, one or more operations are extracted, each carrying structured metadata (see Appendix D.3). 

Actions. Actions are goal-directed sequences composed from con-tiguous operations, where intent first becomes visible. Three op-erations, messaging a friend about a football game on iMessage , searching for ‘Gareth Bale retirement’ on Goog-le , and reading a biography of Gareth Bale on Wikipedia ,span three applications but share a common intent and are grouped into a single action: Following up on a football conversation by researching Gareth Bale’s career . Action boundaries are placed at shifts in intent, not merely changes in application [ 27 , 59 ]. 

Action Inference. Operations accumulate in a buffer until either a batch of operations has been collected or a period of inactiv-ity elapses. The language model then segments the buffer into one or more actions, grouping operations by shared intent rather than application boundaries. The segmentation prompt receives the buffered operations, recent actions, and local graph context as input and returns groups of operations belonging to a single goal-directed action. Each newly created action is then linked to its neighbors in the property graph via three kinds of temporal relations : a follows 

edge to the immediately preceding action, co-occurs edges to other actions extracted from the same buffer, and overlaps edges to recent actions whose time ranges intersect. These edges accu-mulate as the system observes the user and become a key signal for higher-level grouping at the activity tier. 

Activities. Activities are recurring patterns of actions that serve a common motive, broader than a single task but narrower than a life domain. Activities are where cross-application, cross-session synthesis happens. Classical music performances, food delivery orders, family health-monitoring research, and election tracking, spread across YouTube, DoorDash, Chrome, and iMessage over mul-tiple days, converge into a single activity: Managing homesickness and cultural identity through personal interests, food, news, music, and family messages .

Activity Inference. New actions trigger a two-step propose-and-reconcile process. A proposal prompt takes new actions alongside the follows /co-occurs /overlaps edges built in the previous step, existing activities, and user context, identifying candidate activ-ity groupings using semantic similarity, temporal co-occurrence, shared metadata, and graph-local structure. A reconcile prompt then integrates each candidate against the existing repository, de-ciding whether to match, revise, merge, or create a new activity. Activities themselves are then linked by their own temporal edges (so that, e.g., an activity that consistently follows another can later inform striving synthesis), and remain provisional until they recur across multiple batches. 

Strivings. Strivings are ongoing personal pursuits in Emmons’ [ 18 ]sense: recurring goals that organize multiple activities and persist beyond any single project. Activities are already motive-driven, but each activity’s motive is local to its cluster of actions. Managing homesickness and cultural identity and Researching family health monitoring services are two separate activities involv-ing different applications and immediate goals. The striving layer infers that they are expressions of the same thing, synthesizing both into Dedicated to preserving cultural identity and family connections while mitigating the isolation of life abroad .

Striving Inference. A synthesis prompt takes the current set of recon-ciled activities alongside any existing strivings and user context, ei-ther mapping activities to existing strivings or proposing new ones. A second prompt critiques the synthesis against theory-grounded quality criteria, checking for redundancy, evidence quality, and properties drawn from Emmons [ 18 ]. This self-refine pass [ 57 ] can merge redundant strivings, split overly broad ones, or recalibrate confidence scores. The synthesis prompt is invoked once activities have begun stabilizing (a one-batch warmup), with a twenty-four-hour ceiling enforced as a fallback. 

3.1.2 User-Provided Context. The same behavior can express fun-damentally different pursuits depending on the user’s values, cir-cumstances, and self-understanding, none of which are observable on screen. Before passive observation begins, Tempo elicits a light-weight self-description from the user. Twelve questions cover cur-rent roles, daily routines, demands on time and energy, sources of stress, recent life changes, and priorities across six life domains: work, relationships, personal growth, health, finances, and edu-cation (full questions in Appendix A). These questions surface information that screenshots alone cannot reliably reveal, such as caregiving responsibilities, financial pressure, major deadlines, or ongoing transitions. Critically, the elicitation does not ask users to specify their goals. ## 3.2 The Edit Module 

The Edit module gives users a mechanism to bring the hierarchy into alignment with their own self-understanding, resolving ambi-guities that context alone cannot settle. Because the Induce module produces intermediate representations at four levels of abstraction, the Edit module surfaces strivings, activities, and the actions that ground them as editable targets, with the edit operations avail-able at each level calibrated to the kinds of disagreements a user is likely to have there (Figure 3). Users can intervene wherever their disagreement lives, rather than reconstructing meaning from raw screenshots. The act of inspecting the hierarchy, tracing from a striving down through activities and actions to the screenshots that ground them, can itself prompt reflection on how daily behav-ior connects to broader pursuits. We return to these effects in our evaluation (§5.4). For example, the first author noticed that Tempo had grouped Slack notifications about IRB logistics and paper coordination un-der an activity labeled managing lab community engagement, technical awareness, and professional presence , support-ing a striving about building social capital and professional visibility within the HCI community . The behavior was ad-ministrative, not community building. Rather than locating the offending screenshots among hundreds, selecting the activity node immediately showed that the supporting evidence was IRB logistics and paper coordination. Removing the activity triggered Tempo to reassign the orphaned actions to a research activity and merge the two strivings into one: establishing themselves as areliable member of the HCI community through rigorous research, active mentorship, and peer support .

3.2.1 Editing Operations. The Edit module exposes four opera-tions through direct manipulation or natural-language input, each available at the levels where it applies (Figure 3). Inline edit lets a user rewrite the text of a striving or activity when its synthesis is right but its description does not match how the user understands themselves. Reassign moves an activity to a different parent striv-ing. Remove drops a striving, activity, or action whose evidence is wrong or unrepresentative. Merge combines two strivings or two activities the system kept separate, preserving the union of their evidence. Operations are not surfaced as editable because they are descriptive records of screen events rather than inferences about purpose (§3.1.1). 

3.2.2 Edit Feedback Loop. When a user makes an edit, changes to the hierarchy are shown immediately in the interface, and rel-evant pipeline stages are re-triggered. Inline editing, merging, or reassigning activities across strivings re-runs striving synthesis; re-moving a striving orphans its child activities, which are reassigned by the activity inference step. Edits persist as constraints on all subsequent inductions. Edited labels are flagged [user-edited] 

in the property graph and preserved verbatim; reassigned activities carry [user-reassigned] flags. When the pipeline re-runs, these flags are surfaced to the synthesis and reconciliation prompts as constraints the model must respect, while the system enforces—at both the prompt level and through write-time guards in the recon-ciliation pipeline that block any update that would relabel, merge, or delete a flagged entity—that user-edited nodes are never modi-fied without explicit user action [ 30 , 31 , 56 ]. Each induction cycle thus reflects both what the system observed and what the person knows about themselves (see Appendix D.1 for the constraint block injected into each prompt.). 

## 3.3 Privacy, Data Security, and Implementation 

We designed Tempo ’s data practices around the principle that the user should retain control over what is observed, what is inferred, and where their data is stored. This study was approved by our institution’s IRB and underwent a data risk assessment by our institution’s privacy office. Sapkota et al. 

Figure 3: The four operations Tempo exposes for editing the hierarchy: inline edit (A), reassign (B), remove (C), and merge (D). All edits persist as constraints on subsequent induction cycles (§3.2). 

3.3.1 User-Facing Controls. Tempo ’s dashboard lets users exclude specific applications and websites from capture, and users can pause recording at any time. During editing (§3.2), users can remove individual screenshots from the evidence supporting any node. 

3.3.2 Automated Privacy Filters. User-facing controls are strength-ened by a two-stage automated filter. First, a local OCR model checks each screenshot against a library of personally identifiable information patterns (e.g., credit card numbers, API keys, and health record identifiers) and deletes any matching frame before it enters the pipeline. Second, based on prior work [ 70 ], a contextual in-tegrity audit [ 62 ] uses the system’s own inferences to model the user’s disclosure norms, e.g., blocking a banking login page if the system has never observed the user sharing financial information. 

3.3.3 Implementation. Tempo is implemented in Python for Ma-cOS (inference pipeline and property graph) and Node.js (Observer application and editing interface). All screenshots and inferred hi-erarchy nodes are stored locally on the user’s device. Inference calls are routed through Google Cloud Platform under our institu-tion’s enterprise cloud agreement, which covers all data risk levels and prevents data from being used for model training. Upon study completion, all data was deleted from participants’ devices. 

## 4 Applications of Striving Hierarchy 

Once constructed, the striving hierarchy functions as a general-purpose API for downstream applications. The hierarchy is stored as a property graph (§3.1) that applications can query at any level of abstraction, traverse to connect immediate behavior to long-term purpose, and subscribe to for updates when new strivings emerge or confidence scores shift. To illustrate the breadth of applications this API enables, we built two prototypes: a proactive agent that grounds suggestions in long-term purpose rather than immediate task context, and a behavioral coach that detects tensions between competing strivings and proposes interventions informed by the full graph. 

## 4.1 Proactive assistance across strivings 

Proactive agents are systems that anticipate what a user needs and act on their behalf [ 58 ]. Existing proactive agents typically operate at the task level, recommending a movie when a user is browsing entertainment or suggesting a calendar slot during a scheduling conversation [ 55 , 70 ]. The striving hierarchy enables a different kind of proactive support, grounded not in what the user is doing but in the long-term goals their actions serve. The first author was alternating between a job application in Google Docs and a Slack thread about a group project. A task-level agent would have seen two applications and offered only imme-diate help. The hierarchy revealed that these actions served two different strivings, building financial independence after graduation and maintaining collaborative relationships in their research group , each with distinct temporal evidence. Under the financial-independence lens, the system retrieved de-clining momentum for job search actions: “Block 30 minutes this afternoon to finalize the cover letter. Your application momentum has dropped this week and the deadline is Friday.” Under the col-laboration lens, it surfaced a different set of actions: “Reply to [teammate]’s data request before end of day. You’ve addressed 3 of 5 threads today but this one has been open since yesterday.” Be-cause the hierarchy organized actions under competing strivings, the system also surfaced conflicts directly, flagging when both striv-ings demanded the same afternoon block and presenting both paths with their trade-offs. “What Are You Really Trying to Do?”: Co-Creating Life Goals from Everyday Computer Use 

## 4.2 Behavioral coaching grounded in the hierarchy 

Behavior change interventions are most effective when they con-nect to goals the person actually holds [ 37 ], yet most deployed systems deliver generic, decontextualized prompts [ 38 ]. We built a coaching prototype that uses motivational interviewing [ 60 ]grounded in the hierarchy’s evidence. Each day, the coach chat-bot identifies strivings with declining momentum, selects an MI strategy matched to the user’s trajectory, and grounds its opening message in specific behavioral evidence from the graph. When the first author interacted with the chatbot before the UIST deadline, the system opened by noting momentum toward 

establishing yourself as a high-impact researcher but de-clining time allocated toward preserving meaningful connecti-ons : “The parts of your life that ground you are being squeezed into the margins.” When the first author mentioned stress, the coach surfaced a specific pattern: late-night work sessions heightening iso-lation. Drawing on evidence that strategically streamlining logistical friction was already a behavioral strength, it pro-posed repurposing that pattern: “What if you carved out 15 minutes before your late-night food order to call someone back home? It might transform that window from high-stress debugging into a moment that addresses your feelings of isolation.” These responses were possible because the hierarchy let the coach detect tensions between strivings and propose interventions that serve one pursuit without disrupting another. 

## 5 Evaluation 

Tempo ’s architecture contributes two modules: an Induce module that progressively abstracts observations into a striving hierarchy, and an Edit module that lets users trace and reshape the system’s inferences. We evaluate each in turn. For the Induce module, we ask whether the pipeline produces strivings that people recognize as accurate and representative of their lives, and whether hierarchy and user-provided context each contribute to this quality (§5.3). For the Edit module, we ask whether the intermediate representations the Induce module constructs, the operations, actions, and activities linking observations to strivings, serve as a useful surface for co-creation (§5.4). We deploy Tempo with 𝑁 = 14 participants over one week of naturalistic computer use, then assess the resulting strivings through blind ratings and an editing session in a lab visit. 

## 5.1 Participants 

We recruited 19 participants through word-of-mouth and internal messaging channels at our institution. Five did not complete the study or had insufficient data for the pipeline to stabilize activities and synthesize strivings. We required at least 500 processed screen-shots, corresponding to roughly one hour of active screen time at our sampling rate. This yielded 𝑁 = 14 participants (see Appen-dix D.10 for participant background information) for all reported analyses (median 1,498 processed screenshots; range 765–9,435). 

## 5.2 Deployment 

Participants installed Tempo on their personal Macbook and filled out a brief onboarding form to provide the necessary user context. The system then ran for seven days, capturing screenshots of user interactions. Participants were fully informed and consented to screenshots from their personal computer being processed by the Gemini API, and were fully informed of the system’s privacy fea-tures and protections. The full system and both ablations (§5.3.1) ran in parallel on the same observation stream, but the participants were not shown the inferences as they were being generated. After a week of use, participants attended an hour-long lab session com-prising striving ratings (§5.3) and editing tasks (§5.4), followed by a semi-structured interview. 

## 5.3 Evaluating the Induce Module 

The Induce module has two key elements. The hierarchical structure organizes observations into a four-level representation, and user-provided context shapes how that hierarchy interprets what it observes. We compare the full system against two ablations to evaluate whether each contributes to striving quality. 

5.3.1 Ablations. 

• Tempo . The full hierarchical pipeline, seeded with the onboard-ing context. 

• Tempo − {User Context} . The full hierarchical pipeline, but without access to the onboarding context. 

• Tempo − {Hierarchy, User Context} . The hierarchical pipeline is removed entirely. Rather than progressively abstracting obser-vations through operations, actions, and activities, this ablation does striving synthesis on the observations directly using a slid-ing window of past observations that fit into a model’s context window, without intermediate structure or user context. Comparing Tempo to Tempo − {User Context} holds hierarchy constant and varies context, isolating whether the person’s on-boarding input improves individual striving quality. Comparing 

Tempo − {User Context} to Tempo − {Hierarchy, User Context} removes the hierarchical pipeline entirely, testing whether the ar-chitecture produces a more representative set than direct inference from observations. 

5.3.2 Measures. The goal of striving induction is to produce striv-ings that are individually well-formed and collectively representa-tive of what the person is working toward. We evaluate striving quality along two dimensions: precision and representativeness.

## 5.4 Evaluating the Edit Module 

The Induce module constructs intermediate representations that link observations to strivings. The Edit module makes this reason-ing trace a surface for co-creation. Can users follow how the system arrived at its inferences and meaningfully reshape them? Contesta-bility research has identified several properties that systems need to support meaningful challenge of algorithmic inferences, including legibility of the reasoning process [ 30 ], access to underlying evi-dence [ 44 ], and mechanisms for users to shape the representations a system holds about them [ 56 , 76 ]. We evaluate whether Tempo ’s hierarchical trace supports these properties, and whether exposing it, as opposed to the raw observations it was built from, changes the editing experience.

5.4.3 Results. 

The hierarchical trace transforms the editing experience. The hierarchical view was rated substantially higher than the screen-shot view ( 𝛽 = 0.969 , 𝑝 < .001 ; bootstrapped 95% CI [0.613 , 1.321 ]). The strongest effects were on transparency (Δ = +2.14 ) and ev-idence utility (Δ = +1.71 ), the dimensions most directly tied to whether users could follow how the system arrived at its inferences. Participants rated the hierarchical view as providing sufficient trans-parency at 93% compared to 43% for screenshots, and 100% rated the hierarchical evidence as useful compared to 71% for screenshots. 

The reasoning trace changes how participants navigate evi-dence. Participants reported that the hierarchical structure let them follow how the system moved from observed behavior to higher-order goals. P6: “The hierarchy gives you more control– otherwise you Sapkota et al. 

need to synthesize at the action level yourself.” P10 found “text-based interpretations of the actions was a better medium for capturing what was actually happening on the screen.” 

By contrast, participants found raw screenshots difficult to in-terpret as evidence for higher-order strivings. In the screenshot view, screenshot browsing accounted for 36.6% of all clicks on av-erage, with 6 of 14 participants spending more than 30% of their interaction scrolling through evidence they could not connect to the strivings it supported. P10: “It was tough with the screenshots to understand the reasoning behind using certain screenshots as evidence for a particular goal.” Without the reasoning trace, participants could identify that a striving was wrong but not why : “I don’t know how to edit the goal based on what you’ve captured” (P1). 

Reshaping the reasoning trace. Rather than simply accepting or rejecting, participants used the hierarchy to locate where the system’s interpretation diverged from their own understanding. P6 observed: “Certain clusters can appear under different goals. But you can see here that there’s one throughline throughout your life. This helped me understand that even at low-level things, why am I looking into these things is the product of the same life philosophy.” P12 described the experience as “like a therapist looking at my behavior and analyzing what I’m doing and giving me a totally new insight.” 

Participants wanted the system to propose alternatives. De-spite high control and agency ratings, participants identified a gap when a striving was wrong. They often lacked the self-knowledge to specify a replacement. P2: “When I say there’s a problem with this or it’s wrong, I don’t really know what the goal is myself, so maybe it could regenerate a couple options and I could choose from them.” 

Even with the hierarchy showing the reasoning trace, participants wanted the system to go further, proposing candidate replacements rather than leaving them to author corrections from scratch. 

Errors and boundary conditions. Despite the transparency af-forded by the hierarchy, 3 of 14 participants preferred the screenshot view, citing its simplicity and coverage: P5 noted it “required fewer button clicks and was just simpler to understand,” and P8 found that showing more strivings “felt like it had higher coverage and therefore felt more accurate.” Even among those who preferred the hierarchi-cal view, participants noted it took longer (P5) and surfaced more structure than they wanted to engage with. P14 liked the collapsible cards but felt “the number of actions could be lower.” These prefer-ences suggest a design tension between reasoning transparency and cognitive cost. Exposing the full hierarchy aids diagnosis but risks overwhelming users who want to edit quickly. Beyond this transparency tradeoff, we noticed two additional limitations of the hierarchical editing interface. First, when a striv-ing was wrong, participants could identify the problem through the hierarchy but often lacked the self-knowledge to author a re-placement from scratch; the current interface supports deletion and inline editing but does not offer system-generated alternatives. P10 also articulated a boundary on what behavioral inference can reach: 

“my life goals are a lot broader. . . You have to know stuff about my history and my past before I started using this laptop.” Our evaluation also assessed a single editing session. Future work should evaluate whether edit constraints produce increasingly aligned strivings over multiple induction cycles. 

## 6 Discussion 

This paper charts a course from systems that model what a person is doing in the short-term to systems that model what they are working toward over the long-term. Here, we reflect on the broader implications of striving co-creation, the tensions it surfaces, and directions for future work. 

Co-creation as reflection. Reading an inferred striving, even be-fore editing, prompted participants to reflect on what they had been doing and why. When the system presented a higher-order goal, participants traced their own behavior back to it, drawing con-nections across their day they had not previously seen. Matching evidence surfaced behavior participants had forgotten or underval-ued, while mismatched evidence prompted them to reconsider why they had been doing something they could not explain. Similar to prior work [ 39 , 70 ], the tension between the system’s interpretation and the person’s own understanding was a rich source of reflection. The reflective dimension of co-creation may call for richer mech-anisms than direct editing. A conversational interface could let users interrogate why the system grouped certain behaviors together or articulate goals the system has no behavioral evidence for. Partici-pants who struggled to author replacement strivings from scratch might benefit from a system that proposes candidate alternatives rather than leaving the burden of articulation entirely to the person. Future work could explore designing for co-creation as reflection rather than correction alone. This reflective function may also offer a new lens on the intention-behavior gap [ 22 ]. Tempo surfaces this gap from the other direction: rather than asking people to articulate goals and then tracking whether behavior follows, the system in-fers goals from behavior and asks whether they match the person’s self-understanding. The mismatches participants identified during editing are, in this light, instances of the intention-behavior gap made visible through behavioral evidence rather than self-report. 

Strivings as interpretive artifacts, not ground truth. The same actions can support fundamentally different goals, and co-creation is essential for aligning the striving hierarchy with the person’s own understanding. Yet our results surfaced a tension: several par-ticipants accepted strivings readily because the system articulated something they found validating, rather than because they had ver-ified the behavioral evidence. When a system tells someone what they are working toward in confident, well-structured language, the person may accept the interpretation not because it is accurate but because it is articulate. This risk is consistent with sycophancy research, which has shown that models trained on human feedback tend to produce outputs that align with user expectations rather than ground truth [ 10 ], and the Barnum effect [ 73 ], in which people accept vague personality descriptions as uniquely self-relevant. Despite this risk, anchoring strivings in a visible behavioral trace, rather than presenting them as standalone assertions, appears crit-ical for maintaining scrutiny. Because participants could trace a striving through its constituent activities and actions to the screen-shots that generated them, they had the means to judge not only whether a striving resonated but whether the behavior it rested on warranted the interpretation. Future work should explore mecha-nisms for periodic re-evaluation of this hierarchy, prompting users to revisit stale strivings and flagging when new behavior diverges “What Are You Really Trying to Do?”: Co-Creating Life Goals from Everyday Computer Use 

from established constraints. Inferred strivings should be positioned as interpretive artifacts that support self-understanding [ 63 ], not as objective records of a person’s goals. 

Privacy and security risks. When a system for striving co-creation constrains downstream agents, manipulating the observation stream becomes a vector for shaping a person’s inferred goals. Prior work on user modeling demonstrated that spam email could maliciously rewrite user model propositions [ 70 ]. Tempo ’s observation modal-ity could also be made susceptible to injections, especially in shared devices, where unintended observations could distort the inferred hierarchy. The Audit module provides one safeguard, but adversar-ial robustness of the observation pipeline needs further validation. Beyond the raw observations, the striving hierarchy itself be-comes a high-value target. A single data structure encoding what a person is working toward across all domains of their life, grounded in weeks of behavioral evidence, is more sensitive than any in-dividual screenshot. Compromising the hierarchy would expose not just what a person did but the system’s interpretation of why. Thus, future deployments should treat the hierarchy with the same protections as a credential. 

The Observer Effect: Beyond Technical Privacy. We designed 

Tempo to observe behavior passively, but several participants re-ported that the observation itself changed the behavior it aimed to capture. P2 mentioned working harder than usual, P4 actively sup-pressed distractions, and P11 blocked WhatsApp entirely. In each case, participants performed a version of themselves they wanted the system to see. This reactivity is well-documented in clinical and personal informatics contexts [ 45 ], and when the system infers strivings from performed behavior, the resulting model may reflect an aspirational self rather than an actual one [29]. However, this effect may also be transient. A few participants reported that awareness of the observer faded over the course of the week, and one forgot the system was running entirely, inquiring about our privacy mechanisms only after the fact. But forgetting the system is running introduces its own problem. Tempo includes mechanisms to give users control over what is observed: paus-ing recording, an application exclusion list, local OCR-based PII filtering, and removing screenshots during editing. For a system designed to run continuously over weeks, privacy mechanisms that depend on active user engagement are brittle. One alternative is to shift from passive capture to explicit user contributions, but this places the full burden of evidence curation on the person and loses the insights from passive observation in the first place. The right balance remains an open design question, especially as systems move from week-long studies to sustained deployment. 

Limitations. Our participant pool reflects a self-selection effect: all participants were comfortable with AI tools, willing to have their screens recorded for a week, and spent a substantial portion of their day on their computer. People less familiar with AI, less trusting of monitoring, or in non-research settings may respond differently to both the observation and the inferred strivings. The inferred strivings also occasionally exhibited the verbose, confident style characteristic of LLMs. Mitigating this will require better prompting and grounding strategies alongside interface-level cues that communicate uncertainty [ 31 ], distinguishing strivings the system is confident about from those it is speculating on. The system is also limited by what it can observe. Our system captures only desktop screen activity, missing the non-digital dimensions of a person’s life that participants identified as central to their goals. This skews inference toward professionally structured computer use. Future work should explore how users could contribute be-havior from additional sources—wearable data, mobile activity, or self-reported journal entries—to broaden the observation window. Supporting users in selectively adding context would align with the co-creation stance: rather than the system silently absorbing more data, the person would actively shape what evidence the hierarchy draws on. On the architectural side, each induction cycle takes 5–10 min-utes depending on the volume of buffered observations. Because strivings require days of behavioral accumulation to stabilize, this latency may be acceptable, but faster inference would enable tighter feedback loops with downstream interactions. The pipeline also ex-poses parameters such as buffer size, graph-context retrieval limits, activity stabilization thresholds, and confidence cutoffs whose de-fault values have not been robustly tested across diverse computer use behaviors. Finally, our evaluation spans one week of observation and a single editing session. Longitudinal deployments are needed to evaluate temporal persistence and whether edit constraints produce increasingly aligned strivings across multiple induction cycles. 

## 7 Conclusion 

This paper introduces striving co-creation, a process where a per-son and a system jointly construct a representation of the person’s long-term life goals from unstructured computer use. Grounded in Activity Theory and Emmons’ personal strivings framework, our system progressively abstracts screen observations into a hi-erarchical representation and feeds the person’s corrections back into future induction as constraints. In a week-long deployment (𝑁 =14 ), we find that our co-creation process produces strivings that participants recognize as accurate and representative of their lives. Ablation results show that user-provided context improves individual striving quality, while hierarchical structure is especially important for representativeness. The hierarchy also reshapes the editing experience, giving participants the means to trace how the system arrived at its inferences and reshape them. We argue that modeling what people are working toward long-term requires not only hierarchical inference from behavior, but co-creative mecha-nisms that let people shape how they are represented.

## B Evaluation Questions B.1 Individual Striving Evaluation 

Each inferred striving was rated on a 7-point Likert scale ( −3 =Strongly Disagree, +3 = Strongly Agree): (1) Accuracy. “This statement accurately describes something I actually do or try to do.” (2) Alignment. “This statement captures something that cur-rently matters to me.” (3) Abstraction. “This statement captures what I’m trying to achieve, not a specific way to get there.” (4) Characteristicness. “This is an ongoing pattern in my life, not a one-time task.” 

## B.2 Set-Level Evaluation 

After rating individual strivings, participants evaluated each condi-tion’s full set on a 7-point Likert scale ( −3 = Strongly Disagree, +3

= Strongly Agree): (1) Coverage. “This set of statements captures what I am try-ing to achieve.” (2) Discovery. “This set surfaced goals or connections I hadn’t previously thought about.” (3) Motive. “This set reflects what matters to me, not just what I happen to do.” Participants were also asked to provide an open-ended explanation of their rating. 

## B.3 Post-Edit Survey 

After editing each condition’s strivings, participants rated the edit-ing experience on a 7-point Likert scale ( −3 = Strongly Disagree, 

+3 = Strongly Agree): (1) Control. “I felt in control of shaping this set of goals to match my actual life.” (2) Agency. “I could meaningfully change things that were wrong, not just accept or reject them.” (3) Transparency. “I could understand why the system gener-ated each goal.” (4) Evidence Utility. “The evidence the system showed me helped me decide whether to keep or change a goal.” (5) Reflection. “Looking at these goals made me think about my priorities in a new way.” (6) Confidence. “After editing, I am confident this set accu-rately represents my goals.” (7) Ease. “The editing process was straightforward.” 

## C Semi-Structured Interview Questions 

After completing the editing tasks (§5.4), we conducted a semi-structured interview using the following question set. We also asked follow-ups as needed to clarify or probe further. 

Inference quality 

(1) In either editing view, were there goals where you felt like the system really captured something about you? What about ones that felt off? (2) Did either version connect things from different parts of your life, like work and personal, or health and hobbies? How did that land for you? (3) Were there any goals that surprised you? Things you’re actually pursuing but hadn’t thought of as a goal? (4) Did the system surface anything you know is true about you but wouldn’t have thought to write down yourself? 

Editability 

(5) When you disagreed with a goal the system generated, could you figure out where it went wrong? Was that differ-ent between the two views? (6) Can you walk me through one goal you changed or re-jected? What was wrong and how did you fix it? (7) Did looking at the screenshots and activities underneath a goal ever change your mind about it. For example, either keeping something you would have rejected, or rejecting something that initially looked right? 

Comparative reflection and self-discovery 

(8) You saw your goals organized two different ways. Was one easier to work with? Why? (9) If you had sat down a week ago and written out your life goals from scratch, do you think you would have written the same things the system came up with? What would have been different?
