# Abstract 27 

28 

Spatial attention is critical for solving the cocktail party problem, a longstanding problem 29 in neuroscience and artificial speech recognition. The ability to decode where humans 30 are attending in a cocktail party like scene would empower applications in brain computer 31 interfaces and assistive devices such as hearing aids. Here we demonstrate that, in an 32 overt attention task, the attended spatial location can be decoded robustly from single 33 trial hemodynamic responses, using a wearable whole head high density fNIRS system. 34 We also identify critical brain regions that make the highest contribution to decoding 35 accuracy. Specifically, we find that decoding based on a small fraction of channels within 36 the left and right inferior parietal lobule (IPL), achieve maximal decoding accuracy 37

# Introduction 41 

42 

The cocktail party problem (CPP), the problem of following a target speaker in a cluttered 43 scene amongst other competing speakers (Cherry, 1953; McDermott, 2009), is a44 challenging problem for many humans, e.g., those with hearing loss (Arlinger, 2003; 45 Podury et al., 2023), ADHD (Fu et al., 2022; Mihali et al., 2018) and autism (Marco et al., 46 2011). Normal hearing listeners can solve this problem with relative ease, indicating the 47 existence of a solution in the brain. However, human brain regions that enable the solution 48 remain poorly understood. 49 

50 

Spatial attention is thought to play a critical role in processing and analyzing complex 51 cluttered scenes with competing audiovisual objects (Shinn-Cunningham, 2008), as in the 52 CPP. For example, at a crowded gathering, a listener can look at and focus their attention 53 on a target speaker at a spatial location to follow what they are saying. Relatively little 54 remains known about human brain networks underlying spatial attention in the CPP. 55 

56 

Many studies have employed purely visual or auditory paradigms to investigate spatial 57 attention. However, in complex everyday scenes, visual, auditory, and multimodal regions 58 work in concert to select and process audiovisual objects. Indeed, a previous study found 59 an effect of gaze direction on improving cocktail party listening (Best et al., 2023). In 60 everyday scenes, we naturally orient towards an object by moving our eyes and then 61 selectively process that target (overt attention). Here, we investigate the CPP using 62 audiovisual stimuli, i.e., movies of competing speakers from different spatial locations, in 63 an overt attention task. We also compare this with the processing of sounds of competing 64 speakers, when the subject attends to a spatial location without visually orienting and 65 looking at that location (covert attention). 66 

67 

Previous studies in humans have investigated the cocktail party problem using various 68 methods, e.g., ECoG, EEG and MEG. fNIRS offers a number of advantages relative to 69 other methods for investigating cortical networks in humans (Boas et al., 2014; Ehlis et 70 al., 2014; Yucel et al., 2017). First, fNIRS is non-invasive and allows for studies in broader 71 human populations compared to ECoG. Second, fNIRS is portable allowing for 72 experiments in more naturalistic, ecologically valid settings compared to fMRI and MEG 73 (Pinti et al., 2018; von Lühmann et al., 2021). Third, fNIRS has better spatial resolution 74 compared to EEG, providing the potential to reveal specific cortical regions. A previous 75 study provided a preliminary fNIRS dataset in a cocktail party-like setting (Ning et al., 76 2024). However, this study did not employ a high density system or monitor eye 77

We then decode the 80 locus of spatial attention from the measured cortical responses using classification 81 techniques. Such a system has potential for applications in brain computer interfaces for 82 “steering” assistive devices, e.g., hearing aids, to the attended location to assist users 83 with communication in noisy multi-speaker scenes. Additionally, such a system could 84 enable attention-guided resource allocation for audio-visual rendering in AR/VR 85 environments. Our results provide a glimpse into the human brain engaged in solving the 86 cocktail party problem in more naturalistic conditions and reveal specific cortical regions 87 that make an important contribution to decoding attended spatial location using fNIRS. 88

# Discussion 248 

249 

In this study, we attempted to decode the attended spatial location in a cocktail party like 250 scene using whole head high density fNIRS measurements and classification methods. 251 In the overt attention condition, we obtained robust classification performance based on 252 single trials in most subjects. These results demonstrate the viability of a fNIRS based 253 system for decoding attended location in a scene and opens the way to potential 254 applications in brain computer interfaces for steering assistive devices to the attended 255 location. We found that the average latency to peak classification accuracy in the overt 256 condition was ~2.5 seconds, which is within behaviorally relevant timescales in natural 257 conversations. 258 

259 

Our results also shed light on specific brain regions important for decoding attended 260 location, which is difficult to obtain using EEG which has poor spatial resolution compared 261 to fNIRS. Specifically, we found that the classification accuracy obtained using only the 262 channels from the inferior parietal lobule (IPL) region, was not significantly different from 263 all channel classification accuracy. This result is consistent with the attention literature 264 suggesting an important role of IPL as part of the fronto-parietal network in attention. Our 265 results demonstrate that this area is accessible to fNIRS and plays a critical role in 266 decoding attended location. It also opens the possibility of designing compact fNIRS 267 devices specifically targeting the IPL for decoding spatial attention. 268 

269 

We observed significant variation in classification accuracy across subjects. Classification 270 accuracy was significantly correlated with within-subject variability of the single-trial HbO 271 

response, quantified as the coefficient of variation (CV = σ/|μ|) computed across trials per 272 channel, both when averaged across all channels and when restricted to channels 273 overlying the Angular Gyrus (Supplementary Figure 6). Subjects whose single-trial 274 responses were more variable relative to their mean amplitude tended to achieve lower 275 classification accuracy. Thus, lower classification performance in some subjects may 276 have resulted from less consistent trial-to-trial activation in key regions for decoding such 277 as the IPL. Optimizing the collection of signals in key regions, in combination with 278 individual subject scans, may further improve decoding performance in future studies. 279 

280 

Key role of IPL in decoding performance 281 

282 

This study revealed key contributing brain regions to decoding performance, which would 283 be challenging in other modalities such as EEG. The top ROIs included Brodmann 284 regions of left and right angular gyrus (AG), visuomotor cortex / Superior Parietal Lobule 285 (SPL), premotor and supplementary motor cortex, superior temporal gyrus (STG), and 286 supramarginal gyrus (SMG). However, given that angular gyrus is a relatively small 287

The IPL is a key node in both the orientation network and the executive network 290 underlying attention (Petersen & Posner, 2012), has been implicated in both sensory-291 motor integration and sound location monitoring during auditory spatial tasks (Alain et al., 292 2008), and carries direction-specific information during auditory spatial attention (Kong 293 et al., 2014). Thus, the top ROIs contributing to overt decoding 300 performance identified in this study are consistent with their roles established in the 301 attention literature. Oculomotor component in IPL responses 307 

308 

In natural scenes, we visually orient by moving our eyes to attend to an object of interest. 309 In such scenarios, both the oculomotor and attention systems are active and intimately 310 linked. An interesting and challenging question relates to the relative contributions of 311 these two systems under such conditions. Studies on attention have carefully considered 312 whether these systems are independent, identical or overlapping (Corbetta, 1998; 313 Corbetta & Shulman, 1998). Evidence suggests that attentional and oculomotor systems 314 are tightly related and may be identical or overlapping, rather than fully independent 315 (Corbetta, 1998). Our analysis comparing overt vs. covert classification revealed that 316 visual stimuli and/or visual orientation makes a strong contribution to decoding 317 performance. Given the leading contribution of IPL to decoding performance, we were 318 specifically interested in whether eye-movement alone could account for decoding 319 performance in IPL. To further decouple eye-movement signals from evoked responses, 320 we designed an additional set of controls, where we introduced a longer delay between 321 the visual orientation and the movie onset. This design allowed us to examine evoked 322 responses long after eye movements were completed. Data from these additional control 323 experiments revealed that responses in IPL are difficult to account for based on eye 324 movement only (see supplementary methods, results, and supplementary figure 1). 325 Rather such responses may represent attended location and/or eye position (which are 326

Limitations and Future Directions 330 

331 

While we took steps towards a more naturalistic design for investigating the cocktail party 332 problem by incorporating audiovisual stimuli and allowing for visual orientation using eye 333 movements, additional factors arising in natural conditions remain to be explored. For 334 example, future studies should investigate designs that allow for head movements which 335 could occur under natural conditions. 336 

337 

Second, although the decoding of overt attention was robust, we did not obtain robust 338 decoding for the covert attention condition. There are several possible explanations for 339 this. In everyday natural scenes, we visually orient and attend to particular objects in the 340 scene to analyze them in detail. In the overt condition, the visual orientation component 341 and the visual component of stimuli may have generated a stronger signal, which in turn 342 improves decoding. These components were absent in the covert condition. It is also 343 possible that our fNIRS cap did not optimally target auditory cortical regions near the ear 344 which could be sensitive to auditory only stimuli from different spatial locations. Future 345 studies should investigate whether improved coverage of auditory cortical regions 346 improves decoding performance, especially in the covert condition. 347 

348 

Third, this study used decoding based solely on fNIRS. Existing literature suggests that 349 EEG-fNIRS sensor fusion techniques could potentially improve single trial decoding 350 accuracy than fNIRS alone (Codina et al., 2025) . We are currently exploring whether 351 simultaneous measurement of fNIRS and EEG can further improve decoding 352 performance in both overt and covert conditions. 353 

354 

Finally, this study was limited to binary classification between left vs. right. It will be 355 interesting to expand this paradigm to a larger number of spatial locations and investigate 356 the spatial resolution of decoding. 357 

358 

Potential applications to BCI systems and assistive devices 359 

360

BOSSA significantly improves speech recognition in cocktail party-362 like settings for both listeners with normal hearing (Chou et al., 2022) and hearing loss 363 (Boyd et al., 2025). BOSSA requires as input the spatial location a user wants to attend 364 to. The decoded attended spatial location from fNIRS could be used to steer BOSSA to 365 the attended location to better isolate the target sound from competing sounds. Similar 366

Stimuli 404 

405 

The stimuli used in this study were extracted from AVSpeech, a publicly available 406 audiovisual speech dataset (Ephrat et al., 2018). The videos were mined using a custom 407 automated downloading script. Downloaded videos were automatically screened for 408 presence of a single talker in the video, English as a spoken language, context of the 409 language being not offensive, and sentences with at least 4 words that make semantic 410 sense. Videos were further required to show the talker positioned approximately at the 411 center of the frame, defined as cases in which the automatically detected face never 412 entered the leftmost or rightmost third of the screen. All the custom scripts were written 413 in Python with semantic and sentiment analysis done using OpenAI’s GPT-4 API and 414 language detection (speech detection) using OpenAI’s whisper model. A total of 360 415 unique videos were shortlisted after manual inspection and verification of all the above-416 mentioned automated criteria. The audios from the shortlisted videos were spatialized -417 30 to the left and +30 degrees to the right using HRTF filters based on KEMAR head 418 model using MATLAB. 419

A total of 60 trials (30 trials for attend-left and 431 30 trials for attend-right conditions) for both overt and covert conditions were collected. 432 Additionally, 20 trials (10 trials for look-left and 10 trials for look-right) were collected as 433 eye movement control trials where the subjects move their eyes to fixate at the center of 434 left or right screen in absence of any audio or video stimuli. At the end of each trial, the 435 subject answered multiple choice questions by pressing 1, 2, or 3 on the numeric pad of 436 a keyboard to answer audio related questions (What did the target speaker say?) and 437 video related questions (Who was the target speaker?). Audio questions contained 3 438 options with a transcript of the audio played during the trial where one transcript is from 439 the target speaker (one to be attended), another from the masker speaker (one to be 440 ignored), and the last one from the pool of 360 short listed videos. The video question 441 contained corresponding speaker’s face screenshots from the video that was played. In 442 overt condition, subjects answered both audio and visual questions, but in covert 443 condition, they only answered audio questions. For eye control tasks, the subjects move 444 their eyes 30 degrees to the left and right with the same trial structure, totalling 5 seconds 445 of fixation on the directed screen. 446

fNIRS data preprocessing 499 

500 

fNIRS preprocessing was carried out in MATLAB programming language using Homer3 501 software package (Huppert et al., 2009) and in Python programming language using 502 Cedalion toolbox (Middell et al., 2026). Raw intensity at both wavelengths was quality-503 screened for low signal and saturation; channels with amplitudes below 0.001 a.u. or 504 above 0.84 a.u. were pruned. The remaining intensity data were converted to optical 505 density (OD) and motion-corrected using Temporal Derivative Distribution Repair (TDDR) 506 (Fishburn et al., 2019). The corrected OD was band-pass filtered between 0.01 and 0.5 507 Hz using a zero-phase Butterworth filter (order 4). Filtered OD was then converted to 508 hemoglobin concentration changes using the modified Beer-Lambert law, without 509 differential pathlength correction. 510 

511 

For group level average, the General Linear model (GLM) was used to estimate single 512 trials by regressing out the systemic physiological noise using mean of all channels less 513 than 20 mm source-detector separation as global signal regressor. The hemodynamic 514 response was modeled using a sequence of Gaussian basis functions spaced 1 s apart 515 

with a standard deviation of 1 s, spanning −2 to +15 s relative to stimulus onset (Gagnon 516 et al., 2011). The mean pre-stimulus baseline (−2 to 0 s) was subtracted from each trial. 517 For each channel, all of the trials were averaged for each condition (left and right). For 518 each subject, all of the channels were averaged within a defined region of interest (ROI). 519 All of the ROIs were averaged across the subjects to get group average results. 520

Group level Average 604 

605 

Group level average was calculated by averaging all single trials across the channel 606 space and across the subjects in each defined region of interest (ROI). Standard error of 607 the mean was calculated across the subjects to report on hemodynamic response 608 functions (HRF). 609

Statistical analysis for evoked responses 611 

612 

Statistical significance was determined using a t-test on the 2 seconds baseline (t=-2 to 613 t=0) segment compared to the 2 seconds window starting cue (t=n to t=n+2, starting t=0) 614 for both oxy and deoxy hemoglobin concentration within each condition (e.g., left HbO). 615 If any of the segments were statistically significant (p<0.05 after Bonferroni correction), 616 the corresponding evoked response was marked as a statistically significant evoked 617 response. 618

Classification scheme 620 

621 

To avoid information leakage, a cross-validation scheme was employed where OD data 622 was partitioned into training and testing datasets. The training dataset was filtered 623 between 0.01 Hz and 0.5 Hz and converted into concentration. GLM was performed with 624 global signal regression on only the training set. Estimated global signal regressor as 625 systemic physiological contributor was used to regress out physiological noise from the 626 training set on a single trial level. Identical to the group-level analysis, the HRF was 627 modeled using a sequence of Gaussian basis functions spaced 1 s apart with a standard 628 

deviation of 1 s, spanning −2 to +15 s relative to stimulus onset (Gagnon et al., 2011). 629 Single trials on the test dataset were estimated by subtracting global signal regressor 630 weighted trials from the original single test trials (Ning et al., 2024). Baseline (-2 to 0 s) 631 was subtracted from each individual trial. 632

Permutation test 658 

659 

Due to the small number of data points in the dataset (n = 60 trials total), the true chance 660 level is not at 50% (Combrisson & Jerbi, 2015). Permutation test was carried out by 661 shuffling the labels 1000 times (with 5-fold cross validation) to estimate the true chance 662 level. Lower bound was estimated to be 40.7% and upper bound was estimated to be at 663 62.3% in one tailed test. Accuracy above 62.3% was marked as statistically above chance 664 level accuracy. 665
