Abstract 1

The ability to orient or attend to sensory events is generally greater in response to visual and au-2

ditory cues occurring together than in response to single-modality cues occurring alone. In such 3

cases the perceptual fusion of cross-modal stimuli (multisensory integration) depends on low-4

level features (e.g., location, intensity) and follows well established principles. However, less is 5

known about multisensory integration mechanisms when behavioral responses are less direct and 6

require top-down control. Here we investigate this in human participants using an urgent mul-7

tisensory choice task that effectively dissociates stimulus-driven and goal-driven contributions to 8

performance based on their distinct temporal signatures. Task conditions varied the modality of 9

the cues (auditory, visual, or both), their location (left or right), and the rule defining the correct 10 

choice (look toward or away from a given cue). When spatially coincident cues were associated 11 

with the same response rule (“look away”), we observed multisensory enhancement and perfor-12 

mance remained close to a statistical expectation as the choice process unfolded. However, when 13 

spatially disparate cues were associated with different rules but the same target, one cue domi-14 

nated performance and the other produced crossmodal capture, i.e., low-level competition. The 15 

results indicate that the efficacy of multisensory integration is dictated by the stimulus- and goal-16 

driven signals produced by each cue, with all four factors rapidly interacting in accordance to the 17 

dynamics of spatial attention. 18 

Significance Statement 19 

Auditory and visual stimuli located near each other in space and time are typically bound into a 20 

single sensory percept that draws attention most effectively. However, it is unclear whether such 21 

“multisensory integration” occurs during behaviors that go beyond directly attending or orient-22 

ing to cue stimuli and require top-down control. We investigated this using a novel task design 23 

with which stimulus-driven and goal-driven contributions to performance can be accurately iden-24 

tified. We found that multisensory enhancement depends not so much on the complexity of the 25 

requested cue-response associations, but rather on the timing and alignment of the stimulus- and 26 

goal-driven signals derived from each cue (auditory and visual) — similar to the way that such 27 

signals dictate the allocation of spatial attention. 28 

Introduction 29 

Natural events in the environment often yield sensory signals from multiple modalities. As we 30 

see a door moving ever so slightly and hear a faint metal creak, we may suspect that the cat has 31 

sneaked into the room. Together, the movement and the sound generate a stronger, more reli-32 

able signal than either stimulus alone. Many studies in so-called “multisensory integration” have 33 

aimed to characterize and quantify the resulting behavioral benefit (Miller, 1986; Corneil et al., 34 

2002; Burnett et al., 2004; Diederich and Colonius, 2004; Drugowitsch et al., 2014) or the underly-35 

ing neurophysiological substrates (Stanford et al., 2005; Stein and Stanford, 2008; Angelaki et al., 36 

2009) whereby inputs from different modalities are merged into a more effective multisensory out-37 

put. This has resulted in a conceptual framework that accounts for performance based on specific 38 

neural operations (Ma and Pouget, 2008). This framework includes a number of heuristic princi-39 

ples that encapsulate the conditions under which multisensory stimuli may yield a performance 40 

benefit (Meredith et al., 1987; Meredith and Stein, 1996; Stein et al., 1989, 2009), such as temporal 41 

proximity, spatial congruence, and inverse effectiveness (the benefit is larger when the unisensory 42 

signals are similarly weak). 43 

However, all this is generally applicable to situations involving relatively simple behaviors, 44 

such as overtly orienting toward a salient stimulus or covertly attending to it (without shifting 45

type of endogenous control. This case is more complex not only because endogenous control can 49 

take many forms, but also because stimulus-driven contributions are never absent, and their in-50

that the conditions for producing multisensory enhancement may not be the same for exogenous 52 

and endogenous signals, and the degree to which such signals are sympathetic or antagonistic 53 

could have a profound influence on the resulting behavior. With this fact in mind, here we use 54 

a new set of urgent-choice tasks to determine how the interaction between stimulus-driven and 55 

goal-driven factors dictates behavioral outcomes when goal-driven choices are based on multisen-56

Imposing urgency turns out to be extremely useful for dissociating exogenous and endogenous 58 

contributions to visuomotor behavior (Salinas et al., 2019; Poth, 2021; Goldstein et al., 2022, 2024; 59 

Oor et al., 2023, 2025; Zhu et al., 2024; Kattner et al., 2026; Krause and Poth, 2025). Choices made 60 

under time pressure may evolve to various endpoints, ranging from entirely uninformed guesses 61 

(near chance) to fully informed decisions (approaching 100% correct), and the transition between 62 

them provides a direct readout of the timecourse of the underlying perceptual or cognitive evalu-63 

ation (Stanford et al., 2010; Stanford and Salinas, 2021). In general, this approach clearly identifies 64 

choices that are exogenously guided (early, involuntary, stimulus-dependent) from those that are 65 

endogenously guided (late, voluntary, goal-driven), which is a fundamental functional distinction 66 

(Corbetta and Shulman, 2002; Theeuwes, 2010; Carrasco, 2011; Noyce et al., 2023). 67 

Here we investigate saccadic choices that are (1) urgent and (2) based on multisensory (vi-68 

sual and auditory) stimuli. The confluence of these two properties is illuminating both from the 69 

perspective of the mechanisms whereby stimulus information from different modalities can be 70 

combined to yield a stronger, more reliable target representation, and from the perspective of the 71 

attention mechanisms whereby different potential targets are prioritized. Two sides of the same 72 

behavioral coin (Driver and Spence, 1998; Koelewijn et al., 2010; Santangelo and Macaluso, 2012). 73 

We find the effect of combined multisensory cues on a rule-driven choice to be highly context-74 

dependent, yet readily understood as the temporally weighted superposition of two processes: 75 

an early, stimulus-driven one reflecting the spatial congruence of the unisensory cues, and a later, 76 

goal-directed one reflecting the dynamics of endogenous attention. 77

Basic task design 101 

We ran three variants of the compelled multisensory choice ( CMC ) task. These differed depending 102 

on the task goal as well as on the modality and spatial congruence of the cue stimuli presented. 103 

For all task variants and trial types the sequence of events was as follows. 104 

First, a stimulus appeared in the middle of the display and the participant fixated on it. The 105 

color and shape of the fixation stimulus served as a reminder of the task variant and associated 106 

instructions (see below), which remained fixed for each block of (100) trials. After 300 ms of 107 

fixation two open targets appeared, one to the left and one to the right of the central fixation 108 

stimulus (Targets on). These targets indicated two things: the locations where a visual cue could 109 

appear (if such a cue was to be presented in the trial) and the desired endpoints of the choice 110 

saccades. After a short delay (300, 400, or 500 ms) the central fixation stimulus disappeared (Go), 111 

instructing the participant to make an eye movement. Although at this point the correct choice was 112 

still unknown, the participant had to attempt to respond right away in order to meet a reaction 113 

time ( RT ) deadline (350–450 ms adjusted for each participant). The sensory cue indicating the 114 

correct choice was revealed after the go signal (Cue), once a period of time called the gap had 115 

elapsed. The gap varied randomly between trials, typically ranging from 0 ms (cue simultaneous 116 

with go signal) to 250 ms (typical saccadic RT). Negative or “easy” gaps ( −300 or −200 ms) were 117 

also used with some participants. Negative gaps correspond to trials in which the cue stimulus 118 

is presented before the go signal, so more time is available for processing the cue. Visual and or 119 

auditory cues remained until the participant responded by making an eye movement to one of the 120 

open targets (Saccade). 121 

To make sure that the participants responded on time, feedback was provided after each trial; 122 

such feedback was never indicative of the correct saccade direction. Some participants (partic-123 

ipants 1–11) were given negative feedback; for them the words “Go faster!” appeared on the 124 

center of the screen whenever the RT limit was exceeded. The rest of the participants (participants 125 

12–15) were given positive feedback (the open targets turned from gray to green whenever the 126 

RT limit was met). Both approaches helped the participants adjust their response urgency. The 127 

task was run in blocks of 100 valid trials, i.e., trials that were completed within the required RT 128 

window. 129 

The fixation stimuli were small (0.5 ◦ diameter), low-luminance, centrally located shapes that 130 

differed depending on the task variant (see below). Saccade choice targets were large (2.5 ◦ diam-131 

eter), gray, outlined circles placed along the horizontal line 8 ◦ to the left and right of the central 132 

fixation point. Visual cue stimuli were small (0.5 ◦ diameter) gray spots with blurred edges. Each 133 

presented visual cue was displayed inside one of the peripheral choice targets and remained there 134 

until a saccade was made. Auditory cue stimuli were white noise bursts that lasted until a saccade 135 

was made. 136

Task variants 145 

Trials could be either unisensory (only one cue stimulus was presented, visual or auditory) or 146 

multisensory (both visual and auditory cues were presented). For all task variants unisensory 147 

and multisensory trials were interleaved within a block. The trial types and cue locations were 148 

random, so participants could not predict the correct choice or whether one or two stimuli would 149 

be presented. Multisensory trials could be either spatially congruent (cue stimuli presented on 150 

the same side) or spatially incongruent (cue stimuli presented on opposite sides); however, con-151 

gruent and incongruent trials were never interleaved within a block. The three task variants or 152 

experimental blocks were as follows. 153 

During blocks of congruent antisaccades ( CA ) the fixation stimulus was a pink triangle and 154 

the rule was “look away from any visual or auditory cue.” Three trial types were interleaved: 155 

unisensory visual (V), unisensory auditory (A), and congruent multisensory (V +A), in which a 156 

visual and an auditory cue originated from the same side. The correct response was a saccade to 157 

the open target diametrically opposite to whichever cue stimulus was presented. 158 

During blocks of incongruent visual antisaccades ( IVA ) the fixation stimulus was a pink circle 159 

and the rule was “look away from the visual cue.” Two trial types were interleaved: unisensory 160 

visual (V) and incongruent multisensory (V(A)), in which a visual and an auditory cue originated 161 

from opposite sides. The correct response was a saccade to the open target diametrically opposite 162 

to the visual cue, which was always presented. 163 

During blocks of incongruent auditory prosaccades ( IAP ) the fixation stimulus was a green 164 

square and the rule was “look toward the auditory cue.” Two trial types were interleaved: unisen-165 

sory auditory (A) and incongruent multisensory (A(V)), in which an auditory and a visual cue 166 

originated from opposite sides. The correct response was a saccade to the open target on the same 167 

side as the auditory cue, which was always presented. 168 

During their first session, participants completed several practice blocks (of 20 trials) to get ac-169 

quainted with the stimuli and timing requirements of the task. Thereafter such practice runs only 170 

occurred whenever a new variant was introduced. All participants performed the three variants 171 

in the same sequence: IVA first, then IAP, and CA last. 172 

Data processing 173 

A participant made a saccadic choice when their gaze left a central window (2 ◦ radius) around 174 

the fixation stimulus. The exact direction of the choice saccade was calculated offline together 175 

with its onset time (using a velocity criterion of 40 ◦/s). For each choice the RT was computed as 176 

the interval between the go signal and the onset of the corresponding response saccade. Saccades 177 

were excluded from analysis if they occurred before the go signal (fixation breaks), if they were 178 

predominantly vertical, if the RT was too long ( > 800 ms), if a blink occurred within 100 ms of 179 

saccade onset, or if the eyes drifted outside of the fixation window without exceeding the velocity 180 

threshold. The vast majority of excluded trials ( ∼85%) were fixation breaks; others accounted for 181 

approximately 3% of the collected data. 182

Data analysis 199 

Data analyses were performed using Matlab (The MathWorks, Natick, MA) and Python. As in 200 

other urgent tasks (Poth 2021; Stanford and Salinas, 2021; Kattner et al., 2026; Krause and Poth, 201 

2025), performance in the CMC task is fundamentally dictated by the processing time ( PT ), which 202 

is the maximum amount of time during which the participant can see or hear the cue in each trial 203 

before initiating a response. Accordingly, the critical performance metric in the CMC task is the 204 

tachometric curve, which describes the probability of making a correct choice as a function of PT. 205 

Tachometric curves were produced by determining the PT for each trial, sorting trials into PT bins 206 

(which shifted every 1 ms), and calculating the fraction of correct responses for all the trials inside 207 

each bin. Tachometric curves and related analyses were carried out both for individual partici-208 

pants and for their pooled data. Pooling means that all the choices from a group of participants 209 

were combined into one large dataset, as if produced by a single, aggregate participant. Tachome-210 

tric curves for individual participants had a bin width of 80 ms; those for pooled data had a bin 211 

width of 40 ms. 212 

EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN 213 

The basic idea for interpreting the data is to create separate tachometric curves for each of the 7 214 

distinct trial types (found across all task variants) and then perform three types of comparison. 215 

Within each task variant, comparisons between unisensory and multisensory tachometric curves 216 

should indicate whether performance is facilitated or hindered by the presence of a second cue. 217 

Second, the effects may be different for short-PT trials, which are predominantly stimulus-driven, 218 

compared to long-PT trials, which are predominantly goal-driven. Finally, for any such compar-219 

isons, the results may also depend on congruence; in the short-PT regime, stimulus congruence 220 

(i.e., spatial alignment between cues) is likely to be most relevant, whereas in the long-PT regime, 221 

what matters the most may be either target congruence (i.e., spatial alignment between the saccade 222 

endpoints associated with each cue) or rule congruence (i.e., whether the two cues follow the same 223 

or different stimulus-response rules, which are “saccade to the stimulus” or “saccade away from 224 

the stimulus”). In combination, the 7 experimental conditions sampled should reveal key con-225 

straints or principles for how signals from different modalities are combined during goal-driven 226 

multisensory choices. 227

PROBABILITY OF CAPTURE 228 

The probability of capture (Kattner et al., 2026), or PCAP , was defined as 229 

PCAP = XX + Y (1) where X represents the area between the tachometric curve and the chance line when the curve is 230 

below said line, and Y is the area between the curve and the chance line when the curve is above. 231 

Defined this way, PCAP represents the proportion of erroneous choices that cannot be accounted 232 

for by chance; it measures the fraction of saccades that were directed toward the worse alternative 233 

above and beyond the fraction expected by chance. Thus, PCAP values of 0 and 1 correspond, 234 

respectively, to performance curves that either stay at or above chance, or stay at or below chance 235 

for the full PT range considered. Intermediate values result when the tachometric curve swings 236 

below and above chance. For each tachometric curve, PCAP was computed for PT values in the 237 

range 50–300 ms. 238 

As done previously (Kattner et al., 2026), a confidence interval ( CI ) was calculated for each 239 

PCAP value by bootstrapping (Davison and Hinkley, 2006; Hesterberg, 2014). That is, for a given 240 

tachometric curve the underlying trial-wise data were resampled with replacement, PCAP was re-241 

computed from the the corresponding (resampled) tachometric curve and saved, and the process 242 

was repeated many times (10000 iterations) to generate a distribution of PCAP values. Then, a 243 

95% CI was obtained by calculating the 2.5 and 97.5 percentiles derived from the bootstrapped 244 

distribution. Note that PCAP is always positive or zero, by definition. Thus, from the same boot-245 

strapped distribution, we also obtained the fraction of iterations for which PCAP was equal to 246 

zero. We report the corresponding probability, P (0) , as an additional indicator of the significance 247 

of the measurement. It has a precision of 0.0001 given the number of iterations used. 248 

CURVE RISE POINT 249 

For any tachometric curve the rise point was defined as the PT at which performance first exceeded 250 

a threshold value of 70% correct. Defined this way, the rise point indicates how quickly the cue 251 

stimulus is perceptually assessed. If one tachometric curve is simply shifted along the x-axis 252 

relative to another, the difference in rise points is equal to the relative shift between curves (in 253 

milliseconds of PT). 254 

The method for calculating the rise point consisted of two steps. First find all the PTs for 255 

which the tachometric curve is near 70% correct (say, between 65% and 75%). Then, from those 256 

PTs, select the largest, continuous PT interval for which the curve values bordering the left edge 257 

of the interval are below the 65% limit and the values bordering the right edge are above the 75% 258 

limit. The rise point is then set equal to the midpoint of the selected PT interval. For curves that 259 

are relatively smooth, this procedure produces results that are nearly identical to those obtained 260 

by curve fitting (Shankar et al., 2011; Kattner et al., 2026), but it also works reasonably well when 261 

the data are noisy. For each rise point, a CI was calculated by bootstrapping in the same way as 262 

described above for PCAP .263

PREDICTED MULTISENSORY PERFORMANCE 274 

To quantify the effectiveness of cue integration, a novel statistical criterion was used to predict the 275 

performance on multisensory trials based on that observed during unisensory trials. The method 276 

is described in detail elsewhere (Salinas and Stanford, 2024; see also Coen et al., 2023; Oor et al., 277 

2025), but the following is a brief overview. 278 

The approach is based on the concept of conditional independence (Dawid, 1979). Two events 279 

A and V are conditionally independent relative to a third event C if their joint probability given 280 

C is such that 281 

P (A, V |C) = P (A|C) P (V |C) (2) where P (A, V |C) is the probability that events A and V occur given that event C has occurred. In 282 

other words, if C is known, A and V occur independently of each other. An equivalent formulation 283 

of this concept is to say that, once condition C is known, the probability that V occurs is fixed 284 

regardless of whether A occurs or not. That is, 285 

P (V |A, C ) = P (V |C) (3) is an alternate, equivalent definition. Note that these expressions are distinct from the standard 286 

notion of independence, which for events A and V would correspond to 287 

P (A, V ) = P (A) P (V ) . (4) This condition is different and irrelevant to the problem at hand. 288 

In the present study the event C corresponds to the outcome of a choice, which may be correct 289 

(C = 1 ) or incorrect ( C = 0 ); event A corresponds to the presence ( A = 1 ) or absence ( A = 0 ) of 290 

an auditory cue; and V is the same but for a visual cue. Conditional independence is essentially 291 

a constraint on the three-way relationships between the three variables of interest as captured by 292 

their joint probability, P (A, V, C ). Here, such constraint is useful because it means that the effect 293 

that A and V may have on C jointly (together) is fixed once their separate (individual) effects on 294 

C are known. 295 

Specifically, the quantity of interest is the probability P (C|A, V ), which describes how the 296 

likelihood of a correct outcome varies depending on which cues are shown. When A and V are 297 

conditionally independent with respect to C, Equation 2 can be used in combination with Bayes 298 

theorem to derive the following result 299 

P (C = 1 |A = 1 , V = 1) = FF + G H (5) where we have defined the abbreviations 300

For any performance metric (rise point, PCAP , fraction correct, etc.), differences between experi-332 

mental conditions were assessed based on the 95% CIs obtained for each condition or on the un-333

p-value for the difference between m1 and m2. That is, for adjacent 95% CIs with zero overlap, 339 

p-values are between 0.01 and 0.001 under a variety of sample sizes and distribution shapes, and 340 

p-values decrease further as the gap between CIs increases. 341

Basic features of the urgent multisensory task 363 

We developed the compelled multisensory choice ( CMC ) task to investigate how visual and au-364 

ditory stimuli are combined during choices that require top-down control (Fig. 1). In each trial, 365 

the participant makes a saccadic choice by looking at one of two peripheral targets (gray rings) 366 

according to the location of one or two sensory cues. Each trial may be unisensory visual (a spot 367 

of light is presented), unisensory auditory (a noise burst is presented), or multisensory (a spot 368 

of light and a noise burst are simultaneously presented). The participant is instructed to look ei-369 

ther toward or away from a particular stimulus. This setting permits a wide variety of spatial 370 

configurations and task rules for exploring how endogenous goals and exogenous factors dictate 371 

multisensory performance. Here we consider three task variants. In the first one (CA, Fig. 1a), 372 

multisensory trials consist of visual and auditory cues that are presented on the same side and are 373 

associated with the same response rule, which is to look away from any stimulus. In the second 374 

and third variants, multisensory trials consist of visual and auditory stimuli that are presented 375 

on opposite sides and are associated with different sensory-motor rules (saccade away from the 376 

visual cue; saccade toward the auditory cue). In the second variant (IVA, Fig. 1b) the visuo-motor 377 

rule is applicable in all trials, whereas in the third variant (IAP, Fig. 1c) it is the audio-motor rule 378 

which is always applicable. 379 

As in other urgent-task designs, in the CMC task time pressure is imposed by first instructing 380 

the participant to make a choice (Fig. 1, Go) and then, after a delay (Gap), presenting the relevant 381 

stimulus that determines the correct response (Cue). Because the delay is unpredictable (0–250 382 

ms) and the time allowed for responding is limited (RT . 400 ms), the participant must hurry or 383 

else risks aborting the trial. Over many trials, a wide range of outcomes is produced, going from 384 

fast guesses yielding chance performance to fully informed choices approaching 100% correct. 385

Before discussing any multisensory interactions, we consider a condition that serves as a key 393 

reference for interpreting all the data — the unisensory visual (V) condition, in which the partici-394 

pant must make an eye movement away from a lone visual stimulus (Fig. 1a, top row). We discuss 395 

the V trials from the CA variant, but those from the IVA variant were qualitatively the same (see 396 

further below). This trial type corresponds to an urgent, visual antisaccade task. 397 

Antisaccades are characterized by a conflict between the involuntary urge to look at a novel, 398 

salient stimulus and the voluntary intent to follow the task instructions and look away (Munoz 399 

and Everling, 2004; Coe and Munoz, 2017). Nowhere does this conflict manifest more clearly 400 

than in the tachometric curve obtained under urgent conditions (Salinas et al., 2019; Goldstein 401 

et al., 2022). As in past antisaccade experiments, the V tachometric curve in the current dataset 402 

displays a distinctive shape marked by three phases (Fig. 2). At short PTs ( . 100 ms) performance 403 

hovers around chance (50% correct), indicating that participants simply guess if the cue is not 404 

seen for a sufficiently long period of time. At long PTs ( & 200 ms) performance rises steadily 405 

toward an asymptotic performance level close to 100% correct, indicating that participants apply 406 

the task rule almost flawlessly when given enough time. And at intermediate PTs ( ∼100–200 407 

ms) performance dips substantially below chance, indicating that in this range participants are 408 

particularly prone to looking at the cue and making errors. Such oculomotor capture results from 409 

fast, bottom-up signals that promote reflexive saccades to salient objects (Theeuwes, 2010, 2025). 410 

In contrast, the later goal-driven recovery toward asymptotic performance is the consequence of 411 

slower, voluntary guidance to the appropriate target (Salinas et al., 2019; Goldstein et al., 2022, 412 

2024; Oor et al., 2023; Kattner et al., 2026). 413 

The preeminence of the PT is worth stressing. In the current dataset, a lawful relationship 414 

exists between RT and accuracy, as would be expected (Wickelgren, 1977; Chittka et al., 2009), 415 

but it is rather loose and gives no indication of the conditions that facilitate or prevent cue-driven 416 

capture. In contrast, the tachometric curve depicts a relationship between perceptual performance 417 

and the time specifically dedicated to cue processing that is largely invariant to motor urgency 418 

(Salinas et al., 2014). This can be demonstrated by splitting the data by the median RT and noting 419 

that the resulting curves are largely the same (Fig. 2, cyan versus dark gray traces) — even though 420

A statistical benchmark for assessing the multisensory benefit 491 

Clearly, the multisensory product evolves as a function of PT, and the evolution is somewhat com-492 

plicated (Fig. 3b, red trace). For instance, compare the multisensory choices that were predomi-493 

nantly stimulus driven (i.e., with PT . 172 ms) with those that were predominantly goal-driven 494 

(i.e., with PT & 172 ms). For the former, performance in the V +A multisensory trials was generally 495 

intermediate between that in the A and V conditions; for the latter, the multisensory performance 496 

was generally better than for either of the unisensory conditions. 497 

To attain a more intuitive understanding of these temporal changes, we developed a statistical 498 

benchmark for the integration process that fits the conditions of the CMC task (Methods). During 499 

sensory-driven choices, performance given two informative signals may be more or less accurate 500 

than with just one, depending on how such signals interact as they drive the intended behavior. 501 

The concept of conditional independence is useful for characterizing such interaction probabilis-502 

tically (Coen et al., 2023; Salinas and Stanford, 2024; Oor et al., 2025). When the A and V signals 503 

are conditionally independent of each other relative to choice outcome, it is possible to make a 504 

parameter-free, quantitative prediction about their joint effect on performance (i.e., on the proba-505 

bility of making a correct choice) based on how each signal relates to behavior individually (see 506 

Methods for details). In practice, this means that a prediction for the multisensory tachometric 507 

curve can be generated using the two unisensory curves. 508 

In general, the data from the CA experiment were qualitatively close to the statistical pre-509 

dictions derived by assuming conditional independence (Fig. 4a, b). Relative to the V case, the 510 

expected multisensory curves showed an attenuated dip below chance and an earler rise toward 511

to keep in mind that the conditional independence criterion considers the accuracy of the unisen-513 

sory choices with respect to chance (Equation 5). If the A and V cues both yield above-chance per-514

mediate probability (a compromise is struck between conflicting effects). The experimental data 519 

are clearly consistent with these distinctions drawn in relation to chance performance (Fig. 3b). 520

Urgent multisensory integration with incongruent cues 552 

The IVA and IAP variants of the task (Fig. 1b, c) were designed as complementary parts to a 553 

multisensory experiment in which, during multisensory trials, the two cues were spatially incon-554 

gruent, i.e., they were presented on diametrically opposite locations. Critically, though, the spatial 555 

relationship never changed and the response rules associated with such cues never instructed con-556 

flicting saccadic choices. Unisensory trials required participants to make either antisaccades away 557 

from a V cue, in IVA blocks, or prosaccades toward an A cue, in IAP blocks. During IVA multisen-558 

sory trials the additional auditory cue could, in principle, enhance the visually driven anti choice 559 

by guiding the saccade early and directly toward the correct target (via an implicit pro rule). Like-560 

wise, during IAP multisensory trials the additional visual cue could, in principle, reinforce the 561 

sound-driven pro choice by confirming the correct target location (via an implicit anti rule). Thus, 562 

while multisensory trials were stimulus-incongruent due to spatial misalignment of the cues, the 563 

consistent application of different cue-response rules would have kept them target-congruent. The 564 

question was whether, in either variant, the participants would be able to exploit such consistency 565 

and benefit from the presence of two cues instead of one. 566 

What are the possible scenarios for the outcome of this experiment? If integration is at all 567 

feasible, one possibility is that a performance benefit is eventually seen at long PTs, as in the CA 568 

experiment. This would be in line with the effects of practice and repetition on attentional selec-569 

tion (Jiang and Sisk, 2019), and with numerous experiments in which crossmodal features that 570 

seemed to be unrelated, or related only in an abstract way, turned out to be tightly bound percep-571 

tually (e.g., auditory pitch and visual size; Laurienti et al., 2004; Ludwig et al., 2011; Spence, 2011). 572 

On the other hand, given that it is generally difficult to perform multiple sensory-motor tasks 573 

simultaneously (Pashler, 1994; Marois and Ivanoff, 2005), an alternative is that participants pre-574 

dominantly track one stimulus in each trial, essentially filtering out the other one. If participants 575 

focused on the stimulus that is persistent in each task variant, this would predict multisensory per-576 

formance that is very similar to that in the corresponding unisensory trial type. As we describe 577 

next, results were close to the latter scenario, but with the caveat that stimulus-driven interactions 578 

were still clearly visible. 579

Again, when one unisensory signal yields performance above and the other yields perfor-634

Potential contribution of selection history effects 711 

An alternative mechanism distinct from integration could potentially play an important role in 712 

our experiments, and could perhaps account in part for the CA results; this mechanism is selec-713 

tion history. Perceptually guided choices are often influenced by the history of events recently 714 

experienced. The associated biases may be induced by past stimuli, past rewards, or past actions, 715 

for instance, or by specific combinations (Fecteau and Munoz, 2003; Awh et al., 2012; Failing and 716 

Theeuwes, 2018; Jiang and Sisk, 2019; Theeuwes, 2019; Anderson et al., 2021). Importantly, under 717 

certain experimental designs, such biases may explain effects attributed to multisensory integra-718 

tion (Otto and Mamassian, 2012; Shaw et al., 2020; Roberts et al., 2024). 719 

We investigated the potential contribution of selection history effects to our results by generat-720 

ing history-conditioned tachometric curves for each of our task variants (Methods). For this, trials 721 

of each type (A, V or multisensory) were sorted according to their preceding trial type (again, A, 722 

V or multisensory). The main question was whether repetition of a given cue modality (e.g., V →723 

V) reinforced performance, and conversely, whether switching from one cue modality to another 724 

(e.g., A → V) hindered performance. 725 

In all cases, differences between history-conditioned tachometric curves were either minimal 726 

or nonexistent. The largest effect occurred in the IVA variant, where a consistent difference of 727 

approximately 12 ms was observed between rise points when the same cue type was repeated 728 

(e.g., V(A) → V(A)) versus when it switched (e.g., V → V(A)). This shift in PT was similar for 729 

the V and V(A) conditions. In the other task variants, no notable differences were found. We 730 

conclude that the contribution from history-driven effects to the current results was, for the most 731 

part, negligible. 732

how visual and auditory cues can jointly guide saccadic choices, whether synergistically or com-737

First, the interaction between auditory and visual information evolved as the choice process 739 

unfolded. This evolution was rich and fast, reminiscent of the integration dynamics of neurons 740 

in the superior colliculus (Miller et al., 2017), and depended on the particular task configuration. 741 

But a key conclusion is that the nature of the integration process — whether performance guided 742 

by one cue is enhanced or hindered by the presence of a second cue — varies over time. An 743 

additional cue may initially enhance performance but later have no effect (Fig. 5b); it may have 744 

no effect initially but later hinder performance (Fig. 6b); or the combination may shift from a form 745 

of performance averaging to true enhancement (Fig. 3b). In general, a transition occurs between 746 

stimulus-driven and goal-driven regimes. 747 

Contrast this with classic studies in multisensory integration in which discrimination accuracy 748 

is the main behavioral output (e.g., Ernst and Banks, 2002; Alais and Burr, 2004; Kording and 749 

Wolpert, 2004; Gori et al., 2012). Because the corresponding tasks lack urgency, not much can be 750 

inferred about the time dependence of the underlying perceptual processes, nor about the impact 751 

of fast, transient, bottom-up signals. Even in cases in which time and accuracy are considered 752 

together (Corneil et al., 2002; Drugowitsch et al., 2014), reliance on the RT obscures both the low-753 

level contributions and the true time course of the perceptual evaluation. Non-urgent tasks sample 754 

the regime that is eventually observed after a long PT but miss the rich developments that precede 755 

it, which are highly informative of the underlying neural dynamics (Seideman et al., 2022; Zhu et 756 

al., 2024). 757 

Second, the stimulus-driven signals derived from visual and auditory cues always played an 758 

important role; although they did not necessarily manifest in terms of ceiling accuracy (at long 759 

PTs), they always impacted the amount of PT needed to make an informed choice. To appreciate 760 

this observation it helps to realize that, for the types of sensory-guided behavior considered here, 761 

any given cue stimulus inevitably yields two signals, an exogenous one associated with its salience 762 

and an endogenous one associated with its assigned motor response (Goldstein et al., 2022, 2024; 763 

Oor et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Kattner et al., 2026). During urgent antisaccades (e.g., Fig. 2), the 764 

two signals become obvious because they point in opposite directions, one promoting a saccade to 765 

the cue and the other a saccade away. But the two components also contribute, one after the other, 766 

when they are aligned, i.e., even in the simplest case in which a participant intends to look at a 767 

salient stimulus (Oor et al., 2023; Kattner et al., 2026). The dual contribution is just more difficult 768 

to demonstrate in this case. 769 

With this in mind, it is easier to see that in our CMC task the early stimulus-driven (exogenous) 770 

contributions were always significant and lawful. This was most evident when the cues were spa-771 

tially incongruent (IVA, IAP); then the difference between unisensory performance with cue 1 and 772

drawn toward cue 2 (Figs. 5b, 6b). Befitting its exogenous character, this effect occurred precisely 774 

when expected given the latency of cue 2 and regardless of the particular stimulus-response rule 775 

used. Such specific contributions were less discernible during congruent V+A trials, when both 776

the same antisaccade, adding a congruent auditory signal yielded substantial savings in PT in 780

the expected allocation of attention to two cues based on the three established selection mecha-792 

nisms, stimulus-driven, goal-driven, and history-driven (Corbetta and Shulman, 2002; Theeuwes, 793 

2010, 2019; Carrasco, 2011; Awh et al., 2012; Anderson et al., 2021; Noyce et al., 2023). Endogenous 794 

selection of one cue or another was observed for identical cue-target configurations (Fig. 7b); ex-795 

ogenous capture was observed both when the visual cue effectively acted as a distracter (Fig. 6b) 796 

and when the auditory cue did (Fig. 5b); contextual or selection-history effects modulated the per-797

These data speak to the relationship between attention and multisensory integration, which 800 

has been the focus of several studies (for review, see Koelewijn et al., 2010; Santangelo and Macaluso, 801 

2012). The current consensus based on results from non-urgent tasks is that, while cross-modal 802 

attention and multisensory integration often operate independently (the traditional view), some-803 

times they definitely interact (a more recent view). Specifically, attention seems more likely to 804 

independently impact the integration of stimuli that are weakly associated, e.g., because they 805 

are spatially misaligned, or because they involve unrelated modality-specific features. While 806 

we did not attempt to manipulate attention and cue integration independently, our results are 807 

broadly consistent with such summary. They demonstrated both lawful low-level interactions 808 

independent of top-down goals, and strong contextual effects when cues were misaligned. No-809 

tably, whether viewed in terms of multisensory integration or crossmodal attentional allocation, 810 

results are easier to interpret when exogenous and endogenous contributions to performance are 811 

temporally dissociated. 812 

Our experiments revealed substantial asymmetries between visual and auditory performance 813 

that are consistent with intrinsic, qualitative differences in how neural circuits process the two 814 

modalities. To be reliably produced, auditory antisaccades (A trials from CA variant) required 815 

∼88 ms of additional PT relative to auditory prosaccades (A trials from IAP variant), a differ-816 

ence that is comparable to that observed with visual stimuli (Goldstein et al., 2022). However, 817 

there were two notable differences between modalities. First, overt capture was robust during 818 

visual antisaccades but minimal during auditory antisaccades (performance barely dipped below 819 

chance). And second, the asymptotic fraction correct for auditory antisaccades was considerably 820 

lower (0.81) than that obtained with visual antisaccades (0.94) or auditory prosaccades (0.95). This 821 

suggests that the cognitive operation for inverting the stimulus location and specifying the sac-822 

cade target always consumed an additional amount of time ( ∼100 ms of PT), but in the auditory 823 

case was based on a neural signal that — in spite of the high intensity of the originating physical 824 

stimulus — was relatively weak. This is consistent with prior studies reporting strong dominance 825 

of visual information over auditory (Colavita, 1974; Spence et al., 2012; Li et al., 2017; Malevich et 826 

al., 2026). 827

possible even when the stimulus-response rule is indirect and requires top-down control. In this 829
