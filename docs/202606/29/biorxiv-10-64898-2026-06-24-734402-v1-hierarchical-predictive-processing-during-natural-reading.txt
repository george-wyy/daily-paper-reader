Abstract: Key neural mechanisms underlying reading, especially in the context of sentences and 10 

texts, remain elusive. An influential theory states that the brain rapidly generates a hierarchy of 11 

probabilistic predictions about upcoming words at multiple levels of linguistic representation. 12 

When encountering a word, a reader integrates sensory input with the linguistic predictions, and 13 

rely on the resulting prediction error signals to achieve efficient comprehension. Here, we tested 14 

this hierarchical predictive account with a natural reading task while EEG and eye movements 15 

were simultaneously recorded. We find distinct prediction error signals attributable to word 16 

contextual, lexical, and orthographic levels of predictions. These prediction error signals exhibit 17 

a temporal order. Our results also indicate that context-based predictions rapidly constrain 18 

orthographic-level predictions. We thus provide evidence for a hierarchical predictive process 19 

central to efficient natural reading. 20

We comprehend meaningful messages by rapidly scanning words, sentences, and texts. This 22 

ability to read is a foundation to human communications that traverse distances in time and 23 

space. While skilled readers can read with remarkable efficiency ( 1, 2 ), the underlying neural 24 

mechanism remains elusive. The Hierarchical Predictive Processing hypothesis (HPP, 3–5) is a 25 

highly influential idea for understanding neural mechanisms, including those underlying 26 

language comprehension ( 6–8 ). HPP posits that the brain generates top-down probabilistic 27 

predictions about upcoming sensory information on multiple processing levels. Prediction error 28 

signals are in turn elicited at each level that scale with the magnitude of difference between the 29 

prediction and sensory input encountered. Prediction error, which represents sensory information 30 

not predicted by the brain, enables neuronal processing of sensory input with high efficiency ( 4, 31 

9). 32 

For language comprehension, recent studies have shown evidence of HPP on high-level (e.g., 33 

context) and low-level (e.g., visual and auditory features) linguistic representations respectively 34 

(10–14 ). Furthermore, HPP also suggests the integration between high and low level 35 

representations as a core mechanism of comprehension (see 6). This is supported by studies 36 

which showed an optimizing effect of contextual prediction on low-level sensory inputs ( 15–17 ). 37 

However, much current evidence is established from listening comprehension ( 10, 11, 14, 17) or 38 

visual single-word recognition tasks ( 12, 13, 15, 16 ). It remains unknown whether the 39 

comprehension of written text also engages a similar process, particularly in natural reading 40 

conditions. 41 

For decades, the neural mechanisms underlying sentence reading have been mainly studied in 42 

highly simplified laboratory tasks that involve the passive visual presentation of sentences one 43 

word at time (rapid serial visual presentation, RSVP). While the RSVP paradigm avoids certain 44 

measurement artifacts, it is very much different from typical reading conditions (see 18 ). For 45 

example, natural reading requires the reader to actively move their eyes through the text and 46 

allow the reader to perceive visual information in foveal and parafoveal regions. An alternative 47 

method, which allow the study of brain activations in more natural conditions, is the 48 

simultaneous recording of electroencephalography (EEG) and eye movement. This method, 49 

combined with recent advances in artifact correction ( 19 ) and statistical modelling ( 20 ), enables 50 

the synchronization between EEG and eye movement data with sub-millisecond precision, and 51

Here, we report a study in which subjects participated in a natural reading task while their EEG 54 

and eye movement were simultaneously recorded with high temporal resolution (Fig. 1A and 55 

1B). We set out to investigate HPP at three levels: contextual, lexical, and orthographic word 56 

representations. If natural reading involves hierarchical predictions, we expect fixation-related 57 

brain activity to show: (1) distinct prediction error signals at each level of the hierarchy, (2) a 58 

temporal order between the prediction error signals, and (3) that high-level predictions 59 

dynamically constrain low-level predictions. 60 

Prediction error signals at each level were modelled (see Fig. 1C) using word surprisal , lexical 61 

frequency , and the orthographic prediction error (oPE, 13 ). A word’s surprisal value represents 62 

the error signal based on all information provided by the text's prior context (such as semantics, 63 

syntax, morphological expectations). Here, it was defined as the negative-log context-dependent 64 

probability estimated by the Chinese-Llama 2 Large Language Model (LLM, 22 ). Contextual 65 

probabilities derived from LLM have shown to be comparable to those estimated from the classic 66 

cloze procedure ( 23 ). Next, lexical frequency measures a word's probability of occurrence 67 

independent of the prior context (e.g., low-frequency words are less likely to occur in a 68 

language). The inverse of lexical frequency represents the error signal based on general statistical 69 

regularities of words contained in the language and also in the participant’s mental lexicon. 70 

Lexical frequency was retrieved from the SUBTLEX-CH corpus ( 24 ). Finally, oPE ( 13 ) is the 71 

pixel-by-pixel difference between an orthographic prediction image and the actual orthographic 72 

input (Fig. 1D). The orthographic prediction image was derived by averaging pixel intensities 73 

across 23,854 word images. The summed oPE value represents the error signals elicited by word 74 

predictions based on the participant’s knowledge about the language's orthographic patterns. 75 

To this end, we hypothesized that on each of the three levels, any mismatch between the reader’s 76 

prediction and the actual word input will elicit a prediction error signal that scales with the 77 

degree of the deviation. EEG should detect these signals on the scalp and exhibit distinct 78 

temporal and spatial patterns for prediction error signals arising at each level. 79

Results 81 

Distinct prediction error signals at three levels of word representation 82 

We analyzed natural reading EEG data using the regression-ERP framework ( 25, 26 ) with the 83 

unfold toolbox in MATLAB ( 20 ). Importantly, this so-called linear deconvolution framework 84 

allows us to disentangle and isolate the influences of different word properties and from 85 

successive eye fixations on the EEG during natural reading ( 27 ). Within the model, for each 86 

fixation on a word, we used the fixated word’s surprisal, frequency, and oPE to model the EEG 87 

response from 200 ms before to 800 ms after fixation onset. To control for potential confounds, 88 

lexical and oculomotor covariates, known to influence the fixation-related EEG, were included 89 

as regressors in the model. Specifically, it controlled for incoming saccade amplitude, the fixated 90 

word’s length, and the brain response elicited by the initial onset of the sentence stimulus. 91 

Correlations between the regressors are available in the supplementary materials (Table S1). The 92 

beta coefficients (i.e., regression-based FRP, rFRP) of each of the three predictors of interest 93 

were then entered into further analyses. 94 

Surprisal 95 

We first tested whether our model captured prediction error signals at the level of contextual 96 

word prediction. Indeed, cluster-based permutation test on the surprisal rFRP revealed a 97 

significant effect (peak significance at 237 ms, p = .004). To inspect the time course of the effect, 98 

we computed Global Field Power (GFP) and Global Map Dissimilarity (GMD, 28 ) of the 99 

predictor’s rFRP. GFP and GMD measure, respectively, the grand-average topographic map 100 

strength and stability across time. Results showed a relatively strong and stable topographic 101 

distribution approximately from 155 ms to 552 ms (Fig. 2A). Within this period, topographic 102 

maps showed that higher word surprisal elicited increased negativity first at left-lateralized 103 

occipital regions and then at centroparietal regions, as well as increased positivity at left-frontal 104 

regions throughout the entire period. The topographic distribution and the rFRP at centroparietal 105 

electrodes (Fig. 2B) resemble the N400 effect (see 29 ) found in response to reading words with 106 

low cloze probability (e.g., 30–32 ) or high surprisal (e.g., 33, 34 ). The slightly earlier peak in the 107 

rFRP, compared to ERP studies, is in accordance with the N400 effect found in natural reading 108 

studies (e.g., 21, 35 ). The increased positivity at left-frontal regions is also consistent with results 109

Frequency 112 

Next, we sought to see whether predictions are also made based on word lexical frequency. We 113 

found significant clusters in the rFRP of the word frequency predictor (peak significance at 189 114 

ms, p < .001). A high GFP and low GMD of the frequency effect was observed approximately 115 

from 160 ms to 250 ms, indicating a reliable effect in this period (Fig. 2C). Topographic maps 116 

within this period showed that less frequent words elicited increased negativity at occipital 117 

regions, and increased positivity throughout central and frontal regions. These results suggested 118 

that lower word frequency is related to more negative (or less positive) amplitude near the N1 119 

component (Fig. 2D). This is consistent with ERP studies that showed low frequency words 120 

elicited a more negative N1/N170 (e.g., 37, 38 ), or a less positive P2 (e.g., 36 ), when compared 121 

to high frequency words. Similar effects were also found in FRPs ( 39–42 ). On the other hand, 122 

some studies also reported the N400 effect elicited by low frequency words (e.g., 42, 43 ). 123 

However, we did not find reliable evidence that suggested an effect within this time window. 124 

One likely reason is that our surprisal predictor had captured most of the N400 effect. 125 

Orthographic Prediction Error 126 

Lastly, we found significant clusters in the rFRP of the oPE predictor (peak significance at 142 127 

ms, p < .001). There were high GFP and low GMD approximately from 138 ms to 171 ms (Fig. 128 

2E). Within this time window, topographic maps showed that higher oPE elicited increased 129

frontal regions. The topographic distribution resembled that of previously reported oPE effects, 131 

in which higher oPE elicited stronger negativity at occipital areas ( 13 ). Our result is also largely 132 

consistent with previous research suggesting that the N1/N170 component reflects early 133 

orthographic processing (e.g., 44–46 ). 134

A temporal order of prediction error signals among the three levels 142 

We showed in the previous section that the brain generates distinct prediction error signals at 143 

different levels of linguistic representation. Next, we sought to confirm whether there is a 144 

temporal order among these signals. We computed two measures of jackknife-based effect 145 

latencies ( 47 ). First, for each effect, we defined the peak effect latency as the time at which the 146 

correlation was maximal between the topographic map at grand-average GFP maxima (Fig. 3A) 147 

and jackknife-based rFRP maps from 0 ms to 400 ms ( 48, 49 ). Second, we defined the "central" 148 

effect latency as the centroid of each jackknife-based GFP waveform within the same time 149 

window. We tested the latency differences with linear mixed models and found significant 150 

differences in both peak and central effect latencies among the three effects (both p < .001, Fig. 151 

3B). For peak effect latencies, pairwise comparisons revealed that the peak effect of oPE ( M = 152 

149.95, SD = 0.50) occurred significantly earlier than the peak effect of frequency ( M = 175.03, 153 

SD = 0.53), t(117) = 234.0, p < .001, and surprisal ( M = 242.0, SD = 0.39), t(117) = 859.0, p154 

< .001. The peak effect of frequency was also significantly earlier than that of surprisal, t(117) = 155 

625.0, p < .001. With regard to central effect latencies, we also found that the oPE effect ( M = 156 

185.15. SD = 0.90) preceded that of frequency ( M = 207.41. SD = 1.00), t(78) = 93.6, p < .001, 157 

and surprisal ( M = 213.54. SD = 1.38), t(78) = 119.4, p < .001. Finally, the central effect of 158 

frequency occurred earlier than that of surprisal, t(78) = 25.8, p < .001. These results suggest a 159 

temporal ordering among the three prediction error signals, from orthographic to lexical, and 160 

finally, to contextual word predictions. 161 

Hierarchical orthographic prediction during natural reading 162 

So far, our results showed that the brain produces distinct prediction error signals at the three 163 

levels of linguistic representation, with a temporal order among them. Lastly, as a hallmark of 164 

hierarchical prediction, we tested whether the brain utilizes higher-level predictions to inform 165 

orthographic predictions. We derived additional oPE variants (Fig. 4A) to test whether such a 166 

hierarchical relationship exists. The oPE measure used in the analyses above reflects how 167 

strongly the pixels of a given word deviates from a prediction image which is created by 168

prediction images that were the weighted average, rather than simple average, of word images. 170 

For context-based oPE, the prediction images were created by weighting each word image by its 171 

respective contextual probability from the LLM, assuming that contextual word predictions 172 

constrain orthographic predictions. For frequency-based oPE, we weighted each word image by 173 

its lexical frequency value, assuming that context-independent lexical predictions constrain 174 

orthographic predictions. We compared model fits between models with either context-based, 175 

frequency-based, or non-hierarchical oPE. These three models differed only in how oPE was 176 

computed, and they were then fitted to participants’ EEG data (downsampled to 250 Hz for 177 

computation efficiency). Model fits were cross-validated on a leave-one-block-out basis. For 178 

each participant, we first took the median R 2 of each electrode across folds, and then averaged 179 

the median R 2 across all electrodes. This resulted in three R 2 values for each of the three models 180 

and each participant's EEG data. 181 

The models’ cross-validated R 2 were analyzed with a linear mixed model (Fig. 4B). We found a 182 

significant effect of model type, F(2,78) = 4.60, p = .013. Post-hoc contrasts showed that the 183 

model with the context-based oPE ( M = .0196, SD = .0375) explained significantly more 184 

variance than the one with the frequency-based oPE ( M = .019, SD = .0386), t(78) = 2.7, p185 

= .024, and the non-hierarchical model ( M = .019 SD = .0386), t(78) = 2.53, p = .027. We found 186 

no significant difference between the models with frequency-based and non-hierarchical oPE ( p187 

= .85). The results are consistent with evidence of hierarchical prediction found in listening 188 

comprehension ( 17 ). They also suggest that orthographic predictions are constrained by context-189 

dependent word predictions, thus supporting a hierarchical predictive process in which 190 

predictions from higher levels are sent to lower levels during natural reading comprehension. 191

Discussion 193 

Taken together, the three types of evidence presented here support the HPP account during 194 

natural reading. Each type of evidence also provides specific insights into the neural mechanisms 195 

involved during reading. Firstly, we delineated prediction error signals at the orthographic, 196 

lexical, and contextual levels by showing that they each had a significant effect on EEG and 197 

manifested with distinct topographic distributions. Thus, our findings suggest that the brain 198 

generates predictions about upcoming words at multiple levels of linguistic representation, based 199 

on their probabilistic features, to support efficient information processing during reading 200 

comprehension. 201 

Besides showing that orthographic, lexical, and contextual predictions are implemented, we also 202 

show that the three levels of prediction error signals emerged in the expected temporal order. It 203 

suggests that the prediction error signals are propagated from the visual-orthographic level to 204 

lexical and contextual levels through the predictive hierarchy ( 3–5 ). Existing models of reading 205 

and word recognition often suggest that reading a word activates a cascade of cognitive 206 

processes, starting from visual and orthographic information processing to higher-level lexical 207 

processes ( 44, 45 ). Our results reveal a similar cascading process during natural reading. 208 

Furthermore, our data suggest relatively rapid lexical access after fixation, as the peak latencies 209 

of the word frequency and surprisal effects occured within 300 ms after fixation onset. This is 210 

consistent with the proposal that first-order analysis of a word occurs within 300 ms after 211 

exposure ( 38 ), and cognitive processes after this time are elicited by reanalysis and second-order 212 

processes ( 50 ). 213 

In addition to a temporal ordering of signals, we also present evidence that orthographic 214 

predictions are constrained by higher-level contextual word predictions. This suggests that 215 

higher-level predictions influence lower-level ones and supports the interactive account of the 216 

relationship between high-level predictions and low-level visuospatial features during reading 217 

(6). Moreover, since the surprisal effect's peak and central latencies are proximate to the average 218 

fixation duration of a typical Chinese reader (about 230 ms, 51 ), it indicates a rapid update of 219 

contextual predictions that subsequently constrain orthographic prediction on the upcoming 220 

word. This contextual constraint on low-level predictions have also been shown in listening 221 

comprehension ( 17 ), potentially indicating the same constraining role of contextual predictions 222

contrary, we did not find evidence indicating that word frequency constrains orthographic 224 

predictions. One would expect that higher-level lexical predictions should also constrain lower-225 

level orthographic predictions. This was evident in a recent study in which oPE computed based 226 

on the most frequent words fit better to subjects' EEG data than the "original" oPE in a single-227 

word recognition task ( 12 ). However, since we used a natural reading task, the availability of 228 

rich contextual information could better inform orthographic predictions. Therefore, in the 229 

context of natural text reading, readers may select contextual information over lexical frequency 230 

for informing orthographic predictions to achieve more efficient processing. 231 

We did not include predictors related to parafoveal word properties, mainly due to the risks of 232 

overfitting. However, it is worth noting that the brain may also generate predictions about words 233 

located in the parafoveal visual field. For instance, it has been shown that an invalid parafoveal 234 

preview of target word resulted in a more negative N400 elicited by pre-target fixation ( 35 ). 235 

Recent evidence also suggested a hierarchical relationship between orthographic and semantic 236 

information processing in parafoveal area ( 52 ). This suggests that the principles of HPP is not 237 

specific to foveal processing but may operate across fixations and a wide perceptual span. 238 

Thus, we present evidence from a natural reading paradigm suggesting HPP as the underlying 239 

neural mechanism which can facilitate one of the most efficient information-processing skills in 240 

human, that is reading. Brain activations are largely consistent with a hierarchical predictive 241 

process, indicating multi-level prediction errors that are processed in succession and integrated 242 

across levels. This work highlights a hierarchical predictive neural mechanism underlying natural 243 

reading, and this mechanism may in fact be fundamental to linguistic processing across 244 

modalities. 245

Methods 247 

Participants 248 

Data from 40 participants are reported (35 females; age in years, M = 22.9; SD = 2.48). 249 

Participants were all university students who were skilled readers of simplified Chinese and grew 250 

up in Mainland China. They had normal or corrected-to-normal vision, and reported no existing 251 

diagnosis of developmental, neurological, and psychiatric disorders. Most participants (n = 39) 252 

scored > 50 on the laterality quotient of the Chinese version of Edinburgh Handedness Inventory 253 

(53 , M = 82.9; SD = 18.24), indicating a majority of right-handed participants in the sample. One 254 

participant's data in the first experimental block was not recorded due to human error. The rest of 255 

the data from the participant was included in analyses. 256 

The data of four additional participants were excluded from analysis either due to premature 257 

termination of experiment ( n = 1), missing event triggers that compromised synchronization 258 

accuracy ( n = 1), or over 80% of the time windows around fixation onset events being marked as 259 

artifacts ( n = 2, see artifact detection below). 260 

Stimuli 261 

Experimental stimuli were excerpts from Chinese novels. Half the participants read excerpts 262 

from the novel "Mimosa" ( 54 ) and the other half read "Mr Ma and Son" ( 55 ). The excerpts 263 

contained the first 14,456 and the first 14,614 simplified Chinese characters of each novel, 264 

respectively (starting from the beginning), and texts were divided into 400 and 428 sentences, 265 

respectively. On a few occasions, the original novels contain English words or Chinese 266 

characters that are non-existent in the vocabulary of the Large Language Model (LLM). Those 267 

characters and words were replaced with their respective synonyms. 268 

Each character or punctuation mark in the text subtended 1.07° ´ 1.07° degrees of visual angle. 269 

Chinese characters were presented in a Black PMingLiU font (size 40) on a white background. 270 

All stimuli were displayed on a Lenovo Legion 24.5 inch LCD monitor (a model G23245FR1 271 

VA panel) refreshing at 144 Hz (1920 x 1080 pixels). 272 

Texts were presented sentence-by-sentence. Sentences typically ended on a full stop, exclamation 273 

point, question mark, or ellipsis. In the case where multiple sentences were nested within a pair 274

Procedure 283 

Participants were seated in a sound-attenuated chamber at a viewing distance of 75 cm from the 284 

monitor. The experiment was programmed and presented with the Presentation software ( 56 ). 285 

Before the onset of each sentence, participants were asked to maintain fixation on a cross located 286 

1.07° to the left of the first character. Sentence onset was triggered when the eye-tracker detected 287 

a fixation within 0.61° from the center of the cross (horizontally and vertically) that lasted 250 288 

ms. Participants read each sentence silently at their own pace and pressed the spacebar once they 289 

wished to proceed to the next sentence. This button press would trigger the current sentence to 290 

disappear and the left fixation cross to appear again. If sentence onset was not triggered within 291 

10 s of the fixation cross onset, a calibration procedure was prompted to recalibrate the eye-292 

tracker. 293 

The experiment was divided into 20 blocks where each block contained between 15 and 31 294 

sentences. After each block, participants answered two multiple-choice comprehension questions 295 

about the content of the preceding block. Each question contained four possible choices with one 296 

correct answer. Participants responded by pressing the corresponding key on the keyboard ("D", 297 

"F", "J", "K"). Feedback and the correct answer were shown immediately after their response. 298 

For example, one of the questions was "Where did Mr. Ma senior first met the priest?". The 299 

choices were "D - at his son's school", "F - at his school", "J - at his brother's business", and "K -300 

on the ship". An incorrect response would trigger a red background, while a correct response 301 

would trigger a green one. The correct answer was shown regardless of the participants' 302 

response. Participants were instructed to answer the questions based on the contents from the 303 

previous block only, and to take a break in-between blocks. Before the start of the experiment 304

EEG & eye movement recordings 307 

EEG and eye movements were recorded simultaneously during the experiment. EEG was 308 

recorded from 128 Ag/AgCl electrodes placed within an ANT Waveguard Original cap with 309 

equidistant electrode positions (Advanced Neuro Technology, Enschede, Netherlands). The data 310 

was referenced online against an electrode on the vertex (Cz-equivalent) and a clip-on electrode 311 

on one earlobe served as ground. A vertical electrooculogram (EOG) electrode was placed under 312 

the left eye. Two electrodes located at the lateral orbitofrontal edges of the EEG cap recorded 313 

horizontal EOG signals. EEG signals were amplified and recorded with the ANT EEGO HUB 314 

amplifier at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz. Electrode impedance was kept below 40 kΩ. 315 

Eye movements were recorded binocularly with a desktop-mounted Eyelink 1000 Plus eye 316 

tracker (SR Research) at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz. Participants’ head position was stabilized 317 

by a chin rest. A five-point calibration procedure followed by a five-point validation was 318 

completed at the beginning of the experiment and whenever a fixation-check failed before the 319 

onset of each sentence. Fixations and saccades were detected using Eyelink 1000 tracker’s native 320 

algorithm (saccade velocity threshold: 30° degree/s, acceleration threshold: 8000° degree/s 2). 321 

During the experiment, the stimulus presentation computer sent shared triggers simultaneously to 322 

both the EEG and the eye-tracker computer. Offline, both recordings were then synchronized 323 

based on these common triggers using the EYE-EEG extension (version 0.99; 21 ) for EEGLAB 324 

(version 2023.1; 58 ). All participants had a synchronization error < 0.41 ms (M = 0.307, SD = 325 

0.045). 326

ICA training data, EEG data 20 ms before and 10 ms after each saccade (as detected by the eye 336 

tracker’s algorithm) was copied and repeatedly appended to the end of the EEG ICA-training 337

was then used for Infomax ICA decomposition, and the resulted ICA weight matrix was applied 339 

back to the original dataset. Independent components related to ocular artifacts were then 340 

removed by visual inspection. Following ICA, bad channels that were removed before were then 341 

interpolated by spherical interpolation and the dataset was re-referenced to the common average 342 

reference. Channels that recorded EOG signals were excluded for all further analyses. 343 

Regression model 344 

For each participant, the continuous EEG data was the dependent variable; and various word 345 

properties and control variables constituted a matrix of predictors in a large regression model. 346 

Using the unfold toolbox ( 20 ), the design matrix of the regression model was then time-347 

expanded ( 27 ), so that the regression coefficients (or “betas”) for each predictor were modelled 348 

from 200 ms before and 800 ms after the respective fixation or stimulus event. 349 

To detect bad intervals of the EEG, we then used the "continuousArtifactDetect" function of the 350 

ERPLAB toolbox ( 60 ) as modified for the unfold toolbox. Specifically, a boxcar moving window 351 

with a length of 1000 ms was shifted in 500 ms steps across the continuous EEG data. Whenever 352 

the window contained a peak-to-peak difference larger than 100 μV, the interval was flagged. 353 

Flagged continuous EEG intervals were then excluded from the analysis by setting the 354 

corresponding rows of the time-expanded design matrix to 0. For two participants, over 80% of 355 

the data around fixation onset events were marked as artifact and these participants were 356 

therefore excluded from all analyses. In a final step, the regression model was then solved 357 

separately for each EEG channel using the ordinary least-squares method. 358 

The regression model can be described by three separate equations. Each equation denotes a type 359 

of event and the corresponding predictors used to model EEG activity around the event. Note 360 

that within the model, all predictors are included in the same design matrix and are estimated at 361 

the same time: 362

Surprisal 376 

Word surprisal was estimated by probabilities extracted from the Chinese-Llama 2 Large 377 

Language Model (LLM, 22 ). This model adopts the LLaMA architecture ( 61 ), has 13 billion 378 

parameters, and was trained with 20 GB of texts from a Chinese corpus. This LLM adopts a 379 

tokenizer that was trained with 20,000 Chinese words. Each of these words is represented by a 380 

token instead of multiple tokens. Therefore, word probabilities generated by the LLM are 381 

probabilities of a whole Chinese word instead of joint probabilities of multiple tokens. This 382 

tokenization process was also used as the method of Chinese word segmentation. The LLM and 383 

tokenizer were downloaded from Hugging Face’s Transformers package ( 62 ). 384 

Text from each experimental block was entered to the LLM independently. Hence, the context 385 

was reset before every block. The text was first tokenized into word segments (i.e., tokens). The 386 

sequence of tokens then passed through the model, and the conditional log-probabilities of each 387 

token were extracted from the model. These log-probabilities were then transformed to surprisal 388 

by multiplying by -1. Higher surprisal therefore indicates a contextually less probable word. 389 

Surprisal values from the two excerpts range from 0.00011 to 17.97 ( M = 4.16, SD =3.06). 390

Frequency 391 

Word frequency was obtained from the SUBTLEX-CH corpus ( 24 ). We calculated the Zipf value 392 

(63 ), a logarithmic measure of word frequency, for each word according to its frequency count 393 

and the corpus size. This value transforms the frequency count of word segments to a value 394 

proportional to the size of the corpus. It also assigns a value for word segments that have a 395 

frequency count of zero in the corpus, assuming that word segments not recorded in SUBTLEX-396 

CH were not completely unseen by the participants. Zipf values were multiplied by -1 so that a 397 

higher value represents a less frequent word. Zipf values across the two excerpts range from -398 

7.70 to -1.47 ( M = -4.87, SD = 1.70). 399 

Orthographic prediction error (oPE) 400 

We calculated oPE ( 13 ) with the EBImage package ( 64 ) in the software R (65 ). At each word 401 

segment position, 55,296 token candidates (vocabulary size of the LLM) were extracted from the 402 

LLM. Token candidates that contained non-Chinese characters (e.g. English words, 403 

punctuations) were excluded. We also excluded token candidates that were longer than 5 Chinese 404 

characters. A total of 23,854 token candidates remained. Each of them was transformed to an 405 

image of 265 ´ 53 pixels in grayscale, with the first character of the word aligned to the left 406 

border of the image. Characters were of the same font and size as in the experimental stimuli. In 407 

R, each image was represented by a 265 ´ 53 matrix. Numbers in the matrix were continuous 408 

values between 1 and 0 that denoted the pixel intensity with 1 = White, 0 = Black, and grey in 409 

between. We averaged the matrices of all token candidates to obtain the orthographic prediction 410 

image. The difference between the predicted and actual orthographic input was calculated by 411 

subtracting the averaged matrix from the matrix of the actual word image. oPE was the sum of 412 

all absolute pixel intensities from the resulting difference matrix. Higher oPE value indicates 413 

larger difference between the predicted and actual orthographic input. In other words, the oPE 414 

calculated by this simple-averaging procedure represents the probability of seeing an 415 

orthographic pattern based on all words in the participant’s mental lexicon (i.e., a non-416 

hierarchical oPE). Non-hierarchical oPE range from 925.28 to 2509.33 ( M = 1113.82, SD =417 

155.41). 418

Based on the hierarchical predictive processing framework, it is possible that the oPE is 419 

influenced by a reader’s prior experience and the preceding context provided by the text. 420 

Therefore, for a model comparison analysis, two additional types of oPE were calculated by 421 

using weighted-averages, instead of simple-averages, when deriving the orthographic prediction 422 

image. The frequency-weighted orthographic prediction image was generated by first multiplying 423 

each token candidate by their normalized Zipf value, and then summing over all weighted 424 

images. oPE calculated from frequency-weighted prediction image (i.e., frequency-based oPE) 425 

therefore assumed that orthographic predictions are constrained by the lexical frequency of each 426 

word. It therefore reflects a possible hierarchical predictive process where the context-427 

independent probability of each word is used for orthographic prediction. 428 

The context-dependent orthographic prediction images were generated by multiplying each token 429 

candidate by their normalized context-dependent word probabilities extracted from the LLM 430 

(here, a 7-billion parameter version was used for computational efficiency). Since context-431 

dependent word probability changes with context, a unique prediction image was generated for 432 

each word segment position. oPE calculated from context dependent orthographic prediction 433 

(i.e., context-based oPE) represents a hierarchical predictive process where contextual 434 

information constrains orthographic predictions. 435 

Saccade amplitude 436 

Saccade amplitude was the size of the saccade preceding the fixation. It has previously been 437 

shown that the size of the saccade preceding a given fixation onset is a strong predictor of the 438 

FRP waveform, with larger saccades evoking stronger visual brain responses, especially within 439 

the early intervals after fixation onset ( 27 ). This regressor thus captured the variance in FRP that 440 

could be attributed to the size of the incoming saccade. 441 

Word length 442 

Word length was the number of characters of the fixated word. Longer words tended to have 443 

higher oPE values due to more grey pixels in the actual word image. The aim of this regressor 444 

was to act as a covariate for the oPE predictor so that the latter better captured variance in EEG 445 

attributed to the more subtle effects of orthographic prediction error. We also ran separate control 446 

analyses that control for the word length effect on the oPE predictor (see control analyses 447

Sentence length 453 

Sentence length was the number of characters in the sentence. Sentence onset would create an 454 

evoked response in EEG which temporally overlaps with the subsequent FRPs ( 21 ). The 455 

amplitude of this evoked response is also expected to depend on the number of characters in the 456 

sentence. Hence, sentence onset was modelled by an intercept term and by sentence length to 457

Cluster-based permutation with Threshold-Free-Cluster-Enhancement (TFCE) 462 

To establish statistical significance, betas from the word surprisal, frequency, and oPE predictors 463 

respectively entered into cluster-based permutation tests ( 66 ). One-sample t-tests, comparing 464 

against 0, were conducted for betas from each time point. The t-values were then enhanced with 465 

Threshold-free Cluster Enhancement (TFCE), with parameters E and H set to 2/3 and 2 466 

respectively ( 67 ). The resulting cluster-enhanced TFCE values then entered into a permutation ( n467 

= 10,000 random null-hypotheses permutation) test to identify significant clusters. 468 

Global Field Power (GFP) and Global Map Dissimilarity (GMD) 469 

Since significant clusters do not establish exact effect latencies ( 68 ), we also used Global Field 470 

Power (GFP) and Global Map Dissimilarity (GMD, 28, 69 ) to approximate periods of relatively 471 

strong and stable topographic distributions for each predictor's grand-average betas, thereby 472 

providing additional insights into effect latencies. 473 

GFP and GMD are both topographic-level measures (i.e., computed across all electrodes). GFP 474 

measures the strength of a topographic map as a function of time. It was equivalent to the 475

This restriction reduced the chance of getting spuriously high correlation coefficient. 508 

Importantly, the grand average GFPs of all effects peaked within this range and the same 509

For central effect latency, we first calculated the area and the weighted average position of mass 512 

(i.e., moment) between 0 ms and 400 ms with each jackknife-based GFP waveform. We then 513 

obtained central effect latency by dividing the moment by the area. 514

approximation for degrees of freedom ( 71, 72 ). Post-hoc comparisons were controlled for 519

Analysis on hierarchical orthographic prediction during naturalistic reading 521

Fig. 1. The natural reading task, regression-based fixation-related potentials (rFRPs), and 720 

Orthographic Prediction Error (oPE). (A) During the experimental task, participant read 721 

novel excerpts while their EEG and eye-movements were simultaneously recorded. (B) The EEG 722 

and eye-movements data were synchronized offline based on common triggers between the two 723 

datasets. (C) The fixated word's surprisal, frequency, and oPE (along with other control 724 

variables) were used to predict the EEG data in a regression model. The design matrix was time-725 

expanded so that each of these predictors modelled from 200 ms before and 800 ms after the 726 

fixation events. The resulting beta coefficients from each predictor (rFRP) then entered into 727 

further analysis. (D) oPE is the pixel-by-pixel difference between the prediction image and the 728 

image of the fixated word ( 13 ). The prediction image was derived by averaging 23,854 word 729 

images, thus depicting the "average" word orthographic pattern of the language. When 730 

computing oPE, the image of the fixated word was subtracted from the prediction image which 731 

resulted in a prediction error image. oPE is the sum of all absolute pixel intensities (on a 732 

continuous scale from 0 to 1 where 0 = Black and 1 = White) of the prediction error image. 733
