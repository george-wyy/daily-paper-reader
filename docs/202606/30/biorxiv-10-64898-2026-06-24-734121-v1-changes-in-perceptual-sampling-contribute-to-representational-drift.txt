# Abstract 

Gradual changes in neural response patterns to the same stimulus over time, termed 

representational drift , have been widely observed across cortical areas. Drift is typically attributed to intrinsic neural dynamics driven by synaptic plasticity or turnover. Here we test a complementary hypothesis that drift arises due to systematic changes in behavior such as shifts in attention or gaze. We conducted a longitudinal eye-tracking experiment in which fourteen adults freely viewed naturalistic images across 6 experimental sessions spanning 2–4 weeks, with a subset of images repeated within and across sessions. For each participant, we quantified the similarity of fixation density maps between session pairs using the Wasserstein distance. Fixation patterns became increasingly dissimilar with greater temporal separation between sessions, indicating systematic and directional drift in gaze behavior. To assess whether these behavioral changes could plausibly induce changes in neural representations, we passed fixation-masked images through CORnet-S, a hierarchical deep neural network model of the primate ventral visual stream. Representational distances, quantified as the Frobenius norm of pairwise activation differences, increased with the number of sessions that separated the fixation maps across all four model layers (V1, V2, V4, IT). A kernel-based maximum mean discrepancy test further confirmed that the empirical distribution of representational distances differed significantly from shuffled controls. These findings suggest that small but systematic shifts in the sampling of a visual scene over time are sufficient to cause representational drift in visual cortex. More generally, these results suggest that subtle changes in behavior over time are inevitable, even in simple tasks, and that these changes may be sufficient to drive representational drift in the absence of intrinsic reconfigurations of neural codes. 

# Author summary 

In studies of representational drift in visual cortex, shifts in attention and gaze across repeated stimulus exposures can produce drift-like neural patterns. While the current focus is on contributions of changes in visual sampling to drift, the results raise the possibility that other subtle changes in behavior, many that are hard to quantify, could drive drift in other domains. # Introduction 1

Familiar images, such as Vincent van Gogh’s The Starry Night , can be recognized across 2

many encounters and viewing conditions. This kind of stable perception of the external 3

world is essential for making effective predictions and planning appropriate actions. For 4

decades, this intuition engendered the assumption that neural responses to the same 5

stimulus would remain highly stable over time. However, longitudinal neural recordings 6

have challenged this view: neural responses to repeated presentations of fixed stimuli 7

can change systematically over days and weeks [1 –4]. In a landmark study, Driscoll et al. 8

showed that pyramidal neurons in the posterior parietal cortex gradually shift their 9

preferred spatial locations along a T-maze, even in mice that were already trained to a 10 

near-perfect behavioral performance [1]. Decoders trained on neural data from one day 11 

therefore generalized progressively worse to data recorded on more distant days. This 12 

phenomenon, termed representational drift , raises two related questions. First, what 13 

mechanisms drive these directional changes that cannot be explained by task changes or 14 

random noise? Second, how is the brain able to maintain a stable readout of neural 15 

codes in the presence of drift? Recent work has attempted to address these two 16 

questions, but beyond identifying drift in multiple brain areas such as parietal cortex [1], 17 

olfactory cortex [2], hippocampus [3], and visual cortex [4–7], the answers are still 18 

unclear. 19 

Representational drift in visual cortex is especially striking because early visual areas 20 

provide a comparatively stable, topographically organized foundation for downstream 21 

processing in higher-order cortical areas. However, drift has been observed even in 22 

primary visual cortex (V1), despite the need to consistently relay patterns of low-level 23 

sensory information from the retina and the thalamus. However, drift in visual cortex 24 

has been documented most often in response to complex naturalistic stimuli, such as 25 

movies [4, 5] or natural images [6], while simpler tuning properties (e.g., orientation and 26 

spatial frequency for gratings) can remain stable over long timescales [4, 5, 7]. 27 

A straightforward hypothesis reconciles these findings: complex, naturalistic stimuli 28 

afford richer opportunities for active sampling, and this may be accompanied by subtle 29 

changes in behavior (e.g., eye movements) that are challenging to fully capture when 30 

behavior is summarized at a coarser level. For example, when rodents watch movies, 31 

gaze tracking is often omitted from analyses [5] or summarized only as the average pupil 32 

position in each experimental session [4]. The challenges associated with eye-tracking 33 

arise because, unlike primates, mice lack a fovea and can make non-conjugate eye 34 

movements, so accurate mapping of eye position requires specialized calibration and 35 

geometric modeling [8]. Similarly, if animals explore different image regions as content 36 

becomes familiar, then simply monitoring session-by-session differences in arousal (e.g., 37 

pupil size) may also be insufficient. Therefore, unmeasured behavioral restructuring 38 

could bias neural measurements toward drift-like patterns. Consistent with this concern, 39 

modeling behavioral modulators such as running speed and pupil diameter attenuates 40 

drift in several cortical areas [9]. 41 

Here, we test whether human gaze during repeated free viewing of naturalistic 42 

images changes systematically over experimental sessions, and whether such changes are 43 

capable of inducing drift-like shifts in neural representations. We found that 44 

fixation-density maps became increasingly dissimilar for sessions separated by greater 45 

time intervals. To evaluate the potential impact of these changes on neural codes, each 46 

image was gain-modulated using participant-specific fixation profiles and passed 47 

through a biologically inspired, weight-fixed deep convolutional neural network (DCNN). 48 

Activity patterns in this network exhibited systematic changes over time in all layers, 49 

suggesting that altering perceptual sampling is sufficient to induce drift in 50 

high-dimensional neural networks that mimic cortical information processing systems. 51

# Materials and methods 52 

## Longitudinal free-viewing of naturalistic images 53 

Human participants were recruited in compliance with the Institutional Review Board 54 

(IRB) at the University of California San Diego. All participants provided informed 55 

consent and were compensated 15 USD per hour for participating. Fourteen healthy 56 

adults (2 males and 12 females, mean age = 21.2 ± 2.6) with normal or 57 

corrected-to-normal vision and intact color vision performed a free-viewing task while 58 

being eye-tracked. The study was longitudinal, and participants completed the 59 

experiment in 6 separate sessions over an average span of 21 ± 9.6 days. 60 

No two sessions could be performed on the same day to ensure that gaze-pattern 61 

changes were captured over timescales comparable to those used in previous studies of 62 

representational drift. Subsequent sessions were separated by 4.2 ± 3.1 days. Each 63 

session was divided into 12 separate runs, with each run containing 30 trials. On each 64 

trial, a pseudo-randomly selected naturalistic image was displayed on a computer 65 

monitor, and the participants were allowed to freely view the image for 5 seconds. The 66 

images shown were selected from the DIVerse 2K resolution high quality (DIV2K) 67 

dataset [10], because they are more varied and complex than image sets curated for 68 

object classification purposes, such as CIFAR-100 [11] or ImageNet [12]. All images 69 

were centered and cropped to be of a uniform size (1600 × 1200 pixels, 32.45 × 24.63 70 

degrees visual angle). Different subsets of images were shown across participants. To 71 

ensure participants always started by looking at the center of the image, a new trial did 72 

not begin until gaze was directed within a circle with a 50-pixel radius around a gray 73 

cross-hair that appeared at the center of the screen, which corresponds to approximately 74 

1.05 degrees of visual angle. Once participants fixated on the center of the screen, the 75 

gray cross-hair turned black for 1 second signaling the onset of the next image (Fig 1A). 76 

To investigate whether participants systematically changed how they sampled the 77 

same visual stimulus over time, images were presented repeatedly throughout the 78 

experiment at varying timescales. Out of the 260 unique images that each participant 79 

saw during the entire study, 180 images re-appeared once every two blocks for a total of 80 

six times within a particular session, 60 of them were shown six times once per 81 

individual session, and the remaining 20 images were repeated 6 times both within and 82 

across sessions for a total of thirty-six presentations. Participants were not informed 83 

about the purpose of the study and were only instructed that they could explore the 84 

images freely. At the end of each run, however, they were asked to make an aesthetic 85 

rating on 4 additional images that were presented for 10 seconds each. This filler task 86 

was created to keep participants engaged throughout the study, and data collected from 87 

this aesthetic-judgment task were not used in any subsequent analyses. 88

## Simulation of neural activity in primary visual areas 150 

CORnet-S 151 

To investigate whether longitudinal changes in gaze patterns could induce 152 

representational drift-like variability in a high-dimensional neural system, we passed 153 

images that were masked with the eye-gaze-derived fixation density heatmaps through 154 

the biologically-inspired CORnet-S artificial neural network [16]. Note that we did not 155 

re-train CORnet-S using our image set and we passed our images through the original 156 

pre-trained version of the model. Changes in activation patterns in each layer of the 157 

network were then used as a proxy for representational changes in the visual system. 158 

CORnet-S is a convolutional neural network (CNN) with pseudo-recurrence. It 159 

consists of four modules that are conceptually analogous to visual areas V1, V2, V4 and 160 

IT in the ventral stream, followed by a fully-connected layer for linear classification. 161 

Each module is implemented with a canonical bottleneck circuitry that includes 162 

sequences of convolutions followed by batch normalizations, channel expansions and 163 

compressions, skip connections and a ReLU nonlinearity. Apart from the strictly 164 

feedforward V1 module, activity is passed through each of the other three modules more 165 

than once using shared weights, where the output of the first pass is added back as 166 

input on the next pass, effectively refining the network’s representation at each 167 

additional recurrent step. In this implementation, the V2 and IT layers process 168 

information twice, while the V4 layer processes information four times (Fig 3A). 169 

Although there are many candidate networks, we intentionally selected CORnet-S 170 

for its interpretability and functional similarity to the brain. Despite its comparatively 171 

shallow architecture, the pseudo-recurrent structure in CORnet-S allows it to perform 172 

on par with some of the more classic deep models such as ResNet-50 and VGG19. More 173 

importantly, CORnet-S was the leading model when evaluated against Brain-Score [17], 174 

a composite metric that measures how closely a model’s activity resembles that of the 175 

primate brain when the animals and models perform similar tasks. Since we aimed to 176 

investigate how behaviorally-induced changes can affect neural readouts, CORnet-S 177 

strikes an ideal balance between machine learning performance and biological validity. 178

Quantification of model output 203 

With the model input reflecting how a participant differentially scanned a given image 204 

over time, the final output of each module in CORnet-S is thus a proxy for neural 205 

responses in each visual area. For any given input image, the activation output of each 206 

module p has shape [ Hp × Wp × Kp], where the exact dimensions are determined by the 207 

CORnet-S architecture. Therefore, each unit in that module represents the activation of 208 

a specific feature filter ( k) that is spatially localized to a particular part ( h, w ) of the 209 

image after convolution. To quantify session-by-session changes in network activity, a 210 

pair-wise distance matrix D was calculated, with each of its elements Di,j as the 211 

Euclidean distance between two response vectors rsi and rsj : 212 

Di,j = ∥rsi − rsj ∥ (6) where si and sj are session numbers associated with the empirical eye-tracking data 213 

that were used to modulate input images to the model. 214 

The response vector rs can be defined in two different ways: either as a feature 215 

vector of shape [ Kp × 1] for a particular spatial location ( h, w ), or as a flattened spatial 216 

activation map of shape [( Hp × Wp) × 1] for a select feature filter k. The former allowed 217 

investigation of activity based on each unit’s spatial receptive field, while the latter 218 

allowed investigation of activity based on each unit’s feature selectivity profile. For the 219 

purpose of our study, we were interested in the population-level stability of network 220 

representations, and thus did not analyze the distance matrices individually. We first 221 

min-max normalized each pair-wise distance matrix D derived from different sets of 222 

response vectors rs. We then averaged across all distance matrices to derive a single 223 

network-wide pair-wise distance matrix (Fig 3C). Depending on how the individual 224 

response vectors were defined, we ended up with either a single matrix DRF (pooled 225 

across all spatial receptive fields) or DF F (pooled across all feature selectivities) for 226

Nonparametric test of distributional changes 228 

To determine if pair-wise distance matrices DRF and DF F contained meaningful 229 

internal structure that was distinct from random noise, we used the maximum mean 230 

discrepancy (MMD) as a nonparametric two-sample test statistic for quantifying 231 

differences between distributions [18]. The empirically derived distance matrices served 232 

as one set of distributions, while those derived from resampled gaze data (see 233 

Resampling as null distribution ) served as the other. Thus, the MMD statistic 234 

characterizes the distance in mean embeddings between these two distributions when 235 

projected into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space, with larger values indicating greater 236 

dissimilarity between the two datasets (Fig S4A). For details see Supporting 237

## Resampling as null distribution 239 

In addition to parametric statistical tests, we also used resampling to assess the 240 

significance of the results of all analyses described above (i.e., SGE, EMD and 241 

CORnet-S activation patterns). For each participant and image, we pooled their gaze 242 

trajectories by concatenating both the location and the duration of individual fixations 243 

across all six sessions. We then shuffled these fixations and resampled from the pool, 244 

resulting in a new set of fixation profiles across repeated exposures to a given image 245 

(Fig S1A top). This resampling method ensured that the simulated eye-tracking data 246 

were ecologically valid, as each individual segment of fixation was directly derived from 247 

the empirically recorded gaze positions. In addition, the shuffling component disrupted 248 

any longitudinal trends of shifting fixation patterns, thus providing a baseline to 249 

evaluate against our empirical results. We compared the summary statistics (such as 250 

slope of SGE over sessions) derived from the empirical gaze data to the distribution of 251 

those from the 500 resampled datasets. The p-value from this non-parametric test 252 

determines whether the empirical results are distinguishable from the resampled null. 253 

To determine if the constraints of this resampling method introduced any biases, we 254 

also ran the same non-parametric tests on session-shuffled gaze datasets as a more 255 

conservative null distribution. For details see Supplemental Information: Resampling 256 

method caveats and validation. 257 

# Results 258 

## Stationary gaze entropy decreases as a function of repeated 259 

## image presentation 260 

With repeated presentations of an image across sessions, SGE significantly decreased 261 

(ordinary least squares linear regression, slope=-0.037, p <0.05). Similar effects were 262 

present for images that repeated on a shorter time scale within a single session 263 

(slope=-0.034, p <0.05) and for images that repeated both within and across sessions 264 

(slope=-0.005, p <0.05) (Fig 2A). To ensure that the decrease in SGE was not caused by 265 

participants spending less time fixating on an image towards the end of the experiment 266 

due to looking off-screen or blinking more frequently, we ran the same regression 267 

analysis but only retained trials where participants spent more than 80% of their time 268 

fixating on the image, which accounted for 80.4% of all across-session observations. 269 

After removing trials with lower levels of fixation, the decrease in SGE across sessions 270 

remained statistically significant (slope=-0.047, p <0.05, Fig S3A). A non-parametric 271 

permutation test against the distribution of slopes derived from our resampled gaze 272 

datasets further confirmed that this across-session decrease in SGE in the full dataset 273

Although there was variability across images and participants, the population-level 276

memorize or visually search for specific objects [19, 20]. Under a task-based context, 282 

participants are more likely to consistently scan the most informative parts of the image 283 

to optimize task performance. Our results demonstrate that exploration is also 284 

attenuated even in the absence of clearly defined goals to guide behavior, and that gaze 285 

distributions naturally become more localized over time. 286 

## Gaze distribution shifts systematically over time 287 

To better capture changes in the spatial pattern of eye-movements, we computed the 288 

EMD between fixation density heatmaps across pair-wise sessions for each image and 289 

participant. To compare across observations, we performed min-max normalization on 290 

the computed EMDs within each image before averaging across images and performing 291 

a population-level regression analysis. This approach ensured that the EMD values were 292 

always scaled between 0 and 1 according to the overall distribution of EMDs within any 293 

particular image, preventing images with high EMD values from dominating the results. 294 

On average, the EMD between fixation densities that were separated further apart in 295 

time were larger than those that were closer together, as indicated by a systematic 296 

increase in EMD with increasing separation between sessions (ordinary least squares 297 

linear regression, slope=0.007, p <0.05) (Fig 2C). 298 

Like SGE, the EMD is sensitive to gaze dispersion, but it also captures changes in 299 

the spatial patterns of fixations. Thus, even though changes in SGE and EMD across 300 

sessions should generally be correlated, they can diverge if only certain aspects of gaze 301 

changed over time. For example, if fixations are uniformly dispersed over a fixed area 302 

but the centroid of the fixations gradually shifts in the same direction over time, then 303 

SGE will stay the same but EMD will steadily increase. Our observation that both SGE 304 

and EMD change as a function of separation between sessions is consistent with 305 

directional drift in both the spatial extent of fixations and the center of mass of those 306 

fixations as participants explore increasingly familiar images. 307 

While our regression analysis revealed a significant increase in EMD across sessions 308 

separated further in time, the variance explained is small (R2=0.0012) due to high 309 

variability between observations, potentially because each participant viewed a different 310 

subset of images with varying low-level statistics and semantic complexity. Therefore, 311 

we also ran a non-parametric test by performing the same regression analysis but on 312 

resampled null datasets where longitudinal associations between gaze were disrupted 313 

(see Resampling as null distribution ). We found that of the 500 resampled datasets, 314 

the slope of EMD across separation between sessions was consistently smaller than that 315 

of the slope observed in the intact data, and the null distribution was centered around 316 

zero as expected (resampling-based non parametric test, two-tailed p=0.004, Fig S1C). 317 

Together, these results confirm robust, systematic changes in the gaze patterns over 318 

time. 319

## CORnet-S activations show representational drift-like trends 320 

To test whether systematic drift in the sampling of visual information is sufficient to 321 

induce drift in neural signals, we passed the images through CORnet-S while masking 322 

them using fixation densities derived from the empirical data. Thus, the model received 323 

a modulated visual input where more frequently visited features were emphasized and 324 

less frequently visited were attenuated. We again performed a linear regression on the 325 

Euclidean distances between pairs of network activations to test whether they deviated 326 

more from one another as the input images were modulated by gaze data collected in 327 

further separated experimental sessions. We found that across all network modules, 328 

activations became more dissimilar over time as the network processed images 329 

modulated by the empirical gaze data (Fig 3D; ordinary least squares linear regression, 330 

slope=0.0041 for module V1, p <0.05; 0.0049 for module V2, p <0.05; 0.0055 for module 331 

V4, p <0.05; and 0.0053 for module IT, p <0.05). 332 

We followed up with non-parametric resampling tests, where the same analysis was 333 

run using images modulated by masks generated from the resampled null dataset. In 334 

contrast to the empirical data, network activations did not grow systematically more 335 

dissimilar as a function of difference between sessions when using randomized datasets, 336 

and the slope obtained from the regression analysis was smaller than the slope 337 

associated with the empirical data in all four network modules (resampling-based non 338 

parametric test, two-tailed p < 0.002 for all layers, Fig S2). Furthermore, a 1000-fold 339 

permutation test on the MMD statistic of the flattened Euclidean distance matrices 340 

revealed a statistically significant difference in the representational geometry of network 341 

activations induced by empirical versus resampled gaze-modulated inputs (p <0.05 for 342 

all layers when grouped by receptive fields as in Fig S4B; p <0.05 for V2 and V4, p=0.06 343 

in V1 and p= 0 .13 in IT when grouped by feature filters as in S4C). 344 

The comparisons between empirical and randomized data demonstrate that 345 

systematic changes in gaze behavior can induce directional drift in the activity patterns 346 

of a biologically inspired neural network. 347

# Discussion 348 

Representational drift refers to systematic changes in neural responses to the same 349 

stimulus over days and weeks. Our results show that human gaze patterns do not 350 

remain stable across repeated free viewing of the same stimulus. Instead, SGE decreased 351 

and the EMD between fixation-density maps increased with greater separation between 352 

sessions. While SGE strictly measures the spread of gaze allocation, EMD also accounts 353 

for the spatial distribution of gaze. The observation that both metrics change over time 354 

suggests that fixations became increasingly concentrated and that participants gradually 355 

shifted their gaze toward different parts of the image. Note that this can be achieved in 356 

at least two ways: by exploring a similar set of locations on an image but with higher 357 

precision, or by reducing unnecessary exploration and only fixating on a few highly 358 

salient features. Together, these patterns indicate that this behavioral drift changes the 359 

sensory input received by the visual system over time. Accordingly, when images were 360 

masked using empirically-based fixation-density heatmaps, weight-fixed CORnet-S 361 

activations became increasingly dissimilar across sessions in all four modules (V1, V2, 362 

V4, IT). Because each module maps onto corresponding stages of the primate ventral 363 

stream, systematic changes in input sampling may propagate through hierarchical visual 364 

processing and remain detectable in later, more abstract layers. 365 

A central assumption in many drift studies is that behavior is stable once animals 366 

are over-trained, so neural changes reflect internal representational restructuring. This 367 

interpretation often assumes that sensory input remains constant across sessions. 368 

However, even when task accuracy is stable, behavior can continue to adapt in ways 369 

that do not alter measured performance. In rodent navigation, for example, coarse 370 

body-position tracking can appear stable while finer kinematic variables (head tilt, 371 

whisking frequency) still change [21, 22]. If such behavioral changes are systematic, 372 

corresponding changes in sensory input could propagate through neural systems and 373 

manifest as drift-like neural dynamics, even in areas that are considered distal from 374 

early sensory areas. It is also notable that in the present task, systematic gaze 375 

restructuring emerged even in passive free viewing. Task-driven viewing would likely 376 

amplify this effect, as participants may shift from broad exploration towards efficient 377 

scanning of just the most task-relevant features. After training, gaze may therefore 378 

continue to reorganize even when measured accuracy is stable, providing another source 379 

that could drive neural drift in sensory processing tasks. 380 

When CORnet-S readouts were summarized via feature vectors pooled across space, 381 

baseline dissimilarity between conditions increased in later layers, while the 382 

session-dependent increments in dissimilarity were relatively stable across layers. This 383 

pattern likely reflects nonlinear accumulation of input perturbations in a feedforward 384 

neural network, as local gaze-induced changes in image statistics are amplified when 385 

activity passes through successive modules. This does not necessarily contradict findings 386 

that later layers can be more invariant for classification under some transformations [23], 387 

because representational geometry and classification invariance are distinct properties. 388 

For example, prior fMRI work with eye tracking reported stronger gaze-related 389 

representational differences in early visual cortex than in IT [24]. This divergence from 390 

our simulation results may also reflect the absence of long-range feedback and top-down 391 

attention mechanisms in CORnet-S, which stabilize representations in biological vision. 392 

However, when readouts were instead defined by spatial locations pooled across feature 393 

filters, this layer-wise amplification of baseline dissimilarity was weaker, suggesting that 394

A limitation of the current empirical study is that each participant viewed a different 396

influences both the magnitude and consistency of gaze shifts: sparser scenes may 399 

support fewer stable fixation strategies, whereas denser scenes might encourage 400 

continued refinement of sampling. Future work should use a shared image battery with 401 

controlled variation in complexity to better determine if common factors drive drift 402 

across individuals. 403 

Our modeling approach also has limitations. CORnet-S provides a useful but 404 

indirect proxy for biological responses, and convergent evidence will require 405 

simultaneous neural recording and eye tracking in an animal model system that has 406 

more human-like gaze patterns. We also held model weights fixed, which matches the 407 

absence of explicit task goals that our participants experienced. As a result, we are 408 

unable to disentangle implicit from explicit causes of changes in gaze, which might be 409 

learned passively via repeated exposure or actively due to changes in the perceived 410 

relevance of different parts of an image. However, because drift-like effects were already 411 

present under this conservative weight-fixed model, allowing weight updates would likely 412 

further increase representational change. Finally, masking images with fixation-density 413 

maps preserves where participants looked most often but discards within-trial temporal 414 

structure. Follow-up work could use recurrent dynamics in CORnet-S to examine how 415 

network activity unfolds over the course of each trial with gaze trajectories as input. 416 

The present findings do not argue against the existence of intrinsic representational 417 

drift driven by mechanisms such as synaptic turnover [26], learning-related 418 

plasticity [25, 27, 28], changing internal states [9], and efficient coding [29]. Instead, they 419 

offer a complementary account: systematic changes in how sensory inputs are sampled 420 

can produce drift-like neural patterns without requiring the reorganization of internal 421 

circuitry. Contributions from changes in perceptual sampling may thus help explain why 422 

drift is more readily observed with complex naturalistic stimuli, as such stimuli provide 423 

richer context that encourages more exploration compared to simple stimuli such as 424 

oriented gratings. Although our experiment only tested this hypothesis within the 425 

domain of visual processing, the same argument should apply to other sensory systems, 426 

including multi-sensory and memory-related systems such as the hippocampus. Our 427 

results therefore suggest future studies should employ detailed behavioral monitoring as 428 

a prerequisite, with careful consideration of even subtle changes in behavior that are 429 

challenging to quantify. When such behavioral stability cannot be established, 430 

regressing out behavioral modulators and testing whether drift is attenuated is a critical 431 

control, particularly in tasks involving complex naturalistic stimuli where many 432 

strategies might support equivalent accuracy. 433 

# Conclusion 434 

Our study showed that active sampling of the external environment can change 435 

systematically over time even in the absence of a goal-directed task. Such behavioral 436 

changes can induce perturbations in sensory inputs that increasingly deviate over time 437 

and are amplified as they propagate through a processing hierarchy. The net result is 438 

population-level activity changes that resemble directional representational drift but is 439 

purely driven by changes in patterns of input. Future studies will need to take into 440 

account potential longitudinal changes in behavior when investigating the properties of 441 

neural code re-structuring in the context of studying representational drift. Most 442 

importantly, our results further suggest that drift is a normal property of neural codes, 443 

as non-stationary sampling of the environment occurs in natural vision and these 444

## Resampling method caveats and validation 470 

One limitation of our primary resampling method is that we cannot preserve both the 471 

number of fixation segments and the total fixation duration to be strictly the same as 472 

the empirical data. For example, if a participant had only a single 4-second long 473 

fixation when viewing an image in one session, this fixation segment might be 474 

re-assigned to another session and concatenated with other segments to create a fixation 475 

trajectory that exceeds the 5-second trial duration. Theoretically, this doesn’t affect 476
