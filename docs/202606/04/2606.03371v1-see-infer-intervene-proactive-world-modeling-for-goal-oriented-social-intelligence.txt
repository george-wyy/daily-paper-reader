## See, Infer, Intervene: Proactive World Modeling for Goal-Oriented Social Intelligence 

Honghui Zhang 1, Chenmeinian Guo 1, Yichen Yu 1, Guanyu Liu 1, Yongming Qin 1,Chongguo Song 1, Mengyue Yang 2, Lei Yu 3, Tianyu Shi 41Mita Technology 2University of Bristol 3University of Toronto 4McGill University

Abstract 

Multimodal retail agents should not only recog-nize what a customer is doing, but also decide whether and how to assist before an explicit re-quest is made. We study this setting through the See–Infer–Intervene (SII) framework, where a device must see pre-interaction behavior, in-fer latent customer intent, and act by selecting an appropriate service intervention or choos-ing to wait. We instantiate SII with the Proac-tive Intent World Model (PIWM), which rep-resents customer state with AIDA (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action) purchasing phases and BDI (belief, desire, intention) psychological fields, predicts action-conditioned intent tran-sitions, and selects from five response classes: GREET , E LICIT , I NFORM , R ECOMMEND , and HOLD . We further construct GuidanceSales-Bench, a smart-retail benchmark containing state manifests, pre-interaction videos, candi-date responses, action-conditioned outcomes, and best-action labels. When conditioned on ground-truth customer state to isolate action se-lection, PIWM achieves 0.641 macro F1 on 30 held-out target videos, outperforming a zero-shot Qwen2.5-VL-7B baseline and training variants without balanced action supervision; end-to-end video-only selection drops to 0.295, below the 5-class balanced random baseline of 0.414, identifying video-to-state grounding as the dominant deployment-time bottleneck. A preliminary staged real-store pilot (recorded with paid participants performing scripted cus-tomer behaviors) reaches 0.579 action macro F1 on 20 fully annotated videos, with 10 ad-ditional accessible videos released with index-level labels. 

1 Introduction 

Intelligent retail systems are increasingly expected to provide assistance before customers issue ex-plicit requests (Deng et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2025). This setting differs from conventional command-driven interaction: a customer may pause in front

of a device, compare products, shift gaze across shelves, approach the screen, or hesitate before pur-chase, but none of these behaviors is a click, scan, or verbal query. A system that waits passively can miss the service window, whereas a system that intervenes too early can interrupt the customer or create sales pressure (Bailey et al., 2000, 2001). The central problem is therefore not only recogni-tion, but proactive decision making: from a short pre-interaction observation, the agent must infer the customer’s state and decide whether an inter-vention would be helpful (Novick and Sutton, 1997; Horvitz, 1999). Current multimodal models(Lin et al., 2024; Maaz et al., 2024) are strong observers but are not designed as proactive retail agents(Mathur et al., 2024). Given an image or video, they can describe visible behavior or answer questions, yet this de-scriptive ability does not directly specify what the device should do next. Proactive assistance re-1

quires three additional capabilities: modeling latent customer intent beyond visible motion, comparing multiple candidate responses, and predicting how each response may change the customer’s future state. Without this action-conditioned reasoning, a model may produce plausible descriptions while still choosing an intrusive or mistimed service ac-tion(Yao et al., 2022b). We organize this problem as an SII framework: a device should see customer behavior, infer latent in-tent, and intervene only after evaluating candidate responses. The key technical view is to treat proac-tive retail assistance as intent-level world modeling. World models predict future states under candidate actions (Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019), and recent World Action Models connect state prediction with action generation (Wang et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2026). However, existing work mainly targets physical control, where actions are motor commands and outcomes are physical state changes. In retail interaction, actions are commu-nicative service moves, and the relevant outcomes are changes in intent, uncertainty, receptiveness, and purchase readiness. We instantiate SII with the Proactive Intent World Model (PIWM) . Given a pre-interaction observation, PIWM estimates a structured cus-tomer state, predicts the outcome of each candi-date device response, and selects the best response from a constrained intervention set. The state combines AIDA purchasing phases with BDI psy-chological fields (Barry, 1987; Rao et al., 1995). The action space contains five device responses: GREET , E LICIT , I NFORM , R ECOMMEND , and HOLD . Treating H OLD as an action is important be-cause non-intervention can be the correct decision when the customer is browsing smoothly. To train and evaluate this formulation, we con-struct GuidanceSalesBench , a smart-retail bench-mark that records the structured decision process rather than only the observed scene (Rouhi et al., 2026; Singh et al., 2016). Each instance links a customer state manifest, a pre-interaction video prompt and video, an AIDA-conditioned candi-date response set, action-conditioned outcomes, and a best-action label. Experiments on Target-Test (n=30, 30 held-out target-domain videos) show that PIWM reaches 0.641 macro F1 for best-action se-lection when given the ground-truth customer-state representation, compared with 0.313 for Qwen2.5-VL-7B (zero-shot) and 0.390 for a Small-Scale Action Training variant; without oracle state, the Target-Test E2E setting reaches 0.295. We addi-tionally evaluate transfer on a real-store pilot: 30 accessible paid-participant videos are released as sparse frames, 20 have full session-level annota-tions for the primary sim-to-real metric, and 10 have index-level labels only. Our contributions are threefold. First, we formu-late multimodal retail marketing as an SII prob-lem that requires seeing customer behavior, in-ferring intent, and acting through low-intrusion response selection. Second, we propose PIWM, an intent-level world model that predicts action-conditioned customer-state transitions over an AIDA–BDI schema and a five-class device action space. Third, we introduce GuidanceSalesBench and an evaluation protocol for state prediction, out-come simulation, and proactive action selection. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 World Models and World Action Models 

World models learn environment representations and transition dynamics for prediction, planning, and policy learning. Ha and Schmidhuber (Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018) learn a latent model that compresses visual observations and predicts fu-ture latent states with recurrent dynamics, while Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2019) learns behaviors from imagined latent trajectories. Recent World Action Models further combine dynamics model-ing with action generation (Wang et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2026), targeting embodied AI, robotic ma-nipulation, autonomous driving, and other physical control tasks. PIWM also uses action-conditioned future sim-ulation, but differs in task grounding. Vision-language-action systems such as RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023), OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024), and 

π0 (Black et al., 2024) map observations and in-structions to motor actions. PIWM instead targets communicative service interventions in a single-customer retail setting, where success is best re-sponse selection under customer-state constraints and non-intervention is explicitly represented as an action. 

2.2 Proactive Interaction and Mixed-Initiative Systems 

Proactive interaction studies systems that initiate assistance before explicit user requests(Bi et al., 2024). Related work includes proactive dialogue systems(Yan et al., 2023), context-aware agents, 2and mixed-initiative interaction, where control is shared between user and system (Novick and Sut-ton, 1997). A key challenge is timing: assistance can reduce uncertainty or effort, but poorly timed interventions may interrupt ongoing tasks or in-crease cognitive load (Bailey et al., 2000). PIWM formalizes this timing problem as struc-tured selection over simulated intervention out-comes. Rather than directly triggering an ac-tion from the current context, PIWM predicts the next customer state under each candidate response and assigns a relative action score. Compared with broad proactive assistants such as ContextA-gent (Yang et al., 2025), PIWM targets a narrower but more decision-critical setting: selecting which low-intrusion service move should be used for a third-party customer observed from retail video. 

2.3 Intent Modeling in Human-Centered Interaction 

Intent recognition typically infers user goals from text, speech, behavior sequences, or visual cues(Rabinowitz et al., 2018). In retail environ-ments, intent is rarely explicit and must be inferred from dwelling, gaze movement, comparison, ap-proaching, hesitation, and device operation. We represent customer intent with AIDA for macro-level purchasing phase (Barry, 1987) and BDI for micro-level psychological state (Rao et al., 1995). Unlike static intent recognition, PIWM uses in-tent as a transition state for action-conditioned sim-ulation. The model predicts how candidate inter-ventions change the customer’s purchasing phase and psychological state, making intent representa-tion part of the decision process rather than only an output label. 

2.4 Retail Behavior Understanding and Smart Retail Datasets 

Retail behavior understanding has been stud-ied through shopping action recognition, item-interaction detection, trajectory analysis, and multi-view video understanding. The MERL Shopping Dataset focuses on behaviors such as browsing, picking up items, and returning items (Singh et al., 2016), while PRISM provides richer multi-view retail environments for embodied vision-language understanding (Rouhi et al., 2026). These datasets primarily describe what cus-tomers do or what occurs in the scene. Shopping-agent benchmarks such as WebShop (Yao et al., 2022a) and Shopping MMLU (Jin et al., 2024) focus on product navigation or knowledge reason-ing. PIWM targets a different decision layer: how a smart retail device should intervene given an observed customer state, so GuidanceSalesBench includes candidate interventions, predicted next states, relative action scores, and best-action labels.

4.2 Outcome Modeling and Selection 

For each candidate response, hϕ predicts the next AIDA phase, the next BDI state, and a score from 1 to 5. This differs from direct action classifica-tion, which estimates p(a | Vt) or p(a | st) without modeling intervention-specific consequences(Ahn et al., 2022). PIWM instead exposes the interme-diate future state under each candidate and then selects the candidate whose outcome receives the highest score. The selector π is deterministic: it returns the candidate with score 5. Low-intrusion behavior is encoded through the candidate inventory and scor-ing rubric rather than an additional hand-designed cost function. For example, H OLD can receive the highest score when the predicted intervention would disturb smooth browsing, while E LICIT or INFORM can be preferred when the customer shows uncertainty or an information gap. PIWM is best understood as a world-model-augmented proactive action policy. The intent-level world-model objective hϕ shapes the policy during training by supervising state estimation and action-conditioned intent transitions, while the learned policy remains the strongest decision-time com-ponent in the current system. Our empirical anal-ysis (Section 6.5) shows that direct action selec-tion consistently outperforms inference-time coun-terfactual planning over predicted outcomes; we therefore retain the structured formulation in Equa-tions (4)–(6) primarily because it (1) supports the dataset construction protocol (Section 5), (2) en-ables intermediate-state diagnostics (Appendix B), and (3) provides a foundation for future work where stronger next-state prediction could make decision-time planning competitive. 4Figure 2: Overview of GuidanceSalesBench construction and PIWM training. 5 GuidanceSalesBench Construction 

GuidanceSalesBench provides supervision for state estimation, outcome prediction, and response selec-tion. It is constructed through two linked chains: seed → manifest → prompt → video , (7) manifest → candidate actions → outcomes 

→ scores → best action . (8) Manifests define customer states, prompts and videos provide pre-interaction visual inputs, and labeled outcomes provide action-conditioned su-pervision. 

5.1 State and Video Generation 

Each instance starts from a natural-language seed describing a retail scenario. We use schema-constrained structured generation to produce a man-ifest containing the customer persona, visual ap-pearance, AIDA phase, BDI state, observable be-havior, facial expression, posture, and a 10-second timeline. In dataset construction, this generator instantiates fθ; in experiments, the same schema becomes the target for video or text-based state prediction. Each manifest is rendered into a 10-second video prompt. The prompt describes only the pre-interaction state and excludes candidate actions, scores, and best-action labels, preventing the gen-erated video from leaking the target response.

6 Experiments 

We evaluate whether the SII formulation improves proactive retail action selection. The experiments answer three questions: whether target-domain ac-tion training improves response selection, whether AIDA-conditioned candidate filtering is useful, and where the remaining errors arise. 

6.1 Experimental Setup 

The main GuidanceSalesBench evaluation contains Target-Test (n=30) in the smart vending-machine setting, with 6 examples for each action class. The 30 test samples are stratified to achieve balanced best-action class distribution while preserving an AIDA stage distribution representative of the tar-get deployment (Appendix A). Each example is manually QA-checked and represented by three sparsely sampled frames. We report macro F1 over the five action classes, which avoids allowing fre-quent actions to dominate the score. The real-store pilot is reserved for transfer evaluation and is not used for training; its 20 fully annotated videos form the primary sim-to-real metric, and 10 additional index-labeled videos are released as auxiliary real data. Training has two stages. The first stage trains the shared LoRA adapter on 2,544 multitask samples derived from 543 general retail scenes, covering customer understanding and action consequence prediction. The second stage continues training on 190 balanced target-domain action-selection sam-ples. These 190 samples combine 71 original tar-get examples, 15 Greet augmentations, and 104 balanced samples from the general pool to reduce action-class imbalance. Each main training run takes approximately 9 A100-hours on A100 80GB hardware; full configuration details (LoRA hyper-parameters, optimizer, decoding) are provided in Appendix E. 

6.2 Tasks and Metrics 

We evaluate four linked tasks in the PIWM pipeline. AIDA prediction measures whether the model identifies the customer’s sales-funnel stage. In-tent prediction measures whether it captures core customer intent. Next-state prediction measures whether it predicts how a candidate action changes the customer’s state. Best-action prediction mea-sures whether it selects the correct response among GREET , E LICIT , I NFORM , R ECOMMEND , and HOLD .Our primary metric is macro F1 for best-action selection on Target-Test (n=30). We additionally report per-action F1 and collapse H OLD versus the other four actions to evaluate the binary interven-tion decision. 

6.3 Baselines 

We compare PIWM against Random Baseline and Constant-Action Baselines, Qwen2.5-VL-7B (zero-shot) without project-specific training, the State-Outcome Model trained only on customer state and action consequences, Small-Scale Action Training variants, and three closed-source baselines evalu-ated through the same role-separated prompts. To assess transfer from synthetic training data to real footage, we evaluate PIWM on a small staged real-store pilot recorded by paid employee partici-pants enacting scripted customer-behavior scenar-ios. This paper studies multimodal retail marketing as an SII problem: a device must see customer be-havior, infer latent intent, and intervene before the customer makes an explicit request. We in-stantiate this view with PIWM, an intent-level world-model-augmented proactive action policy that learns AIDA–BDI customer state estimation and action-conditioned intent transition predic-tion during training, and selects from an AIDA-conditioned response set at decision time. Experi-ments on held-out target videos show that balanced action supervision and candidate constraints sub-stantially improve best-action macro F1. Specif-ically, PIWM reaches 0.641 macro F1 on Target-Test ( n = 30 ) with ground-truth state, 0.295 in the end-to-end video-only setting, and 0.579 on a 20-video staged real-store pilot, with the world-model training framework providing a substantial gap over the State-Outcome baseline (0.240). We also introduce GuidanceSalesBench, a struc-tured benchmark that links pre-interaction obser-vations with state manifests, candidate interven-tions, predicted next states, relative action scores, and best-action labels. Comparisons with closed-source models show a mixed but informative pic-ture: PIWM is strongest on Cross-Domain oracle selection, while larger models remain stronger in some E2E and real-store settings. The current for-mulation focuses on short single-customer clips and a finite set of high-level device responses. Ex-tending PIWM to longer interactions, richer multi-modal device behaviors, and online feedback from real deployments is an important direction for fu-ture work. 

Limitations 

PIWM has several limitations. 1. Oracle-state dependence. The strongest best-action result conditions on ground-truth cus-tomer state to isolate response selection. End-to-end video-only performance drops from 0.641 to 0.295, showing that video-to-state grounding remains the main bottleneck. 2. Closed-source models remain stronger in some settings. PIWM is competitive on oracle-state action selection, but it does not uniformly exceed larger closed-source sys-tems: Gemini 2.5 Flash is slightly higher on Target-Test oracle, GPT-4o is stronger in E2E selection, and Claude Sonnet 4.6 is stronger on the small real-store pilot. 3. Non-intervention remains difficult. HOLD 

is the weakest class on Target-Test, indicating that deciding when not to intervene is harder than choosing among active service actions. Larger H OLD -rich evaluation sets are needed to measure low-intrusion behavior more reli-ably. 4. The real-store pilot is preliminary. The Real-Store Pilot uses staged recordings from paid employee participants and only 20 fully annotated videos. The subset contains no HOLD cases, so real-world non-intervention behavior and naturalistic customer variation remain open evaluation targets. 

Ethics Statement 

This work involves video recordings of human participants performing retail customer behaviors and computational modeling of inferred customer states. We address ethical considerations as fol-lows. 

Data sources and participant recruitment. The synthetic GuidanceSalesBench training data is gen-erated entirely by text-to-video models from sce-nario seeds and contains no real individuals. The real-video evaluation set (Real-Store Pilot, n = 20 

fully annotated plus 10 group-labeled videos) was collected through a collaborating retailer. Record-ings were performed by paid employees of the retail partner, working from scripted customer-behavior scenarios that the research team prepared in advance. Participants were not actual store cus-tomers, and the videos are staged enactments rather than naturalistic observation of real customer be-havior. Approximately 5–10 individual employees participated, with each individual performing mul-tiple scripted scenarios. 

Informed consent and compensation. All par-ticipants were informed in advance that record-ings would be used for research on proactive retail assistance, including the development of vision-language models for customer-state inference and action selection. Participants signed release forms covering both the use of their image in research and the public release of the recorded materials. Participants were compensated for their time. We 8do not release any biographical information, and in-dividual identification beyond what is visible in the released video frames is not retained or distributed. 

Ethics review. Formal IRB-style ethics review was not conducted; the project followed the com-pliance practices of the collaborating retail part-ner, including the release-form procedure described above. We acknowledge this as a limitation: future iterations of this work, particularly any deployment study involving real customers, will require formal ethics-board approval. 

Data release. The synthetic GuidanceSales-Bench dataset, including all customer manifests, candidate actions, and action-conditioned out-comes, will be released publicly under an open license upon acceptance. The 30 real-store videos and the frame-and-annotation subset used for sim-to-real evaluation will also be released, in accor-dance with the participant release forms. Code and data release links are provided in Data and Code Availability.

(non-intervention), explicitly to discourage over-intervention. Nevertheless, deploying proactive sales systems may exert commercial pressure on customers, particularly when intervention is trig-gered before explicit interest. We caution that any deployment of PIWM-like systems should: (1) pre-serve the customer’s ability to ignore or dismiss interventions, (2) avoid leveraging psychological vulnerabilities such as decision fatigue or indeci-sion, and (3) provide transparency that the customer is interacting with an AI-driven assistant rather than human staff. 

Intent inference and psychological profiling. 

PIWM infers AIDA stage and BDI (belief/desire/in-tention) state from short pre-interaction observa-tions. These inferences are intentionally coarse-grained—four AIDA classes and short schema-constrained BDI fields—and serve as intermediate signals for action selection rather than as persistent customer profiles. We do not retain inferred state across sessions or link inferred state to identity in our evaluation setup; any downstream deployment must commit to the same constraint. 

Demographic representativeness. Our real-video set covers a limited demographic range, draw-ing from the employees available at one retail part-ner. We have not evaluated PIWM for fairness across gender, age, ethnicity, or disability. Broader fairness evaluation is required before any deploy-ment and is left as future work. 

Compute footprint. Each main PIWM training run consumes approximately 9 A100-hours on A100 80GB hardware. The complete project (main run plus four ablations) totals approximately 45 A100-hours, substantially below the pretraining cost of foundation vision-language models.

E Reproducibility 

Training configuration. All trained PIWM vari-ants use Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct as the base vision-language model and ms-swift as the fine-tuning framework. The main checkpoint is trained with LoRA adaptation ( tuner_type=lora ) us-ing rank 16, alpha 32, dropout 0.05, and no LoRA bias. The ms-swift configuration specifies 

target_modules=all-linear ; the saved PEFT adapter materializes this over the Qwen projec-tion and MLP modules ( q_proj , k_proj , v_proj ,

o_proj , gate_proj , up_proj , and down_proj 

under model.* ). The optimizer is AdamW (adamw_torch ) with learning rate 2×10 −5, weight decay 0.1, β1 = 0 .9, β2 = 0 .95 , cosine learning-rate schedule, max gradient norm 1.0, no warmup (warmup_steps=0 , warmup_ratio=0.0 ), bf16 pre-cision, gradient checkpointing, per-device batch size 4, gradient accumulation 2, and 3 training epochs. The random seed and data seed are both 42. 

Data and schedule. The main PIWM train-ing file is the joint Stage-1 plus Stage-2 ms-swift file with 2,734 rows: 543 user-intent rows, 2,001 next-state-prediction rows, and 190 balanced action-selection rows. The archived run arguments record max_length=8192 and 

max_pixels=1003520 . Stage-1-only variants use the same user-intent and next-state objectives with-out the balanced action-selection rows. Zero-shot Qwen2.5-VL-7B is evaluated without loading a LoRA adapter. 

Hardware and compute. The project used an A100 80GB server pool. Each main training run is budgeted at approximately 9 A100-hours (A100 80GB) using ms-swift LoRA fine-tuning. Counting the main PIWM run plus four ablations gives an estimated total of about 45 A100-hours. 

Inference configuration. All local checkpoint evaluations use greedy decoding. In scripts/ eval_ms_swift_checkpoint.py , generation calls model.generate(..., do_sample=False) 

with default max_new_tokens=256 . In scripts/ eval_e2e_decision_loop_checkpoint.py ,perception, deliberation, and action calls also use 

do_sample=False and default to 128 new tokens for each step. The ms-swift checkpoint metadata stores temperature=0.0 and num_beams=1 . Parse failures are counted as errors in strict metrics unless a table explicitly states a parsed-output convention. 

Frame sampling. The standard input contains three sparse frames per video. The frame extrac-tor uses a cue-timeline sampling plan with default 13 timestamps at 2.0s ( cue_onset ), 5.0s ( cue_peak ), and 8.0s ( cue_resolution ) when a prompt does not provide a custom frame_sampling_plan . Ex-tracted frames are stored as 000.jpg , 001.jpg , and 

002.jpg , and the prompt presents them in chrono-logical order.

G Failure Case Analysis 

We summarize three representative PIWM errors on Target-Test (n=30). We report them as short qualitative cases rather than a wide table because the useful information is the error pattern, not the sample identifier. 

HOLD misread as ELICIT . In sample 

target_piwm_810 , the gold action is H OLD , while PIWM predicts E LICIT . The customer is in the interest stage with intent explore_options ; the visible evidence shows steady relaxed scanning without confusion or help-seeking gestures. PIWM treats sustained engagement as an information-need signal, whereas the gold annotation treats it as autonomous exploration that should not be interrupted. This is the main over-intervention pattern. 

RECOMMEND misread as I NFORM . In sam-ple target_piwm_760 , the gold action is R ECOM -

MEND , while PIWM predicts I NFORM . The cus-tomer repeatedly looks at the same item and shows minor anxious behavior, such as lightly rubbing the phone edge. PIWM defaults to information provi-sion under uncertainty cues, but the gold annotation interprets the behavior as readiness to commit and expects a soft confirming recommendation. 

GREET misread as HOLD . In sample 

target_piwm_721 , the gold action is G REET ,14 while PIWM predicts H OLD . The customer faces the terminal and raises a hand to operate it. PIWM applies a conservative non-intervention heuristic because the customer is already acting, but the gold annotation expects a lightweight availability cue. This boundary case highlights the difficulty of distinguishing “do not interrupt” from “briefly acknowledge presence.”
