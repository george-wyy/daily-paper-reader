## 摘要
适应性决策依赖于分别支持价值学习和探索性决策策略的多巴胺能和去甲肾上腺素能系统，但它们的贡献在非侵入性方式下仍难以分离。在这里，我们研究了瞳孔动态是否能为跨感觉模态的基于价值的决策背后的计算过程提供可分离的读数，可能反映不同的神经调节过程。人类参与者执行一项动态觅食任务，他们根据估计的价值在听觉、视觉或视听选项之间进行选择，同时执行一项指令选择的控制任务。基于价值的任务中的行为被概率选择模型很好地捕捉，揭示了奖励历史的自适应整合以及探索与利用之间的平衡。瞳孔反应揭示了决策策略和价值更新的时间特异性计算特征。反应时间与整个决策过程中持续的瞳孔扩张相关，而选择和未选择选项之间的价值差异则选择性地调节了刺激评估和反馈后的瞳孔反应。这些发现分别与探索-利用行为的去甲肾上腺素能调节和多巴胺能价值更新相关的计算过程一致。在基于价值的决策中，这两种效应显著强于指令决策，表明当选择依赖于学习到的奖励价值时，这些计算过程的参与增强。重要的是，这些效应在很大程度上与模态无关，表明计算变量的领域通用编码。总之，这些发现将瞳孔动态识别为适应性决策背后不同计算阶段的时间敏感且非侵入性标记，并建立了一个将瞳孔测量法与价值学习和不确定性处理的神经调节理论联系起来的框架。

## Abstract
Adaptive decision making depends on dopaminergic and noradrenergic systems supporting value learning and exploratory decision strategies, respectively, yet their contributions remain difficult to dissociate non-invasively. Here, we investigated whether pupil dynamics provide dissociable readouts of computational processes underlying value-based decision making across sensory modalities, potentially reflecting distinct neuromodulatory processes. Human participants performed a dynamic foraging task in which they chose between auditory, visual, or audio-visual options based on their estimated value, alongside a control task where choices were instructed. Behavior in the value-based task was well captured by a probabilistic choice model, revealing adaptive integration of reward history and a balance between exploration and exploitation. Pupil responses revealed temporally distinct computational signatures of decision strategy and value updating. Reaction time was associated with sustained pupil dilation throughout the decision process, whereas value differences between chosen and unchosen options selectively modulated pupil responses during stimulus evaluation and following feedback. These findings are consistent with computational processes linked to noradrenergic regulation of exploration-exploitation behavior and dopaminergic value updating, respectively. Both effects were significantly stronger during value-based than instructed decisions, indicating enhanced engagement of these computational processes when choices depended on learned reward values. Importantly, these effects were largely modality-independent, indicating a domain-general encoding of computational variables. Together, these findings identify pupil dynamics as a temporally sensitive and non-invasive marker of distinct computational stages underlying adaptive decision making and establish a framework for linking pupillometry to neuromodulatory theories of value learning and uncertainty processing.
