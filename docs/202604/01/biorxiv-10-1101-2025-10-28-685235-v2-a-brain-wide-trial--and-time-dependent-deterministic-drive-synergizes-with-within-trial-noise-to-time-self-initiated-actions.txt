# Summary 12 

Deciding when to act in the absence of external cues is essential for exploration, learning, 13 and survival. Yet the neural mechanisms underlying such decisions remain controversial, with 14 current views favoring either deterministic or stochastic underpinnings. We simultaneously recorded 15 from large neuronal populations in cortical, thalamic, pallidal, and cerebellar regions as mice self-16 initiated voluntary actions. Action onset timing was predictable from firing patterns up to several 17 seconds in advance with predictions correlated across regions, demonstrating a prominent 18 

deterministic drive that spans regions. Computational modeling indicated that this drive has an 19 

initial value and rate that vary trial-by-trial, and the rate increases within trials. Although the 20 

deterministic drive is sufficient to trigger action, noise within trials also contributes to setting action 21 

timing. Therefore, discrete (across-trial) and continuous (within-trial) sources of variability synergize 22 

to time self-initiated actions. This synergy is observed brain-wide, suggesting a distributed decision-23 

making process rather than a hierarchical, modular one. 24 

# Introduction 25 

The adaptive value of cognition manifests not only in deciding how to act, but also when to 26 act. For instance, prey increase their chances of survival by fleeing at a moment predator s cannot 27 predict, rather than waiting for predators to give chase 1,2 . This raises a fundamental question: what 28 neural mechanisms underlie action timing decisions in the absence of external cues? Seminal 29 studies in humans over 60 years ago established the existence of the “readiness potential”, a slow 30 buildup in electroencephalographic recordings (EEG) that precedes the onset of self-initiated 31 actions by up to several seconds 3-5 . Correlates of the readiness potential have since been found 32 across numerous mammalian species 6-9 , and even in  fish 10  and invertebrates 11,12 , perhaps reflecting 33 a broadly conserved neural dynamics 13,14 .34 

The apparent monotonicity of the readiness potential, and classic experiments suggesting 35 the potential begins before subjects are aware of their desire to act 4,15-18 , led to the claim that its onset 36 

reflects the decision to act, with the buildup thereafter proceeding deterministically toward action 37 initiation 3,7,14,19-22 . However, more recent studies have challenged this classic interpretation by 38

Accordingly, 39 the monotonicity of the readiness potential has been reinterpreted as an artifact of trial-averaging – 40 a method commonly used with noninvasive recording techniques that yield a low signal-to-noise 41 ratio 29 . Indeed, if action initiation requires crossing an activity threshold 7,14 , averaging neural activity 42 aligned on action onset will produce an approximately monotonic trajectory, even if the underlying 43 

activity is dominated by random fluctuations on individual trials. 44 

This alternative, stochastic interpretation has been formalized using drift-d iffusion models 45 (DDM), which were previously used to describe perceptual decision-making 30-36 . DDMs combine 46 contributions from deterministic and stochastic components in a balance that, in perceptually 47 guided tasks, varies depending on the strength of sensory evidence : the stronger the sensory 48 evidence, the larger the deterministic component 32 . In the case of self-initiated actions, the 49 deterministic component was assumed to be minor, insufficient on its own to reach the activity 50 threshold beyond which an action is initiated 23,37 . Conversely, the stochastic component, thought to 51 derive from random fluctuations in the firing rates of neurons underlying the decision 38,39 , was posited 52 to play a prominent role, being necessary to reach the activity threshold and thereby setting action 53 timing 14 . This view well accounts for the variable and seemingly unpredictable timing of self-initiated 54 actions. Nevertheless, other models not involving stochastic fluctuations within trials can also 55 account for this timing variability, such as a deterministic process that starts at random times or 56 whose parameters vary randomly from trial to trial. Here the timing decision is made whenever a pre-57 movement buildup starts, consistent with the classic interpretation of the readiness potential. 58 Without measurements that resolve the decision-related neural dynamics on individual trials, the 59 predictions of these conflicting models appear equally plausible 37 , leaving the field at a conceptual 60 impasse. 61 

Compounding matters , the contributions of different brain regions to action timing decisions 62 remain poorly resolved. Activity modulation preceding the onset of self-initiated actions has been 63 observed in numerous cortical and subcortical regions 40-48 . Lesions 49-58 and other activity 64 perturbations 59-64 in some regions alter action timing. Previous studies have promoted a hierarchical 65 view, in which timing decisions rely on distinct modular contributions from different regions carried 66 out in sequence 16,40,45-47,59,61,65,66 . In line with this, functional imaging in humans has shown a 67 continuous sequence of activations across different cortical regions as action onset approaches 16 .68 

However, this interpretation of hierarchical, modular contributions is based on activity 69 measurements with low temporal resolution or that involve a limited number of neurons recorded in 70 one or two regions at a time. Thus, it is possible that self-initiated action decisions instead rely on a 71 shared, distributed process to which many brain regions simultaneously contribute. 72 

To address these issues, we resolved single-unit, single-trial dynamics across large neuronal 73 populations in eight brain regions simultaneously, in mice making self-initiated action decisions. We 74 demonstrate that action onset timing is predictable up to several seconds prior to onset from neural 75 activity. This predictive activity reflects a prominent deterministic drive that is temporally aligned 76 across cortical, thalamic, pallidal, and cerebellar regions. By developing a DDM variant that 77 accounts for the decision process at a single trial resolution, we show that the deterministic drive is 78 

parametrized differently across trials , contributing to action timing variability, and is on its own 79 

sufficient to trigger action. Yet, in silico ablation analyses reveal that within-trial noise also 80 contributes to action timing and its variability across trials. The same synergistic decision process is 81

# Results 87 

A new behavioral paradigm to elicit self-initiated action decisions in mice 88 

Self-initiated decisions have typically been elicited in human subjects by asking them to 89 initiate a motor action “whenever they wish” with no experimental constraints that reinforce a 90 

specific timing 3,4,15,16 . To study such decisions in mice, we leveraged a behavioral paradigm we 91 recently developed in which mice perform a naturalistic climbing behavior in the absence of external 92 cues 67 . Here, head -fixed mice climb with all four limbs across handholds that extend radially from a 93 wheel positioned in front of them, thereby rotating the wheel (Figure 1A). In the original version of the 94 task, mice can initiate climbing at any time and are rewarded after climbing. This self-paced 95 paradigm therefore implicitly reinforces a rapid succession of climbing bouts that maximizes the 96 

reward rate. To elicit actions whose timing is not influenced by external factors like the pursuit of 97 rewards, we developed a variant of this task. 98 

Our task variant consists of alternating “GO” and “NO-GO” periods, which are 99 distinguishable by the presence or absence of sensory cues. When no movement occurs, both 100 periods last 3 seconds on average (exact lengths are jittered). If the mouse climbs during a GO period, 101 a reward is delivered, the period ends and a NO-GO period begins. If the mouse climbs during a NO-102 GO period, the period timer is reset, delaying the start of the next GO period. Climbing onset times 103 were computed using video-based limb tracking 68 during epochs surrounding the onset of wheel 104 rotation (Figure 1B). All animals trained on this task learned to climb promptly after the start of a GO 105 period (Figures S1A-S1B). All trained animals also occasionally initiated climbing during NO-GO 106 periods (Figure S1C), with an average of 75 ± 27 (mean ± sem) NO-GO climbing bouts per session. 107 Analysis of anticipatory licking behavior indicated that mice are less likely to expect a reward during 108 NO-GO periods than during GO periods (Figure S1D). This argues against the possibility that climbing 109 bouts occurring during NO-GO periods are reward-motivated errors, wherein mice mistake a NO-GO 110 period for a GO period. Thus, NO-GO climbing bouts do not appear reward-driven but self-initiated. 111 We focus on these events in what follows. 112

Self-initiated action timing is predictable from spiking activity 129 

We next sought to examine spiking activity across the brain that precedes self-initiated 130 climbing onsets. Four large-scale multielectrode arrays (Neuropixels 1.0 69 ) were acutely implanted 131 at the beginning of climbing sessions (six mice, one recording session per mouse). We targeted a 132 range of brain regions in the left hemisphere that have previously been implicated in self-initiated 133 action timing, or which receive direct projections from implicated regions (Figures 1C-1F, S1E and 134 S1F, Table S1 ): the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) 46,48,59,61 , the forelimb primary motor cortex 135 (M1) 6,8,20,70 , the forelimb secondary motor cortex (M2) 25,46 , the mediodorsal nucleus of the thalamus 136 (MD) 71 , the globus pallidus (GP) 44,47,59,64,72,73 and, contralaterally, the deep cerebellar nuclei 137 (interposed and dentate nuclei, DCN) and the cerebellar simple lobule (SL) 42,43,51,62 . The array 138 targeting cerebellar regions also recorded activity in the primary visual cortex (V1) which we included 139 among our regions of interest, given recent evidence that this region exhibits movement-related 140 activity 74,75 . The number of single units meeting our quality criteria (“qualifying units”) in individual 141 regions ranged from 35 (median across sessions, DCN) to 398 (mPFC; Figure S1E). In all regions of 142 interest, trial-averaged single unit activity appeared modulated around climbing onset and during 143 ongoing climbing (Figure 1G). 144 

To test for a deterministic process underlying the action timing decisions mice made, we 145 investigated whether the timing of climbing onset could be predicted from the preceding spiking 146 activity across regions. For each recording session, we trained a cross-validated elastic net linear 147 regression model 76 to predict the latency to climbing onset (time-to-movement) from the activity of 148 qualifying units between immobility onset and climbing onset. The average duration of epochs from 149 immobility onset to climbing onset (“trials”) ranged from 1.7 to 3.6 s across mice (Figure S1G), after 150 ignoring here and in all subsequent analysis trials lasting less than 1 s. Firing rates were averaged in 151 50 ms bins, and the forecast model used the rates from the preceding five bins ( 250 ms) to predict 152 time-to-movement at each time bin. Predictions for held-out trials often showed a ramping time 153 course (Figures 2A and 2B), but were not consistent with neural activity merely tracking the passage 154 of time (Figures S2A-S2C) 77 .155 

To determine whether the model’s predictive accuracy was significantly better than chance, 156 we computed two performance metrics for each held-out trial : the mean absolute difference 157 between the forecasted and true times-to-movement (residuals), and the slope of a linear fit to the 158 forecasted time-to-movement over time. To compute a p-value for each metric on each trial, actual 159 values were compared against null distributions generated using the forecast values of models fit to 160 permuted data. Forecast values were significantly better than chance for a majority of trials based 161 on their residuals, and for nearly all trials based on their slopes (Figures 2C-2E). Importantly, when 162 either performance metric was examined as a function of the time elapsed between the last GO to 163 NO-GO period transition and immobility onset , no significant correlation was observed (Figure s S2D 164 and S2E). This argues against the possibility that models exploit lingering sensory-related activity 165 associated with GO to NO-GO transitions. Together, these results indicate the presence of a 166 prominent deterministic component in action timing on individual trials. 167

Decision-related, temporally aligned activity across disparate brain regions 195 

Is the activity used by the forecast model to predict time-to-movement localized in a subset 196 of brain regions? To address this, we tested whether each unit’s activity improved time-to-movement 197 predictions compared to circularly permuted controls. In all eight regions of interest, we found 198 

neurons that significantly improved forecast accuracy (“forecasting neurons” ; Figure 3A), ranging 199 from 27% (median, mPFC) to 39% (M2; Figure 3B) of units in each region. When aligned on climbing 200 onset, the trial-averaged activity of these neurons often exhibited a ramping time course leading up 201 to action onset (Figure 3C). This pattern was also often apparent on single trials (Figure S3A). Thus, 202 the activity of neurons within each region of interest carries information about future action timing. 203 

We then examined how decision-related activity in different regions correlate s on individual 204 trials. We trained and tested separate forecast models using activity from each individual region. The 205 resulting single-trial forecast traces were remarkably similar across all regions, often exhibiting 206 coordinated deviations from an ideal linear prediction of time-to-movement (Figure 3D). Predictions 207 were positively correlated across all region pairs when pooled across all test trials and all time points 208 up to 250 ms before action onset to avoid activity dynamics related to movement execution 15,78 209 (Figures 3E and 3F). Moreover, when these across-region correlations were calculated for each 50-210 ms time bin, they remained consistently positive and significant throughout trials, from their 211 emergence soon after immobility onset leading up to action onset (Figure 3G). To test whether 212 predictive activity in one region consistently led predictive activity in another region, we computed 213 the cross-correlations of the single-trial predictions for all region pairs. The maximum cross-214 correlation peaked at or near a time lag of zero for all pairs (Figures S3B-S3E). This temporal 215 

alignment held even when predictions used firing rates in shorter, 10 -ms bins and did not rely on 216 additional preceding bins (Figures S3C and S3E). This brain-wide temporal alignment throughout the 217 decision epoch suggests that self-initiated action timing emerges from a unifi ed, distributed process 218 rather than a modular, sequential one. 219

The deterministic drive varies within and across trials 235 

The preceding results demonstrate the existence of a substantial, brain-wide deterministic 236 drive underlying self-initiated action timing, in contrast to recent models that center on the 237 contribution of a stochastic process within trials . However, we also found that trial durations varied 238 greatly (Figure S1G) and predictions exhibited substantial errors (Figures 2A and 2E-2G). This 239 variability and unpredictability of action timing could reflect a substantial additional contribution 240 from a stochastic process within trials, but could also arise from features of a deterministic process. 241 To clarify the nature and relative prominence of the deterministic drive, we thus sought to assess 242 

different potential sources of variability in action timing. 243 

We considered four distinct mechanisms. First, stochastic fluctuations in neural activity 244 during individual trials (within-trial noise) might influence the decision proces s, as in the typical 245 DDM. Second, timing variability could be attributed to trial-to-trial variation in the parameters of a 246 deterministic drive. For instance, timing variability could arise from a ramping process whose initial 247 value and slope vary across trials 37,79 . Third, the decision process might start at variable latencies 248 after immobility onset, generating timing variability even if the subsequent dynamics are similar 249 across trials. Finally, timing variability could also arise from trial-to-trial differences in the activity 250 threshold. 251 

To test these possibilities, we leveraged the output of our multi-region, time-to-movement 252 forecast model as a single-trial readout of the decision variable, i.e., the evolution of the decision 253 process over time. If deterministic drive parameters vary across trials, trial duration should correlate 254 with the forecast output’s initial value and/or its slope. If the decision process starts at variable 255 latencies after immobility onset, trial duration should be largely uncorrelated with the forecast 256 output’s initial value. If the activity threshold varies across trials, trial duration should correlate with 257 the forecast output’s final value. 258 

Thus we first plotted three variables versus trial duration : the forecast output’s initial value 259 (250 ms after immobility onset because of the window used for prediction ), its slope when best fit 260 

with a line, and its final value (250 before action onset or at action onset). We found that the initial 261 values and the slopes were negatively correlated with trial duration in all six mice (Figures 4A and 4B). 262 

By contrast, the final values, though highly variable, were uncorrelated with trial duration (Figure s 4C 263 and 4D). To ensure that the negative correlation between initial value and trial duration was robust 264 across trials, and not due to a subset of trials with an exceptionally early decision process start time, 265 we confirmed that the negative correlation holds for subsets of trials (Figure S4). This also further 266 supports an early engagement of the decision process soon after immobility onset. Together, these 267 results suggest that variability in action timing stems from variation in deterministic drive parameters 268 across trials, but not in the start time or activity threshold of the decision process. 269

Within-trial noise is not required for action initiation but contributes to action timing 345 

Having established the trial - and time-dependent nature of the deterministic drive, we then 346 sought to examine how within-trial noise contributes to action timing. Previous work using the typical 347 DDM to account for trial-averaged EEG data has suggested that within-trial noise plays a prominent 348 role, being necessary to reach the activity threshold (Figure S6D) 23,37 . Since noisy fluctuations are 349 

inherent to neural activity and, consequently, to our forecast traces, a diffusion term will necessarily 350 

improve the fit of our models to forecast model output. Thus we did not test whether including the 351 

diffusion term improved fits, but rather investigated its contribution to timing variability and whether 352 it is necessary to reach the activity threshold. 353 

To do this, we performed an in silico ablation analysis: we simulated trials using our DDM 354 variant fit to data from individual sessions, and then repeated these simulations without the diffusion 355 term. We found that the activity threshold was reached in both cases (Figure 5C). However , including 356 

the diffusion term did shorten the duration of the trials by 22 to 45% across mice (Figures 5D and 5E). 357 

As expected, the presence of the diffusion term also increased the variance in trial duration, by 16 to 358 36% across mice. These results suggest that while not required to self-initiate actions, within-trial 359 noise contributes substantially to setting action timing. 360 

As a control, we assessed the possibility that autocorrelation in the within-trial noise may 361 further improve our model 38,91,92 . We augmented our DDM variant with an eighth parameter, a spectral 362 exponent 𝛽𝛽 , which introduces dependence across time points in the diffusion term . However, a dding 363 this parameter did not yield a significantly better fit compared to using temporally uncorrelated noise 364 (Figures S6E and S6F). 365

# Discussion 391 

In this work, we sought to shed light on the neural basis of self-initiated timing decisions by 392 resolving single-trial, single-unit activity across multiple brain regions simultaneously. We found that 393 action onset timing can be predicted from spiking activity up to several seconds in advance, and 394 predictions are temporally aligned throughout the decision epoch across cortex, thalamus, basal 395 ganglia, and cerebellum. This implies that a prominent, brain-wide deterministic drive underlies such 396 decisions. 397 

By assessing different potential sources of variability in action timing , we found that the 398 deterministic drive varies in both its initial value and its rate across trials, and its rate increases over 399 time within trials. In contrast to recent models, within-trial noise is not required for crossing the 400 activity threshold, but does shorten the average time to threshold crossing. Similar decision-related 401 activity dynamics were observed in all brain regions we examined, indicating a broadly shared 402 decision process. Altogether, our results offer a unifying account in which trial-by-trial variability in 403 deterministic drive parameters and within-trial noise act in concert to render the timing of self-404 initiated actions unpredictable. 405 

Our results imply that a buildup of neural activity preceding action onset is a genuine feature 406 of the decision process, not an artifact of trial-averaging. Yet, they stand in contrast to classic views 407 of a purely deterministic process in which action onset is set whenever the buildup starts 3,4,7,14-17,19-22 .408 Our results also stand in contrast to more recent accounts whereby noisy neural activity within trials 409 is the sole driver 23,28 , or principal driver 25 , of timing decisions. This aligns with a previous proposal 37 410 that the early predictability of action timing shown in reference 25 is more consistent with a strong 411 deterministic drive than with accumulation of within-trial noise. 412 

Our ability to resolve a long-standing debate regarding the deterministic versus stochastic 413 underpinnings of self-initiated timing decisions stems from two key features of our approach. First, 414 the vast majority of neural data recorded in relation to self-initiated decisions has been obtained in 415 humans, using methods whose resolution necessitate trial-averaging. Yet single-trial resolution is 416 required to arbitrate between competing models. For instance, in the “stochastic accumulator 417 model”, developed by fitting the typical DDM to such trial-averaged data, within-trial noise is both the 418 sole source of timing variability across trials and is required for threshold crossing (Figure S6D) 14,23 .419 

However, these same data are equally well fit by models such as the linear ballistic accumulator, 420 which lacks within-trial noise, and in which the decision process is fully deterministic once its 421 parameters are set when the pre-movement buildup starts 37,79 . Second, previous models of self-422 initiated decisions have treated within-trial noise and trial-to-trial variation in deterministic drive 423 parameters as mutually exclusive 14,37 , though the same has not been true for models of perceptual 424 decisions 30,36 . By combining these potential mechanisms within a single model, we resolved a 425 dichotomy and established that they act synergistically. 426 

Here we uncovered a temporal alignment in decision-related activity across eight brain areas, 427 from soon after immobility onset to action onset. This alignment was best illustrated by the striking 428 similarity of the errors in time-to-movement predictions (Figure 3D). Key to this observation was our 429 simultaneous spike-resolution recording of large neuronal populations in multiple regions, which 430 allowed the comparison of predictive dynamics across different regions on the same single trials. 431 Such brain-wide coordination appears to contrast with previous suggestions of a more modular 432

organization whereby, for example, deterministic and stochastic components originate in mPFC and 433 M2 respectively, with activities predictive of action timing confined to M2 46 . A more distributed 434 picture emerges from our results, with predictive activity detectable in both of these regions, as well 435 as several others. Contrary to the classical view of a hierarchical organization in which regions 436 

perform distinct roles in a feedforward sequence, our results are more consistent with a unified 437 decision-making process that unfolds synchronously across disparate brain regions, relying on 438 recurrent interactions between regions. These brain-wide dynamics we found in relation to self-439 initiated decisions echo the broad coordination reported recently during motor execution 74,75,93,94 and 440 perceptual decision-making 95-101 , including regions historically seen as inherently motor (e.g., M1) or 441 sensory (e.g., V1). 442 

Our results also reveal additional parallels with perceptual decisions, which remain far more 443 extensively studied than self-initiated decisions. In tasks that require waiting, neural activity that 444 builds up at rates that scale with the waiting time has been observed across cortical regions and the 445 basal ganglia 30,73,102-106 . Moreover, our findings of a drift rate that increases within a trial are 446 reminiscent of urgency signals proposed to underlie perceptual decisions 36,80-88 . These urgency 447 signals have been modeled by either a time-varying drift rate or a collapsing threshold. Thus, urgency 448 may be a general organizing principle of decision dynamics, even in the absence of reward or other 449 incentives to act quickly. This mechanistic similarity between sensory-guided and self-initiated 450 decisions appears well-suited for negotiating complex natural environments, where behavior is 451 rarely dictated purely by internal or external factors, but rather emerges from a dynamic integration 452 of both. 453 

However, w hile mechanistic features appear conserved between self-initiated and sensory-454 

guided decisions, the nature of the decision process differs fundamentally in its input and adaptive 455 function. In perceptual tasks, the deterministic drive reflects the strength of external sensory 456 evidence, and stochasticity primarily represents perceptual noise that can be detrimental to choice 457 accuracy 107 . In contrast, in the absence of external cues, the deterministic drive we observed likely 458 

reflect s an internal imperative to act 14 ensuring that an action will be executed, while stochasticity 459 gives rise to an advantageous unpredictability of behavior. 460 

An important question unaddressed by our results concerns the neurophysiological 461 underpinnings of the sources of action timing variability we have described. Our analysis implicates 462 three such sources: trial -to-trial variability in the initial value and drift rate of the deterministic drive, 463 and noise within trials. While within-trial noise is likely attributable to aspects of neuronal physiology, 464 like ion channel gating and synaptic transmission 39 , the origins of discrete sources of variability are 465 more obscure. Neuromodulatory systems may be one possible source. Notably, the basal forebrain 466 cholinergic system has been causally implicated in self-initiated action timing 47,59,60 and serves as 467 the primary source of acetylcholine to the cortex 108 , including the prefrontal and somatomotor 468 regions where we observed predictive activity. Cholinergic input can modulate correlations across a 469 neuronal population 109 , and acetylcholine appears capable of modulating cortical function on the 470 timescale of individual trials 110-116 . Thus, the basal forebrain cholinergic system could participate in 471 regulating the gain of the deterministic drive (i.e., the drift rate) on individual trials. 472

# Material and Methods 591 

Experimental subjects 592 

All experiments and procedures involving animals were performed in accordance with NIH 593 guidelines and approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Northwestern 594 University. A total of 26 adult C57BL/6J male mice (Jackson Laboratories stock #000664) were used 595 in reported experiments and early pilot studies to establish methodology. All mice were individually 596 housed under a 12-hour light/dark cycle during the course of the experiment. At the time of reported 597 measurements, animals were 12-24 weeks old and weighed approximately 24-30 g. All animals were 598 

used for the first time in this experiment and were not previously exposed to pharmacological 599 substances or altered diets. 600 

Surgical procedures 601 

All surgeries were performed under isoflurane anesthesia (1 -3%) in a stereotaxic frame. The surgical 602 

workflow consisted of two main procedures separated by a recovery and behavioral training period. 603 

Headplate Implantation 604 

First, mice were implanted with a custom 3D-printed plastic headplate. The headplate, which 605 features an open-center design for cranial access, was affixed to the skull using dental cement 606 (Metabond, Parkell). The position of bregma relative to marks on either side of the headplate was 607 measured to facilitate the positioning of craniotomies during the subsequent surgery. The skull within 608 the open window was then covered with a protective layer of dental cement. After a one-week 609 recovery period, mice were placed on a water schedule (1-1.5 ml per day) and began behavioral 610 training. 611 

Craniotomies 612 

After the completion of behavioral training, a second surgery was performed. The protective dental 613 cement was removed to expose the skull, and four craniotomies (0.5-1 mm diameter) were drilled 614 over the target regions. A platinum-iridium ground wire soldered to a connector pin was implanted in 615 a separate craniotomy over the right frontal lobe and cemented to the headplate implant. Silicone 616 elastomer (Kwik-Cast, World Precision Instruments) was used to cover the craniotomy after surgery. 617

Climbing apparatus 627 

The behavioral apparatus, described in detail previously 67 , was housed inside a sound-attenuating 628 

chamber (H10 -24A, Coulbourn). The core of the apparatus was a custom, 3D-printed cylindrical 629 wheel featuring handholds positioned 12° apart. A ratchet mechanism permitted the wheel to rotate 630 only in one direction, downward as the mouse climbed. The wheel’s absolute angular position was 631 continuously tracked by a shaft encoder (A2-A-B-D-M-D, U.S. Digital). Experimental control utilized 632 custom MATLAB (MathWorks) scripts and a DAQ card (PCI-e-6323, National Instruments). 633 

Behavioral training 634 

Mice were initially acclimated to being handled by the experimenter, to head-fixation, and to the 635 climbing apparatus according to previously described procedures 67 . Briefly, following acclimation to 636 handling and head-fixation in front of the wheel, mice underwent several daily training sessions to 637 associate rotating the wheel with a water reward. Initially, rewards were delivered manually for any 638 wheel rotation. Subsequently, an automated protocol rewarded mice for performing progressively 639 longer climbing bouts, with the reward amount for each bout adapted based on the mouse’s 640 performance on recent bouts. This helped maintain motivation. 641 

Once mice were proficient, they were transitioned to the behavioral protocol also used during 642 recording sessions. This protocol consisted of two period types – “GO periods” and “NO-GO periods” 643 – occurring alternately. So that mice could distinguish between periods, auditory ( 9 kHz pure tone; 644 Murata Electronics 7BB-12-9) and visual (green led; Visual Communications Company 645 CNX722C50005B) cues were presented during one of the two period types. Which period involved 646 cues was varied across animals: for some mice, cues were active during the GO periods, while for 647 others, they were active during the NO-GO periods. The duration of the GO and NO-GO periods was 648 contingent on the mouse’s behavior. If the animal remained immobile, the durations of both GO and 649 NO-GO periods were randomly selected from a continuous uniform distribution ranging from 2 to 4 650 seconds. If the mouse climbed, period duration was adjusted as follows: 651 

- During a GO period, a climbing bout would terminate the period and trigger a reward. 652 

- During a NO-GO period, a climbing bout would forestall the end of the period and reset the timer 653 from the moment immobility is detected. 654 

The present study focuses exclusively on the self-initiated climbing bouts, which occurred during 655 NO-GO periods. A typical behavioral session lasted one hour. 656

Climbing onset detection 703 

To precisely identify the onset of self-initiated climbing bouts, we used a two-stage method that 704 combined wheel rotation and limb kinematics. 705 

1) First, during behavioral sessions, climbing bouts were detected using the angular displacement of 706 the wheel. The shaft encoder signal , acquired at 1 kHz, was smoothed, and a climbing bout was 707 

defined as a continuous rotation exceeding an angular displacement of 10 °. Immobility, in this 708 

context, was defined as less than 0.25° of rotation over 400 -ms windows. This step ensured that 709 detected movements corresponded to genuine locomotion that displaced the wheel. 710 

2) Second, this detection was refined offline using the kinematic data from the right hand and foot to 711 determine the precise moment of climbing onset. The DeepLabCut marker coordinates were 712 

upsampled to 1 kHz via Akima spline interpolation 125 , and instantaneous velocity was calculated for 713 each limb. A velocity threshold, empirically set to 0.1 pixel/ms to separate limb movements from 714 baseline noise, was applied to classify time points as either mobile or immobile. Among periods of 715 mobility, t o distinguish genuine climbing from twitches, we applied the following criteria: 1) brief 716 periods of mobility lasting less than 100 ms were classified as twitches, and 2) short pauses of 717 

immobility (< 400 ms) within a larger movement were reclassified as part of the ongoing movement. 718 

The onset of a potential climb was defined as the first moment a limb transitioned from an immobile 719 to a mobile state that was not a twitch. For a potential climbing bout to be validated, the kinematics-720 

defined onset had to be followed by a wheel -defined onset within 500 ms, ensuring the limb 721 movement resulted in a productive wheel displacement exceeding 10°. Finally, the climbing bout had 722 to be preceded by a continuous period of immobility of at least 1 second. 723 

A single trial was defined as the continuous period of immobility preceding the onset of a climbing 724 bout initiated during a NO-GO period. 725 

Quantification of anticipatory licking behavior 726 

To quantify anticipatory licking behavior, we tracked individual licks during climbing from video 727 

recordings. First, using a custom MATLAB routine, we identified potential licks by detecting contrast 728 changes within a region of interest between the water spout and the mouse’s mouth. Second, to 729 eliminate false positives, which could occur when the mouse’s paw crossed the region of interest 730 during climbing, every potential lick was manually curated by visualizing the corresponding frames. 731 

We compared the anticipatory lick rate between GO and NO-GO periods by calculating the fraction 732 of climbing bouts during which at least one lick occurred. To ensure an unbiased comparison 733 

between the two conditions, we enforced two criteria: 734 

1) To prevent reward-triggered licking from contaminating the GO-period data, climbing bouts during 735 GO periods were truncated immediately prior to the moment of bout completion, before the animal 736 could perceive the reward or any reward-predicting cue. This truncation point corresponded either 737

2) Because climbing bouts initiated during NO-GO periods are not rewarded, they tend to be longer 741 than those during GO periods. To prevent this duration disparity from biasing the lick probability, we 742

Cross-validated predictions of time-to-movement 752 

To test whether the timing of self-initiated actions was predictable from neural activity on single 753 trials, we trained a linear forecast model. We chose an elastic net linear regression model 76 for its 754 simplicity and interpretability. The model was trained to predict the time remaining until action onset 755 (“time-to-movement”) at 50-ms time steps during trials. Trials, which had variable durations, were 756 aligned to action onset. The input features for the model at each 50 ms time step consisted of the 757 

firing rates of all qualifying units, over the preceding five time steps ( 250 ms). We used a 5-fold cross-758 validation scheme to train and test the forecast model. The regularization parameter for the elastic 759 net was optimized for each fold using an internal 5-fold cross-validation loop performed exclusively 760 on the training set. To assess the correlation of single-trial forecast predictions across brain regions 761 (Figures 3D-3G), the same procedure was applied using qualifying units from individual regions. 762 

To estimate chance-level predictions, we circularly permuted 126 the time-to-movement relative to the 763 

firing rates for the training data 1,000 times for each cross-validation fold, and tested the resulting 764 null models on the held-out test trials. We could thus compute a null distribution for each 765 performance metric (mean absolute residuals and best-fit slopes) for each test trial. Actual 766 performance metric values were compared against the null distributions to compute a p-value for 767 each performance metric (Figures 2C-2E). 768 

These calculations were repeated for 250-ms windows that began every 50 ms during a trial. This 769 produced a sequence of p-values over time for each trial, where each p-value represented the 770 

forecast’s significance within that specific time window. We applied the Benjamini-Hochberg 771 procedure 127 to control the False Discovery Rate (FDR) across all time windows within a given trial. 772 

The onset of predictability for a trial was then defined as the first time point at which the FDR -773 corrected p-value fell below the significance threshold of 0.05. 774

Cross-correlation analyses 818 

To quantify temporal offset between brain regions in their predictive activity, we performed cross -819 correlation analyses. For each pair of brain regions, cross-correlograms were computed on each 820 trial’s time-to-movement predictions (including data up to 250 ms before action onset). The 821 empirical delay for a given pair of regions (Figures S3B and S3C ) was defined as the time lag that 822 yielded the maximum positive correlation within this trial-averaged cross-correlogram. 823 

To assess whether the observed temporal delay between regions was statistically significantly 824 

different from zero, we employed a non -parametric bootstrap procedure. For each pair of regions, we 825 generated 1,000 bootstrap samples. Each sample was constructed by randomly drawing 𝑁𝑁 trials with 826 replacement from the original pool of 𝑁𝑁 trials. For every bootstrap iteration, we averaged the cross-827 correlograms of the resampled trials and extracted the time lag corresponding to the new peak 828 correlation. This procedure generated an empirical distribution of 1,000 expected lags, from which 829 

we defined the 95% confidence intervals of the true lag. Statistical significance was determined 830 using a two-tailed test on the bootstrap lag distribution. Finally, to account for multiple testing across 831 all regional pairs, raw p-values were adjusted using the Benjamini-Hochberg FDR procedure, with an 832 alpha threshold set at 0.05. 833 

Drift-diffusion modelling 834 

DDMs were fit to time -to-movement forecast traces derived from the entire neural population (Figure 835 5) and from individual regions (Figure 6). Separate models were fit for each recording session. 836

Data fit by the DDM 981 

DDM parameters were estimated by fitting forecast traces derived from held-out test trials, spanning 982 from their beginning up to 250 ms before action onset. The forecast traces were fit from their initial 983 value (250 ms after immobility onset) based on our finding that this initial value is correlated with trial 984 duration (Figures 4A and S4), and that predictive information typically emerges within the first 500 ms 985 for most trials (Figure 2H ), consistent with a decision-making process starting soon after immobility 986 onset. The end point (250 ms before action onset) was chosen to isolate the decision-making 987 process and avoid contamination from potential post-decision, motor execution-related activity. 988 

This choice should be conservative: in humans, the point of no return beyond which a self-initiated 989 action can no longer be aborted occurs approximately 200 ms before action onset 15,78 . This delay is 990 likely shorter in mice, given the much lower limb inertia. 991 

As a pre-processing step, because our DDM formulation assumes a positive drift rate and leak rate, 992 

the forecast traces were first made non -negative by subtracting from all forecast traces the minimum 993 value observed across all traces within that session. 994 

Optimization and parameter fitting 995 

Model parameters were fit by minimizing the negative log -likelihood of the data. We used a two-stage 996 global optimization procedure to avoid local minima. First, a genetic algorithm 133,134 was run five 997 

times with different random seeds to broadly explore the parameter space (function ga in MATLAB). 998 The best-fit parameters from these runs were then used as the starting point for an interior -point local 999 optimizer 135,136 to refine the minimal negative likelihood estimate (function fmincon in MATLAB). 1000 Parameters such as leak rate, noise amplitude, and distribution widths ( 𝑘𝑘 , 𝑐𝑐 , σ 𝑖𝑖 𝑛𝑛 𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖 , σ 𝑑𝑑 𝑖𝑖𝑖𝑖 𝑑𝑑 𝑖𝑖 ) were 1001 constrained to be positive, while the drift’s temporal rate α was allowed to be positive or negative. 1002 

Statistical procedure for model selection 1003 

The improvement in model fit for each parameter was assessed using a non -parametric bootstrap 1004 procedure 137 . We performed 1,000 bootstrap iterations. For each iteration, we constructed a training 1005 set by sampling 𝑁𝑁 trials with replacement from the trials for the given session, where 𝑁𝑁 is the total 1006 number of trials for that session. The remaining trials that were not selected in the training set served 1007 as the held-out test set. We fit full and nested models on the training set and evaluated their 1008 goodness-of-fit on the test set by computing the total log -likelihood for all held-out trials. To ensure 1009 comparability between bootstrap iterations and sessions, the total log-likelihood was normalized by 1010 the number of data points in the given test set. For each iteration, we computed the log-likelihood 1011 gain ( Δ𝐿𝐿𝐿𝐿 ) as the difference between the full and nested models; these values are reported in Figures 1012 5, 6, and S6. A positive Δ𝐿𝐿𝐿𝐿 thus indicates that the additional parameter improved the model fit. 1013 

Statistical significance was determined via an empirical p-value, defined as the fraction of bootstrap 1014 iterations where the full model failed to outperform the nested model ( Δ𝐿𝐿𝐿𝐿 ≤ 0). A parameter was 1015 

considered to significantly improve the model if p < 0.05. This model selection approach was 1016 validated using parameter recovery on simulated data (Figure S5). 1017
