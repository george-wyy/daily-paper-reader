Abstract 9

Neural decoders serve as probabilistic interfaces in co-control brain-to-text BCIs, where pre-10 

dicted uncertainty shapes hypothesis generation and language model integration, enabling decisions 11 

to be made safely under uncertainty. However, it remains unclear whether these decoders produce 12 

reliable and informative uncertainty, or how training objectives shape these properties. This work 13 

characterizes and improves uncertainty representations in brain-to-text decoding. We extend two 14 

metrics, calibration error (ECE) and resolution (RES), to evaluate sequential probabilistic predictions 15 

from frame-level phoneme estimates to word-level hypotheses, quantifying the reliability and infor-16 

mativeness of model uncertainty. Using this framework, we analyze neural decoders trained with 17 

connectionist temporal classification (CTC). To isolate the causal role of uncertainty independent of 18 

accuracy, we manipulate predicted probability distributions while holding predicted sequences fixed. 19 

Motivated by the observed failures, we further examine the role of the training objective and propose 20 

a two-stage cross-entropy (CE) formulation that decouples alignment inference from classification. 21 

We show that widely used CTC-trained neural decoders in brain-to-text BCIs produce systemati-22 

cally over-confident predictions, with high confidence persisting even when predictions are incorrect. 23 

Controlled manipulations of the prediction reveal that improved ECE and RES enhance hypothe-24 

sis generation and language-model integration by promoting diverse alternatives and more effective 25 

re-ranking of hypotheses aligned with user intent. Mechanistically, CTC relies on over-confident 26 

predictions to resolve alignment ambiguity. Replacing CTC with CE loss yields significantly more 27 

reliable and informative probabilistic predictions without degrading decoding accuracy. Uncertainty 28 

emerges as a system-level design variable in brain-to-text interfaces. Calibrated uncertainty from 29 

neural decoders enables effective integration with independently trained language models and reli-30 

able error detection. This work reframes uncertainty from a passive output into an active control 31 

signal, identifies key components and evaluation criteria for probabilistic co-control, and outlines a 32 

pathway toward next-generation BCIs that supports increasingly complex interactions with the world. 33

inference, Uncertainty quantification, Sequence modeling of neural activity, Language modeling 35

# 1 Introduction 36 

Brain–computer interfaces (BCIs) aim to restore communication and movement [1, 2] in a manner 37 

that is effective, safe, and minimizes cognitive burden [3, 4], ultimately restoring independence for 38 

individuals with paralysis. Decoded neural signals may directly drive assistive devices that facilitate 39 

daily activities through communication interfaces [5–12], software applications [13, 14], wheelchair or 40 

robotic limbs [15–23]. As a result, deployed BCIs are often safety-critical, as misinterpreted user intent 41 

can trigger conflicts or irreversible actions [3, 23, 24]. In this setting, average predictive accuracy alone 42 

is insufficient, though it is the performance measure most commonly reported. Uncertainty estimates 43 

provide an informative signal when a system’s outputs should be treated with caution or deferred for 44 

intervention as established in safety-critical domains (e.g., robotics and medical diagnosis [25–30]). 45 

Reliable and informative uncertainty estimates are therefore a fundamental requirement for practical BCI 46 

deployment. 47 

The need for high-quality uncertainty estimates is amplified in modern BCI architectures that employ 48 

co-control [31,32], where neural decoders and behavior models (e.g., language models, motion planners) 49 

jointly determine control outputs. As BCIs scale to more complex behaviors, decoding must resolve a large 50 

space of possible outputs, typically requiring substantial paired neural–behavior data. However, such data 51

As a result, supervision is weak: it lacks fine-grained temporal alignment between neural activity and 55

limitations by leveraging independently trained behavior models that encode statistical regularities over 57 

behavior. These models act as priors over the space of possible outputs, constraining decoding and 58 

thereby reducing reliance on large amounts of paired neural–behavior data while improving performance. 59 

Co-control also enables the extensible integration of additional components, supporting scalable and 60 

modular BCI design (Fig. 1). 61 

Although brain-to-text BCIs are often framed as directly mapping neural activity to text, existing 62 

systems are more accurately understood as inherently operating under co-control. Co-control refers to 63 

the joint construction of control signals in a shared hypothesis space, where multiple modules contribute 64 

by weighting candidate hypotheses according to their estimated uncertainty. In practice, this is realized 65 

through pretrained language models [40–45] correcting, and refining neural decoder outputs [11, 12, 66 

35–39, 46–49]. Similar co-control paradigms have emerged in BCI-enabled high-degree-of-freedom 67 

reach-and-grasp robotic control tasks [22, 50, 51]. In these modular pipelines, neural decoders operate as 68 

components within a broader system in which downstream models impose learned behavioral structure 69 

and constraints. Although such integration enables substantial performance gains, including language-70 

model-guided error correction in brain-to-text decoding [11,12,35–39,46–49, 52,53] and obstacle-aware 71 

motion planning in brain-to-grasp control [22,50,51], it also amplifies the importance of how uncertainty 72 

is represented and communicated across system components. Miscalibrated uncertainty can lead to 73 

failures at both extremes: excessive assistance may override user intent and hinder goal achievement, 74 

while insufficient assistance can result in poor control and uncorrected errors, ultimately leading to 75 

misalignment between behavior-model assistance and user intent that impacts usability, safety, and user 76 

trust [28, 31, 51, 54–56]. 77 

Effective co-control in systems like brain-computer interfaces relies on an uncertainty-governed 78 

division of labor, much like a collaborative human transcription team. In this analogy, the primary 79 

decoder, whether a human transcriber or a BCI neural decoder, serves as the interface that translates raw 80 

signals into initial commands, ideally flagging segments of ambiguity or low confidence. A downstream 81 

“editor”, such as a human proofreader or a BCI language model, refines these outputs by focusing 82 

on flagged regions and using contextual consistency to infer likely corrections without direct access 83 

to the original signal (audio or neural activity). Crucially, the success of this collaboration hinges on 84 

how accurately uncertainty is communicated between these stages: the editor’s ability to intervene is 85 

fundamentally limited if the uncertainty conveyed by the transcriber or neural decoder is misaligned 86 

with the underlying signal variability. In the transcription analogy, this corresponds to expressing high 87 

confidence despite degraded audio (e.g., noise or overlapping speech), or low confidence when the signal 88 

is clear, both of which misguide the editor’s corrections. In the BCI setting, signal variability reflects 89 

ambiguity in neural activity, while uncertainty should indicate how reliably the decoder maps that activity 90 

to intended phonemes or words. When this alignment fails, downstream modules cannot distinguish 91 

between reliable and unreliable predictions, undermining effective co-control. Thus, uncertainty is not 92

In brain-to-text BCIs, this interaction occurs through probabilistic outputs rather than discrete predic-96 

tions, allowing downstream modules to reason about uncertainty. Neural decoders produce a distribution 97 

over text tokens across time, which is processed by pretrained language models to generate and re-rank 98 

candidate sentences [40–45] (Fig. 1). Ideally, elevated uncertainty in the neural decoder output would 99 

signal potential errors, expand the set of candidate hypotheses explored during sequential decoding, and 100 

shift control toward the language model for correction. However, in practice, the quality of this uncertainty 101 

signal is rarely examined explicitly. Most brain-to-text systems are evaluated primarily using end-to-end 102 

metrics, such as error rates (e.g., word, phoneme, or character error rate; WER, PER, CER), commu-103 

nication rate (e.g., words or characters per minute; WPM, CPM), or semantic relevance (e.g., BLEU, 104 

BERTScore) [11,12,35–39,46–49,52,57–62], obscuring how uncertainty is represented, interpreted, and 105 

acted upon within the co-controlled architecture. As a result, neural decoders are often optimized to be 106 

“decisive” at the expense of being “informative”. In practice, this can lead to overconfident predictions 107 

that disrupt uncertainty-governed co-control, impairing appropriate control allocation under ambiguity 108 

and promoting overcorrection that obscures the user’s intended output. This raises several fundamental 109 

questions: Does a more accurate neural decoder necessarily lead to better integrated system behavior 110 

under co-control? What determines the quality of uncertainty estimates in its predictions? And can 111 

uncertainty itself be systematically quantified and can its quality be improved to enable more principled 112 

and reliable co-control? 113 

The challenge of obtaining reliable and informative uncertainty estimates in classification is not 114 

unique to BCIs. In image classification, deep neural networks can achieve high predictive accuracy while 115 

producing poorly calibrated uncertainty estimates [63–66]. This gap between accuracy and reliability has 116 

motivated extensive work on uncertainty quantification, including post-hoc calibration methods [64–66], 117 

conformal prediction [67,68], Bayesian neural networks [69], and ensemble approaches [70,71]. However, 118 

most of these methods are developed for static, instance-level prediction. Extending uncertainty estimation 119 

to sequential, structured decoding, where probabilities propagate across time and interact with search and 120 

downstream models, is substantially more challenging. 121 

Brain–computer interfaces represent a particularly demanding instance of this broader problem. Neu-122 

ral decoding is sequential and structured, and uncertainty estimates must be reliable to preserve user intent 123 

as probabilistic predictions propagate through intermediate inference stages. In this work, we use brain-124 

to-text decoding as a prototype for probabilistic co-control, offering a principled framework to study how 125 

neural decoder predictions of user intent are combined with complex behavioral priors, such as language 126 

models, through probabilistic interfaces. First, we introduce a principled evaluation framework to quantify 127 

the reliability and informativeness of probabilistic predictions across multiple temporal scales by leverag-128 

ing calibration error and resolution, which is defined as the ability of predicted probabilities to distinguish 129 

correct from incorrect outcomes, grounded in proper scoring rule decompositions [72]. Second, we show 130 

that widely used neural decoders trained with the connectionist temporal classification (CTC) loss pro-131 

duce systematically over-confident predictions. Third, through oracle interventions, we demonstrate that 132

In this section, we analyze the role of uncertainty in neural decoding at three levels. First, we evaluate 319 

the quality of the predictive distribution 𝑝 ND produced by neural decoders, showing that commonly 320 

used models exhibit systematic overconfidence (Sec. 3.1). Second, we investigate the functional role of 321

3.3 Balancing Alignment Learning and Classification Produces Calibrated Probabilities 448 

Neural decoders in brain-to-text BCIs are trained to optimize phoneme recognition under weak supervi-449 

sion, yet their predictive distribution are subsequently used as uncertainty signals by downstream modules. 450 

This raises a central question: can predictive distribution be learned that faithfully reflect uncertainty 451 

while preserving decoding accuracy? A fundamental challenge is that neither framewise labels nor 452 

uncertainty are directly observable, as the alignment between neural activity and phoneme is latent. The 453 

CTC loss marginalizes over all valid alignments (Sec. 2.3), often producing “peaky alignments” that 454 

concentrate frame-level probability mass on a single predicted token. This implicitly selects a dominant 455 

alignment during learning (Sec. 2.4), reinforcing iterative fitting to that alignment hypothesis and thereby 456 

introducing artificial certainty [74,75]. In this setting, the over-confidence required to stabilize alignment 457 

may suppress the proper expression of frame-wise uncertainty arising from model uncertainty, ambiguous 458 

neural evidence, or label noise. 459

# 4 Discussion 512 

BCI control signals typically contain errors because neural input features are inherently variable and 513 

rarely provide complete evidence of the user’s intended behavior. To support effective and safety-critical 514 

BCIs, neural decoders must therefore do more than produce a single best estimate of user intent: they 515 

must also communicate uncertainty about their predictions. Most prior work has focused on improving 516 

end-to-end accuracy metrics such as word error rate [11,12,35–39,57]. However, neural decoders operate 517 

as probabilistic inference modules within co-control BCI systems, where predictive distribution function 518 

as uncertainty signals that guide downstream components, including language-model integration and user 519 

feedback (Fig. 1). 520 

Our results show that neural decoder predictive distribution (Sec. 2.2) cannot be assumed to faithfully 521 

represent uncertainty. Widely used neural decoders trained with the CTC objective [11,12,35–39,57,73] 522 

exhibit systematic overconfidence, assigning near-deterministic confidence even when predictions are 523 

incorrect (Sec. 3.1). This overconfidence suppresses alternative hypotheses during sequence decoding and 524 

distorts hypothesis arbitration with language models (Sec. 3.2; 3.3). In the following discussion sections, 525 

we synthesize these findings to characterize the consequences of these failure modes for probabilistic 526 

inference (Sec. 4.1), connect the mechanisms underlying overconfident predictions to concrete directions 527 

for improving uncertainty estimation (Sec. 4.2), and articulate broader implications for the design of 528 

effective co-control and its extension to other BCI tasks (Sec. 4.3). More broadly, our work highlights 529 

that relying solely on end-to-end decoding accuracy can obscure critical properties of the neural decoder’s 530 

probabilistic outputs, potentially biasing system design toward specific AI models or evaluation settings 531 

rather than producing reliable uncertainty signals that can support multiple downstream modules. By 532 

opening this “black box,” we aim to characterize and evaluate the implicit control signal, decoder predicted 533 

uncertainty, that governs interaction between neural decoding and AI components in next-generation BCI 534 

systems. 535

4.1 Neural Decoders Do Not Provide Informative and Reliable Estimates of Uncertainty 536 

Analysis of predictive distribution from widely used CTC neural decoders for brain-to-text BCI [11, 12, 537 

35–39,57] reveals a consistent probabilistic structure across subject (T12 [12] and T15 [35]). Specifically, 538 

neural decoders exhibit three coupled probabilistic failure modes: predictive distribution collapse toward 539 

near-deterministic frame-level predictions; this overconfidence propagates through time, aggregating into 540 

overconfident word-level predictions; and the resulting certainty suppresses alternative hypotheses during 541 

sequence decoding and distorts the language-model integration. 542 

First, frame-level overconfidence causes the predictive distribution to concentrate on the predicted 543 

phoneme even when the prediction is prone to error. This near-deterministic behavior prevents uncertainty 544 

from being localized within a sequence, yielding poor reliability (high ECE) and low informativeness (low 545 

RES) as quantified by the proposed metrics (Sec. 2.7; Fig. 3). Consequently, downstream components 546 

cannot distinguish frames supported by strong neural evidence from those arising from ambiguous or 547 

noisy input, preventing adaptive responses to local uncertainty. 548 

Second, this miscalibration propagates temporally through the decoding hierarchy. Overconfident 549 

frame predictions accumulate into overconfident word-level predictions (Sec. 2.7, 3.1; Fig. 3). Incor-550 

rect words therefore frequently appear both wrong and certain, making them difficult to flag for user 551 

confirmation or correction. This failure is particularly consequential for low-frequency yet semantically 552 

informative words, which are known to exhibit higher error rates in brain-to-text decoding [83]. When 553 

such errors are accompanied by high confidence, both users and downstream modules lose the ability to 554 

efficiently detect and correct mistakes. 555 

Third, overconfident neural predictions can cause multi-stage decoding to fail by constraining both 556 

hypothesis generation and hypothesis integration (Fig. 2). When predictive distributions concentrate 557 

excessively on a single token, beam search prematurely suppresses alternative hypotheses, thus producing 558 

a narrow candidate set and limiting sequence diversity (Fig. 4 B,C), a failure mode widely observed in 559 

general sequence decoding systems [84–90]. This restricted hypothesis space reduces the effectiveness 560 

of language-model integration, which relies on graded score differences among plausible alternatives to 561 

perform contextual re-ranking and error correction (Fig. 4 B,C). Notably, sentences with higher phoneme 562 

error rates benefit most from calibrated uncertainty (Fig. 6 A). Although these cases contain weaker 563 

contextual information, localized uncertainty preserves candidate hypotheses that would otherwise be 564 

pruned during decoding, allowing the language model to recover from errors more effectively. This helps 565 

explain why improvements in phoneme error rate (PER) do not always translate to lower word error rate 566 

(WER) in integrated systems [57]. Scalar accuracy metrics capture correctness, but not how predictive 567 

uncertainty structures intermediate hypotheses and module interactions in multi-stage inference (Sec. 3.2). 568 

These findings suggest that neural decoder predictive distributions act as control signals governing 569 

decoding behavior. When these signals are miscalibrated, a system may appear accurate while silently 570 

failing during ambiguous predictions. Improving the reliability and informativeness of decoder-predicted 571 

uncertainty is therefore essential for enabling robust hypothesis generation, effective language-model 572 

integration, and ultimately safer co-control BCI systems. 573

4.2 Mechanisms of Miscalibrated Uncertainty and Design Implications 574 

An ideal neural decoder should learn to express uncertainty that is localized in time and reflects the 575 

strength of neural evidence supporting ongoing behavior. Through controlled comparisons between 576 

neural decoders trained with CTC (Sec. 2.3) and CE (Sec. 2.4) objectives, we find that training objectives 577 

strongly influence the uncertainty representation in prediction. The CTC objective couples alignment and 578 

classification, encouraging overconfident predictions that resolve alignment ambiguity. In contrast, de-579 

coupling alignment via CE training preserves accuracy while improving probability resolution (Sec. 3.3), 580 

yielding more informative uncertainty estimates. Training objectives serve as a key design lever for 581 

shaping uncertainty estimation, in addition to determining recognition accuracy. 582 

In brain–computer interfaces, single-trial neural signals are inherently noisy and variable [91–93], 583 

and user control signals fluctuate across attempts due to factors such as fatigue, feedback, and task 584 

familiarity [8, 94–103]. Consequently, reliable uncertainty estimates should adapt to variations in the 585

Beyond modifying training objectives, another approach to improving uncertainty estimation is 598

cost and shifts increasing responsibility to large language models, which resolve errors primarily using 610 

semantic context rather than uncertainty conveyed by the neural evidence of user intent. 611 

4.3 Calibrated Uncertainty As A Foundation For Co-control 612 

The central limitation in next-generation co-control BCIs may not be decoding accuracy, but the faithful 613 

representation of uncertainty. While accuracy measures performance in static, monolithic integrations 614 

of neural decoders and behavioral modules, uncertainty estimation determines how effectively neural 615 

decoders collaborate with downstream modules under increasing complexity, partial observability, and 616 

evolving conditions. This shift in perspective reframes uncertainty as a system-level quantity that links 617 

multiple stages of BCI development, from the encoding and decoding of neural signals to the integration, 618 

validation, and execution of predictions by downstream modules. 619 

Uncertainty can take different forms across modalities. In discrete decoding tasks such as brain-620 

to-text, it is expressed as predictive distributions over states, whereas in continuous motor BCIs (e.g., 621 

arm [5, 6, 8–10, 14] and finger movement [105–107]), it naturally appears as variance over movement 622 

trajectories. Across these settings, uncertainty is not merely descriptive, rather it functions as a control 623
