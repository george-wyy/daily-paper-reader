# Summary 

This electroencephalography (EEG) study reveals how expectations dynamically shape visually evoked brain responses through a biphasic temporal mechanism: an early enhancement of encoding for expected image components, followed by a later prioritization of unexpected components. This temporal dissociation suggests that the brain initially integrates sensory input with prior expectations in a Bayesian manner before shifting to emphasize surprising information, thereby supporting both efficient recognition and adaptive model updating. Notably, this updating process first amplifies category ‑level representations before increasing the encoding of image identity. 

# Introduction 

Several decades ago, a paper by Rao and Ballard 1 reinvigorated the Helmholtzian idea that 

perception arises from a synergy between sensations and expectations. Since then, a large cohort of 

human neuroimaging studies 3,5,7  has interpreted contextual effects on brain activity in sensory 

cortices within the theoretical framework of predictive coding (PC framework), which emphasises the importance of prediction ‑error coding, enhanced neural representations of unexpected stimuli.

The resulting empirical landscape, however, remains difficult to reconcile into a coherent account of how expectations shape sensory processing. Most notably, conflicting evidence suggests that expectations can lead to either enhanced or reduced fidelity of sensory representations of expected 

stimuli, processes referred to as 'sharpening' and 'dampening,' respectively 5,7,9,11 . This divergence 

leaves open a fundamental question: does expectation exert a 'prediction-error' effect by suppressing redundant information, or a 'Bayesian' effect by selectively enhancing the precision of expected signals? 

To address this open question, we conducted an EEG experiment designed to compare the relative neural encoding strength of expected and unexpected image components over time. Specifically, we tested whether expectation facilitates an initial Bayesian effect followed by a subsequent prediction ‑error signaling phase, consistent with the dual ‑process theory proposed by Press and 

colleagues 13 . To this end, we measured how expectation influences the extent to which EEG signals 

encode the identity and category (animacy) of image components over time. This approach also clarifies whether expectation effects arise during early sensory processing or are more characteristic of later, higher ‑order cognitive stages, and whether expectation differentially affects image ‑specific representations versus more abstract category ‑level representations. 

# Results 

During our EEG study, participants viewed four natural images depicting a car, a house, a cat, and an elephant. During each 1.5 ‑hour EEG session, these images were presented in a fixed repeating order (e.g., car–house–cat–elephant–car–house–cat–elephant …, as shown in Fig.  1a). Participants viewed this sequence more than 2,500 times per session, rendering the occurrence of each image highly predictable. Each participant completed six EEG sessions, across which they were shown all six possible unique image sequences. Data analysis was performed across all six sessions for each participant to rule out low ‑level perceptual sequence effects, including adaptation ‑related 

transition-patterns 15 . Participants were instructed to continuously fixate the central fixation cross 

and, to ensure that they remained vigilant and attended to all images, we asked them to press a button whenever an upside-down image was presented. These catch trials were shown during 1.6% of all trials, and participants detected them during 97.1% (SD = 2.8%) of all trials with an average reaction time of 663 ms (SD = 79 ms).

Critically, during 24% of image presentations, we showed a composite image consisting of the expected image and an unexpected image (Figure  1a). These composite images allowed us to examine image ‑identity encoding separately for the expected and unexpected components by labeling each composite according to either the identity of its expected or unexpected component (Figure  1b). Moreover, to examine animacy encoding, we also labeled the composite images according to whether their expected and unexpected components were animate or inanimate (Fig 1c). To measure these two types of image component information encoding, we applied Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) separately for each electrode and labeling scheme to quantify each component’s identity information (IIM) and animacy information (AIM) over time. These two measures were computed based on the cross ‑validated posterior probability values of image components, as described in more detail in the Star ★Methods Section. 

As a sanity check, we first quantified IIMs and AIMs for the base images. Consistent with previous 

work 16,17 , this analysis revealed robust decodability of base ‑image identity and animacy from 68  ms 

and 136  ms after stimulus onset, respectively (Supplemental Figure). Identity and animacy were also decodable for both expected and unexpected components at levels clearly above chance (Fig.  2a,b,d,e), although overall less strongly than in the base trials. This reduction was anticipated, given that for the composite images, the LDA classifier had to extract information from lower ‑contrast images while simultaneously unmixing them from the overlapping component images.

P < 0.005; t(14399)  = 3.24–14.70; IIM  = 0.0003–0.0041). # Discussion 

Taken together, the identity and animacy results reveal a consistent temporal pattern of how expectations shape visual encoding. For both features, expectation initially enhances the encoding of the expected image components, suggesting an early phase during which prior knowledge is integrated with sensory input to support veridical perception. This early facilitation was followed by a later phase during which encoding was stronger for the unexpected components, indicating increased processing of surprising information. This biphasic pattern, therefore, provides direct support for the proposal that perceptual expectations first promote Bayesian integration to optimize current perceptual inferences, and subsequently amplify the encoding of unexpected events to refine 

future predictions 13 .

When comparing the temporal dynamics of expectation effects on animacy and identity encoding, a striking difference becomes apparent. Although expectation enhances the encoding of expected component identity and animacy within the same temporal window, the subsequent enhancement for unexpected components unfolds differently across the two feature domains. Specifically, animacy

This temporal offset suggests that surprising stimuli first trigger an increased awareness of the kind of unexpected event that occurred, its broad, categorical “gist”, before the system resolves the more fine ‑grained perceptual details needed to identify what exactly was surprising. In this sense, our findings resonate with the broader “forest before the trees” principle articulated in 

reverse hierarchy theory 18 , in which coarse, global representations are accessed earlier and guide the 

subsequent refinement of detailed perceptual interpretations. 

All expectation ‑related effects we observed emerged no earlier than 300  ms after stimulus onset, far too late to reflect modulation of the initial feedforward sweep. This timing is later than recently 

reported expectation effects on EEG responses 19 , but it aligns with studies that suggest that 

expectation effects are predominantly cognitive rather than strictly perceptual in nature 20,21  . The 

temporal profile of our effects also overlaps with the highly discussed N400 component 22 . Although 

interpretations of the N400 vary, from semantic retrieval and contextual integration to signaling prediction error, contemporary accounts increasingly view it as a graded index of contextual 

facilitation and prediction ‑confirmation during semantic access 23 . The succession of opposing 

expectation effects we observe refines and unifies these perspectives: expectations modulate brain activity through a biphasic mechanism, with an initial enhancement of encoding for expected image components followed by a later prioritization of unexpected components. This temporal pattern suggests a dynamic interplay between early facilitation driven by prior knowledge and subsequent prediction ‑error computations at higher categorical levels, consistent with Bayesian accounts of 

perception and enabled by recurrent interactions within the visual hierarchy 24 . Our findings also 

coincide temporally with recurrent high ‑level effects in the lateral occipital cortex, indicating that 

prior ‑induced top ‑down processing can enhance representations of expected stimulus features 25 .

In conclusion, this EEG study reveals that expectations shape visual processing through a biphasic mechanism: the brain first uses prior knowledge to enhance the encoding of expected information, and then shifts to amplify surprising events. Therefore, our data suggests that Bayesian integration of sensory information and enhanced processing of surprising events unfold sequentially rather than simultaneously in the human brain. Intriguingly, for unexpected stimuli, the brain prioritizes the encoding of high ‑level categorical features before resolving their detailed identity, a pattern that echoes the principles of reverse ‑hierarchy theory, in which coarse, global representations precede fine ‑grained perceptual detail. Finally, the relatively late timing of both effects supports recent 

proposals that expectation effects are more ‘cognitive’ than ‘perceptual’ in nature 20 .

# Experimental model and study participant details 

## Human Subjects 

Six participants took part in six EEG sessions (three females and three males, age range 19 to 38 years). All three authors participated in this study. Experimental procedures were approved by the Ethics Committee of the Chamber of Physicians in Hamburg, and participants provided written informed consent. 

# Method details 

## Stimulus Generation and Presentation 

Stimuli consisted of four grey-scale natural images of a cat, an elephant, a car, and a house. Images were downloaded from the Pixabay and Unsplash stock photo websites under their free-to-use licences. Images were transformed into grey-scale images, and we applied a fade-out mask to them to make the images' peripheral and central areas grey. Subsequently, we reduced low-level differences across images by applying the spectrum, histogram, and intensity normalisation and 

equalisation (SHINE) toolbox 2 for MATLAB. All images were presented using PsychoPy on a 23-inch 

TFT-Screen (SyncMaster P2370; Samsung, Seoul, South Korea) positioned centrally 110 cm in front of

the participant. The circular stimulus area had a radius of 7.24-degree visual angle (°), the central grey area had a radius of .59°, and the black fixation dot had a radius of .08°. Composite images were created while running PsychoPy by drawing one image in the background and drawing the second image with 50% opacity on top of it. All base images were presented with 75% opacity. 

We presented these stimuli to participants in a deterministic fixed repeating sequence (as shown in Fig. 1) during six 1.5-hour EEG sessions on six different days. Each of these sessions was subdivided into five ten-minute runs to accommodate short breaks for our participants. We presented each of the six possible repeating sequence orders (1234, 1243, 1324, 1342, 1423, and 1432) once to each participant (n = 6) during one of the six EEG sessions. Note that one can present a fixed repeating sequence of four images in six and not in twenty-four manners because, e.g., the image orders 1234, 2341, 3412, and 4123 give rise to the same repetitive sequence (...1234123412341234 …). As a result, each of the four base images and each image component had a perfectly balanced stimulus history across all six sessions. The first image within a sequence was pseudorandomly selected such that each of the four images was shown at least once, but no more than twice, as the first image of a sequence across the six sessions. During a trial, an image was presented for 250 ms followed by a 900-1400 ms inter-stimulus interval, during which only the grey background and the black fixation dot were presented. Participants were instructed to fixate the central fixation dot throughout the experiment and to press a button whenever a catch trial (upside-down base image) was presented. 

Note that twelve unique composite images entail the six possible image pairs presented under two different expectation conditions. Hence, the composite image containing the elephant and the house, shown in Figure 1, was presented when a house was expected and when the elephant was expected. Composite images were pseudo-randomly interspersed in the image sequences while ensuring that each composite image was preceded by at least two base images. To preclude the effects of direct repetition, neither the expected nor the unexpected component of each composite image was based on the preceding base image. As a result, across all six sessions, physically identical composite images had the same balanced one-back history across expectation conditions. E.g., cat-house composites were equally often preceded by a car and an elephant stimulus, both for composite images presented when a cat and when a house image was expected. 

## EEG Recording and Preprocessing 

EEG data were sampled at 250  Hz using 64 active electrodes positioned according to the extended 10–20 system (ActiCap, Brain Products, Gilching, Germany) and recorded with BrainVision Recorder.

## Quantification and statistical analysis 

Because expectation effects in EEG can be subtle and difficult to detect 8, we collected a large amount 

of data per participant: 2,400 composite ‑image trials and 10,200 base ‑image trials for each of our six participants. Data were analysed using a fixed ‑effects approach, providing high statistical power while acknowledging that the resulting effects cannot be expected to generalize beyond the measured sample. 

We performed the LDA analysis separately for each participant, using 10 ‑fold cross ‑validation, to obtain posterior probability values for left-out base images and for left-out expected and unexpected components of the composite images. Posterior probabilities for composite images were computed after labeling them as either their expected or unexpected components (Fig.  1), yielding image identity measures (IIMs). Analogously, labeling composite images by the animacy of their expected and unexpected components (cat, elephant vs. house, car) yielded raw animacy identity measures (AIM_raw). To obtain AIM values that more specifically reflect animacy rather than idiosyncratic low ‑level image features, we also computed AIM_control values based on the two alternative pairings (cat, house vs. elephant, car; cat, car vs. elephant, house). These AIM_control values contain no animacy information but capture low ‑level visual structure we aimed to control for. Final AIM values were therefore computed by subtracting the average AIM_control value from AIM_raw. All IIM and AIM values were computed in a mass ‑univariate manner, separately for each timepoint and each electrode (excluding eye electrodes, n  = 60). 

During six of our eight analyses (Figure  2a,b,d,e; Supplemental Figure a,b), we performed one-sided t-tests (positive only) to determine if observed IIM and AIM values were significantly greater than chance level and zero respectively. For base images, this test was applied across all single ‑image IIMs from all participants. For the expected and unexpected components of the composite images, two separate t‑tests were applied across all expected and unexpected component IIMs from all participants. The remaining two analyses (Figure  2c,f) examined how expectation modulated IIM and

To correct for multiple comparisons across electrodes and timepoints, we applied the 

Benjamini–Hochberg procedure to control the false discovery rate (FDR) 10 . Corrected p-values were 

thresholded at p < .005. To further ensure that the observed effects represented robust neurophysiological signals rather than transient artifacts or spurious statistical noise, we imposed additional spatiotemporal cluster thresholds. First, we discarded significant effects lasting only two consecutive timepoints or fewer ( ≤ 8ms). This temporal constraint is based on the principle that meaningful ERP components typically span longer durations (e.g., >10–20 ms) due to the underlying 

kinetics of neural populations 12 . Second, we applied a spatial electrode-cluster threshold, rejecting 

effects that, at any given timepoint, were supported by fewer than three electrodes. This approach 

aligns with established cluster-based permutation logic 14 , which leverages the fact that neural 

sources are projected via volume conduction across multiple scalp sensors. By requiring spatial and temporal contiguity, we prioritize effects with high biological plausibility and replicability, effectively filtering out isolated, non-physiologically grounded Type I errors.
