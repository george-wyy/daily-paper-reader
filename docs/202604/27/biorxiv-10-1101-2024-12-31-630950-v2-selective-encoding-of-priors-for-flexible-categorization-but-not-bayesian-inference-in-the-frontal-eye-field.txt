Abstract 35 

Expectations or prior beliefs about the world have been shown to modulate sensory processing 36 

both at the behavioral and neural levels. Bayesian models predict that such priors compensate 37 

for input uncertainty to optimize sensory judgments. Although Bayesian behavior is prevalent 38 

across sensorimotor systems, the relationship between priors and Bayesian inference is not 39 

obligatory. Priors may simply shift one’s internal decision boundaries without interacting with 40 

sensory uncertainty at all. We recently showed that humans and monkeys use both Bayesian 41 

and non-Bayesian strategies when reporting judgments of visual stability across saccades, 42 

despite using priors in both cases. While they increased prior use to compensate for internal, 43 

movement-driven sensory uncertainty in a Bayesian manner, they decreased prior use when 44 

faced with external, visual image uncertainty. The latter, “anti-Bayesian” pattern was best 45 

explained by a model in which category boundaries were adjusted by the prior but susceptible to 46 

image noise. Here, we recorded neural activity in the frontal eye field (FEF), a prefrontal region 47 

important for visuosaccadic behavior, while toggling between subjects’ prior use for Bayesian 48 

and anti-Bayesian behavior via trial-by-trial manipulation of the two uncertainty conditions. First, 49 

we found that FEF activity signaled the priors in both conditions. The prior-related modulation of 50 

activity, however, predicted only the anti-Bayesian, categorization behavior. The results suggest 51 

that neural activity in the FEF reflects the use of a flexible decision boundary for the perception 52 

of visual stability and, more generally, that neural mechanisms for Bayesian inference and 53 

visual categorization are dissociable and distributed in the primate brain. 54

Introduction 67 

Priors about the environment are known to modulate sensory-guided behavior and its 68 

associated neural activity (Summerfield and de Lange, 2014; Bansal et al., 2018; de Lange et 69 

al., 2018). Bayesian models, which predict that priors may compensate for uncertainty in 70 

sensory inputs, have been successful at explaining many behaviors (Jacobs, 1999; Ernst and 71 

Banks, 2002; Weiss et al., 2002; Knill and Saunders, 2003; Kording and Wolpert, 2004; 72 

Girschick et al., 2011; Fetsch et al., 2012; Darlington et al., 2017; Jazayeri and Shadlen, 2010). 73 

However, prior use does not necessarily imply Bayesian inference. Priors may simply alter 74 

behavior in a flexible, context-dependent manner without compensating for uncertainty as 75 

Bayes’ rule demands (Green and Swets, 1966). 76 

We recently demonstrated this dissociation between priors and Bayesian inference for 77 

visual perception across saccadic eye movements (Subramanian et al., 2023). The challenge of 78 

maintaining coherent perception across saccades is fundamental to primate behavior. Each 79 

saccade causes the retinal image to move, requiring the visual system to disambiguate 80 

saccade-induced retinal shifts from external object movement. One way to solve this problem is 81 

by using a copy of the saccadic command (corollary discharge) to predict the expected visual 82 

inputs after a saccade (Sommer and Wurtz, 2008). Inputs that match the prediction can be 83 

attributed to one’s own movement and mismatches to external movement. This process is 84

susceptible to two major sources of sensory uncertainty: 1) internally generated noise caused 85 

by a degradation of visual processing around the time of saccades, called saccadic suppression 86 

(saccade-driven noise; Zuber and Stark, 1966), and 2) external noise in the visual input (image 87 

noise ). We previously found that humans and monkeys use priors about the probability of 88 

external object movement (Rao et al., 2016; Subramanian et al., 2023). However, while prior 89 

use increased with internally generated uncertainty caused by their own eye movements 90 

(consistent with Bayesian predictions), prior use decreased with additional image noise. This 91 

“anti-Bayesian” pattern was consistent with the flexible adjustment of visual category boundaries 92 

across prior conditions. 93 

Here we studied the neural correlates of noise-affected visual stability judgments in the 94 

frontal eye field (FEF), a prefrontal region important for visual processing across saccades. We 95 

examined whether FEF activity encodes priors and, if so, whether the neural prior signals exhibit 96 

modulation more consistent with Bayesian inference or categorization strategies (Fig. 1a). The 97 

FEF is a primary candidate region for implementing the two computational strategies for the 98 

perception of visual stability across saccades (Fig. 1b; Sommer and Wurtz, 2008; Cavanagh et 99 

al., 2010; Crapse and Sommer, 2008; Hall and Colby, 2011; Rao et al., 2016; Subramanian et 100 

al., 2019; Cavanaugh et al., 2016). Some FEF neurons receive corollary discharge signals that 101 

remap their visual receptive fields to their post-saccadic locations before the onset of the 102 

saccade (Umeno and Goldberg, 1997; Sommer and Wurtz, 2006; Wang et al., 2024; Jin et al., 103 

2025). Since remapping allows neurons to sample the same region of space before and after a 104 

saccade, it can serve as the basis for predicting the expected post-saccadic visual input (Hall 105 

and Colby, 2011; Wurtz, 2018). Moreover, the FEF receives retinocentric information (Bruce 106 

and Goldberg, 1985) and signals the mismatch between this input and its predictions (Crapse 107 

and Sommer, 2012). 108

We recorded FEF neurons while subjects performed the image- and saccade-noise 109 

tasks, interleaved trial-by-trial. First, we found that FEF neurons were selective for priors. Then 110 

we evaluated whether the neuronal prior selectivity increased with saccade-driven noise (the 111 

Bayesian prediction) or decreased with image noise (consistent with visual categorization). We 112 

found that FEF activity did not significantly correlate with increasing prior use in the saccade-113 

driven noise task, but it did correlate with and predict decreasing prior use in the image noise 114 

task. The FEF’s selective participation in visual categorization but not Bayesian inference, even 115 

though both strategies were used behaviorally, suggests that they are mediated by dissociable, 116 

distributed circuits. 117

Receptive field mapping 147 

After a neuron was isolated in each session, we first mapped its visual, motor, or visuomotor 148 

spatial receptive field in two delayed saccade tasks. On every trial, the animal first fixated a 149 

white, central square (1° x 1°) for a randomized duration of 300-500m. All randomized delays 150 

reported in this manuscript were drawn from uniform distributions. A target square (also white 151 

and 1° x 1°) was then presented concurrently with the fixation square for a delay of 500-700ms. 152

target. Upon target acquisition, the animal was further required to fixate on the target for an 154

target, i.e., its onset, is separated by 500-700ms from the time that the animal makes a saccade 156 

to it, we can distinguish the neuron’s visual response (typically 50-100ms after target onset) 157

Experimental design and statistical analyses 167 

Saccadic Suppression of Displacement (SSD) task 168

tested the use of learned priors to compensate for internal, saccade-driven or external, image 170

Model fitting 334 

We fit the combined model to subjects’ behavioral responses on a session-by-session basis 335 

using maximum likelihood estimation, i.e., by minimizing negative log likelihood between the 336 

model’s output and subjects’ binary responses. To simulate the learned weights between the 337 

displacement inputs and the “jump” and “non-jump” outputs for each prior condition in the 338 

perceptron component, we used the value of the psychometric curve fit to all data in that prior 339 

condition for each session. 340 

We allowed 12 parameters to be free: 2 noise types for all 4 experimental conditions each, the 341 

width of the “jump” distribution, and 3 priors. Parameter optimization was implemented using 342 

MATLAB’s fmincon function. 343

Discussion 705 

While we previously showed that judgments of visual stability across saccades exhibit both 706 

Bayesian and anti-Bayesian effects of noise from saccadic and visual sources, respectively, 707 

here we found that FEF neural activity is significantly modulated only by the latter. This 708 

suggests that the two behavioral strategies are implemented by distinct circuits in the brain and 709 

that FEF is part of a pathway that selectively implements the anti-Bayesian behavior. Indeed, 710 

the timing of the observed prior signal in FEF, i.e., its emergence only at the reafferent event 711 

rather than when the prior is first introduced, is consistent with the dissociation between 712 

Bayesian inference and our proposed categorization model in FEF. For Bayesian inference, the 713

We consider a few additional points in interpreting the results. First, in addition to visual 747 

responses, many neurons in FEF exhibit activity synchronized to saccades (Bruce and 748 

Goldberg, 1985; Dias and Segraves, 1999; Robinson and Fuchs, 1969, Sommer and Tehovnik, 749

least part of the prior modulation we observed is due to the higher proportion of response 751 

saccades in the high prior condition. Indeed, individual neurons’ choice probabilities (Britten et 752 

al., 1996) related to whether the animal reported “jumped” or not were highly correlated with 753 

their prior modulation across all noise conditions (Fig. S9, top row). However, saccade-related 754 

modulation is not sufficient to explain our results. Importantly, response saccades were made 755 

into a neurons’ RF for all task conditions, whether uncertainty was introduced by image noise or 756 

saccade-driven noise (Fig. 2d). Indeed, we restricted our analyses to epochs where the spatial 757 

configuration of the task was not different between the image and saccade-driven noise 758 

experiments. If the observed activity were driven solely by the response saccade, then it would 759 

simply be correlated with the output behavior in all conditions (as predicted in Fig. 4d) rather 760 

than displaying a dissociation between non-Bayesian and Bayesian behavior across noise 761 

types. Consistent with this, neuronal choice probabilities related to “jump” vs. “non-jump” 762 

decisions did not show strong correlations with animals’ behavioral prior use on a neuron-by-763

Third, we consider the implications of our results for interpreting the role of Bayesian 800 

inference in sensory processing. The success of the Bayesian framework in explaining behavior 801 

has spurred a search for how Bayesian computations may be implemented by the brain. 802 

Leading theoretical proposals include the ideas of “Probabilistic Population Codes,” in which 803 

sensory variability automatically represents the likelihood and prior distributions required for 804 

Bayesian integration (Ma et al., 2006; Beck et al., 2008); of Bayesian inference via efficient 805 

coding, in which priors are inherently represented by the matched allocation of neural 806 

infrastructure to the natural statistics of the environment (Wei and Stocker, 2012; Ganguli and 807 

Simoncelli, 2014; Park and Pillow, 2024); and of predictive coding models, in which feedback 808 

projections convey prior information that is integrated with feedforward sensory likelihoods 809 

(Aitchison and Lengyel, 2017). These proposals have in common the intuition that Bayesian 810 

inference in simple sensory contexts results from the natural, evolutionarily shaped functioning 811 

of sensory circuitry rather than reflecting an explicit, reward-maximizing strategy that needs to 812 

be effortfully generated. If this were true, Bayesian inference stands to serve as a general 813

It is possible that our observed dissociation between computational strategies in the face of self-821 

generated vs. external uncertainty extends to other perceptual domains as well. Additionally, 822 

Bayesian models are an example of optimal, generative models, and the category-learning we 823 

proposed is an example of a simpler heuristic. Although there is growing interest in the relative 824 

contributions of optimal vs. heuristic models in sensory processing (Carandini, 2024; Gardner, 825

Bayesian inference remains predominant in explaining performance in simpler stimulus 827

Gaissmaier, 2011). Our results addressing the neural basis for how Bayesian and discriminative 830
