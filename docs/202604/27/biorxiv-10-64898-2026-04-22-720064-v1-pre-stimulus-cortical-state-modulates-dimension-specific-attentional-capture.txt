# Abstract 17 Salient distractors capture attention, but whether momentary cortical state modulates 18 capture — and whether this depends on attentional filtering routines — remains unclear. 19 Using fMRI during visual search in human participants (N = 34), we examined how pre-20 stimulus global signal (GS) interacts with dimension-specific capture and location-based 21 suppression learning. Same-dimension distractors activated frontoparietal regions (FEF, 22 IPS/SPL, SMA) and produced robust capture, whereas different-dimension distractors 23 produced little behavioral or neural capture, consistent with dimension-selective gating. 24 Pre-stimulus GS negatively modulated capture-related brain activation. Behaviorally, 25 however, GS effects diverged by learning history: GS predicted slower responses in 26 participants who learned location-based suppression for same-dimension distractors 27 (SS group), but faster responses in participants whose spatial learning involved 28 different-dimension distractors (DS group). Thus, the state indexed by GS did not have 29 uniform behavioral consequences. Instead, the findings suggest that the impact of 30 cortical state depends on the control demands and the filtering routine engaged, 31 consistent with broader views that arousal-related effects on performance vary with 32 processing mode and task utility. 33 34 

Key words : cortical state; selective attention; visual search; statistical learning; fMRI 35

# Introduction 37 A flash of color at the roadside, a sudden movement in a crowd: salient stimuli 38 constantly compete for our attention, often at the worst possible moment. Whether a 39 distractor derails behavior depends on something subtle: its relationship to the feature 40 dimension currently guiding search. A distractor that shares the target's defining 41 dimension, such as an orientation singleton appearing during orientation-guided search, 42 produces robust interference, whereas one defined in a different dimension causes little 43 if any disruption (Geyer et al., 2008; Kumada, 1999; Müller et al., 2009). The dimension-44 weighting account (DWA; Found and Müller, 1996; Müller et al., 1995) attributes this 45 effect pattern to the continuous allocation of attentional weight (a limited resource) to 46 the task-relevant feature dimension, thereby granting same-dimension distractors 47 privileged access to attentional processing while filtering out different-dimension ones 48 (Liesefeld et al., 2024, 2019). 49 Behavioral evidence strongly supports the DWA (for reviews, Liesefeld and 50 Müller, 2019; Müller et al., 2010). Inter-trial priming studies show faster target detection 51 when a singleton, ‘pop-out’ target is defined in the same dimension as that on the 52 preceding trial, with switch costs when the target-defining dimension changes (Found 53 and Müller, 1996; Müller et al., 2004). Distractors that share the target's dimension 54 produce stronger capture than different-dimension distractors, even when matched for 55 salience (Töllner et al., 2011), an effect modulated by top-down dimensional set (Müller 56 et al., 2009; Sauter et al., 2018). ERP evidence further indicates that dimensional 57 weighting operates at an early, pre-attentive stage: the N2pc component, which indexes 58 spatial attentional selection (Luck, 2012), is delayed upon switches vs. repetitions of the 59

Related fMRI work further indicates that 60 dimension changes and attentional reorienting recruit distributed control networks 61 spanning frontal, parietal, and posterior visual regions (Pollmann et al., 2000; Corbetta 62 et al., 2008; Serences et al., 2005). 63 Capture also varies substantially from trial to trial even for identical stimuli, 64 raising the question of what drives these fluctuations (Leber, 2010; Weissman et al., 65 2006). Attention networks do not operate in isolation from broader cortical state: 66 moment-to-moment fluctuations in global cortical activity may shape whether a67 distractor disrupts behavior on any given trial. The global signal (GS) in fMRI — the 68 average BOLD signal across the brain — fluctuates inversely with arousal: elevated GS 69 corresponds to reduced vigilance/arousal, as evidenced by negative correlations with 70 EEG alpha activity and behavioral measure of arousal (Chang et al., 2016; Wong et al., 71 2013). (2018) demonstrated that transient GS peaks in fMRI coincide with 72 electrophysiological signatures of momentary arousal drop and reduced activity in 73 arousal-regulating subcortical circuitry. If attentional control depends on pre-stimulus 74 bias settings, then fluctuations in cortical state may alter how effectively those settings 75 are implemented. We therefore asked whether pre-stimulus GS predicts trial-level 76 capture costs and whether such effects are detectable in capture-network activation. 77 Statistical learning of distractor locations provides a useful example of such pre-78 stimulus bias settings because repeated exposure to a frequent distractor location can 79 give rise to learned spatial suppression, reducing capture at that location over time 80 (Goschy et al., 2014; Wang and Theeuwes, 2018). Crucially, learned 83 suppression differs by distractor type: same-dimension distractors show robust 84 distractor-location probability-cueing effects mediated by early visual cortex suppression 85 (Zhang et al., 2022), whereas different-dimension distractors are handled at the level of 86 dimension-specific saliency signals before integration into the supradimensional priority 87 map (Liesefeld et al., 2019; Liesefeld and Müller, 2021), minimizing the need for 88 additional location-based probability cueing (Sauter et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019). 89 Moreover, acquired location-based suppression ‘templates’ do not simply act as fixed 90 inhibitory weights, but are dynamically maintained and regulated in a task-conditional 91 manner, activating or deactivating as task demands change within a trial episode 92 (Allenmark et al., 2024). The present study exploits this dimension-dependent contrast 93 to ask whether cortical state effects on capture scale with learned suppression strength. 94 Specifically, we addressed these open issues by analyzing an existing fMRI 95 dataset (Shi et al., 2026) recorded while participants performed a dimension-specific 96 visual search task: participants searched for a uniquely tilted target bar standing out in a 97 field of upright (and homogeneously colored) nontarget bars, while ignoring a singleton 98 distractor that was defined either in the same dimension as the target, orientation (a 99 more strongly tilted bar), or in a different color dimension – a uniquely colored upright 100 bar. Of note, such different-dimension distractors produce only minimal interference to 101 begin with, even though they are equated in terms of salience (i.e., feature contrast to 102 their surrounding items) with the same-dimension distractors, which generate strong 103 capture. # Methods 564 

Participants 565 We analyzed a publicly available dataset (Shi et al., 2026) comprising data from 566 thirty-five healthy adults who completed a training phase and an fMRI session (18 567 female; mean age = 24.3 years, SD = 3.2, range = 19–32). One participant was 568 excluded due to excessive head motion (>3 mm translation), yielding N = 34 for fMRI 569 analyses. There were two groups: the Same-dimension Spatial bias group (SS; n = 17) 570 and the Different-dimension Spatial bias group (DS; n = 17). The groups did not differ in 571 age, t(31) = 0.42, p = .68, or gender distribution, χ²(1) = 0.12, p = .73. 572 A post-hoc sensitivity analysis (two-tailed, α = .05, 80% power) confirmed that 573 the study was adequately powered for the primary within-subjects effects. For the fMRI 574 analyses (N = 34, one-sample t-test), sensitivity was d ≥ 0.49; for location learning (N = 575 17, SS group), d ≥ 0.70. The observed GS modulation effects (C4: d = 0.38–0.66) and 576 location learning effects (L1: d = 0.59–0.76) fell within the detectable range, consistent 577 with most reaching statistical significance. All key findings — GS × Group behavioral 578 dissociation, neural GS modulation in frontoparietal ROIs, and location-specific learning 579 in visual cortex — produced medium-to-large effect sizes that exceeded or approached 580 sensitivity thresholds, supporting the reliability of these results. 581

Drift Diffusion Model Analysis 750 To decompose RT into underlying cognitive processes, we applied the EZ-751 diffusion model (Wagenmakers et al., 2007): a closed-form approximation of the drift 752 diffusion model (DDM) that estimates three parameters from behavioral summary 753 statistics: 754 

Drift rate (v): The rate of evidence accumulation, reflecting the quality of 755 perceptual/attentional processing; higher drift rate indicates more efficient extraction of 756 task-relevant information. Boundary separation (a): The amount of evidence required 757 before committing to a response, reflecting response caution or the speed-accuracy 758 tradeoff. Non-decision time (t): The duration of processes outside the decision itself, 759 including perceptual encoding and motor execution. 760 Parameters were estimated from mean correct RT, RT variance, and proportion 761 correct following the closed-form equations of Wagenmakers et al. (2007); the scaling 762 parameter was fixed at s = 0.1, and edge correction was applied ( Pc clipped to [0.51, 763 0.99]) to avoid computational singularities. 764 EZ-diffusion was chosen over hierarchical DDM fitting (e.g., HDDM or brms-765 based approaches) for computational efficiency and robustness with limited trial counts 766 per cell. Furthermore, because EZ-diffusion operates on summary statistics rather than 767
