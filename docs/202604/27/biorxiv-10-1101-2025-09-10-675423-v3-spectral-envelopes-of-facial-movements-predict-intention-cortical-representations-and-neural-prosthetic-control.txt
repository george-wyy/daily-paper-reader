# 1 Abstract 

Animals, including humans, use coordinated facial movements to sample the environment, ingest nutrients, and communicate. Rodents, in particular, produce rhythmic facial movements during spontaneous behav-ior and cognitive tasks. Measuring these movements precisely and linking them to neural activity remains challenging. We introduce face-rhythm, an unsupervised pipeline that combines markerless point tracking, spectral analysis, and non-negative tensor component analysis to decompose facial video into a small set of interpretable components. Applied to videos of mice during a Pavlovian odor-reward task, a brain-machine interface (BMI) task, and free behavior, face-rhythm recovers human-interpretable behaviors such as whisk-ing, sniffing, licking, and more subtle behaviors. The resulting components are consistent across animals, are sufficient to decode task variables or internal belief states, and explain cortical activity using a low-rank representation. We also find that the activity of neurons in face-associated primary motor cortex (M1) is predicted well by a phase-invariant spectral transformation of facial movements above ∼0.5 Hz, while slower movements retain a phase-variant representation better predicted by the instantaneous position of the face; individual neurons can show either or both forms of tuning. A systematic comparison against deep-learning point-tracking models, contrastive-learning embeddings, and vision-transformer features places face-rhythm competitively across tasks while also achieving the goal of producing a low-dimensional, in-terpretable description of rodent facial behavior that is closely linked to cortical activity. 

# 2 Introduction 

A central question in motor neuroscience is how neural activity in the motor cortex is transformed into be-havior. The face is a uniquely useful system for investigating this question: sensory organs and dozens of muscles are densely clustered in a compact area, enabling a rich repertoire of coordinated movements that serve many ethological roles (Galen 1968). Facial behavior also embodies a high-dimensional projection of internal state (Darwin 1872; Dolensek et al. 2020), such that information about what an animal has ex-perienced, is experiencing, or will do is often represented in the appearance and movements of the face. Although several neural circuits that produce specific facial behaviors have been described, fundamental principles governing how cortical activity is represented in and transformed into coordinated facial move-1

Early examples of facial behavior analysis used manual codification to quantify static facial expressions and actions (Ekman and Friesen 1976; Bartlett et al. 1999; Calder et al. 2001; Cohn and Ekman 2008). Although these approaches remain useful, advances in machine learning have since produced more automated, ob-jective, and less invasive methods. Machine vision and unsupervised learning algorithms are now widely used to extract representations of facial pose and movement and to decompose them into lower-dimensional components. Focusing on tools applicable for nonhuman animals and rodents in particular, these methods can be divided into a few categories: pose tracking, pose description, and behavior description. Modern supervised facial pose tracking algorithms such as DeepLabCut (A. Mathis et al. 2018) and SLEAP (Pereira et al. 2022) use fine-tuned neural networks to perform markerless point tracking on defined body parts. The widespread adoption of pose tracking methods has democratized and rapidly advanced behavioral quantification methods, which are now commonly used in a large number of neuroscience studies. Despite ongoing efforts (Ye et al. 2024; Syeda et al. 2024), these tools often require extensive manual curation to track movements collected by different video sources across laboratories. Modern facial pose description methods, including most automatic facial action coding and facial expression recognition systems (Li and Deng 2022), are also highly useful; however, by construction, they do not analyze time-varying patterns. One example in rodents is the recent use of histograms of oriented gradients to describe emotional states (Dolensek et al. 2020). These pose description methods apply human-interpretable labels to static poses. In contrast, behavioral description methods typically chunk continuous behavior into interpretable ‘syllables’ of behavior, like rearing or scurrying. Notable models of this class include MoSeq (Wiltschko et al. 2015), B-SOiD (Hsu and Yttri 2021), and VAME (Luxem et al. 2022), among others (Batty et al. 2019; Bohnslav et al. 2021; Mackevicius et al. 2019), which have been applied to whole-body poses. These works highlight the dominant trend in systems neuroscience toward more expressive machine learning methods that consider higher-dimensional movement patterns. However, behavioral segmentation approaches represent move-ment as a sequence of discrete states, which does not capture the continuous and overlapping nature of the motor patterns that animals can express simultaneously. Whole-face video recordings can also be used to associate facial movements with neural data. A method uti-lizing principal component analysis (PCA) of pixel intensity changes called “motion energy” decomposition revealed that a large fraction of neural variance correlates with uninstructed facial movements (Musall et al. 2019; Stringer et al. 2019; Salkoff et al. 2020). FaceMap later demonstrated that markerless point tracking of rodent faces combined with analysis methods that considered temporal movement patterns significantly improved predictions of single-unit neural activity (Syeda et al. 2024). These approaches provide valu-able biological insight; however, the latent representations they produce tend to be high-dimensional and challenging to interpret. There remains a need for unsupervised models with greater inductive biases that allow for the generation of low-dimensional and interpretable components that match latent neural activity. Discovering methods with these properties greatly aids in our understanding of the motor system. Here, we present face-rhythm (FR), an analysis framework and pipeline designed to meet this need. FR per-forms dense-mesh point tracking, spectrally transforms tracked point movements, and decomposes them into a sparse set of interpretable components. This approach is motivated by studies of the function and connectivity of central pattern generators for facial movements (Cramer and Keller 2006; Kaku et al. 2025; Kleinfeld et al. 2023; Moore et al. 2013), as well as the success of multi-way tensor factorization methods in finding interpretable components within single-unit neural data (Williams et al. 2018) and time-frequency representations of EEG signals (Mørup, Hansen, and Arnfred 2007). Spectral representations are especially natural for central pattern generator (CPG)-driven behaviors because CPGs produce intrinsically oscilla-tory output, and descending cortical commands may modulate the amplitude envelope of these oscillations rather than their instantaneous phase. In essence, FR aims to discover and demix facial behaviors that over-lap in space, time, and spectral patterning to reveal latent “rhythms”. The architecture of the FR pipeline is designed to discover short temporal patterns (milliseconds to sec-onds) that can be mapped onto known behaviors in rodents (i.e., whisking, sniffing, licking, and more subtle motifs) or associated with performance variables in cognitive tasks. Consistent with achieving this goal, the application of FR to facial video data acquired during classical conditioning and brain-machine interface (BMI) tasks successfully decodes cognitive task variables and performance using a small set of FR 2

In addition, due to the existence of specialized motor circuitry for generating rhythmic facial behaviors in rodents, we hypothesize that the FR-identified patterns of facial movement map onto the natu-ral modes of activity in the facial motor cortex. # 3 Results 

## 3.1 Face-rhythm methodology 

The face-rhythm (FR) pipeline consists of 3 major components: 1) unsupervised and markerless keypoint tracking of a mesh of points distributed across the face; 2) transformation of the point-tracking signals into spectrograms; and 3) decomposition of a 3-dimensional tensor (spatial location by frequency by time) into components that describe discrete parts of behavior ( Figure 1A , and see Methods 5.1 ). The standard output of the pipeline is a set of components that each describes a single movement pattern. Each FR ‘component’ is defined by three ‘factors’: a spatial factor corresponding to the points on the face that move, a spectral factor that captures the dominant frequencies of the movement of those points, and a temporal factor that describes the movement magnitude of the component in each video frame ( Figure 1B-C ). 3

The output components of this pipeline have properties that are suitable for defining discrete behaviors. Spectral analysis results in non-negative data, which naturally allows for a non-negative decomposition constraint. Such matrix and tensor factorization methods are known to extract a ‘parts-based’ representation that tends to yield interpretable and ‘demixed’ components (D. D. Lee and Seung 1999). We find that, when applied to videos of mouse faces, FR components correspond to human-recognizable components of facial behaviors such as whisking, licking, and multiple forms of sniffing and twitching ( Figure 1C )(Supplementary Movie 1–2 ). This approach does not require training neural networks, maintains high interpretability, and can be used to analyze movements of individual mice captured in single video sessions. The non-negative tensor factorization method used does not impose an orthogonality constraint on the out-put components (as is seen in principal component analysis). An intuitive consequence of this is that mul-tiple components can be simultaneously expressed and contribute to compound or correlated behaviors. For example, whisking and sniffing are often expressed together, but are typically decomposed as separate components that can be coactive. More precisely, the set of all components sums to approximate the mag-nitude spectrograms from each point tracking signal, and each component tends to align with a statistical mode of the data that is typically interpretable as a discrete behavioral pattern. FR components can be matched across different animals in similar environmental conditions. We matched nnTCA components across 5 mice using Hungarian assignment (Williams et al. 2018) of FR spectral and spa-tial factors and found high cross-animal similarity significantly exceeding chance at all tested ranks ( Figure 1 supplement 2 ; p < 0.001 , permutation test; see Methods 5.2 ; 13 sessions; animals are head-fixed on a linear treadmill with infrequent random water rewards and random sensory stimuli; see Methods 5.11.2 ). 6

## 3.2 Comparison with motion energy principal component analysis 

Given that FR components align with natural facial behaviors, we examined how well FR temporal factors explain neural activity ( Figure 2 ). We used an established experimental approach to quantify how well facial behavior data can explain neural activity data (Musall et al. 2019), and compared FR temporal factors against other common methods like video motion energy PCA (ME PCA). We used the standard FR pipeline to perform point tracking and decompose facial movement ( Figure 2A ) and monitored neuronal activity with widefield fluorescence imaging of calcium transients reported by GCaMP6s. Neural activity was recorded cortex-wide in mice performing a visual discrimination task ( Figure 2B , Supplementary Movie 3 ). The task structure includes a visual stimulus, a subsequent delay, and a response epoch during which mice can respond to the stimulus by licking one of two water spouts. The posterior visual cortical regions respond strongest to the visual stimulus, whereas medial regions respond persistently during the delay period, and frontal regions are most active during the response period ( Figure 2B ). To link cortical activity to the behavioral video data, we fit a ridge regression model that explains neural activity (as measured by fluorescence transients) in each pixel of the widefield imaging as a weighted com-bination of different behavioral inputs, such as FR components or point tracking data ( Figure 2C ). Hierar-chical regression shows that FR temporal factors encode a low-rank representation that is significantly more aligned with cortical activity patterns than ME PCA ( Figure 2D ). In line with previous findings (Musall et al. 2019), the cross-validated explained variance ratio (EVR) of the model is consistently high across much of the cortex, with particularly strong performance in anterior regions. This shows that behavioral video data provides a robust basis for predicting cortical activity ( Figure 2E ). In a direct comparison of performance, the EVR obtained using only 10 FR components ( 0.245 ± 0.087 s.d.) exceeds that of 10 ME PCA components (0.144 ± 0.078 s.d.) ( p = 0 .0039 , Wilcoxon signed-rank test, n = 9 sessions from 7 mice; linear mixed-effects model (LMM) accounting for repeated sessions within mice: p = 4 .27 × 10 −8) and approaches the perfor-mance achieved with 200 ME PCA components ( 0.279 ± 0.066 s.d.), indicating that FR components are more predictive of cortical dynamics at matched rank. 9

To further test whether FR’s advantage depends on the specific tracker or on the spectral decomposition framework, we compared six input sources—FR, FaceMap (Syeda et al. 2024), CoTracker (Karaev et al. 2024) (a transformer-based dense tracker applied at FR mesh positions), CEBRA (Schneider, J. H. Lee, and M. W. Mathis 2023) (contrastive-learning embeddings of FR tracking data at dimensions 10 and 50), and frozen ViT-MAE embeddings (He et al. 2021) (768-dimensional CLS-token features per frame, motivated by BEAST (Y. Wang et al. 2025)). Each is processed through the same decomposition methods ( Figure 2 supplement 1C ). We find that FR-derived decompositions perform favorably against other keypoint-tracking-based approaches across nearly all decomposition methods. Deep network approaches that are trained by reconstructing raw image/video representations such as frozen ViT-MAE also perform well; however, they can use extra infor-mation about the frame such as hand and body posture, can require extensive fine-tuning, and their embed-dings are less directly relatable to fiducial points or muscle dynamics. We conclude that FR point-tracking provides a strong basis for decomposing facial behavior into a low-rank representation that is predictive of widefield cortical activity, and that spectral transformation plus non-negative TCA steps are critical for achieving this performance at low-rank. 11

## 3.3 Conditioning of facial behaviors during reward-guided tasks 

We examined whether FR captures uninstructed facial movements that are predictive of the conditioned-stimulus identity during Pavlovian/classical conditioning. We analyzed a separate facial video dataset ac-quired during the reward probability conditioning of a previously described task (Matsumoto et al. 2016) (Figure 3 ; see Methods 5.5 ). In this paradigm, head-restrained thirsty mice are presented with one of three conditioned stimuli (CS+, CS0, or CS-); after a 1 s odor presentation and 1 s delay, mice receive water, no out-come, or air puff, respectively ( Figure 3A ). FR components extracted from these videos show CS-dependent temporal modulation during the cue and delay epochs, including factors consistent with jaw/licking move-ments for CS+, respiration-related movement for CS0, and ear twitching for CS- ( Figure 3B , Figure 3D-E ). Using elastic net multinomial logistic regression on FR temporal factors across 20 sessions from 5 mice, CS identity is decoded with high accuracy: 0.92 ± 0.05 , 0.87 ± 0.03 , and 0.83 ± 0.01 (mean ± s.e.m.) for CS+, CS0, and CS-, respectively (all p < 0.003 , Bonferroni-corrected across the 3 CS classes, one-sample t-test against chance level of 1/3, N = 5 mice; Figure 3F ; see Methods 5.5 ). 15

## 3.4 Spectral transformation between facial behaviors and neural activity 

The accurate decoding of task variables from FR temporal factors described above demonstrates that a spec-tral transformation of facial movements yields a description that is related to cognitive and task variables (i.e., conditioned output expectation) and cortical activity (i.e., neural trajectories controlling a BMI). In addition, the existence of central pattern generators that control rhythmic facial behaviors motivates the hypothesis that a spectral transformation may lie between patterns of M1 neural activity and of facial move-ments. We therefore examined whether the activity of neurons in M1 is better explained by a direct trans-formation of facial position or by a spectral transformation of facial movements ( Figure 5 ). To systematically assess the degree to which phase information is conserved or spectrally transformed be-tween facial behavior and M1 neural activity, we developed three regression models ( Figure 5C ) that vary in whether phase information from behavior data can be used in predicting neural data. We compared the quality of the fits to quantify the degree to which phase information is encoded in M1 neural activity. In all of these models, the input is convolved with a learned kernel whose shape is optimized to minimize the output prediction error. The models differ in how this convolution step proceeds and, as a result, whether this step performs a spectral transformation, which here denotes an operation in which a signal is convolved with a kernel (real or complex-valued) and then the magnitude of the resulting output is taken. The first “basic convolution” model simply applies a standard real-valued convolution to map inputs to outputs. This transformation is not capable of extracting the oscillatory power envelope from the input signals and can only translate, smooth, sharpen, and shape signals; therefore, this model is phase-variant. The second “spectral convolution” model employs a complex-valued kernel. In this model, both the real and imaginary parts of the kernel are optimized independently, and then the magnitude of the convolution output is taken, which discards explicit phase information and results in a phase-invariant spectral transformation. Finally, the third “Hilbert-spectral convolution” model is similar to the spectral convolution model, but the imag-inary part of the complex kernel is constrained to be an exactly 90 ◦ phase-shifted version of the real part. This is achieved by optimizing only the real part and setting the imaginary part as its Hilbert transform, resulting in a complex spectral convolution that is matched in parameter number to the basic convolution. Assessing the performance of these three models provides a means to isolate the relative contributions of phase-variant and phase-invariant spectral representations in explaining the transformation from signals in one space to another (i.e., movement to neural space). We propose that this approach can be used to measure bounds on the relative amount of information that M1 encodes about the absolute position of the face versus the spectral content of movements in the face. 21

## 3.5 Similarity between face-rhythm components and neural activity components 

Based on the anatomy of the facial motor system as well as the utility of spectral transformations in explain-ing neural variance, we hypothesized that FR transformations may approximate the inverse of the transfor-mations present in the facial motor pathway. This hypothesis predicts that the signals observed along the temporal dimension of the behavior data prior to undergoing the tensor decomposition might be closely related to time-varying neural activity observed in cortex. Therefore, the application of a similar tensor decomposition method to neural data might yield temporal factors that are closely related to FR temporal factors. We examined whether a one-to-one mapping exists between FR temporal factors and the temporal factors ex-tracted by non-negative tensor decomposition of the simultaneously recorded neural data (in 2-dimensional data, this is better known as non-negative matrix factorization, or NMF) ( Figure 6A ). We recorded M1 ac-tivity using two-photon calcium imaging and simultaneously recorded facial behavior using a camera. Mice were free to run on a linear treadmill and given soy milk at random infrequent intervals. We found pairwise similarities in the temporal factors extracted from the two domains, despite the complete independence of the neural and facial movement analyses ( Figure 6B ). We also observed greater correspondence, as mea-sured with correlation coefficients, between neural temporal factors and FR temporal factors for FR compo-nents with a higher peak frequency in their spectral factor ( Figure 6C ), demonstrating a bias toward higher frequencies in the similarity between face-rhythm and neural components. Overall, 41 of 50 matched factor pairs ( 82 %; 70 –90 % per mouse, N = 5 mice) show significant correlations (Bonferroni-corrected p < 0.001 

against 25 ,000 circular time-shift shuffles; median r = 0 .35 , range 0.03 –0.69 ) ( Figure 6D ). Thus, the dom-inant modes of the recorded neural activity resemble the dominant modes of spectrally transformed facial movements. 25

# 4 Discussion 

This study introduces face-rhythm, an unsupervised analysis pipeline that combines markerless point track-ing, spectral analysis, and non-negative tensor component analysis to extract interpretable facial behaviors. The output components of face-rhythm are predictive of task and internal variables, and their time courses align closely with demixed neural activity components. Alongside the pipeline, we developed several sup-porting computational methods including a joint decomposition for discovering shared neural-behavioral motifs. In addition, we report that for facial movements above ∼0.5 Hz, primary motor cortex activity is better described by a phase-invariant spectral transformation of behavior than by the instantaneous point positions themselves. Together, these results support spectral analysis as a natural featurization for relating ongoing facial behavior to motor cortical activity. 

## 4.1 Comparison to other methods 

Automated facial and whole-body behavior analysis has advanced rapidly, and several tools are now in common use. Many new methods exploit machine learning architectures like deep neural networks and semi-supervised frameworks (e.g., DeepLabCut-Live! (Kane et al. 2020), SLEAP (Pereira et al. 2022), VAME (Luxem et al. 2022), CEBRA (Schneider, J. H. Lee, and M. W. Mathis 2023)) to analyze and decompose movements. Although powerful, large neural networks pose challenges for data-constrained experiments. Supervised approaches require manual labeling, and all networks trained on specific datasets are typically only usable on new datasets that are similar to the training data. In contrast, the FR pipeline is end-to-end unsupervised and can be run on small and even ‘ N = 1 ’ datasets; the individual tracking, transformation, 29 

> was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission. The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 23, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.09.10.675423 doi: bioRxiv preprint

and decomposition methods are not trained models, and thus have no dataset-specific learned biases. The standard FR pipeline provides a principled approach to behavioral decomposition and is appropriate for use within defined conditions. In particular, FR works best when the behavior of interest moves like a deformable mesh without occlusions, a spectral transformation is a reasonable representation of underlying movement, and a sparse parts-based continuous-time description of behavior is desirable. 

## 4.2 Interchangeability with other methods 

The FR pipeline is composed of three modular analysis steps: unsupervised point tracking, spectral transfor-mation, and tensor decomposition. Although we present specific, optimized implementations for each stage, FR is designed as an extensible framework that can be integrated with other behavioral analysis pipelines. This modularity offers several avenues for customization. For instance, the initial modified Lucas-Kanade-based point tracking could be replaced with outputs from deep-learning-based pose estimation tools like DeepLabCut, SLEAP, CoTracker, FaceMap, or other methods, allowing researchers to feed pre-existing key-point data directly into FR’s spectral analysis stage. Alternatively, FR’s point-tracking output could be used for different decompositions, as was shown in this work. Finally, the final nnTCA-derived temporal factors from FR can serve as low-dimensional inputs for subsequent behavioral segmentation algorithms such as MoSeq or B-SOiD. The codebase was developed with this interchangeability in mind and is designed to be a flexible, rather than monolithic, tool. 

## 4.3 Relationship of FR factors to neural activity 

Our findings echo the concept of motor primitives (Shadmehr and Mussa-Ivaldi 1994; Hogan 1984; Botvinick, Niv, and Barto 2009) and align with recent evidence on central pattern generators in rhythmic behavior (Dempsey et al. 2021; Moore et al. 2013; Kaku et al. 2025). Together, these results suggest that M1 employs a multiplexed encoding strategy, integrating phase-variant and phase-invariant representations, reminiscent of representations in sensory systems like the auditory and somatosensory cortices (Peterson and Heil 2019; Bendor and X. Wang 2007; Ewert, Vahle-Hinz, and Engel 2008). Practically, closed-loop brain-machine inter-faces and neural decoder designs may benefit from the introduction of spectral transformations into models. Previous studies have also shown that a large fraction of the total observed variance in neural activity can be explained by spontaneous and uninstructed behavior (Musall et al. 2019; Stringer et al. 2019), and that point-tracking and convolutional neural networks can improve estimations of neural variance (Syeda et al. 2024). The observations that higher frequency behaviors are more likely to be represented in M1 via a spectral transformation, and that the major axes of M1 activity align with spectrally transformed behavior, are consistent with existing theories for how high-frequency rhythmic motor behavior is produced. Our findings are consistent with the hypothesis that motor cortical regions propagate phase-invariant movement signals to lower central pattern generators, where absolute amplitude is converted into the amplitude of oscillatory components (Cramer and Keller 2006; Hill et al. 2011; Friedman, Zeigler, and Keller 2012). The dominance of spectral-envelope representations of higher frequency movements in cortex has several implications for coding efficiency and the architecture of the motor system. First, because spectral envelopes have lower frequency content than time-domain phase-variant signals, they require less bandwidth and im-pose lower bit-rate demands; such representations are also easier to maintain and propagate through the noisy low-rank dynamics thought to characterize motor cortex (Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic 2018; Omrani et al. 2017; Laughlin, De Ruyter Van Steveninck, and Anderson 1998). Second, a related implication is that the rhythmic motor patterning itself may be compartmentalized, with discrete central pattern generators acting as low-plasticity reservoir networks that offload specialized computation to specialized anatomical struc-tures (Kaku et al. 2025). Finally, if the primary output of motor cortical regions encoding higher frequency behaviors is a phase-invariant spectral envelope sent to CPGs, then fine cortical control of instantaneous movement phase may be limited for these behaviors, or a secondary descending signal carrying explicit phase information may interact with brainstem CPGs. 30

## 4.4 Analysis of conditioned uninstructed movements 

Our observation of subtle behaviors that are robustly associated with, and predictive of, brain-machine interface performance highlights the need for careful behavioral tracking during BMI tasks. In addition, it suggests that micromovements, while not explicitly required to perform a BMI task, may be present in many commonly studied BMI tasks and calls into question the utility of using the lack of grossly observ-able movement as a common inclusion criterion. In the BMI decoding analysis, the similar performance of all four decoders (Points-linear, FR-linear, FR-RNN, and Points-RNN) is consistent with the interpretation that much of the decodable reward-related signal is broadly accessible across both high-dimensional and low-rank feature representations, and is retained after FR dimensionality reduction. This supports the in-terpretation that FR temporal factors provide a compact representation that preserves behaviorally relevant information while still capturing temporal structure important for decoding. A study may demonstrate that BMI-cursor-related neural activity is output-null and volitionally controlled, which can provide evidence of sufficiency to claim that embodied computations are not involved. However, the corollary that output-potent neural activity implies the existence of task-critical non-local computation is not necessarily true. Instead, a more thorough attempt to identify uninstructed BMI-associated behaviors is warranted to better interpret the strategy taken by brains and animals to achieve control over cursor dynamics. Similar consid-erations apply for the analysis of signals acquired during tasks with waiting periods, such as the Pavlovian odor association task in Figure 3 . Uninstructed movements and associated neural activity present during the waiting period can cause spurious correlations between movements, cognitive signals, and task vari-ables (Mohebi et al. 2024). These observations argue for monitoring subtle uninstructed movements during BMI experiments. 

## 4.5 Computational tools 

Several computational approaches were developed here to improve on existing methods: a mesh-physics-constrained modification of the Lucas-Kanade algorithm that reduces noise and drift; an exact formulation of the variable-Q transform with more flexible filter design; convolutional reduced-rank regression models that build a spectral transformation into the inductive bias; and a coherence-based joint tensor decomposition for identifying shared neural-behavioral components. The face-rhythm software package and related code is made available here: https://github.com/RichieHakim/face-rhythm . See Methods 5.16 for details on software dependencies and all other code made available for this work. 

## 4.6 Conclusion 

Face-rhythm (FR) is an unsupervised, data-efficient method for decomposing facial movements into inter-pretable behavioral components. Applied to rodent facial video, the standard pipeline recovers common behaviors such as whisking, sniffing, and licking, along with subtler movements that track internal cogni-tive states, task variables, and dominant modes of motor cortical activity. These components are consistent across mice, indicating that the decomposition captures stereotyped motor patterns rather than animal-specific artifacts, and they highlight the value of measuring subtle uninstructed movements in behavioral analyses, particularly during BMI experiments. In the recording conditions used here, most M1 neurons showed a significant relationship to the spectral envelope of higher frequency facial movements, and a smaller fraction were phase-locked to instantaneous movement; individual neurons could show both. To-gether, these results support a role for phase-invariant spectral representations of movement in rodent facial motor cortex, and motivate the use of explicit spectral transformations in models that relate cortical activity to ongoing behavior. 31

# 5 Methods 

## 5.1 Face-rhythm pipeline 

The standard face-rhythm pipeline consists of three major steps: (1) point-tracking, (2) spectrogram gener-ation, (3) dimensionality reduction via tensor factorization. These steps are separable and composable. A user-oriented library has been developed and is available here: https://github.com/RichieHakim/face-rhythm 

5.1.1 Defining the region of interest / point positions 

A region of the face is circumscribed, and equidistantly spaced points are placed to fill the region. The interpoint distance is set such that there are around 500 –3000 points. For analyses in which multiple sessions or multiple animals are used, a set of points is defined on one mouse face, rigid image registration is performed to align the fields of view, and then the points are warped to matched positions across all recording sessions. Rigid registration is performed using the roicat.tracking.alignment 

module from the ROICaT library: https://github.com/RichieHakim/ROICaT 

5.1.2 Point-tracking 

Point tracking is performed using the Lucas-Kanade sparse optical-flow algorithm (Lucas and Kanade 1981; Baker and Matthews 2004) and recursively integrating velocities calculated for each point after each frame. Two additional forces were applied to each point after every frame. First, a ‘mesh physics’ force similar to Hooke’s law was used to shape the way individual points could move. Each point was ‘pushed’ and ‘pulled’ by its neighbors to maintain the local inter-point distances. Second, a gentle ‘relaxation’ force was applied to each point toward its initial position to counteract drift. Optical-flow was chosen for its efficiency and lack of domain priors (i.e., it is not trained on a specific dataset). We use OpenCV’s (Bradski 2000) implementation which exposes a variety of parameters to the user. The optical-flow local integration window size (parameter name: ‘winSize’) is set to be equivalent to roughly 1–3 mm in the field of view, which is typically around 20 –60 pixels in our recordings. The optimiza-tion parameters were chosen to minimize drift and jump errors (‘maxLevel’ ≈ 3, ‘maxCount’ ≈ 3). Contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization (CLAHE) is applied to each frame of the input video prior to point tracking using OpenCV’s createCLAHE class. This normalization step improves robustness to occlusions (such as whisker and tongue movements), changes in lighting, and drift. Output 

The resulting point-tracking tensor is denoted as X ∈ RT ×P ×2, where T is the number of frames, P is the number of points, and the last dimension contains the coordinates ( x and y) of each point. The elements of the output tensor contain the tracked position of each point for each time frame. 

5.1.3 Spectrogram generation 

Spectrograms are computed using a custom implementation of the variable-Q transform (VQT) (Brown 1991; Chatterji et al. 2004) (see Section 5.14 for details), followed by normalization. A spectrogram is com-puted for each point from the point-tracking output separately for the x and y dimensions and is denoted as S ∈ RT ×P ×F ×2, where F is the number of VQT filters/frequencies. For consistency in future steps, the spectrogram is reshaped to S ∈ R2P ×F ×T , by concatenating the x- and y-displacements along the points dimension. The time dimension is typically downsampled ( ∼15 Hz) relative to the original point-tracking tensor ( ∼250 Hz).

This normalization boosts the variance al-lotted to higher frequencies, time periods with less movement, and points with more subtle movements. 5.1.4 Dimensionality reduction via tensor factorization 

Non-negative tensor component analysis (nnTCA) is applied to the spectrogram tensor (which is naturally non-negative) to obtain a set of components. Each output component describes a unique ‘part’ or ‘kind’ of behavior, as defined by each component’s unique combination of spatial, spectral, and temporal factors. Tensor factorization is performed using CANDECOMP/PARAFAC (CP) decomposition (Harshman 1970; Paatero and Tapper 1994; Carroll and Chang 1970; Mørup, Hansen, and Arnfred 2007; Williams et al. 2018). The primary optimization algorithm used is non-negative hierarchical alternating least-squares (nn-CP-HALS); though for very large tensors, alternative algorithms are used, such as custom mini-batch implemen-tation of nn-CP-HALS or tensorly’s Alternating Optimization Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (AO-ADMM) algorithm. The tensorly library was used to perform all tensor factorization operations (Kos-saifi et al. 2018). The input spectrogram tensor is denoted (same as above) as S ∈ R2P ×F ×T . The output of the tensor factor-ization is a set of components, each with a spatial, spectral, and temporal factor. The number of components in the decomposition is defined by the user-defined ‘rank’ R parameter. Each component can be denoted as a set of vectors (sometimes called a Kruskal-tensor), one for each factor: [[ A, B, C]] = {ar , br , cr }Rr=1 , with the following loadings: • ar ∈ R2P or A ∈ R2P ×R: ‘Spatial factor’ loads onto face points (both x and y). • br ∈ RF or B ∈ RF ×R: ‘Spectral factor’ loads onto VQT filters. • cr ∈ RT or C ∈ RT ×R: ‘Temporal factor’ loads onto time bins. The components can be combined to reconstruct a rank-R approximation of the original spectrogram tensor. ## 5.4 Widefield imaging regression comparison analysis 

5.4.1 Video motion energy and PCA analysis 

To capture facial movements from the behavioral video, we computed the absolute difference between pixels across each frame to generate the video motion energy (video ME). To isolate movements of specific parts of the face, we manually selected a rectangular area of 50 pixels around the nose, jaw, or whiskers and created the average ME across all pixels within each area. To reduce the dimensionality of the behavioral data, we then used principal component analysis (PCA) to compute the 200 highest-variance dimensions. These dimensions accounted for at least 90% of the total variance in the data, indicating that 200 dimensions were sufficient to describe the main temporal dynamics in the video. We then used the PCA scores as temporal components of animal behavior and used either 10 or all 200 components in the linear encoding model to predict cortical dynamics. 

5.4.2 Widefield imaging and ridge regression analysis 

Experimental details of the cortical widefield imaging are described previously in Musall et al. 2019 (Musall, Kaufman, et al. 2019). For data processing, we reduced the dimensionality of the video data with singular value decomposition (SVD) and retained the top 200 components, which explained ≥ 95 % of the total vari-ance. SVD yielded spatial components (U; pixels × components), temporal components (VT; components 

× frames), and singular values (S). Subsequent analyses were performed on the product SVT to reduce computational load. SVT was high-pass filtered above 0.1 Hz using a zero-phase lag, second-order Butter-41

worth filter. To project results back into pixel space, outputs were multiplied with U. All widefield data were rigidly aligned to the Allen common coordinate framework. To link behavioral video components to cortical activity, we used a linear encoding model with a ridge penalty where the regularization penalty λ was estimated separately for each column of the widefield data (Couto et al. 2021; Musall, Sun, et al. 2023). 5.4.3 Decomposition ablation analysis 

To evaluate the contribution of each pipeline stage, we compared the standard FR decomposition (rank 10 non-negative TCA on VQT magnitude spectrograms) against alternative decompositions spanning three families. Spectrogram-domain methods operate on the VQT spectrogram tensor with the original shape 

R2×P ×F ×T , which is flattened along the x- and y- coordinate dimensions to give R2P ×F ×T . The nnTCA-spectrogram (rank 10) decomposition operates directly on this 3D tensor, while all 2D decompositions op-erate on a flattened version of this tensor ( R2P F ×T ); these include PCA (rank 10 and 1000), NMF (rank 10), and related decompositions. Point-tracking-domain methods operate on the raw displacement traces (prior to any spectral transforma-tion) with the original shape ( R2×P ×T ). For the “TCA: 3D unflattened point tracking rank=10” decomposi-tion only, the original 3-way point-tracking tensor was used, preserving the separation between spatial and coordinate dimensions. For matrix decomposition methods like PCA, the x- and y-coordinate dimensions were first concatenated along the spatial dimension to form a 2D matrix ( R2P ×T ). For the “PCA: lagged point tracking” decomposition, time-shifted versions of the 2D matrix were concatenated to construct a truncated Hankel (time-delay embedding) matrix with 200 lags ( ∼3.1 s), then PCA (rank 1000) was per-formed; anticausal, causal, and centered lag variants were tested and the center lag is presented in the main text. For the “TCA: bandpassed point tracking rank=10” decomposition, the 2D flattened point tracking matrix was bandpass filtered using the same spectral filters as the VQT spectrograms, then the resulting bandpassed tensor was reshaped to R2P ×F ×T and nnTCA (rank 10) was performed on this tensor. All decompositions were aligned to the widefield imaging timebase and evaluated using the same cross-validated ridge regression framework (Section 5.4.2). The same set of decompositions was applied to each alternative input source (FaceMap, CoTracker, CEBRA, and ViT-MAE; see below), enabling controlled com-parisons across tracking and embedding methods. For decompositions (like ‘PCA with rank=1000’), the requested rank serves as an upper bound on the number of extracted components: the achieved rank equals min (requested rank , input feature dimensionality ). For example, using CEBRA embeddings with 10 di-mensions as input, requesting rank 1000 yields the same result as requesting rank 10.

5.4.5 CoTracker point-tracking comparison 

To test whether FR’s custom Lucas-Kanade tracker could be replaced by a modern deep-learning tracker, we applied CoTracker v3 (Karaev, Rocco, et al. 2024; Karaev, Makarov, et al. 2024) to the same behavioral videos used in the widefield regression analysis. CoTracker is a transformer-based dense point tracker; we used the pretrained offline model ( scaled_offline.pth ) with 6 refinement iterations. Query points were initialized at the FR mesh positions ( ∼1,481 points per session). Videos were processed in non-overlapping 300-frame chunks, with tracked positions carried across chunk boundaries. CoTracker outputs absolute pixel coordinates; to obtain displacement signals comparable to FR, the temporal mean position was subtracted from each point. The resulting displacement tensor was processed through the full decomposition ablation pipeline (Section 5.4.3), aligned to the widefield timebase, and evaluated via the same ridge regression framework (Section 5.4.2). 

5.4.6 CEBRA comparison 

To evaluate whether a learned nonlinear embedding could substitute for the explicit spectral transforma-tion in FR, we applied CEBRA (Schneider, Lee, and Mathis 2023), a contrastive-learning method that op-timizes an InfoNCE objective (Oord, Li, and Vinyals 2019) to map high-dimensional time series into low-dimensional embeddings. We used the self-supervised CEBRA-Time variant (positive pairs defined by tem-poral proximity, no behavioral labels). The encoder was a single-frame MLP ( offset1-model , 128 hidden units, GELU activations, L2-normalized output), trained with batch size 1024, learning rate 10 −4, for 5,000 iterations. Input to CEBRA was the first 50 PCA components of the flattened FR point-tracking data. Em-beddings were computed at dimensions 10 and 50. Each embedding matrix was reshaped to T × d × 1 (where d is the embedding dimension) and processed through the full decomposition ablation pipeline, including VQT spectrogram computation, all decompo-sition methods, temporal alignment, and ridge regression evaluation. 

5.4.7 ViT-MAE feature extraction 

Motivated by BEAST (Wang et al. 2025), which demonstrated that vision-transformer representations of behavioral video predict neural activity, we evaluated frozen ViT-MAE embeddings as an alternative be-havioral representation. We used a ViT-Base/16 model pretrained with masked autoencoding (He et al. 2021; Dosovitskiy et al. 2021) (checkpoint: facebook/vit-mae-base ). To restrict the encoder to the same facial region used by FR, video frames were masked to the face region defined for FR point tracking (Sec-tion 5.1.1); pixels inside the region retained their original values, and pixels outside were set to zero. Masked frames were then resized to 224 × 224 pixels, normalized to ImageNet statistics, and passed through the en-coder with masking ratio 0. The CLS-token embedding (768 dimensions) was extracted per frame using batched inference (batch size 128, float32). CLS embeddings were aligned to the widefield timebase by linear interpolation. For compatibility with the decomposition pipeline, the 768-dimensional embedding was reshaped to 384 × 2 pseudo-channels; this reshaping is numerically transparent because all decomposition methods except TCA operate on a flattened 2D matrix and are therefore invariant to the (768 , ) versus (384 , 2) representation. VQT spectrograms were computed on all 768 channels using the same parameters as for FR data. The full decomposition ablation suite was then applied. We additionally evaluated 200-dimensional SVD and 10-dimensional PCA reduc-tions of the CLS embeddings directly via ridge regression. 43

## 5.5 Pavlovian conditioning analysis 

To analyze the utility of FR components in describing uninstructed behaviors during Pavlovian condition-ing, we used a separate facial video dataset acquired during the high- and low-reward probability classical conditioning tasks described previously (Matsumoto et al. 2016). These facial video data were not analyzed in that study and are distinct from the 60-Hz eye-video dataset used there for eye-blinking analysis. 

5.5.1 Behavioral paradigm and data acquisition 

Thirsty mice were head-fixed and randomly presented with one of three conditioned stimuli (CS) odors on each trial: • CS+: odor predictive of a water reward, • CS0: odor predictive of no outcome, • CS-: odor predictive of an aversive air puff. In the high-reward probability task, reward was delivered in 90% of CS+ trials, whereas in the low-reward probability task, reward was delivered in 20% of CS+ trials; air puff was delivered in 90% of CS- trials in both task variants, as described previously (Matsumoto et al. 2016). Each trial consisted of a 1 s CS odor presentation, followed by a 1 s delay epoch and then the unconditioned stimulus (US). Analyses were restricted to cued trials, yielding 240 analyzed trials per session (80 trials per CS class), across 20 sessions from 5 animals. Behavioral event times were taken from the original experimental records for alignment of FR temporal factors to task events. Continuous video of the face was recorded at ∼ 30 Hz. The standard face-rhythm pipeline was used to extract 10 temporal factors using right-asymmetric (‘causal’) kernels/filters (see Section 5.14.2 for details). The four temporal factors shown in Figure 3D-E were selected based on visual inspection for display purposes only; all 10 temporal factors were used for the classification analysis. 

5.5.2 Elastic net CS classifier 

CS identity was predicted from FR temporal factors using multinomial logistic regression with elastic net regularization (L1 +L2 penalty). For each trial, the 10 temporal factor traces were concatenated across all time bins in a [−2.5, +1 .9] s window relative to CS onset ( ≈25 bins per factor, ≈250 features total), ending one time bin before US onset ( 2.0 s) to exclude the US-evoked response. Regularization strength was selected via cross-validation within each session, and classifier performance was evaluated using stratified k-fold cross-validation ( k = 5 , 20 repeats). For each session, a confusion matrix (rows: true class, columns: predicted class) was computed by summing across folds and row-normalized so that each entry gives P (ˆ c | c). The null expectation for each cell is the marginal prediction frequency P (ˆ c), estimated from the column sums of the raw confusion matrix; under a classifier with no information about the true class, all rows of the confusion matrix are identical and equal to these marginal frequencies (sometimes referred to as a ‘mean expectation model’). Per-animal means were computed by averaging across sessions within each animal. Significance for each cell was assessed with a two-sided one-sample t-test ( N = 5 animals), testing whether the observed confusion-matrix entry differed from the corresponding null expectation P (ˆ c) of 33.3%. A multiple-comparison Bonferroni correction over classes would give α/3 = 0 .017 .

5.11.4 BMI design 

Mice are trained to control their neural activity to achieve rewards in a closed-loop brain-machine interface (BMI) experiment. The data gathered during this experiment fall into two categories: (i) baseline recordings of natural behavior prior to training, and (ii) BMI training sessions in which mice are provided with real-time feedback of their neural activity and are rewarded for achieving a target. During BMI training sessions, the neural activity of the animal is processed, decoded, and converted into a controllable auditory and visual cursor that is played back to the animal in real-time to allow the animal to learn to control the cursor’s dynamics (see Section 5.11.6 and Section 5.11.7 for details on decoder design and Section 5.11.8 on the decoder forward algorithm). The input to the decoder is the instantaneous activity of all neurons being monitored in the field-of-view (FOV). The output, referred to as the ‘cursor’, is a bounded scalar value c ∈ [0 , 1] representing the animal’s instantaneous performance. The cursor value is converted into a tone frequency that is played back to the animal, as well as the position of an illuminated LED on a linear LED strip. If the cursor value is above a certain threshold, the animal is also rewarded with a drop of soy milk. 5.11.5 Baseline session recordings Baseline session Baseline recordings are collected for 1–4 days / sessions prior to BMI training. Reference image acquisition A three-dimensional reference image is acquired on the first day of the base-line sessions to enable precise spatial alignment across all experimental days. A dense z-stack is acquired with 41 optical slices at a 0.8 μm step size, spanning a total axial range of 32 μm centered around the chosen focal plane. Each slice is averaged across 10 acquisition iterations with 60 frames each to create a high-quality reference image for each z-position. The final z-stack that is used for online motion correction is a subset of the dense z-stack, consisting of 5 evenly spaced slices at 4 μm intervals for computational efficiency during online motion correction.

Lastly, a scalar power coefficient p is used to control the angular selectivity of the decoder. An offline simulation of BMI performance is conducted on the selected factor using the baseline recording dataset to validate the decoder’s performance and tune any task-related parameters. We typically select the factor with the second highest explained variance, dependent on the simulation being able to achieve a satisfactory number of rewards. We avoid choosing w1, the first factor / principal component, as a target factor, as a previous report suggests that the first factor is significantly related to a “neural engagement” during the trial period (Hennig et al. 2021). The main parameter that is tuned is τR, which defines the cursor reward threshold; this value is tuned so that the simulated reward rate is ∼ 1.25 rewards per minute. Qualitatively, the cursor output increases linearly with increases along the axis of the selected factor (i.e., the ‘decoder axis’), but falls off sublinearly with angle changes away from the decoder axis. When the power coefficient is zero ( p = 0 ), the decoder becomes a simple projection magnitude decoder, and when p → ∞ ,the cursor output is only non-zero when the neural activity is perfectly aligned with the factor direction. For the BMI task, we use p = 2 to balance selectivity and linearity. 

5.11.8 Task structure 

Here, we describe the methods used to control the task’s state-transitions and the delivery of rewards during the BMI task. The task is structured as a state machine with several states: (i) Trial periods, when the sensory feedback is on and the animal is eligible to receive a reward, (ii) reward periods, when the feedback is briefly paused at the current value and a ∼3.5 μl soy milk droplet is delivered, (iii) the inter-trial-interval, when feedback is off and rewards are not available, (iv) timeout periods when the animal has not achieved a reward over the maximum trial period duration ( ∼20 s), and (v) ‘waiting for quiescence’ periods where the animal must reduce its neural activity along the decoder axis in order to start a new trial period. 55

On ‘day 0’ (defined as the day prior to the first BMI session with feedback), the decoder weights are com-puted, ROI masks are defined, and a simulation is conducted to set the cursor reward threshold. The decoder weights are then used to compute the cursor value in real-time during the BMI sessions. Closed-loop latency was found to be 10 –25 ms, defined as the time from the end of scanning a frame to the output of new feedback output signals. 

Non-rigid image transformation 

To compensate for tissue deformation over days/sessions and maintain spatial correspondence of neural decoder masks across sessions, we apply a non-rigid transformation to the ROI masks, neuropil masks, z-stack, and spatial decoder weights on each experiment day. To compute the warping field, we use MAT-LAB’s imregdemons function with 500 iterations across 4 pyramid levels and an accumulated field smoothing parameter of 2. The reference image is acquired on ‘day 0’ and each experiment day we acquire a 10-minute-long calibration recording and generate an image to warp to the reference. During this time, the animal is in an idle state without sensory feedback. On each experiment day, after manually aligning the current field of view to coordinates from the day 0 session, a mean reference image is computed using an average of the most stable frames (identified by minimal motion artifacts). Then, we apply a non-rigid image warp to ensure robust coordinate alignment between sessions. 

Motion correction 

Online motion correction has two components: a fast planar correction to the current imaging frame and a slower axial correction to a rolling average of the imaging plane. 

Planar correction Online motion correction during a BMI experiment is implemented using a custom phase-correlation algorithm. To improve robustness, we apply bandpass filtering to the input images us-ing a third-order Butterworth filter with cutoff frequencies at [1/64, 1/4] cycles/pixel (image resolution is cropped from 512 × 1024 to 512 × 512 ). 

Axial correction For online axial (‘z-axis’) drift correction, we use the warped reference z-stack (from day 0) to compare the current image frame in order to determine the current z-slice position. A rolling average of 60 frames is correlated against the warped z-stack, and the estimate of current z-position is determined by maximum phase-correlation. When the estimated current z-position is different from the center plane, we move the imaging plane by 0.5 μm in the direction of the center plane. Axial drift correction is only performed during the inter-trial-interval (ITI) period of the task.

## 5.14 Variable-Q transform (VQT) 

The VQT is a time-frequency analysis method that is similar to the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) but with a logarithmic frequency scale and a tunable frequency-dependent bandwidth. The VQT is often used to allow for low-frequency signals to be represented with high temporal-resolution and low frequency-resolution (Q ≈ 4), while high-frequency signals can be represented with low temporal-resolution and high frequency-resolution (Q ≈ 20). The VQT is related to a Morlet wavelet transform, and is similar to the constant-Q transform (CQT) but with a variable Q-factor. 58

## 5.17 Best practices 

5.17.1 Video capture and acquisition 

Although FR is fairly robust to acquisition conditions, several choices can improve the discovery and signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of the extracted components. Acquisition frame rates should satisfy the Nyquist crite-rion for the highest frequency of interest; in practice we use 60 –250 Hz to capture the full range of rodent fa-cial rhythms (e.g., whisking up to ∼25 Hz, licking ∼5–10 Hz, sniffing ∼3–15 Hz). Spatial resolution should provide ∼10 –30 px /mm over the region of interest, ensuring that the Lucas-Kanade integration window (∼1–3 mm, Section 5.1.2) contains adequate local texture. Global-shutter sensors are preferred over rolling-shutter sensors to avoid intra-frame motion artifacts, and short exposure times (e.g., <4 ms) reduce motion blur. Heavy video compression, especially codecs with temporal compression (e.g., low-bitrate H.264), can intro-duce block artifacts that propagate through point tracking, and thus should be avoided. In our experiments, we use MPEG-2 compression at a high bitrate (e.g., >50 Mbps), though many other settings may be suitable. 60 

> was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission. The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 23, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.09.10.675423 doi: bioRxiv preprint

Stable, high-contrast illumination is the second major determinant of tracking quality. We illuminate the face with bright and diffuse infrared light (two 50 W, ∼850 nm LED chips, invisible to rodents) positioned at a distance to produce shadowing over the face. 

5.17.2 Processing hardware 

The face-rhythm pipeline is designed to run on consumer-grade hardware. For video processing, a modern multi-core CPU with at least 16 GB of RAM is recommended for the kinds of datasets presented in this work (∼1500 points, ∼1 hr duration, ≥ 600 × 600 pixels). For spectral analysis and tensor decomposition, any CPU is sufficient, but a GPU with at least 8 GB of VRAM is recommended for efficient processing.
