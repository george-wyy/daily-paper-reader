Corresponding author Eye tracking (ET) plays a critical role in augmented and virtual reality applications. However, rapidly deploying high-accuracy , on-device gaze estimation for new products remains challenging because hardware configurations (e.g., camera placement, camera pose, and illumination) often change across device generations. Visual foundation models (VFMs) are a promising direction for rapid training and deployment, and they excel on natural-image benchmarks; yet we find that off-the-shelf VFMs still struggle to achieve high accuracy on specialized near-eye infrared imagery. To address this gap, we introduce DistillGaze , a framework that distills a foundation model by leveraging labeled synthetic data and unlabeled real data for rapid and high-performance on-device gaze estimation. DistillGaze proceeds in two stages. First, we adapt a VFM into a domain-specialized teacher using self-supervised learning on labeled synthetic and unlabeled real images. Synthetic data provides scalable, high-quality gaze supervision, while unlabeled real data helps bridge the synthetic-to-real domain gap. Second, we train an on-device student using both teacher guidance and self-training. Evaluated on a large-scale, crowd-sourced dataset spanning over 2,000 participants, DistillGaze reduces median gaze error by 58.62% relative to synthetic-only baselines while maintaining a lightweight 256K-parameter model suitable for real-time on-device deployment. Overall, DistillGaze provides an efficient pathway for training and deploying ET models that adapt to hardware changes, and offers a recipe for combining synthetic supervision with unlabeled real data in on-device regression tasks.

## 1 Introduction 

Eye tracking (ET) is a core sensing capability for augmented and virtual reality (AR/VR) applications, enabling foveated rendering, gaze-driven interaction, and attention-aware interfaces [ 1]. However, building a production-grade ET system is costly and time-consuming. Conventionally, teams have to iterate through hardware prototyping, calibration, data collection, and annotation to obtain ground-truth gaze supervision [ 2]. Each new device generation can trigger a repeat cycle of data acquisition and model retraining [ 1] due to hardware configuration changes (such as sensor characteristics, camera placement, optics, illumination, or on-device image processing). Moreover, gaze supervision itself is often unreliable: even controlled protocols suffer from imperfect fixation and incomplete compliance, producing label noise that is difficult to detect at scale [ 2]. A low-cost and efficient approach to rapidly train and deploy high-accuracy eye tracking is needed to accelerate product iteration and development cycles. Recently, visual foundation models (VFMs) such as DINOv3 [ 3] and SAM3 [ 4] have dramatically improved representation quality and transfer across natural image benchmarks, offering the promise of bypassing costly and time-consuming labeled real data collection for eye tracking. However, these gains have been less consistent in specialized imaging modalities, where both the sensing modality and downstream objective can differ substantially from web images. Consequently, many prior works have developed specialized foundation models tailored to domains such as medical imaging [ 5, 6], remote sensing [ 7, 8], and autonomous driving [ 9]. On-device ET for AR/VR suffers from the same problem: images are often infrared near-eye, and contain sensor-, device-, and subject-specific nuances. Our proposed approach consists of two stages, illustrated in Figure 1b. In the first stage (blue arrow), we optimize an off-the-shelf VFM for the eye tracking domain by leveraging both labeled synthetic images and unlabeled real data. Notably, no real labeled data is needed, significantly reducing data collection complexity. By combining labeled synthetic data with unlabeled real data, we can successfully transfer priors of off-the-shelf VFMs to the eye tracking domain, bridging the domain gap between web-scale pretraining and task-specific requirements. In the second stage (red arrow), we distill the optimized model into a lightweight student with two complementary objectives: representation alignment with the fine-tuned VFM teacher, and consistency regularization via an exponential moving average (EMA) of the student itself. The distilled model preserves high-quality ET performance while remaining suitable for real-time on-device deployment. We evaluate DistillGaze on a large-scale ET dataset collected with Project Aria glasses [ 2] from over 2,000 crowd-sourced participants under in-the-wild conditions. Without ground-truth gaze labels on real data, our approach yields substantial improvements over synthetic-only baselines in both median and tail error. Our optimized VFM reduces the median gaze error of the median subject (E50U50) from 3.48 ◦ to 1.33 ◦ (61.78% reduction), and the 90th-percentile error for 90th-percentile users (E90U90) from 14.84 ◦ to 8.32 ◦ (43.78% reduction). Our final distilled model achieves 1.44 ◦ E50U50 and 8.45 ◦ E90U90, improving 58.62% and 42.91% over the synthetic-supervised baseline, respectively, while maintaining a 256K-parameter model size suitable for real-time on-device deployment. However, intra-subject distributions exhibit smooth gaze-correlated structure, indicating strong potential for adaptation. 

• We propose DistillGaze , a two-stage framework that first optimizes a VFM using synthetic labels and unlabeled real data–requiring no real-world ground truth–then distills it to a compact student suitable for on-device deployment. 

• We demonstrate state-of-the-art results on a large-scale Project Aria eye tracking benchmark, achieving significant gains over synthetic-supervised baselines while requiring no ground-truth gaze labels on real data and no increase in model size. 

## 2 Related Works 

2.1 ET in AR/VR 

ET is essential for AR/VR applications. Typically, ET takes an eye image as input to predict the gaze directions (pitch and yaw angles). Eye tracking methods fall into two categories: geometry-based and learning-based approaches. Geometric methods assume an explicit eye model (e.g., sphere-on-sphere) [ 10 , 11 , 12 ]and optimize gaze to align predictions with image observations. While interpretable and efficient, these methods struggle with large gaze angles and occlusions. Learning-based methods are now dominant, with CNN and transformer architectures achieving improved robustness and accuracy. However, these methods are usually fully supervised and require large amounts of annotated gaze data to generalize across users and conditions. For instance, much prior work focuses on webcams or screen-mounted cameras that capture near-frontal eye views and relatively controlled illumination [ 13 , 14 , 15 , 16 , 17 ]. In contrast, head-mounted infrared (IR) cameras in AR/VR headsets operate at extreme off-axis angles with limited field of view, producing images with perspective distortion, partial iris visibility, and strong specularities due to active IR illumination [ 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 , 2]. An additional challenge is label quality: even controlled calibration protocols suffer from imperfect fixation and user compliance, introducing supervision noise that is difficult to detect at scale [2]. To address data acquisition and label quality challenges, synthetic data and domain adaptation methods have gained interest in recent years. Synthetic eye images [ 22 , 23 ] can be generated at scale, covering subject diversity and imaging conditions that are impractical to capture with calibrated ground truth [ 1]. Yet, the synthetic-to-real domain gap limits transfer performance, capping accuracy when training relies primarily on rendered data. Domain adaptation offers a complementary strategy to bridge the synthetic-to-real gap [ 24 , 23 , 25 , 26 , 27 , 18 ]. Most recently, self-supervised learning has emerged as a promising technique to train encoders with unlabeled data, significantly improving cross-domain generalization [28, 29]. 

2.2 Self-Supervised Representation Learning 

Self-supervised learning (SSL) learns representations by encouraging invariance across augmented views of unlabeled data. Modern SSL methods can be categorized into four broad families [ 30 ]: contrastive learning, self-distillation, canonical correlation analysis, and masked image modeling. Early contrastive methods [ 31 , 32 ]require large batch sizes or memory banks, limiting scalability. Self-distillation methods often adopt a student-teacher framework in which the teacher is an exponential moving average (EMA) of the student to prevent collapse [ 33 , 34 , 35 , 3], achieving state-of-the-art performance. Canonical correlation analysis based methods, such as [ 36 ], avoid representational collapse through variance-invariance-covariance regularization without imposing categorical structure on the embedding space. Mask-image-modeling methods [ 37 , 38 , 39 , 35 ]take inspiration from language modeling and predict masked out tokens on the images. Most SSL methods are evaluated on classification or segmentation benchmarks, and many use loss functions that encourage a soft clustering of representations. These self-supervised learning techniques lead to visual foundation models when combined with large web-scale datasets. 2.3 Visual Foundation Models and Knowledge Distillation 

Foundation models pretrained on large-scale data transfer strongly to diverse downstream tasks. CLIP [ 40 ]aligns visual and language representations via contrastive pretraining on web-scale image-text pairs. SAM3 [ 4]provides promptable segmentation with text and visual prompts for open-vocabulary detection. DINOv3 [ 3]scales self-supervised learning to a family of models of different sizes, yielding features with improved semantic structure. Despite success on natural images, we find these models transfer poorly to near-eye gaze estimation due to the significant domain shift between web-scale RGB images and the infrared, off-axis imagery of AR/VR headsets. Beyond domain shift, VFMs achieve their best performance at large scale but are impractical for on-device deployment. Knowledge distillation (KD) can transfer representations from a larger teacher to a smaller student. There are many early KD works, including [ 41 , 42 , 43 , 44 , 45 ], that have laid the foundation for modern approaches, leveraging soft target logits, intermediate feature matching, attention map alignment, relational structure preservation, and contrastive objectives. Recently, Community KD [ 46 ] has been developed for language modeling, where multiple online students are trained jointly through co-distillation, along with a pretrained teacher, enabling bidirectional knowledge transfer. We build on Community KD, adapting it to combine supervision from an optimized teacher and from self-distillation of the student.

With the optimized VFM from Section 3.2, we now distill its representations to a lightweight model suitable for on-device deployment. We present a distillation framework to transfer the learned representations to a compact student network. As illustrated in Figure 2b, our framework comprises three models: 

• A large teacher ft: our finetuned VFM from Section 3.2; 

• A student fs: an on-device model pretrained on synthetic data; 

• An EMA student fe: an exponential moving average of fs.Our design is inspired by community KD [ 46 ] with key differences: (1) we distill in both feature space (via a VIC-KD loss [ 47 ]) and output space (via pseudo-labels); and (2) one student is updated via EMA rather than gradients, drawing inspiration from momentum-based self-supervised learning [ 33 , 34 ]. All three models share a common structure: a backbone extracts image features, followed by projectors and a prediction head. Projectors map backbone outputs to a representation space for distillation, while heads predict gaze. The teacher uses the optimized VFM backbone ft and gaze head gt from Section 3.2; the student models use backbones and heads pretrained on synthetic data. Projectors are randomly initialized. During training, the teacher and EMA student receive weakly augmented images Xw, while the student receives strongly augmented images Xs.Given an image, the teacher produces embedding ht = ft(Xw), projection zt = pt(ht) for distillation, and gaze prediction ˆyt = gt(ht). The student backbone fs extracts features hs = fs(Xs), which are mapped to projections zs→t = ps→t(hs) and zs→e = ps→e(hs) for distillation with the teacher and EMA student, respectively. The head gs produces gaze prediction ˆys = gs(hs). The student receives gradient updates from all loss terms. The EMA student maintains backbone fe, projector pe, and head ge, all updated via exponential moving average from fs, ps→e, and gs, respectively. This yields stable embedding he = fe(Xw),projection ze = pe(he), and prediction ˆye = ge(he).We train the framework with complementary objectives in both feature and output space. For teacher-student distillation in feature space, we use an invariance loss between teacher and student projections, along with variance and covariance regularization: 

LKD = λinv ∥zt − zs→t∥22 + λvar v(zs→t) + λcov c(zs→t), (10) where v(·) and c(·) denote variance and covariance regularization terms [ 36 ]. λinv , λvar , and λcov are hyperparameters. The teacher also provides pseudo-labels: 

LPseudo-t = LGaze ( ˆ yt, ˆys). (11) For self-distillation with the EMA teacher, we use an L2 alignment loss and pseudo-label supervision: 

LSD = ∥ze − zs→e∥22; LPseudo-e = LGaze ( ˆ ye, ˆys). (12) The total loss is: 

L = λt(LKD + LPseudo-t) + λe(LSD + LPseudo-e) (13) where λt and λe are hyperparameters to balance two learning objectives. λe follows a cosine schedule, prioritizing distillation from the optimized VFM first, then adding EMA self-distillation as training progresses. This combines the benefits of both training paradigms: the student acquires knowledge from the VFM, and further refines these representations through EMA self-distillation with stable targets. At inference time, we deploy the on-device student network fs.

## 4 Experimental design 

4.1 Dataset 

We evaluate our method using data collected with Project Aria [ 2 ]. Following the Aria convention, our real dataset comprises 6,299 recordings from 2,222 crowd-sourced participants, split into training and validation 6sets with 1,825 and 397 participants, respectively. Gaze ground truths of the 1,825 participants in the training set are not used during training to mimic unlabeled data streams. Gaze prediction uses binocular eye-tracking camera streams from two global-shutter monochrome cameras with diffused IR illumination. The ET cameras record at 20 fps for approximately 60 seconds per recording; we subsample by a factor of 10 for training efficiency. Each eye camera captures at 640 × 480 resolution, which we downsample to 320 × 240 following [ 2]. The dataset provides aligned 3D gaze targets, from which we compute gaze rays using a fixed origin derived from a nominal interpupillary distance of 63.5 mm. Our synthetic dataset consists of 165K frames with 998 subjects, rendered using Blender. 

4.2 Implementation Details 

Our on-device ET pipeline comprises 256K parameters and uses an FBNet [ 48 ] backbone shared across left and right eye images. We train with AdamW and cosine learning rate schedule following a 10% warm-up period, using a batch size of 256. The learning rate is adjusted between 10 −3 and 10 −5; each experiment trains for up to 50,000 iterations. For self-distillation, EMA teacher weights are updated every 100 iterations with a momentum coefficient between 0.95 and 0.99. The feature projection head consists of three fully connected layers with GELU activations. For experiments with VFMs, we initialize the models using the ViT-B variant of DINOv3 [ 3], which has 86M parameters. All experiments are conducted on 4 × NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs. 

Image transformations. We use two augmentation pipelines: strong augmentation for the student and weak augmentation for the teacher and EMA student. Weak augmentation consists of gamma jitter and random scaling. Strong augmentation additionally applies camera and illumination augmentations, each with probability 0.3. Camera augmentations simulate sensor degradation via modulation transfer function (MTF) filtering, motion blur, and compression artifacts. Illumination augmentations include brightness and contrast adjustment, glint inpainting, random shadows, and coarse dropout. 

4.3 Evaluation Protocol 

We evaluate gaze estimation accuracy using the angular deviation between predicted and ground truth 3D gaze vectors, reported in degrees. For each frame, angular errors are averaged across left and right eyes. Following [49, 2], we report results using an Error-User (EU) table to capture both frame- and subject-level performance. We summarize each user’s per-frame error distribution via percentiles E50, E75, and E90, then aggregate across users via U50, U75, and U90. E50/U50 reflects the median error for the median user, while E90/U90 characterizes tail performance. For efficiency, we uniformly sample 64 frames per recording; 9 frames are used for test-time personalization, and metrics are computed for the remaining 55 frames. We report 95% confidence intervals via a hierarchical bootstrap over users and frames (1,000 iterations).

5.2 Optimize VFM Teacher for Eye Tracking 

We optimize our VFM by combining synthetic supervision and in-domain self-supervised learning. Unsupervised domain adaptation (UDA) is a natural way to leverage synthetic data to optimize VFM. DARE-GRAM [ 50 ] is a state-of-the-art UDA method designed for regression tasks. Unfortunately, as shown in Table 1, it performed worse than DINOv3 SynFT, potentially due to overfitting to the synthetic data distribution, which may amplify the mismatch between synthetic and real data. We then optimize the VFM using self-distillation and synthetic supervision as proposed in Section 3.2. The result is shown in Table 1. Compared to DINOv3 SynFT, our optimized VFM reduces E50U50 error from 2.01 ◦ to 1.33 ◦, a 33.83% relative improvement. Gains are consistent at the tail: E90U90 decreases from 12.11 ◦ to 8.32 ◦ (31.30% improvement). Notably, our optimized VFM substantially outperforms the synthetic on-device baseline (1.33 ◦ vs. 3.48 ◦ at E50U50, a 61.76% reduction). This demonstrates that self-supervised learning on unlabeled real data can effectively bridge the synthetic-to-real gap. Our optimized ViT-B VFM serves as the distillation source in Section 5.3, as our ultimate goal is a deployable on-device model. 

5.3 Distill to On-Device Model 

We evaluate our distillation framework (Section 3.3) to train our on-device ET model. DistillGaze is benchmarked with DARE-GRAM [ 50 ], and several knowledge distillation baselines, including VIC-KD [ 47 ]and Community KD [46], with detailed results in Table 2. 

Methods without VFM. To demonstrate the value of optimizing the VFM, we trained the small, on-device model without VFM using synthetic supervision and unlabeled real data. We conducted two experiments. First, we trained DARE-GRAM, a state-of-the-art UDA method. It reduced E50U50 to 2.96 ◦, outperforming training with synthetic supervision. Second, we conducted self-distillation following Section 3.2, using an on-device model, which reduces E50U50 to 2.20 ◦, a 36.9% improvement. Teacher loss function. Conventional self-supervised methods [ 34 , 35 ] employ centering and prototype-based soft clustering to prevent representation col-lapse. However, we find that a simple MSE regression loss substantially outperforms the DINO objective in our setting (Table 3). We attribute this to two key factors. First, soft clustering encourages discrete, categorical representations that may limit the fine-grained resolution required for continuous regression tasks like gaze estimation. Second, our use of syn-thetic supervision provides an inherent regularization signal that prevents collapse, reducing the reliance on these explicit mechanisms employed in self-supervised settings. 

VFM model architecture. One of our hypotheses was that ConvNext models would perform better than ViTs, since eye tracking features are concen-trated near the pupil, and model performance may be constrained by ViT tokenization resolution. In-terestingly, as we can observe in Figure 5, this is not the case. ViT-B exhibits stronger linear probe performance, and this advantage propagates through synthetic finetuning and self-distillation. However, the performance gap progressively narrows. Notably, both VFMs substantially outperform the on-device model with synthetic supervision baseline, which we attribute to the larger capacity of the models and the strong priors inherited from DINOv3 initialization. We speculate that peripheral regions of the eye visible within each patch may provide sufficient contextual cues, enabling ViT to match or exceed ConvNext’s performance. 10 5.5 Limitations and Future Work 

While our method substantially improves over on-device baselines, a gap remains to the fully supervised upper bound, using data that would not be available during product development. This gap is larger at the tail, suggesting that challenging cases, such as users with atypical eye appearance or extreme gaze angles, remain difficult without labeled supervision. Potential directions include improved synthetic data, stronger self-supervision objectives, or lightweight online adaptation. Additionally, training and evaluation are conducted on a single device configuration. Extending this framework to multiple devices with varying camera geometries, illumination, and sensor characteristics is an important direction for future work. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We presented DistillGaze, a framework for rapid deployment of on-device ET systems leveraging VFMs, without requiring real ground-truth gaze supervision. Our work reveals that while DINOv3 representations of ET images are clustered by subjects, intra-subject distributions exhibit gaze correlation. To address this finding, we optimize the DINOv3 features using a self-distillation framework, combined with synthetic data supervision and unlabeled data. After we distill the optimized features to an on-device model, we achieve 58.62% and 42.91% reductions in E50U50 and E90U90 errors, respectively, compared to a synthetic supervised baseline, without using any real data ground truth. DistillGaze allows rapid iteration of hardware changes, and also provides valuable broader insights to accelerate product design and development cycles.

21 B.7 Loss Function Weight Ablations for Optimizing VFM 

As described in Section 3.2, our loss function for optimizing the VFM consists of synthetic supervision, self-distillation, and pseudo-labels, with a cosine scheduler that emphasizes synthetic supervision at the beginning of training and gradually transitions toward self-distillation and pseudo-labels. In this section, we show additional ablation studies over the loss functions and weighting. The results in Table 8 confirm that optimized VFM relies on the combination of all of our loss components, and the scheduled weighting between them. Removing either synthetic supervision or the scheduler leads to a worse performance across metrics, with larger error than linear probing, highlighting the importance of first adapting the VFM to the eye-tracking task through synthetic supervision, as naively applying self-distillation and pseudo-label losses can degrade the VFM representation. In contrast, removing self-distillation and pseudo-label loss yields results comparable to synthetic fine-tuning, suggesting that its benefit mainly appears when combined with synthetic supervision under the proposed schedule. Overall, these findings support our design choice of first adapting the VFM representation to the eye-tracking task through synthetic supervision, and then further refining it with self-distillation and pseudo-labels under a gradual transition in loss weighting.
