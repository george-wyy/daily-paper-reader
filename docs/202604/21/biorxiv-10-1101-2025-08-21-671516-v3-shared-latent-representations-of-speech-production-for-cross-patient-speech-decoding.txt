Abstract 16 Speech brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) can restore communication in individuals with 17 neuromotor disorders who are unable to speak. However, current speech BCIs limit patient 18 usability and successful deployment by requiring large volumes of patient-specific data collected 19 over long periods of time. A promising solution to facilitate usability and accelerate their successful 20 deployment is to combine data from multiple patients. This has proven difficult, however, due to 21 differences in user neuroanatomy, varied placement of electrode arrays, and sparse sampling of 22 targeted anatomy . Here, by aligning patient-specific neural data to a shared latent space, we show 23 that speech BCIs can be trained on data combined across patients. Using canonical correlation 24 analysis and high-density micro-electrocorticography ( μECoG), we uncovered shared neural 25 latent dynamics with preserved micro-scale speech information. This approach enabled cross-26 patient decoding models to achieve improved performance relative to patient-specific models 27 facilitated by the high resolution and broad coverage of μECoG. Our findings support future 28 speech BCIs that are more accurate and rapidly deployable, ultimately improving the quality of 29 life for people with impaired communication from neuromotor disorders. 30

Main 50 Speech is an intricate coordination of muscle movements that shape sounds into words 1.51 Paralysis of these muscles by neuromotor disorders such as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) 52 and brainstem stroke can disrupt speech, resulting in debilitating losses to communicative ability 53 and a reduced quality of life 2–6 . Speech brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have shown promise in 54 restoring communication to individuals with neuromotor disorders by using machine learning 55 models to accurately decode intended speech units directly from brain activity 7–10 .56 Speech BCIs can be broadly divided into non-invasive approaches, such as 57 electroencephalography (EEG), and invasive approaches, such as electrocorticography (ECoG). 58 Non-invasive BCIs offer advantages in safety and ease of deployment but are limited by lower 59 spatial resolution and susceptibility to noise, which can constrain their ability to resolve fine-60 grained articulatory representations required for high-accuracy speech decoding 11,12 . In contrast, 61 invasive BCIs provide higher spatial and temporal resolution recordings directly from cortical 62 populations, enabling more accurate decoding of speech-related neural activity 7–10 , but introduce 63 challenges related to surgical implantation, limited and variable electrode coverage, and patient-64 specific sampling of neural populations. Invasive recording modalities have become a primary 65 focus for achieving high-accuracy speech decoding despite these challenges. 66 A key limitation of invasive speech BCIs is their reliance on patient-specific approaches that 67 require resource-intensive, individualized data acquisition from each patient – typically spanning 68 multiple weeks – before their associated machine learning models can be trained to achieve 69 maximal accuracy 8. This approach is taken to mitigate the effects of varied sensor placement, 70 sparse sampling of the targeted anatomy, and neuroanatomical variability across patients 13 .71 These long training and deployment times make patient use cumbersome and hinder their 72 adoption as assistive communication devices. Future speech BCIs that successfully combine data 73 across multiple patients may accelerate training by requiring minimal data collection from new 74

Such cross-patient speech BCIs could be rapidly deployable and demonstrate greater 75 robustness across a broader patient population. 76 For successful cross-patient speech decoding, there must be similarity in the neural 77 representations of speech production across patient recordings. Invasive speech decoding 78 studies typically record from the sensorimotor cortex (SMC) 14–16 with intracranial 79 electroencephalographic devices, such as ECoG 17–20 or intracortical microelectrode arrays 21,22 ,80 from which sampled representations of speech production may differ from patient to patient due 81 to variability in electrode array placement or individual neuroanatomy. However, recent work has 82 demonstrated that for motor activity, there is a low-dimensional structure in the time-varying 83 neural population activity of the motor cortex 23–25 , known as latent dynamics, that is shared across 84 individuals. For example, in non-human primates, the latent dynamics of individual subjects 85 performing limb movement can be aligned and pooled across animals to train a cross-animal 86 movement decoding model 26 . We hypothesize that this phenomenon extends to human speech 87 production. That is, the latent dynamics underlying the motor control of articulator muscles in the 88 vocal tract to produce speech have a shared representation across separate patients despite 89 variability in electrode placement and individual neuroanatomy (Fig. 1a). These latent dynamics 90 can be aligned and pooled across patients to enable training of cross-patient speech BCIs. 91 We additionally hypothesize that the elucidation of shared latent dynamics requires an accurate 92 sampling of speech-relevant neural population activity. We have previously shown that accessing 93 the rich spatiotemporal representations of speech production in the SMC requires precise neural 94 interfaces with high spatial-specificity and broad coverage. This dense, broad sampling of the 95 cortex has been achieved using micro-electrocorticographic ( μECoG) arrays to record from a 96 large surface of the brain with a high density of micro-contacts 27 , which has allowed resolution of 97 micro-scale features for both epilepsy 28 and speech production 29 . While surface recording via 98 

μECoG relies on activity in neural local field potentials (LFPs) and cannot access the same multi-99 unit activity that previous studies used to investigate these cross-patient motor latent dynamics 26 ,100 previous work has shown strong similarity between latent dynamics extracted from high-frequency 101 LFP bands and multi-unit sources 30 . Together, these results suggest that μECoG has sufficient 102 spatial-specificity, coverage, and signal quality to enable successful cross-patient alignment for 103 speech BCIs. 104 Here, we investigate cross-patient representations of speech production by performing acute 105 neural recordings with high-density μECoG arrays over the SMC of eight patients during a speech 106 repetition task. We use dimensionality reduction techniques to reduce high channel-count μECoG 107 recordings into low-dimensional latent dynamics, and we subsequently learn linear 108 transformations that align these latent dynamics across patients. We show that cross-patient 109 latent dynamics encode speech information relevant to the control of articulator muscles in the 110 vocal tract. These shared speech encodings enable the training of cross-patient speech decoding 111 models, which in turn outperform patient-specific decoding models. Lastly, we show that micro-112 scale sampling of the SMC with broad coverage is critical for learning the proper alignment 113 between patients that preserves speech information. 114

Alignment of cross-patient latent dynamics 137 Speech trials for each patient were grouped into four categories representing types of articulation 138 corresponding to the phonemes presented in our stimuli (low: /a/, /e/ , high: /i/, /u/ , labial: /p/, /b/, 139 

/v/, dorsal: /k/, /g/ ). For simplicity, we classified trials by the articulator type of the first phoneme 140 in the spoken sequence, resulting in 12 “low” non-words, 14 “high” non-words, 14 “labial” non-141 words, and 12 “dorsal” non-words. There were distinct spatiotemporal patterns of HG activity 142 specific to each type of articulation (Fig. 2a, all patients in Fig. S4). While patterns differ across 143 articulators, they also differ across patients. This can be seen across all patients, but we illustrate 144 this difference for two representative patients (S1 and S2 – Fig. 2a). These patient-specific 145 differences in spatiotemporal speech representations demonstrate the necessity of specialized 146 alignment techniques in uncovering a shared representation of speech across patients. 147 148 To extract latent dynamics from these high-dimensional data, we use principal component 149 analysis (PCA) as a linear dimensionality reduction tool to decompose HG activity (± 500 ms 150 around speech onset) across the significant channels into a low-dimensional set of principal 151 components (PCs) while preserving timing information. To further show that speech-relevant neural 195 activity is important to cross-patient alignment, we also quantified articulatory representational 196 similarity in a control condition using surrogate data. Specifically, we replaced the true neural data 197 with surrogate data generated by the Tensor Maximum Entropy (TME) method, which is randomly 198 generated with the constraint of preserving the first and second order statistical structure of our 199 neural data (equal mean and covariance across trials, channels, and time; see Methods). As such, 200 this surrogate data is random with respect to the speech sequences spoken during trials and 201 should not encode any relevant articulatory information. Cross-patient representational similarity 202 from this surrogate data control was significantly lower than that of our original aligned latent 203 dynamics (Fig. S7; Wilcoxon signed-rank test, W = 0, n = 8, FDR-corrected p = 0.01), indicating 204 that speech information encoded in the true neural activity is important to successful cross-patient 205 alignment. These results demonstrate the success of CCA in aligning latent dynamics across 206 patients to elucidate a shared representation of speech production. 207

To investigate 214 this, we first calculated single-electrode tuning to the four articulator categories. For each 215 electrode, we trained a linear discriminant analysis decoding model to classify articulator type. 216 We quantified the tuning of an electrode to an articulator by its receiver-operator-characteristic 217 area-under-the-curve (ROC-AUC) value. To investigate the cross-patient preservation of these maps, we used CCA alignment to project 220 spatial articulator information from one patient’s space to another. We first used PCA to extract 221 latent dynamics from two patients and learned a CCA transformation to align them. The 222 transformations learned in this process enable the projection of data from a source patient’s 223 “electrode-space” to a target patient’s electrode-space, which we call cross-patient projection. 224 Specifically, electrode-level data can be projected to the source patient’s latent space with the 225 source patient’s PCA transform, (optionally) aligned to the target patient’s latent space, and then 226 projected to the target patient’s electrode-space by applying the inverse of target patient’s PCA 227 transform. We illustrate this analysis with S3 as the source patient and S1 as the target patient 228 (Fig. 3b). After learning the PCA and CCA transformations, the electrode-articulator tunings for 229 the source patient (Fig. 3b, left) were projected to the target patient’s space. We denote these 230 projected spatial maps as reconstructed articulator maps (Fig. 3b, right). The Pearson correlation 231 between ground-truth articulator maps and reconstructed articulator maps was calculated to 232 quantify similarity in the reconstructed spatial tunings. We found that the similarity between 233 reconstructed and target articulator maps was higher when cross-patient projection was 234 performed with alignment than without (Fig. 3c, left). Additionally, we found that the target patient’s 235 articulator maps could be reconstructed with high similarity for certain articulator categories (low, 236 labial, dorsal) by averaging reconstructed maps across all source patients (Fig. S8 – illustrated 237 for target patient S1), showing that articulator information in one patient is well-represented across 238 multiple patient sources. Across all target patients, we found significantly higher similarity between 239 reconstructed and ground-truth maps with alignment (Fig. 3c, right) than without (Wilcoxon 240 signed-rank test, W = 3, n = 8, p = 0.04), indicating that cross-patient alignment results in improved 241 preservation of spatial articulator tuning. To confirm this similarity between ground-truth and 242 reconstructions of articulator maps with alignment was indeed driven by shared speech 243 information encoded in the recorded neural activity, we performed a control analysis again using 244 TME-generated surrogate data in place of our original neural data (Fig. S9). When decomposition 245 and alignment transformations were learned using this surrogate data that is random with respect 246 to speech information, we found reduced mean correlations between ground truth and 247 reconstructed articulator maps with alignment across all patients and articulators (Fig. S9a; mean: 248 surrogate aligned = -0.09, aligned = 0.16), though with no significant effect (FDR-corrected p = 249 0.07, exact two-sided paired permutation test), which may be due to our limited number of 250 patients. 251 We validated the preservation of articulatory information across patients by training support vector 252 machine (SVM) decoding models to classify articulatory features from patients that had not been 253 used for training. Specifically, we trained a singular SVM on HG signals (± 500 ms around speech 254 onset) from one patient and evaluated its accuracy when tested on all other patients projected to 255 the single patient’s space, both with alignment and without (Fig. 3d – illustrated for S1). As a 256 baseline, we compared cross-patient accuracies to the accuracy of patient-specific SVMs for each 257 patient. Expanded cross-patient datasets improve speech decoding accuracy 280 Given the preservation of speech information in aligned, cross-patient latent dynamics, we 281 investigated if cross-patient datasets could be used to address data limitations in speech BCI 282 training and improve their performance. With a selected target patient, additional data from other 283 patients can be aligned to this target patient and appended to the target datasets, enabling vastly 284 expanded training datasets relative to patient-specific contexts. To test this, we trained SVM 285 models on aligned cross-patient latent dynamics during speech (total combined dataset size 286 across all patients: ~8.2 minutes utterance duration, ~68.5 minutes experiment duration, see 287 Table S3 for amount of speech data per patient) and we evaluated their accuracy on a held-out 288 set from the target patient using 20-fold cross-validation. We then compared these results to 289 patient-specific latent dynamics and unaligned cross-patient latent dynamics. While thus far, our 290 analyses focused on decoding articulatory features, here we evaluated the accuracy of classifying 291 distinct phoneme units present within these articulator categories (e.g. /k/, /a/, /b/) and in all three 292 position of the spoken phoneme sequence to mimic translational BCI use cases where phonemes 293 are rapidly decoded and strung together into words and sentences. 294 We found significant differences in decoding accuracy across patient-specific, unaligned cross-295 patient, and aligned cross-patient decoding contexts (Fig. 4a; 9-way phoneme decoder, chance 296 = 0.11; repeated measures ANOVA, F 2,14 = 12.59, p = 0.007). Discussion 512 Speech BCIs have recently demonstrated impressive performance in rapidly and accurately 513 translating neural signals to words in patients with an inability to speak due to neuromotor 514 disorders. However, these BCIs typically require long periods of patient-specific data collection 515 that limit their deployability and effectiveness. In this work, we investigated shared, cross-patient 516 representations of speech production in the SMC to reduce the deployment time of speech BCIs 517 by enabling cross-patient decoding models. While cross-patient speech decoding has been 518 previously investigated using transfer learning 14 , as of this writing, this is the first study, to our 519 knowledge, to use neural activity aligned across human patients for speech decoding. Our results 520 support the idea that cross-patient models can reach sufficient data requirements for advanced 521 decoding methodologies with minimal data collection from any individual patient and can be easily 522 transferred across patients, resulting in robust and rapidly deployable speech BCIs that are more 523 accessible to a broader range of patients. 524 A novel aspect of this study is the investigation of shared latent dynamics in human speech. 525 Previously, the alignment of latent dynamics across individuals has been constrained to non-526 human primates and rodents performing limb movement tasks 26 . Here, we show this phenomenon 527 of shared neural activity across individuals extends to the human motor speech system, where 528 the implications for translational BCI contexts stand to provide substantial benefits for training 529 machine learning models and accelerating deployment following device implantation. Despite 530 differences between limb movement and speech production, the presence of shared speech 531 latent dynamics is a logical extension as both movement contexts pertain to the alignment of low-532 level neural motor representations underlying highly-coordinated muscle movements. 533

We hypothesized that neural population activity underlying speech articulator movement is well-534 represented in patients’ latent dynamics and shared across patients. This is an important 535 motivation for the use of functional alignment in our study, as the transformations learned through 536 our alignment procedure do not simply reflect efforts of domain adaptation between a target and 537 source patient. Instead, this method aims to exploit the existence of a shared neural manifold of 538 speech motor control to account for differences in neural sampling across patients that arises 539 from variability in neuroanatomy and electrode placement. This distinction is critical, as it allows 540 for principled pooling of neural data across patients while maintaining a consistent decoding 541 model, and provides a complementary alternative to existing transfer learning strategies in neural 542 decoding. 543 To extract latent dynamics from HG activity across many channels, we used PCA as a544 dimensionality reduction method to approximate latent dynamics. Further, to perform alignment 545 between latent dynamics from different patients, we used CCA to learn linear transformations 546 between latent dynamics. We chose these methods for their simple and interpretable 547 transformations, where aligned latent dynamics are the result of spatial filters applied to the 548 original electrode space. However, PCA and CCA are limited because they 1) fail to capture 549 relevant nonlinear latent representations of cortical neural activity during motor control 24,39 2) treat 550 data from different time points as independent observations, which discards potential temporal 551 information that could be useful in learning a better alignment across patients. Recent dynamical 552 systems frameworks have been developed that use deep representation learning to elucidate 553 nonlinear latent dynamics while explicitly modeling temporal information, such as Latent Factor 554 Analysis for Dynamical Systems (LFADS) 39 and MAnifold Representation Basis LEarning 555 (MARBLE) 40 . Future studies should investigate nonlinear cross-patient representations of speech 556 production extracted from these advanced dynamical systems models to determine if these 557 changes improve alignment quality between patients. However, study designs must be wary of 558 limited interpretability, relative to linear decomposition methods, with deep learning models. 559 Additionally, the amount of data per patient required to successfully learn latent dynamics should 560 be considered in these deep models, as a steep data requirement to learn and align latent 561 dynamics is antithetical to the desired rapid deployability for translational BCI contexts. 562 We found that alignment of latent dynamics by CCA preserved spatial properties of speech 563 information. In many cases, we found strong correlations between ground-truth articulator maps 564 and articulator maps projected to that patient’s space from another source patient’s space. 565 However, we found some cases of negative correlation, and thus misalignment, when 566 reconstructing articulator maps with weak representations, suggesting this alignment may fail to 567 properly capture weakly represented speech information. Statistically, CCA alignment is designed 568 to maximize correlation between cross-patient latent dynamics but does not enforce a one-to-one 569 spatial correspondence. As a result, reconstruction into an individual patient’s electrode space 570 can introduce distortions, particularly when the shared latent space captures only a subset of the 571 variance present in the target patient. As described earlier, this may be a failure of the linear latent 572 dynamics being unable to approximate necessary nonlinear activity to accurately preserve 573 observed spatial activations 24 . From a neurophysiological perspective, these discrepancies may 574 reflect true, minor inter-patient variability in speech motor representations. While similar cortical 575 regions are engaged across individuals, the combined differences in anatomy, electrode 576

However, we believe 594 future work should consider a comprehensive ROI-based analysis with stereo-595 electroencephalography (SEEG), as the distributed coverage of SEEG recordings would be well 596 suited to answer this question, assuming electrode density and sampling within ROIs are sufficient 597 to extract relevant speech-related latent dynamics. Overall, these findings suggest that cross-598 patient alignment provides the greatest benefit when patient-specific models are limited by 599 insufficient data or lower signal quality, effectively acting as a form of data augmentation that 600 stabilizes decoding in these settings. 601 Our CTC-RNN results demonstrate that cross-patient alignment can substantially improve real-602 time speech decoding performance in contexts that more closely reflect operational BCI systems. 603 Using simulated real-time μECoG recordings and causal sliding windows of HG activity, we found 604 that recurrent neural network models with CTC loss trained on aligned cross-patient data 605 consistently outperformed both patient-specific and unaligned cross-patient models. Importantly, 606 these benefits were observed even with minimal amounts of target patient data, indicating that 607 cross-patient alignment can reduce the data collection burden required to achieve accurate 608 decoding. Performance improvements scaled predictably with the amount of aligned cross-patient 609 data, and log-extrapolation to long-term dataset sizes suggested that cross-patient data could 610 serve as a viable substitute for extensive patient-specific training. 611 Critically, the latency required to compute alignment transformations and apply them to incoming 612 neural data is minimal relative to the overall experimental timescale and decoding window, 613 demonstrating that this approach is compatible with real-time BCI constraints. These findings 614 suggest that cross-patient alignment provides a practical and effective means to leverage shared 615 neural structure across individuals, stabilizing decoding in low-data regimes while maintaining 616 real-time feasibility. High-gamma extraction 737 The high-gamma band (HG: 70-150 Hz) of the neural signal was extracted to estimate neural 738 population dynamics in the SMC. HG signals have previously been shown to encode rich speech 739 production information 8,19,31 and strong correlations with neuron spiking activity 32–34 , suggesting a 740 valid estimation of speech-relevant SMC population dynamics. Following preprocessing, neural 741 signals were bandpass filtered using frequency-domain Gaussian filters centered at eight 742 logarithmically spaced center frequencies (with logarithmically scaled bandwidths around each 743 center frequency) between 70 and 150 Hz 50 . Specifically, the neural timeseries were decomposed 744 into their corresponding frequency representations with the fast Fourier transform (FFT) and 745 multiplied in frequency space with a Gaussian kernel centered around each center frequency to 746 select and smooth information from the frequencies in the desired band. The inverse FFT was 747 then performed to convert bandpass filtered signals at each band back to the time domain. 748 Envelopes of signals in each band were calculated as the absolute value of the Hilbert transform 749 and subsequently averaged across the eight bands. The resultant timeseries was then 750 downsampled to 200 Hz with a finite impulse response anti-aliasing filter (Kaiser window, 751 MATLAB’s resample function). For visualization of HG activity across arrays (Fig. 2a), HG activity 752 was z-score normalized to the baseline period (500 ms prior to auditory stimulus presentation) for 753 each trial. For all other analyses (latent dynamics extraction, decoding), HG activity was 754 normalized by subtracting the average HG envelope of the baseline period. Analyses in this work 755 used HG activity in a one second window (-500 ms to 500 ms) around patient response onset. 756 Channels with significant speech HG activity were determined by statistical comparison between 757 response and baseline periods (see Statistical Analysis ). HG extraction was performed in 758 MATLAB R2021a. 759

Simulated real-time data generation 1062 We simulated the real-time collection of neural features from our offline speech data to evaluate 1063 the effectiveness of our proposed alignment method in contexts more applicable to speech BCI 1064 deployment. To accomplish this, we adapted the methods of Willett and colleagues 7 to our high-1065 gamma processing and phoneme decoding pipeline. We began with 2ksps neural data split into 1066 trials by alignment to the presentation of auditory stimuli during the speech task (as real-time 1067 methods would not have knowledge of patient response times to use response-aligned data). We 1068 split each trial into 20 ms bins (40 timepoints per bin at 2 ksps), applied a common average 1069 reference within bins, and performed a modified HG filtering method within these short time bins. 1070 HG filtering methods for offline, trial-based data typically employ a Hilbert transform to extract the 1071 envelope of the logarithmically-spaced and sized frequency bands in the HG range (see High-1072 

gamma extraction) , which can be difficult to accurately implement in low-latency contexts with 1073 short processing bins. Because of this, we chose to calculate the root-mean-square (RMS) HG 1074 power in each of these 20 ms bins instead. We again bandpass the signal into 8 logarithmically-1075 spaced and sized frequency bands as with our offline data, then calculate RMS power in each 1076 band as simply the square root of the mean of the squared signal. Bandpass filtering was 1077 performed causally using a 4 th order Butterworth filter designed to match frequency band 1078 characteristics of the offline filtering frequency bands 50 . We finally average across these bands to 1079 calculate a single HG power value for each channel. This is repeated for every 20 ms bin within 1080 each trial, effectively downsampling the output HG power to a sampling rate of 50 sps. Here, trials 1081 were initially defined 0 s to 3.5 seconds relative to stimulus onset. However, we trimmed the initial 1082 500 ms to remove filter artifacts, resulting in trial definition as 0.5 s to 3.5 s relative to stimulus 1083 onset. 1084 To mimic clinical speech BCI deployment contexts with designated training and evaluation 1085 periods, we split our three-block speech task into training blocks (first two blocks) and a testing 1086 block (last block). Due to the single block of data collection for patient S3, we chose to exclude 1087 them as a target patient in our simulated real-time decoding analyses (though we still used their 1088 data to increase training set size in other target patients). Following simulated real-time processes 1089 of the data from the training period, we performed channel and trial outlier removal identically to 1090 our offline methods (see Neural data preprocessing ). Identified bad channels from the training 1091 period were saved and excluded from the data in the test period by imputing their values with the 1092 mean across all good channels. Real-time processed HG data aligned to the stimulus were 1093 normalized (mean-subtraction) by a pre-stimulus baseline (-500 ms to 0 ms relative to stimulus 1094 onset as with offline data). Due to the short time period of experimental data collection, we chose 1095 to save this baseline HG data from the training period of normalization of the test data as well 1096 instead of using rolling normalization methods present in studies with longer collection and 1097 evaluation periods. 1098

Connectionist temporal classification recurrent neural network (CTC-RNN) decoding 1099 Following simulated real-time processing of our training period and testing period data, we trained 1100 stacked recurrent neural network (RNN) models with connectionist temporal classification loss 1101 (CTC) to continuously predict phoneme identity throughout the trials. We used CTC loss and 1102 decoded stimulus-presented phonemes (instead of patient response phonemes), as in previous 1103 speech BCI work 7,8 , to avoid use of information on patient-response timing which would not be 1104 available in real-time speech BCI settings. Specifically, we set the label for each speech trial as 1105 the sequence of phonemes presented in the auditory stimulus. Then from 0.5 s to 3.5 s post-1106 stimulus onset, we decoded phoneme identity using a causal sliding window of HG power features 1107 (280 ms window size, 80 ms stride, i.e. 14 sample window size, 4 sample stride) as performed by 1108 Willett and colleagues 7. Training with CTC loss enables the comparison of different length 1109 sequences in “decoding window” space (predicted phoneme every 80 ms) to “stimulus sequence” 1110 space (three-length sequence of phonemes) by collapsing repeatedly predicted phonemes and 1111 ignoring periods of “blank” predictions with no associated phoneme. In patient-specific decoding 1112 cases, we trained models to directly translate real-time processed HG data across all channels to 1113 phoneme sequences via our stacked RNN model with CTC loss. In unaligned cross-patient 1114 decoding cases, high-channel count data was first reduced to patient-specific latent spaces via 1115 PCA, pooled together into a single training set across patients, the trained to predict phoneme 1116 sequences with the stacked RNN model. To enable data pooling across different latent sizes, 1117 latent spaces were truncated to the minimum latent size across all patients. Latent size was 1118 determined by the number of components explaining 90% of the variance in the input data. 1119 However, for patient S5, noise in a single channel that was not removed by our initial channel 1120 removal resulted in a single component explaining greater than 90% of the data’s variance and 1121 poor subsequent alignment.
