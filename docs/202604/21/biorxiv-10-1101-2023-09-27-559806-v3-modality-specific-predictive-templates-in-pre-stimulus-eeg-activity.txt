# Abstract11 

Perceptual decision -making is a combination of sensory information and prior beliefs. In order to  perform 12 

actions in a timely fashion, it is necessary to  anticipate  the timing at which events occur, but also what event 13 

is more likely than the other.  While EEG signatures of anticipation  have been identified  it is less clear whether 14 

classifiers trained on informative (cued) trials generalize to uncued trials and whether such decoded 15 

templates predict trial -by -trial shifts in decision strategy (e.g., drift -rate or starting -point changes in a 16 

Diffusion Decision Model ). This study aimed to determine whether human participants anticipated a visual or 17 

auditory stimulus at the single -trial level in both cued and uncued trials. We found that pre -stimulus brain 18 

activity contains information about the expected upcoming stimulus and that this information can be 19 

successfully extracted from single -trial brain activity. Behavioral analyses revealed a connection between 20 

correct anticipation and shifts in decision strategy, while also validating the classification of uncued trials. 21 

Importantly, the classification of uncued trials confirms that expectations build even in the absence of 22 

triggers. These findings highlight the presence of single -trial, stimulus -specific neural signatures of 23 

anticipation, offering new insights into trial -to -trial variability in decision -making and advancing our 24 

understanding of cognitive processes. 25 

Keywords: decision -making, anticipation, EEG, single -trial, behavior, diffusion  decision model 26

# Introduction 32 

Consider a situation in which a  researcher  is pouring coffee while awaiting an important phone call  from a 33 

funding agency . Visual attention is directed toward the rising coffee level, whereas auditory attention is 34 

oriented toward the ringtone. At some point, an action will be required, but only one sensory modality can be 35 

prioritized at a time. Effective behavior in such situations requires not only anticipating when an action will be 36 

needed, but also which sensory modality should be preferentially prepared. 37 

Anticipation is a fundamental property of brain function. Rather than passively responding to incoming stimuli, 38 

the brain continuously generates predictions about future events in order to optimize perception and action 39 

(Petro et al., 2019; Poulton, 1950) . Within predictive processing frameworks, expectations are thought to bias 40 

sensory systems in advance  (Railo et al., 2021) , shaping neural processing before external input is received. 41 

Such anticipatory mechanisms have been shown to improve behavioral performance, leading to faster 42 

responses and reduced error rates when upcoming events are predictable  (Petro et al., 2019) , as well as 43 

enhance decision confidence  (Iemi et al., 2017; Samaha et al., 2017) .44 

However, the mechanisms underlying these behavioral improvements remain debated. One account 45 

proposes that anticipation enhances sensory processing  (Carrasco, 2011; Iemi et al., 2017; Kok et al., 2012) ,46 

thereby increasing the quality of evidence available for decision -making  and subsequently increasing the rate 47 

of evidence accumulation  (Kloosterman et al., 2019; Urai et al., 2019; White & Poldrack, 2014) . Alternatively, 48 

anticipation may bias the decision process itself, for instance by shifting the starting point toward the 49 

expected alternative  (Bode et al., 2012; Mulder et al., 2012; Yu & Cohen, 2008)  or lowering the decision 50 

threshold, without directly affecting sensory encoding  (Bogacz et al., 2006; Cisek et al., 2009) . Distinguishing 51 

between these mechanisms is not straightforward at the behavioral level, as both can lead to similar 52 

improvements in response times and accuracy. Computational models of decision -making, such as the 53 

diffusion  decision  model, provide a principled framework to dissociate changes in evidence accumulation 54 

from changes in decision bias. 55 

Electroencephalography (EEG) has played a key role in the study of anticipatory processes due to its high 56 

temporal resolution. In particular, pre -stimulus modulations of event -related potentials (ERPs), such as 57 

stimulus -preceding negativities  and contingent negative variation  (Poulton, 1950; Walter et al., 1964) , as well 58 

as changes in low -frequency oscillatory activity  (Denison et al., 2024; see Tan et al., 2024 for a review) , have 59 

been linked to anticipatory attention, sensory readiness, and expectation. These effects are often interpreted 60 

as reflecting the allocation of attentional resources  (Jensen & Mazaheri, 2010) , sensory encoding  (Hetenyi et 61 

al., 2024; Lou et al., 2014) , or  top -down processing to prepare  for upcoming stimuli  (Min & Herrmann, 2007) .62 

However, much of this literature has focused on averaged signals and univariate contrasts,  which reduces 63 

trial -by -trial variability. This is particularly limiting for anticipatory processes , which are inherently variable 64 

across trials,  leaving open the question of whether anticipatory activity carries information about the specific 65 

content of upcoming events.  Moreover, the unconscious nature of anticipation  (Koch & Preuschoff, 2007) 66 

complicates the accessibility to the ground -truth knowledge of what was expected, as direct participant 67 

questioning might alter neural activity  (Trevena & Miller, 2002) .68 

A key unresolved issue is whether pre -stimulus neural activity reflects a generic preparatory state , such as 69 

heightened arousal or nonspecific attention , or  whether it encodes detailed, content -specific expectations, 70 

such as the sensory modality of an anticipated stimulus.  Classical ERP components such as the contingent 71 

negative variation and modulations of alpha -band activity have often been interpreted as reflecting general 72 

preparatory or attentional states, supporting the view of a largely non -specific anticipation signal. More  recent 73

studies have reported modality -dependent differences in pre -stimulus EEG activity  (Fiorini et al., 2021, 2023; 74 

Lucia et al., 2023) , suggesting that anticipatory activity may carry more specific information about upcoming 75 

events . However, t hese effects are often subtle and difficult to interpret at the  single -trial level. As a result, it 76 

remains unclear whether anticipatory states can be reliably distinguished based on their neural signatures 77 

prior to stimulus onset , in particular in situations where the modality of the upcoming stimulus is 78 

unpredictable . These should however exist, since priors are built even in the absence of statistical regularities 79 

in the task  (Bévalot & Meyniel, 2024; Yu, 2007; Yu & Cohen, 2008) .80 

Single -trial analyses are pivotal in cognitive neuroscience as they unveil the variability and dynamics of neural 81 

activity that are often obscured by averaging across trials  (Williams & Linderman, 2021) . By examining 82 

individual trials, previous studies have unveiled distinct engagement states in perceptual decision -making 83 

tasks in mice  (Ashwood et al., 2022) , as well as the time course of decision strategies in rodents and humans 84 

(Roy et al., 2021) . Single -trial EEG analyses have been employed to predict memory retrieval success  (Nakuci 85 

et al., 2023) , demonstrating that trial -by -trial fluctuations in neural activity can reveal latent cognitive states ,86 

even in the absence of explicit reports, processes that are typically invisible when  applying classical signal 87 

averaging techniques . Additionally, these analyses facilitate the identification of trial -to -trial behavioral 88 

variability  (Steinemann et al., 2024) , enabling a more nuanced understanding of the neural correlates 89 

underlying cognitive functions  (Nunez et al., 2017; Ratcliff et al., 2009) . As anticipation is believed to be a 90 

major source of variability in decision -making  (Summerfield & de Lange, 2014; Tarasi et al., 2025) , its 91 

quantification at the single -trial level is key to understanding decision dynamics. 92 

In the present study, we investigated whether anticipatory EEG activity prior to stimulus onset contains 93 

modality -specific information that can be decoded at the single -trial level. Participants performed a task in 94 

which visual or auditory targets were anticipated either explicitly via cues or implicitly in uncued trials. We 95 

first examined behavioral indices of anticipation, including response times and error rates, and characterized 96 

pre -stimulus  ERP activity by testing for pre -stimulus cue -related modulations in voltage amplitude using a 97 

TFCE -based cluster permutation analysis  to identify spatiotemporal regions showing significant anticipatory 98 

modulation.  We then applied multivariate decoding techniques to assess whether the expected sensory 99 

modality could be predicted from pre -stimulus EEG on individual trials. Finally, we tested whether neural 100 

decoding generalized to uncued trials and linked anticipatory neural states to behavioral performance using 101 

diffusion decision modeling. Together, these analyses provide converging evidence that anticipatory neural 102 

activity is both modality -specific and behaviorally relevant .103

# Time -domain  EEG comparisons 158 

Event -related potentials (ERPs) were analyzed to assess whether anticipatory neural activity could be 159 

distinguished in the time domain between anticipation and no anticipation, as well as between visual and 160 

auditory anticipation.  The pre -stimulus period was defined as the window  [−400 : 0] 𝑚𝑠  preceding the target 161 

stimulus for both stimuli and in both cued and uncued conditions. It can equivalently be defined as the 162 

[500 : 900 ] 𝑚𝑠  time window following the onset of the noise clip and cue. 2 comparisons were performed: 163 

anticipation vs. no anticipation (defined as cued vs. uncued trials), and visual vs. auditory anticipation  (defined 164 

in the cued condition as the type of cue presented : eye or ear ).165 

EEG data were low -pass filtered at 35 Hz using a minimum -phase finite impulse response (FIR) filter to focus 166 

on event -related activity while preserving temporal precision and hence avoiding contamination by post -167 

stimulus activity. Data were then epoched relative to pre -stimulus period onset. Linear detrending was 168 

applied at the epoch level to remove slow drifts, rather than relying on high -pass filtering, in order to avoid 169 

introducing distortions in pre -stimulus activity and anticipatory signals. A  conservative a rtifact rejection was 170 

performed automatically by excluding epochs with peak -to -peak amplitudes exceeding  ±200 μV  (Delorme, 171 

2023) . To preserve sufficient trial counts while maintaining data quality, an adaptive procedure was used in 172 

which electrodes were iteratively removed and interpolated them using spherical spline interpolation, when 173 

trial rejection exceeded 20% for a given participant. We ensured tha t fewer than  4 channels were interpolated 174 

for each participant (corresponding to ~10% of channels). 175 

For the initial investigation of significant differences between the different periods of interest described 176 

above, ERP components were computed by averaging trials within each category (cued trials, uncued trials, 177 

face anticipation, and sound anticipation). 178 

# Classification pipeline 179 

To assess whether anticipatory neural activity contained information about the expected sensory modality at 180 

the single -trial level, we performed a multivariate classification analysis on pre -stimulus EEG data. Spatial 181 

filters were computed using the  XDawn  algorithm  (Rivet et al., 2009)  for each class using the train set, 182 

selecting  5 components  which went through minimum -maximum scaling. Logistic regression with  L1-183 

regularization was then applied to  predict  the class of each trial . This classification method is based on 184 

building spatial filters to split data into categories. Since we expect that visual and auditory anticipation utilize 185 

different networks in the brain,  leveraging  the spatial structure of data is relevant.  Because anticipatory EEG 186 

activity has been reported across multiple low -frequency ranges and no strong a priori frequency band could 187 

be specified, we performed an exploratory grid search over several canonical frequency bands (delta (1 -4Hz), 188 

theta (4 -8Hz), and alpha (8 -13Hz) ranges) , as well as a 35Hz low -pass filter,  using only cued trials.  Cued trials 189 

were first partitioned into separate training and test sets using a stratified shuffle split. The test set consisted 190 

of 25% of the trials for each participant (i.e. 60 trials). Frequency -band selection was performed exclusively 191 

within the training set using stratified 10 -fold cross -validation . This analysis was used to characterize the 192 

spectral range carrying anticipatory information and to inform the selection of a single frequency band for 193 

subsequent generalization analyses.  To determine a single frequency band for subsequent analyses, we 194 

averaged cross -validation accuracy across participants for each band and selected the band yielding the 195 

highest mean validation performance.  Importantly, the final decoding models were trained on the full training 196 

set  for each participant  and evaluated on the  held -out  test set. 197 

# Classifying pre -stimulus activity of uncued trials 198 

The afore -described classification pipeline  is  based on supervised learning, i.e.  it  require s the knowledge of a 199 

ground -truth. In this study, it requires a knowledge of the type (visual or auditory) of anticipation. However, 200 

the ground -truth label of anticipation is unknown on  uncued trials . To circumvent this issue,  if  we assume the 201 

stationarity of the anticipation phenomenon across  phases of the task , the brain activity patterns of 202 

anticipation over uncued trials should be similar to those found on the cued trials. Therefore, classifiers 203 

trained on cued data can be applied on uncued data of the same participant. We hence re -trained the 204 

classifiers obtained on all the trials of the cued phase of the experiment for each participant and predicted 205 

the corresponding anticipation class using the thus trained classifier on uncued trials.  We treat the classifier's 206

# Linking brain and behavior 209 

One of the main assumptions  regarding the behavioral effects of anticipation is that correct expectations lead 210 

to reduced response times and error rates. Several mechanisms have been proposed to account for these 211 

effects. On some accounts, anticipation increases sensory sensitivity, thereby enhancing the quality of 212 

evidence accumulation during decision formation  (Iemi et al., 2017; Kloosterman et al., 2019; Samaha et al., 213 

2017; Urai et al., 2019) . Other accounts instead suggest that anticipation biases the decision process by 214 

shifting the starting point toward the expected alternative (or, equivalently, lowering decision threshold), 215 

without directly affecting sensory evidence  (Bode et al., 2012; Mulder et al., 2012) . Distinguishing between 216 

these mechanisms requires a computational framework that can dissociate changes in evidence quality from 217 

changes in decision bias.  If the classification algorithm was indeed trained to distinguish the type of 218 

anticipation, we should observe that correct anticipations are more represented among shorter reaction 219 

times relative to long ones. Conversely, incorrect anticipations should result in longer reaction times. 220 

To analyze the effects of anticipation on behavior, we fitted diffusion decision models (DDMs)  (Ratcliff, 1978; 221 

Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008; Ratcliff & Tuerlinckx, 2002)  to behavioral data. According to DDMs, sensory 222 

evidence is accumulated linearly from a starting point  𝑧 𝑟  until reaching a fixed decision boundary a, at which 223 

time a decision is made. 224 

The DDM is defined by the equation: 225

Our DDM analysis aimed at identifying whether changes in anticipation resulted in changes in the 232

in faster and more accurate decisions than incorrect anticipation, one could expect that correct anticipation 234 

either increases the rate of evidence accumulation, or that the decision is initially biased towards the correct 235 

decision. 236 

On cued data,  we hypothesized that correct  anticipations should result in larger drifts or larger starting points 237

the correctness of anticipation. 244

classifier to assess the correctness of anticipation.  This serves as a validation step to the classification: if we 246 

observe the same trends as in the cued dataset, this would indicate that the classifier captures anticipatory 247 

neural states that have functional consequences for decision formation, rather than reflecting task -specific 248 

or stimulus -locked confounds. 249

# Statistical analyses 250 

# Behavioral effects 251 

We first tested the effects of the presence of the cue and the stimulus on the response time and accuracy at 252 

the group level using a repeated measures ANOVA procedure, with “Stimulus type” (auditory, visual) and 253 

“Condition” (cued, uncued) as within -subject factors. We further tested the effect of congruent and 254 

incongruent cues and stimuli on the response time and accuracy on the cued condition only, using repeated 255 

measures ANOVA again, with “Stimulus type” and “Cue congruency” as within -subject factors. Note that the 256 

tests were done separately in each condition due to the unbalanced number of trials. 257 

# Significance of  time -domain  differences 258 

Group -level statistical significance of anticipatory effects in the ERP time series was assessed using 259 

threshold -free cluster enhancement (TFCE , Smith  & Nichols  (2009) ) on ERP differences combined with non -260 

parametric permutation testing, as implemented in MNE -Python (Maris & Oostenveld, 2007). This approach 261 

evaluates effects that are both strong and extended across time and electrodes while controlling for multiple 262 

comparisons. TFCE avoids the need to define an arbitrary cluster -forming threshold by integrating information 263 

across a range of thresholds. For each comparison, one -sample t -statistics were computed at each channel 264 

and time point across participants. Condition labels were randomly permuted to generate a null distribution 265 

of TFCE values, and for each permutation the maximum TFCE value across all spatio -temporal points was 266 

retained. Observed TFCE values were compared against this null distribution to determine statistical 267 

significance. A total of 1024 permutations were performed, providing a stable estimate of the null distribution 268 

while remaining computationally efficient. For visualization, spatio -temporal clusters belonging to TFCE 269 

effects with a corrected p -value below 0.05 were marked as significant. 270 

# Significance of classification performance 271 

We compute  the  classification accuracy on the test set for each participant on cued trials . The classification 272 

scores are compared to chance level that we obtained through permutation testing over  1000  iterations. At 273 

each iteration, the labels are shuffled, and the classifier is re -run to compute the classification accuracy over 274 

the randomly labeled data. The null -hypothesis distribution of the chance level classification performance is 275 

thus obtained for each participant, and the classification performance is compared against this distribution. 276

# Results 281 

We analyzed EEG recordings from  42  participants who performed a sensory categorization task. At each trial, 282 

participants decided whether the randomly presented was a sound or a visual stimulus consisting of a 283 

drawing of a face. Each trial was preceded by a pre -stimulus period lasting  0.9 seconds. On some trials, a 284 

cue was presented at the start of this pre -stimulus period, indicating with  80%  confidence the class of the 285 

upcoming stimulus. In such cases, the pre -stimulus period was referred to as the  anticipation  period. 286 

Our analyses aimed to characterize brain activation patterns associated with specific stimulus anticipation, 287 

both at the group level and at the single -trial level. 288

# Discussion 444 

The present study investigate s whether anticipatory neural activity prior to stimulus onset reflects modality -445 

specific expectations and whether such activity is functionally relevant for behavior. Using a combination of 446 

ERP analyses, multivariate decoding, and computational modeling, we show that pre -stimulus EEG activity 447 

differs between visual and auditory anticipation, can be decoded at the single -trial level, and predicts 448 

behavioral performance even in the absence of explicit cues. These findings support the view that anticipation 449 

is not merely a nonspecific preparatory state, but instead involves content -specific neural representations  –450 

here at the level of sensory modality  - that shape subsequent decision processes. 451 

At the neural level, ERP analyses revealed significant differences in pre -stimulus activity between 452 

anticipation conditions. Importantly, these effects were observed prior to target onset, ruling out 453 

contamination from stimulus -evoked responses.  Our cluster analyses reveal that anticipatory activity is 454 

distributed, which  is  consistent with  findings in the mouse that prior information is encoded in various brain 455 

areas  (Findling et al., 2025) . Importantly, we observe significant differences not only between cued and 456 

uncued trials, but also within cued trials depending on what cue has been presented.  This finding  extends 457 

earlier accounts  that associated  ERP  pre -stimulus activity with non -specific temporal expectation  (Walter et 458 

al., 1964) , temporal estimation  (Kononowicz & Penney, 2016) . Rather than reflecting a purely temporal 459 

readiness signal, our findings indicate that pre -stimulus ERP activity can carry information about the content 460 

of upcoming sensory events , specifically their sensory modality . This interpretation aligns with more recent 461 

works distinguishing  pre -stimulus activity related to temporal expectation and temporal attention  (Denison 462 

et al., 2024) , and that anticipatory theta activity relate to causal predictions in infants  (Begus & Bonawitz, 463 

2024) . In that line, our ERP analysis suggests that  anticipatory activity links to  predictions of the upcoming 464 

event , which are stronger when reliable  cueing is presented and attenuated in uncued trials. 465 

Crucially , pre -stimulus activity was successfully decoded for 3 1 out of 42 participants displaying above -466 

chance single -trial classification performance in cued trials. Other works demonstrated to potential to 467 

differentiate anticipation at the single -trial level in Go/NoGo tasks, specifically distinguishing trials requiring 468 

motor responses from those in which responses are withheld  (Chavarriaga et al., 2012; Garipelli et al., 2009, 469 

2011; Khaliliardali et al., 2012, 2015) . Notably, Chavarriaga et al.  (2012)  eliminated the influence of motor 470 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license made available under a (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted April 18, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.09.27.559806 doi: bioRxiv preprint

preparation by including tasks without motor output, arguing that the differences are ascribable to attentional 471 

shifts following errors. Our findings extend these results by demonstrating that the  pre -stimulus activity 472 

reflects stimulus -specific anticipation, opening new insights into the complexity of anticipatory processes. 473 

Other works, in particular those studying language, have found that pre -stimulus activity is modulated by 474 

word predictability  (Kölbl et al., 2025; León -Cabrera et al., 2024) . In our work, while cued trials indicate a n475 

80% probability of having the corresponding stimulus, there is a 50% chance of either stimulus occurring at 476 

any time in uncued trials. The apparent distinguishability between visual and auditory expectation even in 477 

uncued trials aligns with previous accounts that participants estimate probabilities even in the absence of 478 

statistical regularities  (Yu, 2007) . Arguably, participants in our work may have displayed stronger differences 479 

if they were not instructed about the task  (Bévalot & Meyniel, 2024) . However, the training of classifiers in our 480 

work relies on the existence of cued blocks, which have to be displayed at the beginning or the end of the 481 

experiment to counterbalance learning effects. Future works, notably using unsupervised learning 482 

techniques, could test this hypothesis. 483 

This finding goes beyond traditional ERP analyses by showing that anticipatory states contain sufficient 484 

information to distinguish between visual and auditory expectations at the level of individual trials. 485 

Importantly, Fiorini et al.  (2023)  have compared conditions where the modality of the cues matched the 486 

modality of the stimulus. In one of the condition s, the cues were mixed such that the second stimulus was 487 

unpredictable. They show that there are differences in the aP and vN components depended on the 488 

conditions in which they were presented.  However, their observations cannot completely discard the 489 

influence of sensory processing of the cue in the ERP traces.  While we observed a similar ramping of activity 490 

(which was also observed in other tasks and characterized as temporal attention  (Denison et al., 2024) ), we 491 

managed to classify them at the single -trial granularity, even in the absence of any informative cue.  Barik et 492 

al.  (2019)  have classified  single -trial  alpha -band activity in face pareidolia, showing that the detection of 493 

faces in the absence of evidence  relates to lateralized alpha -band activity . Their approach however does not 494 

allow to distinguish between motor processes (they indeed observe a lateralization of alpha -band activity, 495 

consistent with motor mu -band lateralization) and anticipation.  By using the same response modality for both 496 

stimuli (click of the dominant hand), our results cannot be explained by motor preparation components. 497 

The generalization of decoding performance to uncued trials provides further insight into the nature of 498 

anticipatory processes. Even in the absence of explicit cues, classifier predictions remained were 499 

behaviorally meaningful, suggesting that participants formed endogenous expectations based on  perceived 500 

task structure. This result argues against a purely cue -driven interpretation of anticipatory signals and instead 501 

supports the idea that the brain continuously generates predictions about upcoming sensory events, even 502 

when such predictions are implicit.  By linking neural decoding to diffusion decision model parameters, our 503 

findings offer a mechanistic account of how anticipation influences behavior. Correct anticipatory states 504 

were associated with higher  bias , indicating more efficient accumulation of sensory evidence.  Previous works 505 

have also established a relation between starting point and previous trials  (Bode et al., 2012; Grosjean et al., 506 

2001) , while others have  discovered higher drift rates with higher expectations  (Urai et al., 2019)  (although 507 

see covariation of drift and bias in Hoxha et al.  (2023) ). We note that these studies were only performed in the 508 

visual modality, and that parallel networks may be at play in our bimodal task , hence the absence of 509 

significant difference between drift terms. 510 

Decoding performance was highest when signals were filtered in the theta band (4 –8 Hz), suggesting that 511 

anticipatory information is preferentially carried by low -frequency neural activity.  Importantly, our analysis 512 

was conducted in the time domain, and therefore does not directly speak to ongoing oscillatory activity per 513 

se, but rather to slow fluctuations in pre -stimulus neural signals.  This finding is  nevertheless  consistent with 514 

a large body of literature linking theta oscillations to top -down control  (Cavanagh & Frank, 2014; Sauseng et 515 

al., 2010)  and predictive processes  (Arnal & Giraud, 2012; Engel et al., 2001) . Theta -band activity has been 516 

associated with the coordination of distributed neural networks during expectation and attention  (Galas et 517 

al., 2025; Michel et al., 2022; Von Stein & Sarnthein, 2000) , as well as with the maintenance of task -relevant 518 

information over time  (Hsieh & Ranganath, 2014; Lisman & Jensen, 2013) . In particular, low -frequency 519 

oscillations are thought to support long -range communication between frontal and sensory regions  (Arnal et 520 

al., 2015; Cohen et al., 2008; Fries, 2005) , making them well -suited for conveying anticipatory signals prior to 521 

stimulus onset  (Cohen & van Gaal, 2013) . In the context of temporal and sensory expectation, theta activity 522

has also been implicated in aligning neural excitability with expected events, thereby facilitating upcoming 523 

processing  (Cravo et al., 2013; Schroeder & Lakatos, 2009) . In line with this interpretation, our regression 524 

analysis revealed that electrodes exhibiting stronger anticipatory differences also showed stronger stimulus -525

This result is consistent with the idea that anticipatory low -frequency activity may bias subsequent sensory 527 

processing by modulating the excitability of task -relevant neural populations prior to stimulus onset , a result 528 

that has already been demonstrated with oscillatory activity in temporal expectation  (Barne et al., 2021) .529 

One alternative interpretation of the decoded pre -stimulus signal is that it reflects sustained maintenance of 530 

the cue representation rather than a predictive sensory template per se. In this view, the classifier would 531 

discriminate trials based on the continued representation of the visual or auditory cue, without necessarily 532 

indexing preparatory activation of modality -specific sensory systems. Although this account would require 533 

maintenance of cue -related information for one second, such sustained representations are not implausible, 534 

particularly in task contexts encouraging active retention  (Jung et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) . Importantly, the 535 

present data are formally compatible with both interpretations. However, several aspects of our findings 536 

argue in favor of a predictive account. First, decoding generalized to uncued trials, where no explicit cue 537 

representation was available to maintain, suggesting that similar neural states can arise in the absence of a 538 

physical cue. Second, the observed modulation of decision parameters indicates functional consequences 539 

of the decoded state, consistent with a preparatory role rather than passive maintenance. Future work  with a 540 

total absence of cue  will be necessary to  adjudicate between sustained cue maintenance and genuinely 541 

anticipatory sensory templates more directly .542 

A related assumption underlying our generalization analysis is that the neural patterns decoded in cued trials 543 

reflect the same type of anticipatory states that occur in uncued trials. Although cross -condition 544 

generalization suggests partial overlap between these states, it does not formally demonstrate their 545 

equivalence. Successful generalization indicates that the classifier trained on cued trials can detect 546 

informative structure in uncued data, but this could arise from shared components embedded within broader, 547 

potentially distinct neural configurations. In other words, uncued anticipatory states may recruit similar low -548 

frequency spatial patterns while differing in their strength, stability, or additional contextual components. 549 

Future work using representational similarity analysis or cross -temporal generalization approaches could 550 

more directly test whether cued and uncued anticipation rely on identical neural codes or instead on partially 551 

overlapping but distinct anticipatory mechanisms. 552 

Why do we anticipate, even when that leads to longer reaction times upon incorrect anticipation? Indeed, in 553 

ecological environments, it is more likely than not that anticipation is incorrect, as cues are generally absent. 554 

Previous works in have suggested that biasing decisions given local patterns is a robust strategy in changing 555 

environments, in a Bayesian framework  (Yu & Cohen, 2008)  and in a reinforcement -learning framework 556 

(Hoxha et al., 2025) . Our findings provide a neural counterpart to these theoretical perspectives. We show 557 

that anticipatory states are expressed as modality -specific patterns of pre -stimulus EEG activity that 558 

influence subsequent behavior, even in the absence of explicit cues. Crucially, these anticipatory signals 559 

were associated with both benefits and costs: correct anticipations were linked to faster responses and 560 

reduced error rates, whereas incorrect anticipations incurred performance penalties. This trade -off suggests 561 

that anticipatory neural states do not merely reflect strategic guessing, but rather a proactive biasing of 562 

sensory and decision -related processes that persists across trial contexts. 563 

The  present results contribute to a growing body of evidence supporting the role of predictive processes in 564 

perception and decision -making. By demonstrating that pre -stimulus EEG activity encodes modality -specific 565 

expectations that generalize beyond explicit cues and influence decision dynamics, this study highlights the 566 

functional significance of anticipatory neural states. More broadly, our findings underscore the importance of 567 

studying neural activity prior to stimulus onset to understand how the brain prepares for and ultimately 568 

shapes perception and action.  Finally, by identifying robust neural features of anticipatory states in cued and 569 

uncued contexts, this work provides a principled starting point for future studies to characterize anticipation 570 

in the absence of explicit cues, for instance by leveraging unsupervised or data -driven clustering approaches 571 

to dissociate distinct anticipatory states. 572
