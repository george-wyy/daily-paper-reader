> Listeners face many challenges when trying to maintain attention to a target source in everyday settings; for instance, reverberation distorts acoustic cues and interruptions capture attention. However, little is known about how these challenges affect the ability to maintain selective attention. Here, we measured syllable recall accuracy and pupil dilation during a spatial selective attention task that was sometimes disrupted. Participants heard two competing, temporally interleaved syllable streams presented in pseudo-anechoic or reverberant environments. On randomly selected trials, a sudden interruption occurred mid-sequence. Compared to anechoic trials, reverberant performance was worse overall, and the interrupter disrupted performance. In uninterrupted trials, reverberation reduced peak pupil dilation both when it was consistent across all stimuli in a block and when it was randomized trial to trial, suggesting temporal smearing reduced clarity of the scene and the salience

> I.  INTRODUCTION

1

> Laboratory studies of attention often utilize anechoic listening conditions that do not reflect the

2

> acoustic complexity of real-world acoustic settings. In naturalistic listening environments, such as

3

> classrooms, hospitals, and other common settings, listeners must focus on a target source while

4

> ignoring competing sounds and background noise. This ability to attend to different parts of an

5

> acoustic signal is critical for successful communication, yet it becomes more difficult when

6

> reverberation and unexpected distractions distort the target signal or distract a listener. In such

7

> conditions, the auditory system must support sound segregation (parsing the sound mixture to

8

> individual sound sources) and sound selection (prioritizing the target sound source for further

9

> processing) (Best et al., 2006; Middlebrooks and Waters, 2020; Shinn-Cunningham, 2005).

10 

> Reverberation occurs in common place environments, resulting in sound energy reflecting off

11 

> surfaces and reaching the listeners’ ears at multiple delays (Bradley et al., 2003; Duquesnoy and

12 

> Plomp, 1980; Guski, 1990; Nabelek et al., 1989; Soulodre et al., 1989). Though early reflections can

13 

> sometimes improve perception of a sound by adding energy without significantly degrading the

14 

> content, they nonetheless degrade features important for source segregation, including spatial cues

15 

> and pitch cues. As a result, even when the target stream remains audible, maintaining attention over

16 

> time in natural, reverberant settings can require substantial cognitive resources. Later reflections not

17 

> only make it harder to segregate sound sources, they smear acoustic energy over time, which can

18 

> reduce information-conveying temporal modulation, increase background energy, and reduce the

19 

> perceptual distinctiveness of individual sound sources (Culling et al., 2003; Culling and Stone, 2017;

20 

> Duquesnoy and Plomp, 1980; Kidd et al., 2005; Lavandier and Culling, 2007; Lee and Shinn-

21 

> Cunningham, 2008; Nabelek et al., 1989; Picou et al., 2016; Ruggles and Shinn-Cunningham, 2011).

22 

> As a result, these larger-scale distortions from late reflections reduce speech intelligibility and speech

23 

> perception in addition to impairing source segregation. Thus, in reverberant settings, maintaining

24 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 3
> selective attention to make sense of a target source in the presence of other competing sounds may

25 

> be especially difficult.

26 

> In addition to ongoing reverberation distorting acoustics, everyday listening also often involves

27 

> salient interruptions that can capture attention and disrupt ongoing processing. Such events are

28 

> behaviorally relevant because they may trigger involuntary, bottom-up attention to the interrupter;

29 

> interfere with encoding of target content; and require reorienting of top-down, selective attention

30 

> back to the ongoing target stream (Liang et al., 2022; Mondor and Zatorre, 1995; Parmentier, 2014;

31 

> Parmentier et al., 2011; Shapiro et al., 1997). A recent series of psychophysical experiments explored

32 

> the behavioral impacts of interruption during spatial selective attention (Figarola et al., 2025; Liang

33 

> et al., 2022, 2025). In these experiments, listeners were asked to selectively attend to a target syllable

34 

> stream from one direction while ignoring a distractor syllable stream from a different direction. On

35 

> randomly selected trials, an interrupter occurred, which disrupted recall of target syllables, especially

36 

> the syllable immediately after the interruption. In anechoic simulations, varying the working memory

37 

> and the temporal position of the interrupter did not change the basic pattern: An interrupter

38 

> impaired listeners ability to recall syllables from the target stream, especially the syllable immediately

39 

> after the interruption (Liang et al., 2022, 2025). One study that used target and distractor streams of

40 

> five syllables each found that including naturalistic reverberation had little impact on the ability to

41 

> recall a target stream for uninterrupted trials; however, in interrupted trials, the interrupter hurt

42 

> performance more in reverberant than in anechoic simulations (Figarola et al., 2025).

43 

> Although behavioral measures can provide some insight into how reverberation and interruption

44 

> affect cognitive processes, physiological measures can reveal perceptual and cognitive differences

45 

> that may be unobservable from behavior alone. One such measure is pupil dilation. Pupil changes

46 

> can be broken down into relatively slow tonic responses and more rapid phasic responses. Prior

47 

> studies have shown that tonic responses are linked to arousal, alertness, and overall listening effort

48 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 4
> (Beatty, 1982; Bradley et al., 2008; Kahneman and Beatty, 1966; Vazey et al., 2018; Winn et al.,

49 

> 2018), and relate to performance accuracy (Heitz et al., 2008; McGinley et al., 2015; Winn et al.,

50 

> 2018). Anticipation of difficult trials can even elicit larger tonic pupil dilation before the stimulus is

51 

> presented (McCloy et al., 2017). In contrast, rapid phasic responses relate to stimulus events and

52 

> reflect surprise, momentary changes in cognitive effort, and even decision-making processes (Beatty,

53 

> 1982; Dayan and Yu, 2006; Lisi et al., 2015; Soulodre et al., 1989; Vazey et al., 2018; Winn et al.,

54 

> 2018; Zekveld et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2019b). Peak latency and amplitude in phasic responses

55 

> correspond to the maximum processing time and processing load, respectively (Koelewijn et al.,

56 

> 2015; Zekveld et al., 2011). Pupillary dynamics thus capture continuous, time-resolved changes in

57 

> cognitive and sensory processing that cannot be inferred from behavioral accuracy alone. This

58 

> makes pupillometry well-suited for evaluating how processing evolves during sustained auditory

59 

> attention and how listeners respond to salient disruptions embedded in ongoing streams.

60 

> Here, we measured behavioral performance and pupil dilation simultaneously while listeners

61 

> performed a spatial attention task in which target and masker streams were presented either in

62 

> anechoic or reverberant conditions. Our paradigm closely followed that used in the previous study

63 

> investigating interactions between reverberation and interruption (Figarola et al., 2025), but used

64 

> slightly shorter target / distractor streams to reduce trial duration (four vs. five syllables per stream).

65 

> On a subset of trials, a brief interruption occurred during the ongoing stream. We considered two,

66 

> not mutually exclusive, hypotheses: Reverberation could make the onsets of all events, including the

67 

> interrupter, less salient by smearing the acoustic features of the ongoing streams; this would reduce

68 

> phasic responses to syllables in the target and distractor streams as well as making the interrupter

69 

> onset less distinct, and thus less disruptive. Additionally, reverberation could make the task harder

70 

> overall, both by decreasing the clarity of the target speech and by smearing out the spatial cues

71 

> allowing listeners to focus and reorient spatial attention, compounding the detrimental effect of the

72

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 5
> interrupter. Physiologically, we expected the tonic pupil response linked to effort to be greater in

73 

> reverberation than in anechoic settings due to the increased difficulty; however, we expected the

74 

> phasic pupil response to ongoing stimulus events (including the interrupter) to be reduced by

75 

> reverberation, as the acoustic smearing of reverberation should reduce the salience of event onsets.

76 

> We found that reverberation impaired behavioral performance and altered pupil dynamics.

77 

> Unlike in the previous study (Figarola et al., 2025), we found that performance was worse in

78 

> reverberant than in anechoic trials, even for uninterrupted trials. Moreover, the current study found

79 

> a greater effect of the interrupter in anechoic trials compared to reverberant trials, while the previous

80 

> study found a smaller, but opposite effect. The baseline (tonic) pupil responses were equivalent

81 

> across anechoic and reverberant settings, rather than being larger in reverberation as we

82 

> hypothesized. In uninterrupted trials, reverberation reduced phasic responses, consistent with our

83 

> expectations that onsets in the ongoing streams would be less distinct; however, interruptions

84 

> elicited similar, robust pupil responses across the two environments, suggesting that onsets of new

85 

> streams were equally salient in anechoic and reverberant settings. Together, these results

86 

> demonstrate that the effect of reverberation on performance depends on task demands: there are

87 

> larger behavioral effects when cognitive load is relatively low and acoustic differences across room

88 

> conditions play a relatively greater role in determining performance. In addition, pupillometry

89 

> reveals differences in how reverberation shapes underlying sensory and cognitive processes beyond

90 

> what can be inferred from behavioral accuracy alone.

95

97

98

> B.  Stimuli

123 

> Both target and distractor streams comprised four syllables randomly drawn with replacement

124 

> from the same set. The set consisted of three syllables, [/ba/, /da/, /ga/], produced by a male,

125 

> native English-speaking talker. Each syllable had a duration of 450 ms. The target and distractor

126 

> streams differed only in their location, with one stream spatialized to 30 degrees left and the other to

127 

> 30 degrees right of center. Which stream was left and which right was chosen randomly and

128 

> independently for each trial. Within each stream, syllables were separated by 150 ms of silence. The

129 

> two streams were temporally interleaved, with the leading stream starting 500 ms after the cue ended

130 

> and the lagging stream starting 300 ms later. Thus, the syllable-to-syllable onset time within each

131 

> stream was 600 ms, but the two streams together created an isochronous mixture with syllables

132 

> alternating between target and masker every 300 ms. The target stream started first in half the trials

133 

> and the distractor was first in the other half; in half the trials, the first stream was on the left and in

134 

> the other half, the first stream was on the right. In 50% of the trials (randomly selected), a novel

135 

> “interrupter” (duration = 250 ms) occurred 125 ms before the onset of the 2nd syllable in the target

136 

> stream, from 90 degrees left/right of center contralateral to the target.

137 

> Interrupters were brief, non-speech sounds selected to be acoustically and perceptually distinct

138 

> from the syllable streams, ensuring they remained salient attention-capturing events even under

139 

> reverberant conditions. A set of 80 unique (half of trials) interrupter sounds was drawn from the

140

152

> F.  Main Task

190 

> In all experiments, participants performed 160 trials in which they heard two competing,

191 

> temporally interleaved, spatialized streams from opposite hemifields. At the start of each trial, a

192 

> single spatialized /ba/ syllable played from either 30-degrees left or right, cueing the listener to

193 

> attend to the stream at that location (the target) and ignore the other (the distractor). Once the

194 

> stream ended, there was a 3-second silent interval to allow the pupils to return to baseline.

195 

> Participants were then asked to report back the sequence of four target syllables played in the target

196 

> stream via a computer keyboard. After their response, there was an additional three seconds of

197 

> silence before the next trial began (FIG 1).

198 

> The 160 trials were divided into eight blocks of 20 trials each, where the acoustic condition

199 

> (anechoic or reverberant) was consistent across all stimuli within a block. Half of the blocks

200 

> presented anechoic simulations and half reverberant simulations, alternating across blocks. Half of

201 

> the participants began with an anechoic block and half with a reverberant block.

202 

> The direction of the leading stream was manipulated between subjects: half of the subjects

203 

> always heard stimuli with the left stream leading and half always heard stimuli with the right stream

204 

> leading. Importantly, all subjects were presented with an equal number of target-left and target-right

205 

> trials. Although target stream direction and target timing (leading vs. lagging) were not

206 

> counterbalanced within subjects, this design choice should not affect our primary question

207 

> concerning the effect of the interrupter and its interaction with reverberation.

208 

> Within each block, trials were fully counterbalanced such that half the trials were uninterrupted

209 

> and half contained an interrupter, and half had the target on the left and half the target on the right.

210 

> Trial order was randomized independently for each block and each participant. Participants were

211 

> instructed to rest in between blocks.

229 

> Behavioral performance was computed as the proportion of target stream syllables correctly

230

231

232

> H.  Pupillometry

239 

> 1.  Pre-processing

240 

> Pupil dilation from the left eye was recorded with the EyeLink 1000 at a sampling rate of 1000

241 

> Hz. Instances where participants had full or partial eye closure (e.g., blinks) were treated as missing

242 

> data. Participants with excessive missing data (>50%) were excluded from further analysis. A total of

243 

> 9 participants were excluded for this reason (refer to the Participants section in Methods).

244 

> Artifacts such as eye blinks were removed and replaced by linearly interpolating 50 ms before

245 

> and 150 ms after an eye blink. A 4th order zero-phase, lowpass Butterworth filter at a 10 Hz cutoff

246 

> was applied. Data were then epoched from -1 second to 7 seconds. To separate out tonic from

247 

> phasic pupillary responses and account for between-participant differences in absolute pupil size,

248 

> each trial was baseline corrected using a pre-cue interval (-0.5 to 0 sec). Specifically, the mean pupil

249 

> size over the baseline window was computed for each trial. Tonic responses were calculated by

250 

> averaging these baseline values across all trials; the across-trial standard deviation of these baseline

251 

> values was also computed per subject. Phasic responses were calculated as z-scores based on the

252 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 13
> distribution of across-trial baseline responses. First, the trial-specific baseline was subtracted from

253 

> the corresponding epoch. The resulting baseline-corrected time traces were then averaged and

254 

> divided by the participant-specific baseline standard deviation to generate z scores. For robustness,

255 

> we also computed phasic responses using two alternative approaches: baseline subtraction alone (no

256 

> scaling) and proportional change relative to the trial baseline mean (data provided in supplemental

257 

> figures FIGS S3A and S3B, respectively). These alternative analyses yielded similar results and

258 

> supported the same conclusions; therefore, here we report z-scored pupil responses.

> 2.  Analyses

260 

> Tonic responses were compared across the different block types within each experiment (i.e.,

261 

> comparing anechoic and reverberant conditions in the main experiment and between uninterrupted

262 

> and interrupted conditions in the follow-up experiment).

263 

> Three analyses were performed to quantify the effect of interruption and acoustic environment

264 

> on the phasic pupil trace (described in further detail below): (1) cluster-based permutation testing on

265 

> the entire pupil trace, (2) analysis of peak pupil dilation latency (time to maximum peak in seconds),

266 

> and (3) analysis of peak pupil dilation amplitude. Peak pupil latency was computed as the time it

267 

> took for the pupil dilation to reach its maximum peak. Peak amplitude was found by averaging pupil

268 

> dilation across a ± 50 ms window around the peak latency.

269 

> To assess time-resolved differences in pupil dilation between interrupted and uninterrupted trials

270 

> within each acoustic environment, we conducted a cluster-based permutation test on the baseline-

271 

> corrected pupil time courses using custom MATLAB scripts based on the procedure described by

272 

> (Maris and Oostenveld, 2007). For each acoustic environment (anechoic and reverberant), we

273 

> compared the uninterrupted and interrupted conditions using a paired-sample (dependent) cluster-

274 

> based permutation test across the entire pupil trace. We used 10,000 permutations with a two-sided

275 

> threshold of p = 0.05 to define clusters of continuous time points exhibiting significant differences.

276 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 14
> The test statistic was the sum of t-values within each cluster (T-sum). The null distribution was

277 

> generated by randomly sign-flipping the difference waveforms for each subject and constructing the

278 

> same test statistic. Empirical clusters were considered significant if their T-sum exceeded the 95th

279 

> percentile of the null distribution. This procedure controls for multiple comparisons across time

280 

> while maintaining sensitivity to temporally sustained effects.

281 

> To examine the effects of reverberation and interruption on the latency and amplitude of the

282 

> peak pupil dilation, peak pupil latency and amplitude were extracted for each participant and

283 

> condition. Normality of the residuals was assessed using Shapiro-Wilk tests, which revealed that the

284 

> data were not normally distributed (all p <0.05). Therefore, nonparametric statistical analyses were

285 

> performed. For each participant, condition effects were quantified using subject-level difference

286 

> scores. Main effects of interruption and environment were tested by sign-flipping these difference

287 

> scores across participants to generate null distributions of the mean effect (10,000 permutations).

288 

> Interaction effects were calculated by computing difference-of-differences for each participant and

289 

> applying the same sign-flipping procedure. Two-tailed p-values were computed as the proportion of

290 

> permuted effects whose absolute value exceeded the observed effect.

> IV.  DISCUSSION

412 

> This study examined how auditory spatial attention, stimulus interruption, and reverberation

413 

> jointly shape auditory perception and listening effort. Using a selective attention task with

414 

> interleaved, spatially separated syllable streams with and without interruptions in anechoic and

415 

> reverberant environments. In addition to behavioral performance, we simultaneously measured pupil

416 

> dilation to provide a complementary index of attentional allocation and cognitive load. We

417 

> considered two competing behavioral hypotheses: 1) Reverberation would smear the acoustic

418 

> features of the mixture, reducing the salience of the interrupter and decreasing its disruptive impact,

419 

> or 2) reverberation would make the task more difficult overall, compounding the effect of the

420 

> interrupter. Physiologically, we hypothesized that reverberation would increase task difficulty

421 

> generally, which would be reflected in larger pupil dilation, particularly in the tonic component of

422 

> the pupil response, relative to anechoic conditions.

423 

> A.  Effects of Reverberation on Behavioral Performance

424 

> Many of our behavioral results replicated findings from the previous experiments using

425 

> variations of the current experimental paradigm (Figarola et al., 2025; Liang et al., 2022, 2025).

426 

> Specifically, we found performance tended to be best for the first syllable and second best for the

427 

> final syllable, in line with classic serial-recall phenomena in which early (primacy) and late (recency)

428 

> list items are remembered best (Murdock, 1962). Also consistent with past, similar studies, the

429 

> interrupter significantly impaired syllable recall performance in both anechoic and reverberant trials

430 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 22
> (Figarola et al., 2025). In the current study, specifically, interruption impaired syllable recall accuracy

431 

> for syllables 1-3, and, as in past studies, the largest effect was on recall of the syllable immediately

432 

> following the disruption (Syllable 2). This pattern is consistent with prior work on attentional

433 

> capture, in which an abrupt stimulus disrupts selective attention and interferes with ongoing stream

434 

> segregation and memory encoding (Figarola et al., 2025; Liang et al., 2022; Luthra, 2024; Mondor

435 

> and Zatorre, 1995; Parmentier, 2014; Parmentier et al., 2011). This disruption can be especially

436 

> costly when the interruption is temporally close and overlaps with the subsequent syllable, where

437 

> limited time is available to reestablish attentional focus (Larson and Lee, 2013; Logan, 2005).

438 

> However, participants were able to recover towards the end of a trial, demonstrating that they were

439 

> able to refocus attention on the target stream. These dynamics highlight the everyday demands made

440 

> on attentional control in complex auditory environments.

441 

> While many of the current behavioral results align with past findings, there was one interesting

442 

> difference: The effect of reverberation on performance in the current experiment was much larger

443 

> and more consistent than what was reported previously in a very similar study (Experiment 4 in

444 

> (Figarola et al., 2025). This previous study used the same task, the same anechoic and reverberant

445 

> spatialization methods and rooms, the same syllable set, the same blocking of trials, and the same

446 

> participant interface. In the current experiment, performance was consistently worse in reverberant

447 

> than in anechoic trials (ranging between about 5%-10% difference in percent correct performance)

448 

> both for uninterrupted and interrupted trials. Yet in the very similar previous study, anechoic and

449 

> reverberant performance was nearly identical across the two room conditions for all syllables in the

450 

> uninterrupted trials and for all syllables except the one occurring right after the interruption in

451 

> interrupted trials.

452 

> The most notable methodological differences between the two experiments were that the

453 

> current study:

454 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 23
> ● was conducted in a laboratory setting, rather than online, in order to allow us to track

455 

> pupil dilation over the course of each trial;

456 

> ● added extra time in between trials to allow phasic pupil responses to return to

457 

> baseline after each trial; and

458 

> ● used four syllables in each target and masker stream, rather than five, to reduce the

459 

> duration of individual trials.

460 

> We believe that the key difference lies in the difference in the length of the target and distractor

461 

> streams. In the current experiment, with only four syllables, the working memory load is lower,

462 

> which places greater emphasis on perceptual differences related to the room acoustics. This in turn

463 

> may lead to more consistent effects of reverberation on performance: When cognitive demands on

464 

> working memory determine performance, outcomes may be less tightly coupled to the room

465 

> acoustics. However, the fact that the prior study was conducted online may also play a role, leading

466 

> to differences in everything from the degree of experimental control to the demographics and

467 

> motivation of the participants. Future work could explore these factors to confirm our interpretation

468 

> that reducing the memory load accounts for why reverberation has a large impact on performance in

469 

> the current experiment, even though it had a limited impact in the previous, very similar study.

470 

> Finally, in the current study, the interrupter tended to have a larger effect in anechoic than in

471 

> reverberant trials. We believe this reflects effects of reverberation on the perceptual salience of the

472 

> interrupter. Late reverberation from the preceding target and distractor syllables overlaps with the

473 

> interrupter, energetically masking its onset (Darwin and Hukin, 2000; Nabelek et al., 1989; Ruggles

474 

> and Shinn-Cunningham, 2011; Shinn-Cunningham, 2000). Reverberation also reduces the clarity of

475 

> the scene by blurring segregation cues, such as spatial and pitch differences (Darwin and Hukin,

476 

> 2000; Devore and Shinn-Cunningham, 2003; Nabelek et al., 1989; Shinn-Cunningham et al., 2005).

477 

> In contrast, in anechoic environments these details remain better defined, so the interrupter may be

478 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 24
> more salient and more disruptive. Thus, though the interrupter impaired performance in both

479 

> environments, its greater impact in anechoic conditions suggests that attentional capture from an

480 

> unexpected event is stronger when there is greater perceptual clarity of the interrupting event.

481 

482 

> B.  Effects of Reverberation on Pupil Responses

483 

> As noted above, performance was significantly worse in reverberant blocks than anechoic

484 

> blocks. Importantly, reverberant performance still exceeded chance performance, demonstrating

485 

> that participants were actively engaged in the task even in these difficult, reverberant trials (i.e. the

486 

> difficulty of the condition did not cause them to give up). We hypothesized that participants would

487 

> have stronger pupillary responses in reverberant blocks compared to anechoic blocks, reflecting

488 

> their increased difficulty; pupil dilation is often argued to increase with task difficulty and cognitive

489 

> load, whether from acoustic degradation, linguistic complexity, or memory demands (Contadini-

490 

> Wright et al., 2023; Koelewijn et al., 2012, 2015; Winn et al., 2015). However, the current results

491 

> suggest that attentional salience strongly drives the pupillary response in a way that was not explicitly

492 

> tested by these previous studies.

493 

> Tonic (baseline) pupillary responses were no different across reverberant and anechoic blocks.

494 

> That is, reverberation did not substantially elevate  sustained  arousal or global listening effort in this

495 

> paradigm, even though reverberation diminished behavioral performance. Additionally, there was no

496 

> tonic pupil size difference across blocks defined by the presence or absence of interruption (see

497 

> supplemental follow-up study). Together, these observations suggest that if sustained effort is

498 

> elevated by the expectation of ongoing stimulus difficulty, the stimulus conditions in the current

499 

> experiment did not rise to the level of eliciting that sustained difference.

500 

> Even more surprising, peak pupil dilation was significantly larger and later in anechoic trials

501 

> compared to reverberant trials. This further reinforces the notion that performance accuracy on its

502 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 25
> own – which was higher in the reverberant condition – cannot explain the magnitude of phasic pupil

503 

> responses. A similar mismatch between performance and elicited pupil size has previously been

504 

> observed for sentence recognition tasks as well (Winn and Teece, 2021, 2022).

505 

> One possible explanation for the reduced phasic pupil responses in reverberation is that

506 

> participants did not engage as fully on the task in the more difficult, reverberant-trial blocks. For

507 

> instance, the FUEL framework proposes that effort depends not just on task demands but also on

508 

> task engagement, motivation, and the perceived likelihood of success (Pichora-Fuller et al., 2016).

509 

> Following this, studies have reported larger pupil dilation for acoustically clearer or more

510 

> informative speech, suggesting that pupil responses track a participant’s willingness to invest effort

511 

> when it is likely to yield a benefit (Fitzgerald et al., 2024; Mechtenberg et al., 2024). Although task

512 

> performance was not in the sub-40% range that some studies have identified as resulting in effort

513 

> withdrawal (Ohlenforst et al., 2017; Wendt et al., 2018), listeners still may have adapted to the

514 

> reverberant condition, neutralizing the novelty of this particular challenge. To directly test this idea,

515 

> we conducted a follow-up study in which we varied the blocking of trials so that harder reverberant

516 

> trials were randomly intermingled with easier anechoic trials, so that listeners could not predict

517 

> whether an upcoming trial was difficult or easy. Specifically, over the experiment the same

518 

> distribution of trials was presented, but half of the blocks contained only uninterrupted trials and

519 

> half contained only interrupted trials (Supplementary FIG S1 and S2). Phasic responses were

520 

> generally lower in this supplemental experiment, likely reflecting the fact that the structure of the

521 

> stimuli within a block were identical, and therefore less surprising overall. Critically, just as in the

522 

> main experiment, for uninterrupted trials the phasic response was lower for reverberant than for

523 

> anechoic simulations—even though the two trial types were intermingled. This makes it less likely

524 

> that differences in the phasic responses for uninterrupted trials reflect differences in participant

525 

> effort, engagement, or long-term expectations over an entire testing block.

526 

> .CC-BY 4.0 International license available under a was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made The copyright holder for this preprint (which this version posted April 2, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.03.31.715639 doi: bioRxiv preprint 26
> We argue that reverberation results in weaker phasic pupil dilation because it degrades

527 

> segregation cues, making the onsets of individual syllables less distinct and thus less attentionally

528 

> salient. Specifically, in anechoic trials, each successive syllable may have evoked a phasic response

529 

> that added to the accumulated pupil dilation over the course of the trial, leading to larger peak

530 

> responses that built up over a longer time. In reverberant trials, each syllable is less clear, driving a

531 

> weaker phasic response. In this view, rather than reflecting task difficulty, phasic responses index the

532 

> acoustic salience of individual syllable onsets (see Klingner et al. 2011 and Winn et al. 2018 for

533 

> further examples of the buildup of individual components to a larger pupil response).

534 

> In both acoustic environments, the interrupter elicited a large, robust pupil response, consistent

535 

> with salient and behaviorally relevant events eliciting pupil dilation (Dayan and Yu, 2006). The

536 

> cluster-based permutation analyses confirmed sustained increases in pupil dilation for interrupted

537 

> relative to uninterrupted trials in both room types. Moreover, interruption shifted peak latency

538 

> earlier compared to uninterrupted trials. These results confirm that in both anechoic and reverberant

539 

> trials, the interrupter was salient, driving a robust phasic pupil response, consistent with rapid

540 

> orienting to a salient, unexpected event. Given that the target and distractor streams were speech

541 

> syllables dominated by a consistent vowel sound that would carry strongly in reverberation, but the

542 

> interrupters were brief, non-speech sounds, this result makes sense—the reverberation can cause

543 

> syllables spoken by the same talker to smear together and be difficult to segregate, but do not

544 

> disrupt segregation of the novel interrupters, which are acoustically distinct from the target and

545 

> distractor streams. Consistent with this, the difference in peak pupil dilation between anechoic and

546 

> reverberant trials was smaller in interrupted trials than in uninterrupted trials. This conforms to the

547 

> idea that the early interrupter-evoked response, which dominated the phasic pupillary responses, had

548 

> a similar strength in both rooms.

> pupil response reflects activity of the locus-coeruleus-norepinephrine (LC-NE) system, which plays

553 

> a key role in processing interrupting events in the environment (Dayan and Yu, 2006; Lim et al.,

554 

> 2021; Sara and Bouret, 2012). Through this mechanism, the pupil-linked LC-NE system is

555 

> particularly sensitive to abrupt, bottom-up changes in the auditory scene, (Zhao et al., 2019a),

556 

> making it a reliable marker of disrupted attention. In addition, the interrupter increases cognitive

557 

> demands by requiring participants to refocus attention back on the target stream after the

558 

> involuntary disruption. The compounding effects of the interrupter align with Load Theory, which

559 

> proposes that interference from irrelevant stimuli increases when perceptual and cognitive resources

560 

> are taxed (Lavie et al., 2004). In the present study, the phasic response triggered by the interrupter

561 

> may thus reflect a combination of both a salience-driven attentional reset and an increase in

562 

> cognitive load, with the enhanced pupil response indexing this compounded burden.

563 

> Taken together, the pupil data suggest that pupil dilation in this task is driven more strongly by

564 

> discrete, salient acoustic events and dynamic processing demands than by sustained cognitive effort

565 

> related to performance accuracy. The spectrotemporal smearing caused by reverberation degrades

566 

> performance because it makes it difficult to segregate the competing target and masker syllables,

567 

> which are identical except in their direction. However, the interruptions—abrupt and behaviorally

568 

> salient events that are distinct in their spectrotemporal properties from the speech—elicits large,

569 

> rapid pupil responses in both anechoic and reverberant environments. Thus, pupil dynamics reveal

570 

> changes in sensory scene organization and salience that are not directly inferred from behavioral

571 

> accuracy alone.

> C.  Future Directions

574 

> Future work should explore whether similar salience-driven pupil dynamics dominate responses

575 

> for more naturalistic and linguistically rich materials. Dominance of acoustic salience was observed

576 

> by Winn and Teece (2021) in sentence recognition tasks where a disruption was either a clear

577 

> departure from the ongoing speech stream (a noise masking a word) versus a change within the

578 

> speech stream itself (a mispronunciation of a word in a sentence). In their study and in the current

579 

> study, the disruptor that was more obviously different from the target stream elicited stronger pupil

580 

> dilations. However, this previous work was not designed with salience explicitly as a stimulus factor,

581 

> leaving opportunity to examine this issue in greater depth. Considering the greater pupil dilation for

582 

> vocoded (i.e. less perceptually salient) speech versus clear speech (Winn 2016), there is likely a

583 

> competing tension between pupil dilation driven by effort to complete a task versus dilation driven

584 

> by stimulus salience. Following up on the current work, manipulating reverberation strength

585 

> parametrically would allow characterization of the relationship between acoustic smearing and phasic

586 

> response magnitude. In addition, systematically varying working memory load within the same

587 

> laboratory paradigm would directly test the proposal that reducing working memory demands

588 

> increases the relative contribution of acoustic factors to performance.

589 

> Expanding the participant population is also important to ensure that the current results are not

590 

> over generalized. Older adults, listeners with hearing impairment, and individuals with attentional or

591 

> cognitive differences may show distinct interactions between reverberation, interruption, and pupil-

592 

> linked indices of effort. Individual differences in working memory capacity and attentional control

593 

> could alter how reverberation and bottom-up interruptions interact when participants deploy top-

594 

> down selective attention.

595 

> Finally, combining pupillometry with electrophysiological measures (e.g., EEG or MEG) could

596 

> tease apart the contributions of early sensory encoding, attentional reorienting, and memory-related

597

> processes during interrupted attention. Such multimodal approaches could clarify how cortical

598 

> dynamics interact with pupil-linked neuromodulatory signals during sustained auditory attention in

599 

> complex environments.

> D.  Conclusions

601 

> Reverberation degrades acoustic scene clarity, reducing the distinctiveness of temporally adjacent

602 

> events and impairing segregation of competing speech streams. In the present task, this degradation

603 

> produced reliable declines in syllable recall performance. Critically, reverberation also reduced phasic

604 

> pupil responses during uninterrupted trials, indicating that individual syllables were less salient when

605 

> temporally smeared by room reflections. These physiological results provide converging evidence

606 

> that reverberation weakens event-level sensory encoding rather than simply increasing global

607 

> cognitive effort.

608 

> Despite the reduction in clarity caused by reverberation, abrupt interruptions with clear

609 

> spectral deviations from the ongoing syllable stream elicited robust behavioral disruption and large

610 

> phasic pupil responses in both anechoic and reverberant environments. Peak pupil responses

611 

> occurred earlier and were significantly larger in interrupted trials, consistent with a framework of

612 

> pupil dilation driven by rapid attentional capture by salient, unexpected events. Importantly, the

613 

> interrupter-evoked pupil response was comparable across room conditions, suggesting that

614 

> perceptually distinct, novel sounds retain their salience even in strongly reverberant environments.

615 

> Together, these findings demonstrate that pupil dynamics in this paradigm are driven more

616 

> strongly by discrete, bottom-up acoustic salience than by sustained task difficulty. Although

617 

> reverberation impaired performance, it did not elevate tonic pupil diameter and instead reduced

618 

> phasic responses to ongoing speech events. In contrast, sudden interruptions dominated the

619 

> pupillary response regardless of room acoustics.
