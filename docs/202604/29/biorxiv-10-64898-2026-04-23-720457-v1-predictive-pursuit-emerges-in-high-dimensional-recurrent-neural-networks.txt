Abstract 12 

Tracking dynamic moving objects in the external world is ethologically important for many organisms. 13 

Recent experiments have examined neural dynamics supporting such behaviors by employing visually-14 

guided pursuit in freely moving rodents, yet computational principles underlying this cognitive process 15 

are not well understood. To address this, we developed a recurrent neural network model for examining 16 

the predictive behaviors and computations that emerge during pursuit. We demonstrate that the model 17 

generates internal predictions of the target’s future locations, with anticipatory behaviors increasing 18 

with exposure to stereotyped trajectories of the target. These internal predictions can be used by the 19 

model to pursue a target in a complex environment, and the model’s emergent strategy is aligned with 20 

behavior when tested in rodents. In investigating the computations that underlie the model’s ability to 21 

perform predictive pursuit, we found units sensitive to the position of the target relative to the artificial 22 

agent, a representation analogous to egocentric target neurons observed in animals performing pursuit 23 

tasks. Ablating these units significantly reduced model performance, establishing a causal role of this 24 

functional response type in efficient pursuit. Given the complexity of the task and agent behavior, we 25 

hypothesized that RNN models may use high-dimensional neural codes to support predictive pursuit. To 26 

test this, we trained models of varying rank and found that anticipatory behavior emerged only when 27 

the rank was sufficiently high, despite strong pursuit performance in lower rank models. All RNNs 28 

encoded the egocentric location of the target, whereas allocentric self and target locations emerged only 29 

in high-dimensional networks. Overall, our results suggest that, unlike commonly studied vision, motor, 30 

or memory tasks, predictive pursuit emerges in high-dimensional networks with sufficient resources. 31 

# Introduction 32 

Pursuit is a ubiquitous behavior in biology, with a wide a range of organisms chasing others for predation and 33 

reproduction. In cases where the pursuer is faster than its target, moving to minimize the relative distance 34 

between the two can be an effective strategy for capture. However, when the target can move at higher 35 

speeds than the pursuer or is more agile, effective pursuit requires utilizing dynamic information about the 36 

target to make predictions of where it is headed. The complex way in which environmental affordances 37 

(e.g., boundaries, burrows), movement statistics (e.g., range of typical speeds), and stereotyped actions (e.g., 38 

common routes, zigzagging, slowing and accelerating) inform this prediction makes pursuit a rich paradigm 39 

for studying the neural computations underlying model-based inference and predictive planning [1]. 40

Evidence of the ability to predictively pursue others has been found in dragonflies [ 2 ], snakes [ 3 ], bats 41 

[ 4– 6], rats [ 7], and non-human primates [ 8 , 9], demonstrating the widespread use of this behavior. For some 42 

organisms (e.g., snakes), the neural circuitry enabling this predictivity may be evolutionarily hard-wired. 43 

However, for other organisms (e.g., rats and non-human primates), predictive pursuit can emerge with 44 

learning [ 7 –9 ]. As planning and inference drive many intelligent behaviors, a central challenge in neuroscience 45 

is to identify neural computations that enable prediction. 46 

To address this open question, we develop a recurrent neural network (RNN) model for examining the 47 

behavior and computations that emerge during continuous pursuit of a moving target. RNN models are 48 

powerful tools for normatively studying the computations that underlie cognitive processes [ 10 –17 ] and have 49 

been used to study observed neural computational properties in spatial navigation [ 18 –22 ] and decision 50 

making [ 23 –26 ] tasks. In addition, RNNs enable manipulations not easily possible in systems neuroscience 51 

experiments, such as ablating specific functional classes of neurons [ 27 , 28 ] and changing the inherent rank 52 

of the network’s connectivity [ 29 –38 ] (i.e., the number of degrees of freedom with which the recurrent units 53 

of the RNN are wired together). 54 

Here, we interrogate RNNs trained on pursuit tasks. We find that RNNs can accurately intercept 55 

targets, and learn to do so using strategies that suggest the development and deployment of an internal 56 

model. Specifically, we find that RNNs can anticipate future locations of the target, generating pursuit 57 

trajectories that are significantly more predictive than those exhibited by a non-predictive control model. 58 

Model predictive behaviors can be maintained even when information about the target is intermittently 59 

masked, supporting an internal model of target behavior during pursuit. RNNs make experimentally testable 60 

predictions about how a predictive biological agent behaves during novel target pursuit tasks, which we 61 

validate against newly collected rodent behavioral data. Beyond behavior, we find representations in the 62 

RNNs that resemble neural egocentric receptive fields reported in target-chasing rats [ 39 , 40 ], and lesions of 63 

these units disrupt model performance. Collectively, these results demonstrate that the RNNs learn complex, 64 

predictive pursuit strategies that closely parallel rodent behavior at both the single-unit and behavioral 65 

levels. 66 

To analyze how the RNN learns predictive pursuit strategies, we perform a mechanistic study of predictive 67 

pursuit in networks with constrained connectivity (i.e., low-rank RNNs). Earlier studies have primarily 68 

focused on visual, motor, or memory tasks that are often solvable via low-dimensional networks with 69 

limited computational resources [ 13 , 30 –34 , 36 ]. When trained on such tasks, even when networks are 70 

not explicitly constrained, RNNs tend to converge to low-rank solutions after training [ 32 , 41 , 42 ]. This 71 

observation is generally attributed to the inherent low-dimensionality of the behavior they model [ 11 , 24 , 43 ]. 72 

Predictive pursuit, by contrast, requires modeling the environment and the target, a complex demand that 73 

we hypothesize would require high-dimensional network connectivity. To test this, we train RNNs of varying 74 

rank and examine their pursuit trajectories. We find that anticipatory behavior emerges only in RNNs 75 

that are high-dimensional, even though lower rank RNNs solve the task with high accuracy. Examining the 76 

learned representations of the models, we find that all RNNs learn to represent the egocentric location of 77 

the target. However, only higher rank RNNs learn to represent allocentric self and target locations, in which 78 

allocentric representations presumably support anticipatory trajectory planning. This suggests that complex, 79 

ethologically relevant behaviors may require high-dimensional network solutions, which are inherently harder 80 

to interpret than the low-dimensional solutions that suffice for idealized tasks [ 24 , 31 ]. As the field moves 81 

toward studying neural computation in naturalistic settings [ 44 , 45 ], our work constitutes a mechanistic 82 

step in this direction, providing a tractable RNN framework for studying the rich computational structure 83 

that underlies predictive pursuit. 84

# Results 85 

RNN models learn to pursue moving target 86 

We develop an RNN model that learns to steer an agent (“RNN-agent”) in pursuit of a “target-agent”. The 87 

RNN takes as inputs the initial positions of both agents and the instantaneous velocity of the target-agent, 88 

and outputs its own velocity (Fig. 1A; see Appendix A for details). The RNN is trained to move the 89 

RNN-agent towards the target-agent, so that the distance between the two on the last time-step of the 90 

trial is minimized. This is done to mimic “capturing” the target, but in a supervised learning setting. Each 91 

trial begins with the RNN-agent and target-agent initialized at distinct locations and lasts for T time-steps. 92 

Importantly, except for the starting positions,  xRNN (0) , y RNN (0)  and  xtarget (0) , x target (0) , the RNN is 93 

not given any ground truth spatial information. Thus, to solve the pursuit task, the RNN must integrate 94 

the dynamic information of both agents [46]. 95

(RTs) and characteristic trajectories (CTs). During RT trials, the target-agent pseudo-randomly samples 97

RNN models leverage egocentric representations for pursuit 113 

Electrophysiological recordings in rats pursuing a dynamic target (in trials similar to our RTs) have identified 114 

neurons in posterior parietal cortex (PPC) and retrosplenial cortex (RSC) that demonstrate tuning to the 115 

location and orientation of the target relative to the self [ 7 , 40 ]. These egocentric target cells (ETCs) were 116 

hypothesized to play an important role in pursuit during RTs, although causally demonstrating this in freely 117 

behaving animals is challenging. Given this putative role of ETCs in pursuit, we asked whether such a 118 

representation emerged at the level of individual units in the RNN. Plotting the average unit activations as 119 

functions of egocentric target position, we find units whose activations are selectively increased when the 120 

target-agent is at specific distances and orientations relative to the RNN-agent (Fig. 2A, top row). The 121 

egocentric target tuning of these units becomes especially clear when comparing them to units that exhibit 122 

no such selectivity (Fig. 2A, bottom row). 123 

A standard method for quantifying the strength of egocentric representations is through the computation 124 

of the associated mean resultant length (MRL) [ 7, 39 ]. This metric is independent of the distance between 125 

two agents and quantifies how strongly a neuron’s activity is concentrated around a preferred egocentric 126 

direction to the target (see Appendix C for details). We compute the MRL for all units in the RNN models 127 

and compare them to a shuffle control, where the activations are randomly shuffled across time points for 128 

each unit (Fig. 2B; see Appendix C for details). We classify units as egocentric target units (ETUs) if their 129 

MRL is greater than the 99 th percentile of the shuffled distribution and their MRL values are consistent 130 

across two splits of trials. Overall, we find 36% of all recurrent units are ETUs (range: 29% -42% across five 131 

independently trained RNNs), which is consistent with proportions observed in neural recordings [7]. 132 

To understand whether this emergent egocentric target representation is used by the RNN to perform 133 

pursuit, we order ETUs by their MRL and remove (or “ablate”) an increasing number of them from the model. 134 

We find that the RNN is sensitive to the loss of ETUs and performance quickly deteriorates significantly 135 

more than when removing randomly selected units (Fig. 2C; compare red and gray curves). In addition, we 136 

find that removing units with the smallest MRL values impacts model pursuit performance significantly less 137 

than when removing randomly selected units (Fig. 2C; compare green and gray curves). Together, these 138 

observations establish a causal role for ETU representations in successful pursuit in RNNs. 139 

To further characterize the nature of these representations, we next examine whether ETU activity 140 

reflects past, current, or future target states. Specifically, we shift the egocentric target coordinates forward 141 

(“prospective”) or backwards (“retrospective”) in time, relative to the unit activations. Recomputing the 142 

egocentric target ratemaps on the shifted data, we find that the average MRL exhibits strong decreases for 143 

retrospective shifts, but not prospective shifts (Fig. 2D). In addition, the percent of units classified as ETUs 144 

peaks at a slight prospective shift (Fig. 2E). This suggests that the emergent egocentric target representations 145 

are not merely products of encoding the past relative positions of the target-agent, but instead skewed 146

RNN models learn to conduct predictive pursuit 149 

While the RNNs are able to successfully pursue the target-agent, this does not imply that they are doing it 150 

in a predictive manner as observed in rodents or other species [ 2, 6–8 ]. For instance, the RNN-agent could 151 

simply learn to minimize the distance with the target-agent at every time step (i.e., pure pursuit). Since 152 

RNNs are scored based on the final distance between two agents, this strategy could still represent one 153 

minimum of the loss function. However, our analysis in Fig. 2D, E reveals prospective coding in ETUs, a 154 

result suggestive of the RNN models’ capability of performing predictive pursuit. We therefore quantify and 155 

test the capabilities of the RNNs to conduct pursuit with predictive strategies. 156 

We compare the pursuit trajectories generated by the RNNs to a non-predictive control model, which 157 

moves in the direction that minimizes its distance to the current position of the target-agent (Fig. 3A). This 158 

control model is implemented by a set of equations (see Appendix D for details). The control model uses the 159 

same instantaneous speed as that outputted by the RNN model (as opposed to the maximum possible speed), 160 

meaning it does not perform an optimal greedy pursuit, but rather a realistic approximation of reactive 161 

pursuit . By construction, this reactive (non-predictive) model ignores all the dynamical history of the 162 

target-agent that is inputted to the RNN, as well as any information about the boundaries of the environment, 163 

which may act to constrain the future behavior of the target-agent. Hence, if the pursuit strategy used by 164 

the RNN seeks to minimize instantaneous distance with the target-agent, then the non-predictive model 165 

should have the same trajectory as the RNN-agent. However, deviations from the non-predictive control 166 

trajectory alone do not necessarily imply prediction, as they could also reflect a poorer pursuit strategy. We 167 

therefore use the non-predictive control as a baseline for reactive pursuit, and quantify predictive behavior 168 

using geometric metrics derived from the resulting trajectories (described below). 169 

Visually inspecting pursuit trajectories of both models, we find that the RNN performs pursuit along 170 

trajectories that differ from those used by the non-predictive model, on both RTs and CTs (Fig. 3B). 171 

Notably, the RNN and non-predictive control models’ trajectories differ on average by 22% of the total 172 

length traveled on RTs, and 40% of the total length traveled on CTs (Fig. 3C). These differences also 173 

manifest in differences in performance, as the RNN model outperforms the non-predictive control model, 174 

particularly on CTs (Fig. 3D; median distance to end location of target-agent on RTs: RNN = 0.054 m vs. 175 

non-predictive control = 0.062 m; CTs: RNN = 0.018 m vs. non-predictive control = 0.077 m). 176 

Next, we quantify the difference in predictivity between the RNN and non-predictive control models’ 177 

trajectories by defining a geometrically motivated “shortcut metric”. First, we define a reference trajectory 178 

given by the direct path between the starting location of the RNN-agent and the final location of the 179 

target-agent (i.e., the path an agent with perfect knowledge would take; Fig. 3E). For each model, we then 180 

compute the average distance between its trajectory and this reference path. This quantity serves as a 181 

measure of how “shortcut-like” a trajectory is (see Appendix D.1 for more details), as an agent with full 182 

knowledge of the target’s final position should in principle follow this path, yielding a lower bound of 0. As 183 

a baseline for non-shortcut-like trajectories, we compare against the non-predictive control model. 184 

We find that, on both RTs and CTs, the RNN model’s trajectories have significantly smaller shortcut 185 

metric (i.e., are more shortcut-like) than the non-predictive model (Fig. 3F; two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov 186 

test between RNN and non-predictive control model shortcut metrics D(5000) = 0 .23 , p < 0.001 on RTs 187 

and D(5000) = 0 .58 , p < 0.001 , on CTs). We measure the difference in shortcut metric distribution across 188 

trials using the Wasserstein distance, where greater distance corresponds to more distinct strategies between 189 

the RNN and non-predictive control models. We find that the Wasserstein distance between the shortcut 190 

metric distributions of the RNN and non-predictive models is more than doubled on CTs when compared to 191 

RTs (Fig. 3F; Wasserstein distance = 0 .03 on RTs and 0.07 on CTs), demonstrating that the presence of 192 

repeated structure is more heavily exploited by the RNN model. Furthermore, the significant difference 193 

between shortcut metrics on RTs, albeit being weaker compared to CTs, suggests that the RNN utilizes 194 

repeated aspects of the target-agent’s pseudo-random trajectory (e.g., its bias away from walls, its bias 195

RNN model maintains and updates internal representation of target 199 

A hallmark of predictive computation is the maintenance, updating, and use of an internal model of the 200 

environment, which in turn shapes ongoing behavior and neural processing [ 47 –50 ]. Beyond generating 201 

predictions about future states, an internal model is expected to be acquired through structured experience 202 

and to persist autonomously even when sensory input is removed. We have already provided evidence for 203 

the predictive nature of the RNNs through representational (Fig. 2) and behavioral (Fig. 3) signatures. 204 

Here, we provide two further lines of evidence. Namely, that the RNNs’ predictive behavior is shaped by 205 

increasing exposure to structured trials (Fig. S1) and that RNNs can perform predictive pursuit even when 206 

a fraction of the dynamical information about the target-agent is masked (Fig. 4). 207 

To start with, if the RNNs are indeed leveraging an internal representation of the target-agent to 208 

guide anticipatory trajectories during CT trials, then we would expect the strength of this behavior to 209 

be dependent on exposure to structured trials (i.e., CTs) during training. Thus, we hypothesize that this 210

Next, if RNNs are leveraging an internal representation of the target-agent to plan their pursuit 220 

trajectories, then they should continue to perform well even when information of the target-agent is occluded. 221 

To test this, we train RNNs with varying percent of the inputs “masked out” (Fig. 4A). Here, we still 222 

provide the RNNs with the initial location information of both RNN-agent and target-agent, but provide 223 

the velocity of the target-agent only at a fraction of the time steps, inputting 0 otherwise. As the mask 224 

percent is increased, the RNNs receive fewer inputs (or equivalently, experience an increase in the temporal 225 

spacing between inputs). Unsurprisingly, we find that greater masking percentages lead to slower learning 226 

and worse overall performance (Fig. 4B). However, RNNs are still capable of conducting pursuit accurately 227 

(Fig. 4A, D). For instance, even when information about the target-agent is only input half the time, the 228 

RNNs can still get to within approximately 0.1 m of the target-agent on RT trials (Fig. 4C). Furthermore, 229 

the RNNs demonstrate evidence of predictive behavior (Fig. 4D, E). Masking the inputs more than 50% of 230 

the time leads to less difference between the RNN and non-predictive models’ trajectories on RT trials (Fig. 231 

4D, E; left column), suggesting that large gaps in input leads the RNNs to learn a more reactive strategy on 232 

RT trials. In contrast, for CT trials, the RNNs maintain shortcut-like behavior, even as the masked percent 233 

increases (Fig. 4D, E; right column). This demonstrates that the repeated structure of the CTs can still be 234 

learned and exploited, even when instantaneous information about the target is withheld from the RNN 235 

models. 236 

Taken together, RNNs show hallmark properties of systems with an internal model of their environment. 237 

Namely, their predictive behavior is grounded in representational and behavioral signatures, shaped by 238 

structured experience, and maintained autonomously in the absence of complete sensory input [2, 51, 52]. 239 

Task and environmental determinants of predictive pursuit 240 

We hypothesized that the predictive behavior learned by the RNNs was encouraged by the task objective, 241 

which penalized the end distance between the RNN-agent and target-agent. We tested this by changing the 242 

loss function such that the RNNs were trained to minimize the average distance between the target-agent 243 

and the RNN-agent at each time step. We reasoned that the need to minimize the distance at all times 244 

would incentivize a reactive pursuit strategy. As expected and shown in Fig. S2, the differences in behavior 245 

between the RNN and non-predictive controls (observed in Fig. 3) is weakened. In particular, when the 246 

RNNs are trained to minimize the mean distance between the RNN-agent and the target-agent across the 247 

trial, the learned trajectories are similar to their non-predictive controls on both RTs and CTs (Fig. S2; see 248 

Appendix E for more details). Thus, the predictivity (or conversely, the reactivity) of the RNN models can 249 

be controlled by the choice of loss function that defines task. 250 

Next, to explore the environmental determinants of predictive pursuit, we train an additional set of 251 

networks on an environment with periodic boundaries. In this setting, the target-agent is allowed to pass 252 

through the boundaries and appear on the opposite side of the environment (Fig. 5A; black lines in left 253 

column). This addition makes the RT trials significantly harder (median distance to the end location of the 254 

target-agent in RT trials = 0 .34 m), as the target-agent can quickly cover large amounts of distance through 255 

the connectedness of the boundaries. However, the RNN is still able to perform with high accuracy on CTs 256 

(Fig. 5A, left column; median distance to end location of target-agent in CT trials = 0 .04 m). In these 257 

trials, the RNN generates trajectories that move towards the side of the environment where the target-agent 258 

will appear, as opposed to following the target-agent along its movement direction (Fig. 5A; left column, 259

From these observations, the RNN models make specific and testable predictions of how an agent (even 263 

a biological one) might conduct predictive pursuit on CT trials in an environment with periodic boundaries. 264 

In particular, the pursuer should move opposite to the target-agent and then wait, in anticipation of its 265 

emergence from the opposite boundary. We test these two predictions by extending the experimental 266

RNN models of pursuit, highlighting the potential of RNNs to generate experimentally testable predictions 273

Predictive pursuit emerges in high-dimensional RNNs 275 

Given that pursuit can be performed either predictively (Fig. 3) or reactively (Fig. S2), we next seek to 276 

understand what aspects of the RNN model architecture decide the nature of the learned strategy. We 277 

hypothesize that the degree of predictive behavior scales with computational resources, such that stronger 278 

predictive behavior emerges in networks with higher-dimensional neural codes. To test this hypothesis, we 279 

turn to RNNs with low-rank weight matrices [ 29 , 30 ]. These networks have the same number of recurrent 280 

units as the original (“full-rank”) model, but have weights that are increasingly interdependent as the rank 281 

decreases, reducing the number of free parameters (see Appendix A.2 for details). In low-rank RNNs, the 282 

intrinsic dimensionality of the neural code is bounded above by the rank of the weight matrix [ 33 ], therefore 283 

manipulating the rank of the network provides a method for controlling the number of coding (“latent”) 284 

variables without changing the network size. 285 

We find that RNN models with varying ranks (ranging from 10 to 1000) can learn to perform pursuit 286 

well, on both RTs and CTs, although higher-rank models achieve slightly better pursuit accuracies (Fig. 287 

6A). Increasing the rank of the RNNs leads to an increase in the effective dimensionality of the network 288 

activations and latent variables, as measured by the number of principal components needed to explain 95% 289 

of the variance (Fig. 6B). This is confirmed when using participation ratio as an alternative dimensionality 290 

estimate (Fig. S4). These observations are in direct contrast to previously studied low-dimensional tasks, in 291 

which latent dimensionality remains constant with increasing ranks [ 33 ], or the notion that the dimensionality 292

High-dimensional networks learn to represent allocentric information 314 

Having shown that the degree of predictive behavior scales with the dimensionality of the neural code, we 315 

next set out to extract the qualitative changes in neural representations in high-rank networks that may 316 

underlie this emergent strategy. Specifically, we examine how well distinct task features can be linearly 317 

decoded from the population activations of RNN models trained with varying ranks (see Appendix G for 318 

more details). This linear decoding acts as a proxy for how well these features are represented by the RNN 319 

models and can be used to extend our retrospective and prospective coding analysis in Fig. 2 from individual 320 

units to the entire RNN population. 321 

Given that removing ETUs led to significantly worse pursuit performance (Fig. 2), we first decode current, 322 

retrospective, and prospective egocentric target distances from population activations. Intuitively, even an 323 

agent performing a purely reactive pursuit would need to keep track of the distance to the target-agent. 324 

In-line with this intuition, the distance can be decoded with high accuracy across RNN models of all ranks 325 

(Fig. 7A). While the lowest decoding error was achieved for the current egocentric target distances, the 326 

decoding was highly accurate for both past and future values. The qualitative uniformity of the decoding 327 

performance suggests that the use of egocentric target coding as a pursuit strategy is common across all 328 

RNN model ranks, and therefore is unlikely to be the driver of the emergence of predictive behavior that we 329 

observe in higher-rank models. 330 

Next, we turn to allocentric features (i.e., positions of the target-agent and the RNN-agent). Notably, the 331 

RNNs are not explicitly given position information in allocentric coordinates (except the initial positions). 332 

Therefore, any allocentric position estimate that exists in the RNNs must be constructed by integrating 333 

velocity inputs over time. Unlike egocentric target distance, allocentric variables exhibit a strong rank 334 

dependence, with increasing rank leading to better accuracies (Fig. 7B, C). Furthermore, positions of both 335 

agents can be decoded with higher accuracies in CTs than in RTs (Fig. 7B, C; compare top row with 336 

bottom row), consistent with the repeated structure of CTs, which constrains trajectories and thereby 337 

makes positions more predictable. In addition, for both agent’s positions, decoding error decreases with 338 

prospective shift and increases with retrospective shift (Fig. 7B, C), indicating that population activations 339 

preferentially reflect predictions rather than past states. We observe similar results when we quantify the 340 

decoding performances via explained variances (Fig. S7). 341

# Discussion 354 

Development of an RNN framework for examining predictive chasing behaviors. 355 

Predictive pursuit of a dynamic target is an evolutionarily important and widespread behavior [ 2–9 ]. 356 

Understanding the neural computations that support this behavior offers the potential to gain insight 357 

on fundamental cognitive processes, such as planning and forming internal models [ 1 ]. Studying these 358 

ethologically relevant computations in a controllable laboratory setting is challenging, and prior experimental 359 

paradigms have either prioritized ethological relevance over control of the target-agent [ 54 , 55 ] or prioritized 360 

control of the target-agent over ethological relevance [ 8 , 9 ]. The recent development of tasks that involve 361 

unrestrained rodents pursuing (or avoiding) target-agents controlled by experimenters has demonstrated 362 

their ability to develop complex, long-time scale strategies that involve predictive decisions [ 7, 40 , 56 ]. 363 

Simultaneous electrophysiological recordings have also provided insight into neural representations that may 364 

underlie this behavior (e.g., egocentric target cells) [7, 40]. 365 

Motivated by these experimental results, as well as the desire to probe – at a level not yet possible 366 

experimentally – the computations that support predictive pursuit, we developed an RNN model and trained 367 

it to chase a moving target. We find that, when using a loss function that penalizes the distance between 368

predictive manner, which is increased when the target-agent has trajectories with repeated structure (Fig. 370 

3). Leveraging knowledge of the target-agent’s behavior is experience dependent, with increased exposure 371 

leading to increased anticipatory behavior (Fig. S1). This is in contrast to RNN models trained to minimize 372 

the average distance between the RNN-agent and the target-agent (Fig. S2), highlighting how the intrinsic 373 

goal (i.e., loss function) shapes the learned behavioral strategy. 374 

Emergent egocentric target representations in RNNs trained to pursue moving targets. 375 

Previous experimental works have identified egocentric target cells in PPC and RSC during pursuit task 376 

[7 , 40 ]. We find that the RNNs develop units with analogous tuning (Fig. 2), despite receiving velocity inputs 377 

anchored to the allocentric coordinate system of the simulated arena. This demonstrates that the RNNs 378 

have learned to perform an allocentric-to-egocentric transformation, a process which has been proposed to 379 

be critical for spatial navigation tasks [ 57 –60 ] and has been found to support multi-agent path integration in 380 

RNNs [ 46 ]. Accordingly, future work could use RNNs to explore population level computations underlying 381 

coordinate transformations across spatial reference frames. 382 

While experimental recordings have identified ETCs in visual association cortices, they have not yet 383 

shown that they play an important, causal role in pursuit (though see [ 61 ]). As is the case with many 384 

functionally-defined representations of this nature, it is in fact unlikely that a total ablation of solely 385 

egocentric target sensitive neurons is even possible with current experimental techniques. Thus, our RNN 386 

framework provides a unique opportunity to establish a theoretical causality between these representations 387 

and pursuit behaviors of simulated agents. By ablating the units with the highest egocentric target 388 

information, we find a significantly greater degradation of performance than randomly removing units (Fig. 389 

2C). Thus, in our RNN model, information about the target, in the transformed egocentric reference frame, 390 

is useful to pursuit performance. 391 

RNNs develop internal models that subserve predictive pursuit. 392 

To explore the capability of the RNNs to develop and maintain an internal representation of the 393 

target-agent’s position, we trained on trajectories where a percentage of the inputs to the RNN model were 394 

“masked.” This loosely mimics the presence of obstacles that occlude the target-agent, as well as changes in 395 

attention. Even when the inputs are masked 50% of the time, the RNN-agent pursues the target-agent along 396 

shortcut-like trajectories (Fig. 4), demonstrating its ability to leverage an internally maintained prediction of 397 

the target-agent’s position. These results motivated us to consider whether the RNN model could similarly 398 

learn to perform predictive pursuit in a more complex environment. To this end, we trained the RNNs to 399 

pursue the target-agent in an environment with periodic boundaries, introducing a non-Euclidean geometry 400 

to positions in the environment. We find that the RNNs learn to anticipate the target-agent’s trajectory 401 

from one boundary to another on CTs, with the RNN-agent waiting near its starting location, until the 402 

target-agent has emerged (Fig. 5A–C). 403 

After observing the strategy developed by the RNN, we tested whether similar behaviors emerged in 404 

biological agents by examining target pursuit in mice under conditions that also featured periodic boundaries. 405 

To this end, we developed a behavioral-closed-loop target presentation system that allowed precise control 406 

of target movement, teleportation, and interception. We demonstrate that, like the RNN, mice ignore target 407 

movement towards the “disappearing” boundary and instead navigate directly to the “reappearing” edge, 408 

where they wait to efficiently intercept (Fig. 5A, D, E). The similarity in capture strategies highlights rich 409 

behavioral-level agreement between real and artificial agents. In addition, this suggests that our RNN model 410 

may be useful for piloting possible experimental paradigms that can later be tested in the lab. 411 

Low-rank RNNs are sufficient for successful pursuit, but higher dimensional computations are 412 

required to mimic predictive behaviors observed in biology. 413 

Having shown that the RNN model learns to perform an allocentric-to-egocentric transformation 414 

and develops an internal representation of the target-agent’s position, we asked whether and how the 415 

computational capacity of the RNNs enable these complex processes to emerge. To probe this, we trained 416

of RNN models enable the testing of novel experimental paradigms (i.e., pursuit in a periodic boundary 431

the ablating of ETUs), and the identification of underlying computational principles (i.e., the necessity of 433 

high-dimensional network structure). 434 

Limitations. 435 

Our RNN model assumes true allocentric movement direction and speed of the target-agent as inputs, 436 

and true allocentric movement direction and speed of the RNN-agent as outputs. Incorporating noise 437 

in the estimate of self-motion [ 66 ] and target-agent motion, as well as including other types of inputs 438 

(e.g., visual-scene information) could bring our RNN model closer to biological systems. However, we note 439 

that the RNNs being able to continue to perform predictive pursuit when inputs were masked (Fig. 4) 440 

provides evidence that it would be able to perform well with noisy movement estimates. The steering of the 441 

RNN-agent is controlled by only two outputs, the RNN-agent’s movement direction and speed. This greatly 442 

simplifies the transformation that occurs between associations areas (e.g., PPC, RSC) and primary motor 443 

areas. In addition, it makes the RNN model sensitive, as any perturbations to weights or activations can 444 

directly cause changes to the RNN-agent’s behavior. While this could cause the low-rank RNNs to be less 445 

maneuverable, we note that the high performance on CTs and RTs, across ranks (Fig. 6A), suggests that this 446 

is not the major driver of the behavioral differences we see between low-rank and high-rank networks. Adding 447 

redundancy in the output layer (e.g., multiple outputs that are averaged together to determine RNN-agent 448 

movement direction and speed) could enable more robust steering. The supervised training of our RNN 449 

model simplifies aspects that shape rodent behavior, such as motivation and reward. Reinforcement learning 450 

(RL), which has been used to study aspects of pursuit-like behavior [ 65 , 67 – 71 ], offers a useful framework for 451 

capturing these components. While our results demonstrate that supervised learning is sufficient to generate 452 

similarities between the RNN model and rodent experiments at the behavior (Fig. 5) and computational 453 

level (Fig. 2), training the RNN model using RL offers the ability to generate a broader range of behavioral 454 

and computational solutions. 455 

Future directions. 456 

Our work opens the door for a number of exciting new directions to computationally study predictive 457 

pursuit. First, the target-agent we train our RNN to pursue is not “aware” it is being chased and therefore, 458 

does not perform any evasive strategies. While this may be appropriate for modeling some kinds of predatory-459 

prey interactions (e.g., bats hunting mosquitoes [ 4– 6]), many interesting scenarios involve both the predator 460 

and the prey exhibiting adaptive and predictive behavior. Thus, understanding how the RNN model can 461 

learn to pursue an “intelligent” target-agent and how the anticipatory behavior of the target-agent shapes the 462 

needed level of prediction by the RNN will provide a deeper understanding of the neural computations that 463 

may support predictive pursuit. Second, the environment the RNN-agent and target-agent are constrained 464 

to is empty and has a simple geometry and topology. Studying the representations that emerge in RNNs 465

# F Periodic boundary condition environment 806 

To test the capacity of RNN models to learn to perform predictive pursuit, we developed an environment with periodic 807 

boundary conditions. That is, we connected the left and right walls, as well as the top and bottom walls. This 808

left), led us to develop a similar environment to test mice in. Below, we discuss each of these environments in detail. 811

# G Decoding Analysis 866 

To perform our decoding analyses, we focus on fully trained low-rank RNNs on the two types of pursuit tasks, i.e., 867 

when the loss function focuses on the final distance (Figs. 7, S7, and S8) and on the trial-averaged distance (Fig. 868 

S9). For a given RNN and trial type (random vs characteristic), we generated independent trials and collected the 869 

RNN-agent ( zRNN (t)) and target-agent ( ztarget (t)) trajectories. From these, we also computed the egocentric target 870 

distance as zego (t) = ztarget (t) − zRNN (t). Conjunctively, we also noted the neural activations ( r(t)) during these 871 

trials. We refer to these targets collectively as o(t) below and specify whenever necessary. 872 

Decoding setup. We trained the decoders using r(t) as inputs and o(t + ∆) as outputs, where ∆ is the temporal 873 

shift used to assess whether neural activations contain information about past states or predictions about future 874 

locations. The decoders are always trained on random trajectories and evaluated on either random or characteristic 875 

trajectories, with 320 training trials and 80 test trials. We perform 3 such train-test splits for each given data point, 876 

and save two metrics: i) the median Euclidean distance (in meters) between predicted and true positions, computed 877 

across all test samples (flattened time points and trials), and ii) the R2 computed by first flattening x,y coordinates 878 

and then summing squared residuals over all test samples. The plots in Fig. 7 report the median, first, and third 879 

quartiles for a given rank K over both all random seeds used to train independent RNNs and the random splits. We 880 

compute the shuffled baselines by shuffling the locations within individual trials. 881 

Architecture and training. We trained linear decoders using a ridge regression. The regularization hyperpa-882 

rameter α was selected via 3-fold cross-validation over the 320 training trials, evaluated on a logarithmic grid of 5 883 

values spanning [10 −3, 10 0]. The value of α minimizing the median Euclidean error on the validation fold was then 884 

used to refit the decoder on all 320 training trials, and this final decoder was evaluated on the 80 held-out test trials. 885
