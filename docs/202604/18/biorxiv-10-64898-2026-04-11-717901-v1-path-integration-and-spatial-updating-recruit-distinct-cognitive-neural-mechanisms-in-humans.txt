# Abstract 1

Path integration and spatial updating refer to the integration of self-motion information 2

during navigation to update one’s start location and the positions of other locations, 3

respectively. Even though path integration has been described as a fundamental process 4

whose output may serve as a building block for other navigational computations like spatial 5

updating, the exact relationship between path integration and spatial updating is unknown. 6

Here we addressed this question with an eye-tracking behavioral experiment and a 7

subsequent fMRI study. Despite experiencing identical self-motion cues, participants showed 8

differential eye fixation patterns and responded more quickly during spatial updating than 9

during path integration, casting doubt on the fundamental role of path integration. 10 

Neuroimaging results showed that the precuneus and the dorsal premotor cortex were more 11 

activated during spatial updating, but the precuneus had stronger functional connectivity with 12 

the thalamus and the frontal cortex during path integration. Further supporting this 13 

dissociation, the two tasks invoked distinct brain-wide inter-regional functional networks. 14 

Together, the combined findings of both experiments suggest that spatial updating and path 15 

integration are dissociable navigation processes supported by distinct behavioral and neural 16 

mechanisms, rather than one process operating on the basis of the other. 17

# 1. Introduction 1

Spatial navigation is an essential everyday function that relies on a variety of spatial cues. One 2

important source of spatial information is self-motion cues, which refers to the spatial 3

information derived from internal idiothetic (proprioceptive cues, motor efference copies, 4

vestibular feedback) and external allothetic cues (optic flow) during locomotion. In contrast 5

to static environmental cues (e.g., visual landmarks), navigating with self-motion cues 6

requires continuous integration of these cues over travel distance and direction. Importantly, 7

self-motion-based spatial navigation can be further classified into two different processes: 8

spatial updating and path integration. While spatial updating involves continuously tracking 9

the positions of external objects relative to one’s own moving body, path integration refers 10 

to the updating of one’s position relative to a location one has previously occupied (e.g. the 11 

origin of travel). 12 

Given that path integration and spatial updating both rely on the integration of self-13 

motion cues and involve online updating of spatial representations, the underlying 14 

mechanisms may not be fundamentally different. After all, the home location, just like any 15 

other spatial location in the environment, is simply a location external to the navigator’s body 16 

(Wang, 2016). Consistent with this view, behavioral studies typically do not draw a strong 17 

conceptual distinction between spatial updating and path integration, and the two terms are 18 

sometimes even used interchangeably (He et al., 2018; He & McNamara, 2018). 19 

In contrast to this view, neuroscience findings suggest that path integration and spatial 20 

updating recruit at least partially distinct brain regions. In human spatial updating, activation 21 

of the precuneus is proportional to the number of to-be-tracked objects when the navigator 22 

moves in a virtual environment (Jahn et al., 2012; Wolbers et al., 2008), and a virtual lesion 23 

to the precuneus induced by repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation impairs spatial 24 

updating performance, demonstrating a causal role of the precuneus (Müller et al., 2018). In 25 

contrast, path integration has typically been associated with computations in medial temporal 26 

lobe (MTL) structures. For example, hippocampal activation in humans positively predicts 27 

individual path integration performance (Wolbers et al., 2007), and reflects the distance from 28 

the navigator to the home location (Chrastil et al., 2015). Moreover, grid-cell-like activation 29 

in the human entorhinal cortex positively predicts path integration performance in older 30 

adults (Stangl et al., 2018). Activation of the human parahippocampal cortex reflects the 31

inherent in path integration (Valerio & Taube, 2012), highlighting the thalamus as another 5

core component of the path integration circuitry. Taken together, prior findings suggest that 6

whereas spatial updating primarily engages medial parietal regions along the dorsal stream, 7

path integration relies more heavily on MTL structures and the thalamic head-direction 8

network. 9

Despite extensive investigation of these two processes, it remains unclear whether 10 

path integration and spatial updating rely on common or distinct cognitive-neural processes. 11 

This uncertainty arises primarily from a lack of direct comparisons between the two 12 

processes. To address this gap, we conducted two separate experiments with a novel 13 

experimental paradigm that enabled a direct comparison between path integration and 14 

spatial updating (Figure 2). In this paradigm, participants moved along a predetermined 15 

curved trajectory (the bolded solid curve) while keeping track either of the start position of 16 

the trajectory (path integration) or of a target position that was not located on the trajectory 17 

(spatial updating). Upon reaching the end of the trajectory, participants had to point to the 18 

start position for path integration and to the target position for spatial updating. 19 

Beyond determining whether spatial updating (SU) and path integration (PI) recruit 20 

common or differential behavioral and neural mechanisms, we also aimed to more precisely 21 

illustrate the relationship between these two processes. Based on the existing literature, we 22 

conceived three competing hypotheses. First, the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis argues that 23 

path integration is a primitive navigation process (Mittelstaedt & Mittelstaedt, 1980), while 24 

more advanced navigation processes, such as spatial updating, build upon it (Wang, 2016; 25 

Whitlock et al., 2008). Under this account (Figure 1A), path integration operates in a relatively 26 

automatic manner, meaning the start location is continuously updated. Hence, at the time of 27 

response, the pointing vector to the start position (e.g., homing vector) is already represented 28 

in the cognitive system and only needs to be retrieved. In contrast, because the target location 29 

is not automatically tracked during the movement, the pointing vector to the target is not 30 

directly available at the time of response. Instead, it must be inferred from the output of the 31 

path integrator (i.e., the homing vector). This inference process introduces additional 32

cognitive demands compared to direct retrieval, leading to performance costs like increased 1

reaction time (Klatzky, 1998). Accordingly, this hypothesis predicts worse behavioral 2

performance in path integration than in spatial updating. 3

In contrast to the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis, the PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis 4

posits that while spatial updating operates automatically, path integration operates on the 5

output of spatial updating (Figure 1B). Under this account, at the end of a trajectory, the 6

pointing vector to the target is already represented in the cognitive system and can be directly 7

retrieved to generate the response. In contrast, the home vector is not tracked during the 8

movement. Instead, it must be computed at the time of response using the pointing vector. 9

This additional computation imposes greater cognitive demands on path integration than on 10 

spatial updating. Consequently, this hypothesis predicts better behavioral performance for 11 

spatial updating than path integration . 12 

Although the two hypotheses make opposite assumptions, they share one core idea: 13 

one process is dependent on the other process, meaning path integration and spatial 14 

updating cannot be dissociated. Figure 1C presents a third hypothesis, which posits that the 15 

two processes are largely independent ( PI-SU-independent hypothesis ). Like the PI-depends-16 

on-SU hypothesis (Figure 1B), this hypothesis assumes that the target location is continuously 17 

tracked and updated relative to participants’ current position during movement. Thus, at the 18 

time of response, the pointing vector to the target is readily available, facilitating 19 

performance by shortening the reaction time. However, this hypothesis differs from the SU-20 

depends-on-PI hypothesis in its treatment of the pointing-to-home response. Rather than 21 

being inferred from the pointing vector to the target, the pointing direction to the start 22 

position is inferred within a fixed reference frame established by a prominent direction during 23 

the task. The initial heading of movement is a strong candidate for establishing this reference 24 

frame, as it remains stable across trials (He et al., 2018). Within this reference frame, all 25 

directional vectors, such as the current heading and the home direction, are represented 26 

relative to the initial heading of movement. To compute the pointing direction toward home, 27 

which is not directly represented, the system subtracts the current heading from the home 28 

direction. This additional computation imposes extra cognitive workload, leading to 29 

performance cost such as increased reaction time. Because this inference process does not 30 

involve the target representation, path integration and spatial updating emerge as largely 31 

dissociable processes. 32

In brief, the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis predicts better behavioral performance 1

(e.g., faster responses) in path integration than in spatial updating. In contrast, both the PI-2

depends-on-SU hypothesis and PI-SU-independent hypothesis predict better behavioral 3

performance in spatial updating than in path integration, albeit through different mechanisms. 4

Because the latter two hypotheses shared the same behavioral prediction, fully distinguishing 5

between all three hypotheses requires additional evidence, such as from eye-tracking and 6

neural data. 7

To preview, our behavioral, eye-tracking, and fMRI results provide converging 8

evidence that spatial updating and path integration are relatively independent processes that 9

recruit dissociable cognitive-neural mechanisms, supporting the PI-SU-independent 10 

hypothesis. 11

# 2. Behavioral Experiment 20 

In the experimental task, participants were briefly presented with an object (the target) in a 21 

virtual environment. After memorizing the object’s location, it disappeared, and participants 22 

were informed whether the current trial required them to update the object location (spatial 23 

updating) or the start location (path integration). Following a subsequent motion phase, 24 

during which participants were passively transported along a curved path, they used a joystick 25 

to point either to the remembered object or start location. Gaze was recorded binocularly. 26 

27 

2.1. Methods 28 

2.1.1. Participants 29 

Twenty naïve healthy participants with normal or corrected to normal vision participated in 30 

the experiment (10 women; age range: 20-30 yrs). This sample size was comparable to 31 

previous fMRI studies on path integration and spatial updating (Jahn et al., 2012; Wolbers et 32 

al., 2007, 2008; Zajac et al., 2019). 33 

Most participants were students from the University of Freiburg and were paid 8€ per 34 

hour for participation. Behavioral data for two participants and gaze data of one participant 35 

were excluded from the analysis as the corresponding data files were corrupt. 36

2.1.2. Experimental Stimuli and Paradigm 1

We used Vizard 2.53 (WorldViz, Santa Barbara, CA) to animate a desktop virtual environment 2

(Figure 2) that participants viewed from a first-person perspective (eye-height: 1.8m). The 3

environment contained a ground floor consisting of white dots that extended 50m in depth 4

and covered the entire field of view laterally (see Figure 2). The remainder of the scene was 5

kept black. To prevent participants from attending to single dots, we used limited lifetime 6

dots. Each dot was moving towards the observer until its initial lifetime (random duration 0 – 7

2 sec) had expired. It was then redrawn at a random location and its lifetime was reset to 2 8

sec. 9

To study path integration and spatial updating, 12 curved outbound paths were 10 

created that were comprised of a short initial straight segment (3m), a curved segment, and 11 

a final straight segment (1.5m). The form of the curved segment depended on the overall 12 

turning angle along the path. The design ensured identical path length (15m) despite different 13 

turning angles (45°, 60°, 75°, 90°, 105°, 120°) between paths (see Figure 2). 14 

For each path one location in space was defined – the object location – at which a 15 

geometric object was briefly presented before the onset of the passive transportation. These 16 

locations were selected such that the egocentric vector from the final position of the path to 17 

the object location was identical to the egocentric vector to the start point of the path, but 18 

only differed in the sign. For example, if, after reaching the endpoint of the trajectory, the 19 

start location would be at a distance of 7m and 100° to the right, the object location would 20 

be at a distance of 7m and 100° to the left (see Figure 2). For training sessions we designed a 21 

different set of paths (50°, 65°, 80°,95°, 110°, 125°) that also required different pointing 22 

responses. 23 

24 

2.1.3. Apparatus 25 

The virtual environment was displayed at a resolution of 1280 x 1024 pixels on a 20“ CRT 26 

monitor. The screen refresh rate was 100 Hz. Participants sat in front of the monitor at a 27 

distance of ~60 cm, such that the resulting visual angle of the stimuli displayed was 37° 28 

(horizontally) x 28° (vertically). A standard joystick was mounted onto a desk in from of 29 

participants. They responded by deflecting the joystick in direction of the respective target 30 

location and confirmed their direction judgments by pressing a joystick button with their 31 

index finger. Pointing responses and reaction times were recorded when the participant 32

2.1.4. Procedure 7

All participants completed one training session and one experimental session. Each trial 8

started with a static presentation of the virtual environment lasting 7.75 sec. During the first 9

5 seconds – the encoding phase –, participants saw one geometrical object (cylinder, cube, 10 

pyramid or sphere) positioned at one out of 12 possible positions on a ground plane. The 11 

ground plane consisted of 3000 white dots scattered randomly across the surface (Figure 2). 12 

Participants were instructed to memorize the locations as precisely as possible during 13 

this encoding phase. In order to prevent any interference effects from previous trials, the 14 

shape of the object was changed from trial to trial to. Next, the object sank into the ground 15 

and disappeared (duration: 2 sec), and the animation then remained still for another 0.75 sec. 16 

Participants were then instructed via a text message on the screen whether they had to point 17 

to the object location ( spatial updating trial) or to the start location of the travel ( path 18 

integration trial) upon reaching the end of the path. They were instructed to respond as 19 

quickly and as accurately as possible. During the subsequent movement phase (duration: 12.5 20 

sec), participants were passively transported along the current path. Given that self-motion 21 

perception in virtual environments is most accurate when displacement velocity resembles 22 

natural locomotion (Ellmore & McNaughton, 2004), we adopted a speed of a moderately 23 

paced walk (1.5m/sec) and set eyeheight at 1.5m. 24 

In the training phase , participants were presented with a number of paths and were 25 

asked to point back to the starting place or to an object. Training paths had different lengths 26 

and turning angles than test paths. Participants were presented with a random selection of 27 

training trials until they understood the experimental procedure. During the test phase ,28 

participants were passively transported along all 12 test paths twice (see Figure 2); each path 29 

was once paired with the path integration task and once with the spatial updating task. The 30 

order randomized of the presentation of the 24 trials was randomized. 31

2.1.5. Statistical analysis of behavioral data 1

For each trial, we measured absolute pointing error and reaction time. Larger pointing error 2

for low vs. high dot density would reveal uncertainty related to the amount of self-motion 3

information provided by the ground plane. In addition, differences between start and target 4

trials would be indicative of different processing demands related to updating locations 5

occupied by the observer or by the object at encoding. To assess the effects of our 6

experimental manipulations on both dependent variables, we performed two-way repeated 7

measures analyses of variance (ANOVA) with task and dot density as within-group factors. 8

Violations of sphericity were addressed with Greenhouse-Geisser correction of the degrees 9

of freedom. 10

2.2. Results 1

2.2.1. Pointing Performance 2

Pointing accuracy was measured as absolute pointing error, which equaled to the absolute 3

difference between the correct pointing angle and the actual pointing angle. Absolute 4

pointing error did not differ between left and right turn paths (t(17)= -0.56, p = 0.58) and was 5

therefore pooled for the remaining analyses. As shown in Figure 3a, absolute pointing error 6

increased with increasing turning angle, but it did not differ between path integration and 7

spatial updating trials. A 2x6 repeated measures ANOVA revealed a significant main effect of 8

absolute turning angle (F(5, 85) = 3.49, p < 0.01), but neither a main effect of task (F(1, 17) = 9

0.01, p = 0.94) nor a significant interaction (F(5, 85) = 0.71, p = 0.61). 10 

In contrast, response time data showed a slightly different pattern (Figure 3B): 11 

participants responded faster during spatial updating (mean: 1936 msec) than during path 12 

integration (mean: 2045 msec) trials (main effect of task: F(1, 17) = 5.74, p = 0.03), indicating 13 

a large effect. Reaction times were independent of absolute turning angle (F(5, 85) = 1.31, p 14 

= 0.27), and there was no interaction between both factors (F(5, 85) = 1.06, p = 0.39). 15 

Taken together, these results demonstrate that representations of locations became 16 

less accurate with larger turning angles, but this effect was similar for both tasks. Despite this 17 

comparable task difficulty, participants responded consistently faster when the task required 18 

the updating of an external location . This result is difficult to reconcile with the SU-depends-19 

on-PI hypothesis (Figure 1A), which suggests that path integration is a fundamental process 20 

underlying various forms of updating spatial locations. In contrast, this result is consistent 21 

with both the PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis (Figure 1B), which proposes that path integration 22 

operates on the output of spatial updating, and the PI-SU-independent hypothesis (Figure 23 

1C), which proposes that path integration and spatial updating rely on largely dissociable 24 

mechanisms. 25

revealed a significant main effect of task (F(1, 18) = 155.78, p < 0.001), demonstrating that 14 

the average distance between gaze and object location on the screen was significantly smaller 15 

for spatial updating trials (109 pixel) than for path integration trials (332 pixel; Figure 3D). 16 

Figure 4 also reveals that the difference in horizontal gaze position between path 17 

integration and spatial updating trials was markedly reduced once the remembered object 18 

location left the screen. Specifically, participants moved their fixation back in the direction of 19 

motion in spatial updating trials, presumably to bring the focus of expansion – an area of 20 

minimal optic flow with maximum information content for judging self-motion direction on 21 

curvilinear paths – into their focus of attention (Authié & Mestre, 2012). 22 

In brief, the eye tracking results revealed distinct gaze patterns between spatial 23 

updating and path integration. During spatial updating, participants’ gaze continuously 24 

tracked the target location until it moved out of the screen. In contrast, during path 25 

integration, they primarily fixed their gaze in the direction of self-movement. These results 26 

contradict the PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis. Under this hypothesis, the output of spatial 27 

updating is required to compute the homing vector in path integration; therefore, it predicts 28 

that participants in the path integration task should exhibit a eye-gaze pattern similar to those 29 

observed in spatial updating trials, which was not supported by the data. 30 

In addition, the results also conflict with the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis, which 31 

posits that spatial updating is not automatic but instead dependent on the output of path 32

In contrast, the eye-tracking findings are broadly consistent with the PI-SU-6

2.2.3. Summary of Behavioral and Eyetracking Results 10 

Two important conclusions can be drawn from the behavioral experiment. First, while both 11 

tasks were of equal difficulty, participants were quicker to respond in spatial updating trials. 12 

This behavioral finding is inconsistent with the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis, but is consistent 13 

with both the PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis and the PI-SU-independent hypothesis. 14 

Second, during spatial updating, participants initially tracked the remembered 15 

location of an external object by following it with their eyes. However, once this position left 16 

the field of view, participants directed their attention in the direction of motion. In contrast, 17 

during path integration, this latter strategy was applied from the beginning of a trial, 18 

suggesting that participants focused on the area of minimal optical flow, which maximizes the 19 

ability to monitor the direction of self-motion as accurately as possible (Authié & Mestre, 20 

2012). This eye-tracking finding is inconsistent with the PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis and the 21 

SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis, but is broadly consistent with the PI-SU-independent 22 

hypothesis. 23

Considering both the behavioral and eye-tracking results, the PI-SU-independent hypothesis 26 

appears the most plausible. However, direct evidence for this account remains limited, as 27 

behavioral and eye-tracking measures provide only indirect insight into the specific cognitive 28 

processes underlying both tasks. To more directly investigate the relationship between path 29 

integration and spatial updating – and to better distinguish among the three competing 30

3.1. Methods 10 

3.1.1. Participants 11 

Sixteen healthy volunteers with normal or corrected-to-normal vision gave written informed 12 

consent to participate in this study, which was conducted at UC Santa Barbara’s Brain Imaging 13 

Center and was approved by the local ethics committee. All participants understood the 14 

instructions without difficulties and none were aware of the hypotheses at the time of testing. 15 

The sample size was similar to previous fMRI studies on path integration and spatial 16 

updating (Jahn et al., 2012; Wolbers et al., 2007, 2008; Zajac et al., 2019). 17 

18 

3.1.2. Experimental Stimuli 19 

The paradigm used in the fMRI experiment was very similar to the behavioral experiment; 20 

however, two key differences were introduced: participants were required to fixate during 21 

self-motion to eliminate eye movement confounds, and we introduced a perceptual 22 

manipulation in terms of varying the number of dots. Hence, a 2 x 2 factorial design with 23 

factors task (path integration vs. spatial updating) and dot density (750 vs. 3000) served to 24 

characterize the neural mechanisms that differentiate path integration from spatial updating. 25 

As in the behavioral experiment, trials consisted of three phases: an initial encoding phase 26 

followed by a movement phase and a subsequent retrieval phase. Each trial started with a 27 

static presentation of the virtual environment lasting 7.75 sec. During the first 5 seconds – 28 

the encoding phase –, participants saw one geometrical object (cylinder, cube, pyramid or 29 

sphere) positioned at one out of 12 possible positions on a ground plane. The ground plane 30 

consisted of 750 or 3000 white dots scattered randomly across the surface (Figure 2). 31

Participants were instructed to memorize the location of the object as precisely as possible. 1

Importantly, the shape of the object was changed from trial to trial to prevent any 2

interference effects from previous trials. Next, the object gradually sank into the ground until 3

complete disappearance (duration: 2 sec), and the animation then remained still for another 4

0.75 sec. Finally, a central white fixation cross and the task instruction for the current trial 5

were displayed (Figure 2): when the word ‘start’ appeared above the fixation cross, 6

participants had to point towards the starting location at the end of the trial. Conversely, the 7

word ‘target’ signaled that participants would have to point to the remembered location of 8

the target. 9

In the subsequent movement phase (duration: 12.5 sec), participants experienced a 10 

passive translation along one of eight curved paths, using the same layouts as in the 11 

behavioral experiment (Figure 2). Participants were instructed to pay close attention to the 12 

optic flow provided by the limited lifetime dots – while maintaining fixation on the central 13 

cross – in order to monitor their movements through space. In the final retrieval phase, the 14 

fixation cross turned red to signal to participants that they were required to point towards 15 

either the starting location (start trials) or to the object’s remembered location (target trials) 16 

within a 3.5 sec interval. Participants indicated their response with an MR-compatible joystick 17 

(Mag Design and Engineering, Sunnyvale, CA) that was positioned on the right thigh. 18 

Participants deflected the joystick with the right hand and pushed a button with the left hand 19 

when the joystick was oriented in the desired direction. Pointing responses and reaction times 20 

were recorded when the participant pressed the button. Participants were not given any 21 

feedback about their performance, and a black screen with the white fixation cross was 22 

presented during inter-trial intervals that randomly varied between 3 and 5 sec. 23

experimenter was out of sight (i.e. behind the participant), participants were asked to stop 31

3.1.5. Statistical Analysis of FMRI Data 9

3.1.5.1. First-level GLM to detect brain activation differences between tasks 10 

To detect brain activation differences between path integration and spatial updating, we 11 

constructed a first-level GLM. 12 

At the single-participant level, we applied a high-pass filter to remove low frequency 13 

artifacts. Design matrices containing separate regressors for each of the four trial types 14 

(spatial updating / path integration * low / high dot density) were specified. We also included 15 

six regressors coding for rotation and translation of the head as obtained by the realignment 16 

procedure. Encoding, movement and retrieval intervals were modeled as boxcar functions 17 

convolved with a hemodynamic response function (HRF); however, trials in which participants 18 

failed to respond within the 3.5 sec interval were modeled as separate regressors and were 19 

discarded during statistical analyses. Specific effects were tested with appropriate linear 20 

contrasts of the parameter estimates, and the corresponding contrast images were entered 21 

into a random effects analysis. 22

3.3.2. Psychophysiological Interaction (PPI) Analysis 1

The preceding univariate analysis revealed greater activation in spatial updating than path 2

integration trials in the precuneus and the dorsal premotor cortex. To further investigate if 3

path integration and spatial updating recruited dissociable neural mechanisms in other 4

aspects of neural responses, we conducted the psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analysis, 5

using the two areas as the seed regions. The PPI analysis examines whether effective 6

connectivity from the seed region to other brain regions across the entire search volume 7

differed between the two task contexts (Friston et al., 1997). 8

Recall that the behavioral results of this experiment could not arbitrate between the 9

PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis and the PI-SU-independent hypothesis. A critical distinction 10 

between these hypotheses is that PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis predicts path integration 11 

relies on the output of spatial updating (Figure 1B), whereas the PI-SU-independent 12 

hypothesis posits the involvement of additional cognitive components different from the 13 

output of spatial updating, such as a head direction system that establishes a stable reference 14 

frame (Figure 1C). PPI is well-suited for testing this distinction, as it can reveal qualitative 15 

differences in effective connectivity between tasks, beyond the quantitative activation 16 

differences detected by the univariate analysis. If path integration requires computations 17 

beyond those of spatial updating, we would predict stronger connectivity between the 18 

precuneus or the premotor cortex with other brain regions, such as those supporting head 19 

direction processing. 20 

Given the high sensitivity of connectivity analyses to outliers, we performed the PPI 21 

analyses using a first-level GLM in which outlier volumes were modelled as individual spike 22 

regressors. Outlier volumes were identified using the SPMUP toolbox (Pernet, 2021). 23 

Specifically, we calculated global signal intensity and framewise displacement for every 24 

volume. Volumes exhibiting atypical values on either metric were flagged as outliers using a 25 

robust S-estimator–based detection method (S-outliers), which provides high sensitivity to 26 

extreme observations while minimizing the influence of already-contaminated data. All other 27 

aspects of the first-level GLM were identical to those used in the mass univariate analysis. 28 

When using the precuneus cluster as the seed region, we observed greater effective 29 

connectivity with the thalamus, superior frontal gyrus, and the inferior frontal gyrus in path 30 

integration trials compared to spatial updating trials (Table 3, Figure 8). These three clusters 31 

also reached statistical significance when a nonparametric permutation test was applied 32

3.3.3. Whole Brain Analysis of Connectivity Differences 10 

The preceding PPI analysis showed that effective connectivity from a selected seed region 11 

differed between path integration and spatial updating, indicating that the two tasks recruit 12 

qualitatively different neural mechanisms. To examine connectivity differences at a broader, 13 

whole-brain level between these two processes, we examined inter-region functional 14 

connectivity using a multi-edge pattern analysis, based on 264 representative brain nodes 15 

(Power et al., 2011). For each task type, we computed a connectivity matrix capturing the 16 

pairwise correlations between all node pairs, and asked whether the pattern of these 17 

correlations across the entire brain was sufficiently different to reliably identify which task a 18 

participant was performing. As shown in Figure 9, a classifier trained on these connectivity 19 

patterns distinguished spatial updating from path integration with an accuracy of 84.375%, 20

# 4. Discussion 6

In this study, we examined whether spatial updating and path integration - two navigation 7

processes that rely on the integration of self-motion cues - are supported by shared or distinct 8

cognitive-neural mechanisms. By isolating visual self-motion information in the form of optic 9

flow, we directly compared the two processes under identical sensory input. Across 10 

behavioral, eye-tracking, and fMRI measures, our findings converge on the conclusion that 11 

spatial updating and path integration rely on relatively independent spatial computations. In 12 

the behavioral experiment, participants responded faster during spatial updating than during 13 

path integration, and eye-tracking revealed distinct task strategies associated with this 14 

reaction-time difference: while participants fixated the remembered target location during 15 

spatial updating, gaze was oriented in the direction of motion during path integration. 16 

Importantly, the reaction-time advantage for spatial updating persisted in the fMRI 17 

experiment even when eye movements were controlled, indicating that the behavioral 18 

dissociation cannot be attributed to overt differences in visual sampling. Critically, spatial 19 

updating was associated with stronger BOLD responses in the precuneus and dorsal premotor 20 

cortex, whereas path integration showed stronger task-dependent effective connectivity 21 

between the precuneus, thalamus, and frontal movement-related regions, pointing to a 22 

context-dependent reconfiguration of functional interactions. In addition, whole-brain 23 

connectivity patterns distinguished the two task states. Together, these findings support the 24 

view that spatial updating and path integration engage distinct cognitive-neural mechanisms, 25 

even when driven by the same visual self-motion information. 26 

A central strength of the present study is the integration of behavioral testing, eye-27 

tracking, and fMRI within a single experimental framework. This multimodal approach 28 

enables converging evidence across measurement modalities and helps offset the relatively 29 

small sample sizes typical of many fMRI studies. In particular, this approach allowed us to 30 

evaluate three competing hypotheses proposed in the literature (Figure 1A–C). Under the SU-31

depends-on-PI hypothesis, path integration is assumed to operate automatically, whereas 1

spatial updating requires an additional inference step based on the output of the path 2

integrator, resulting in worse behavioral performance (e.g., slower responses) for spatial 3

updating. The PI-depends-on-SU hypothesis reverses this dependency: during spatial 4

updating, participants continuously track the target location, while path integration requires 5

additional computation based on the output of spatial updating at the response stage. This 6

would predict worse behavioral performance (e.g., slower responses) for path integration due 7

to additional computation, but attributes this cost to an inference process within a stable 8

reference frame that is independent of spatial updating. Although the latter two hypotheses 9

share the same behavioral prediction, they differ fundamentally in their proposed underlying 10 

mechanisms. 11 

Behaviorally, while absolute pointing errors were comparable across the two tasks, 12 

responses were faster during spatial updating than during path integration. This pattern is 13 

incompatible with the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis but remains consistent with both the PI-14 

depends-on-SU and the PI–SU-independent hypotheses. Eye-tracking data provided critical 15 

additional constraints. During spatial updating, participants maintained fixation at the 16 

remembered target location until it left the field of view, despite the absence of a visible 17 

stimulus – a form of “looking at nothing” behavior that has been linked to goal-directed 18 

attention and facilitated memory processing in other cognitive domains (Ferreira et al., 2008; 19 

Henderson & Castelhano, 2005; Johansson & Johansson, 2014; Lakshminarasimhan et al., 20 

2020). In contrast, during path integration trials, gaze was oriented in the direction of travel, 21 

a strategy associated with monitoring self-motion direction via optic flow (Authié et al., 2015; 22 

Authié & Mestre, 2012). Importantly, the reaction-time advantage for spatial updating 23 

persisted when participants had to maintain central fixation in the fMRI experiment, 24 

indicating that this effect cannot be explained by overt gaze behavior alone. Together, these 25 

findings suggest that both overt and covert spatial attention to the target location can 26 

facilitate processing during spatial updating, while path integration relies on a different 27 

computational strategy. 28 

While these behavioral results we re incompatible with the SU-depends-on-PI 29 

hypothesis they could not distinguish between the PI-depends-on-SU and PI–SU-independent 30 

accounts. Eye-tracking findings favored the PI–SU-independent hypothesis, yet the underlying 31 

cognitive processes remained unclear, motivating the fMRI experiment. A first key fMRI result 32

was stronger task-related activation of the precuneus and the middle frontal gyrus (close to 1

the dorsal premotor cortex) during spatial updating compared to path integration. This 2

pattern is consistent with a core assumption shared by the PI-depends-on-SU and PI-SU-3

independent hypotheses, namely that the target location was continuously tracked during 4

spatial updating, such that the pointing direction to the target is readily available at response. 5

Consistent with the eye-tracking results, these fMRI findings support an egocentric updating 6

strategy, in which the target’s location is represented and continuously updated relative to 7

the observer’s changing position and orientation. Converging evidence from previous work 8

supports a central role of the precuneus and the dorsal premotor cortex in egocentric 9

updating of object locations during navigation. For example, activation in these regions scales 10 

with the number of tracked locations (Jahn et al., 2012; Wolbers et al., 2008), a defining 11 

feature of the egocentric updating (Wang et al., 2006). Moreover, the precuneus is causally 12 

involved in spatial updating, as TMS-induced disruption of this regions impairs participants’ 13 

behavioral performance (Müller et al., 2018). These activation-based fMRI results are 14 

therefore inconsistent with the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis, which proposes a reversed 15 

dependency between the two navigation tasks. However, because both the PI-depends-on-16 

SU and PI-SU-independent hypotheses predict continuous target updating, this activation 17 

difference in the precuneus and the dorsal premotor cortex alone cannot adjudicate between 18 

them. 19 

The PI-depends-on-SU and PI-SU-independent hypotheses can be discriminated by the 20 

connectivity results. Although the precuneus showed stronger activation during spatial 21 

updating, its coupling with the thalamus was enhanced during path integration. The thalamus 22 

has been implicated in representing directional information relevant for navigation, including 23 

head-direction signals in rodents (Taube & Muller, 1998) and humans (Shine et al., 2016). 24 

Head direction signals in the thalamus can be anchored to the global environmental space 25 

(Shine et al., 2016). Under the PI-SU-independent hypothesis, stable heading information in 26 

the thalamus could serve to establish a fixed spatial reference frame during path integration 27 

trials, likely anchored to the participant’s facing direction at the beginning of a trial. At 28 

response, directional relations defined in this frame were computed to obtain an egocentric 29 

vector pointing to the starting position, which was represented in the precuneus and the 30 

dorsal premotor cortex. Such head direction computations based on the thalamus are not 31 

necessary for spatial updating in the present task. In contrast, the PI-depends-on-SU 32

hypothesis does not naturally predict this task-specific engagement of thalamic connectivity, 1

as it assumes that path integration operates primarily on the output of spatial updating, 2

without requiring supplementary computations carried out in other brain regions. 3

Finally, whole-brain task classification analyses provided converging evidence for the 4

PI-SU-independent hypothesis by showing that spatial updating and path integration can be 5

reliably distinguished based on inter-regional functional connectivity patterns. These analyses 6

captured large-scale interactions among distributed brain regions, defined using a widely 7

adopted set of functionally meaningful brain nodes derived from meta-analytic and resting-8

state connectivity work (Power et al., 2011). The successful classification of the two task states 9

indicates that spatial updating and path integration recruit distinct large-scale network 10 

configurations, extending beyond the focal precuneus–thalamus connectivity effects 11 

identified in the PPI analysis. Although this network-level dissociation is broadly consistent 12 

with the PI-SU-independent hypothesis, the specific computational mechanisms underlying 13 

these differences remain to be clarified. 14 

Although the current study provides valuable insights into the cognitive-neural 15 

mechanisms underlying path integration and spatial updating, its limitations should be 16 

considered when generalizing the findings to other contexts. First, because participants could 17 

not physically walk during MRI scanning, the self-motion cues under investigation did not 18 

include body-based cues, which are an important source of spatial information in real-world 19 

navigation. Previous research has shown that during path integration, when body-based self-20 

motion cues are available, participants can adopt an egocentric updating strategy by 21 

continuously tracking the starting location to themselves, provided that they are instructed 22 

to do so (He & McNamara, 2018). 23 

Second, during spatial updating in the current study, participants were required to 24 

track the position of only a single target. With such a limited tracking demand, participants 25 

may have relied primarily on transient egocentric spatial representations maintained in 26 

working memory. However, real-life navigation in complex environments often requires 27 

tracking multiple objects. When the number of objects exceeded working memory capacity, 28 

individuals may instead rely on allocentric spatial representations stored in long-term 29 

memory, as simultaneously tracking of many objects is cognitively costly and ineffective. This 30 

tendency is especially pronounced when the objects form a regular layout (Mou et al., 2006). 31 

In this case, people may update their own position and only reconstruct object positions at 32

the SU-depends-on-PI hypothesis may therefore be more plausible. 2

In addition to revealing distinct mechanisms for spatial updating and path integration, 3

our results also inform how perceptual properties of visual self-motion input influence brain 4

responses. Manipulating dot density strongly modulated activity in early visual cortex 5

(V1/V2), with higher densities eliciting larger BOLD responses. In contrast, regions further 6

along the visual motion and navigation pathways, including hMT+ and the precuneus, were 7

insensitive to this manipulation. This pattern is consistent with findings from non-human 8

primates showing that neurons in area MST, which are functionally related to human hMT+, 9

are relatively insensitive to low-level visual features like dot density (Duffy & Wurtz, 1991). 10 

Because dot density provides detailed information about local visual structure but less 11 

information about movement trajectory or direction, these findings are consistent with a role 12 

of hMT+ and the precuneus in extracting higher-level self-motion information rather than 13 

encoding low-level stimulus properties. 14 

Dot density also modulated responses in the hippocampus and thalamus, with 15 

stronger BOLD signals observed in the high-density condition. Although this effect was not a 16 

primary focus of the study, it may reflect indirect consequences of altered self-motion 17 

perception. Computational modeling suggests that the spatial sparsity of optic flow cues can 18 

bias perceived self-motion speed, thereby affecting path integration performance 19 

(Lakshminarasimhan et al., 2018), and intracranial recordings in humans show that 20 

hippocampal theta activity is modulated by movement speed during navigation (Goyal et al., 21 

2020). Accordingly, variations in dot density may have influenced speed perception, which in 22 

turn modulated hippocampal and thalamic responses. However, in contrast to the neural 23 

findings, we did not observe reliable effects of dot density on behavior. This dissociation 24 

between brain activity and behavior may indicate that the dot-density manipulation was 25 

insufficiently strong to elicit measurable behavioral differences. 26

The present study examined whether spatial updating and path integration, while often 29 

treated as overlapping forms of navigation, engage distinct cognitive and neural mechanisms. 30
