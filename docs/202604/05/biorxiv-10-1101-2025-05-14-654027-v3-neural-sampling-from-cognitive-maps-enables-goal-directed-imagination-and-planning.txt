Abstract 

AI systems are becoming more intelligent, but at a very high cost in terms of energy consumption and training requirements. In contrast, our brains only require 20W of energy, they learn online, and they can instantly adjust to changing contingencies. This begs the question what data struc-tures, algorithms, and learning methods enable brains to achieve that, and whether these can be ported into artificial devices. We are addressing this question for a core feature of intelligence: The capacity to plan and solve problems, including new problems that involve states which were never encountered before. We examine three tools that brains are likely to employ for achieving that: Cognitive maps, stochastic computing, and compositional coding. We integrate these tools into a transparent neural network model, and demonstrate its power for flexible planning and problem solving. Importantly, this approach is suitable for implementation by in-memory computing and other energy-efficient neuromorphic hardware. In particular, it only requires self-supervised local synaptic plasticity that is suited for on-chip learning. Hence a core feature of brain intelligence, the capacity to generate solutions to problems that were never encountered before, does not require deep neural networks or large language models, and can be implemented in energy-efficient edge devices. 

# 1 Introduction 1

Planning and problem solving are at the core of human intelligence. This capability is apparent in 2

multiple domains, such as when planning routes to reach a goal location in a physical environment 3

or when imagining solutions to abstract problems. Importantly, we can apply it also for coping with 4

new challenges that go beyond previous experience. 5

Numerous experimental data suggest that cognitive maps , i.e., data structure for coherently orga-6

nizing learned experiences by encoding relational information between states and actions, are essential 7

tools that the brain employs for planning. The most widely studied ones are allocentric spatial maps 8

in the rodent hippocampal formation and entorhinal cortex that relate locations in an environment 9

with changes caused by ego-motion (O’Keefe and Nadel, 1978; Behrens et al., 2018; Raju et al., 2024; 10

their formation (Behrens et al., 2018; Hafting et al., 2005; Dong and Fiete, 2024; McNaughton et al., 12 

2006). These cognitive maps support goal-oriented spatial navigation by providing self-localization 13 

and a sense of direction towards goals (Epstein et al., 2017; Bush et al., 2015; Tsoar et al., 2011). 14 

Recent experimental work in neuroscience and cognitive science goes beyond a purely spatial defini-15 

tion of cognitive maps, suggesting that they are also employed by the brain, especially the human 16 

brain, for problem solving in non-spatial domains, including abstract concept spaces (Behrens et al., 17 

2018; Bottini and Doeller, 2020; Tavares et al., 2015; Xiao et al., 2025; Buzs´ aki and Moser, 2013; Iyer 18 

et al., 2024; FeldmanHall et al., 2025). 19 

Multiple lines of evidence suggest that cognitive maps of the brain not only encode past experience 20 

but also serve a prospective function, commonly referred to as imagination , which enables individuals 21 

to anticipate and prepare for situations they have never directly experienced. A rodent study showed 22 

that sampling from cognitive maps during replay of sequences of place cells in the hippocampus can 23 

be decoded as imagined trajectories in 2D spaces that resemble locomotion plans (Pfeiffer and Foster, 24 

2013; ´Olafsd´ ottir et al., 2018; Vollan et al., 2025). Furthermore, when novel barriers are introduced 25 

in the environment, replay trajectories depict goal-directed trajectories around them (Widloski and 26 

Foster, 2022). 27 

The human brain is able to imagine and plan also in compositional domains, such as natural 28 

language, where a virtually unlimited repertoire of new states and potential goals can be described 29 

through new combinations of known components. The underlying brain processes have recently been 30 

analyzed in (Schwartenbeck et al., 2023) for composition of novel 2D silhouettes from a set of building 31 

blocks (tiles), or more precisely for the NP-hard task to decide whether a given silhouette can be 32 

decomposed into these building blocks. 33 

We integrate these three identified tools that brains employ for planning and problem solving, 34 

cognitive maps, sampling, and compositional computing, into a novel model for planning and problem 35 

solving. This model can be implemented through simple and transparent neural networks that do not 36 

require Backprop or Backprop through time for learning. Instead, it learns autonomously in an online 37 

manner from own experiences, using only biologically plausible rules for local synaptic plasticity that 38 

are easy to implement in neuromorphic hardware. We examine functional capabilities of this model 39 

in three very different application domains. 40 

Reinforcement learning (RL) is the standard tool in AI for learning to solve planning or more 41 

general problem solving tasks (Sutton and Barto, 2018). However, most RL methods require relearning 42 

and substantial computational overhead when the goal changes, and often also deep learning. In 43 

contrast, our model can instantly adapt to changes in goals and contingencies through local synaptic 44 

plasticity. 45 

Surprisingly, cognitive maps provide a sense of direction even in complex compositional task 46 

domains such as composing and decomposing silhouettes from given building blocks. Essential for 47 

that is that the standard forward model that defines the relational structure of a cognitive map 48 

is complemented by an inverse model that maps state difference to neural codes for actions which 49 

cause these state differences. Furthermore, very simple inverse models for cognitive maps often suffice, 50 

which provide especially attractive generalization capabilities. In general, inverse models have become 51 

a standard modeling tool for biological motor control and in robotics (Wolpert and Kawato, 1998; 52 

Kawato, 1999), but they have apparently not yet been considered in the context of cognitive maps. 53 

We will first demonstrate functional abilities of our model in 2D spatial navigation. We use there 54 

a simple model for learning cognitive maps that is based on the grid cell system, which uses path 55 

integration to update the spatial location that is encoded by grid cell firing (McNaughton et al., 56 

2006; Dong and Fiete, 2024). To address goal-directed sampling in non-spatial domains, we use a 57 

simple model for learning cognitive maps that is based on learning predictions of action outcomes 58 

through local and biologically-plausible synaptic plasticity rules (St¨ ockl et al., 2024). We introduce 59 

a generative variant of this Cognitive Map Learner (CML) model, the Generative Cognitive Map 60 

Learner (GCML) , where observations of next states that result from own actions are replaced by 61 

internally generated state predictions. If noise is present in the selection of virtual next actions, one 62 

arrives at a probabilistic generative model that permits sampling of possible trajectories to any given 63 

goal in the cognitive map. Importantly, we show that cognitive maps provide a sense of direction even 64 

in complex compositional task domains such as composing and decomposing silhouettes from given 65 

building blocks. 66

## 2.1 Sampling from a 2D cognitive map reproduces brain data on 68 

## imagined spatial trajectories 69 

Numerous neural recordings during self-generated imagination processes in the brain are available 70 

from the rodent hippocampus. In particular, it was shown in (Pfeiffer and Foster, 2013) that before 71 

navigation towards a known goal location (“home”), the hippocampus produced during specific resting 72 

phases (short wave ripples), sequential activity of place cells that could be decoded as imagined 73 

trajectories from the current location to the goal location. These decoded trajectories exhibited a 74 

startling diversity, even for the same starting and goal location, and were rarely straight (see Fig. 75 

2 and Suppl. Fig. 11 and 17 of (Pfeiffer and Foster, 2013)). A subsequent study showed that these 76 

decoded trajectories can flexibly reach goals even when this requires rerouting around novel barriers 77 

(Widloski and Foster, 2022). These findings cannot be easily accounted for by standard sampling 78 

approaches used in statistics and machine learning, such as Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and 79 

Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Bishop, 2006; Gelly and Silver, 2011; Buesing et al., 2011), since 80 

these are lacking the goal directed feature. Hence new model for goal-directed stochastic sampling is 81 

needed, which we will present here. 82 

We start with a standard model for a cognitive map for spatial navigation that is based upon the 83 

grid cell system, see Fig. 1a, and Methods for details. Grid cells produce an allocentric map of a 2D 84 

environment based on path integration, thereby relating ego-motion to estimates of spatial locations 85 

in the 2D environment (McNaughton et al., 2006; Behrens et al., 2018; Hafting et al., 2005; Liu et al., 86 

2023; Dong and Fiete, 2024). Experimental data suggest that the grid cell system is largely genetically 87 

programmed in the rodent, but its development is sped up through spatial navigation (Ulsaker-Janke 88 

et al., 2023). Importantly for our context, it has been shown in (Vollan et al., 2025) that the grid 89 

cell systems is instrumental in generating imagined trajectories (forward sweeps) in the absence of 90 

movements. In fact, their data suggest the presence of a forward model where grid cell states are 91 

sequentially updated in the direction of an imagined movement. The allocentric map formed by the 92 

grid cell system corresponds on a large scale to a torus (Gardner et al., 2022). We focus on a local 93 

segment of the torus, which is approximately flat (Fig. 1b). Whereas each grid cell fires for numerous 94 

locations in 2D, they can be used as basis for training place cells that each fire only around a specific 95 

location, see Fig. 1a. 96 

Previous analyses and models suggested that the diversity of 2D trajectories that are decoded from 97 

sequential activity of place cells exhibits signs of an underlying stochastic sampling process (Ujfalussy 98 

and Orb´ an, 2022; Stoianov et al., 2022). But a model was missing that could reproduce sampling of 99 

goal directed trajectories, such as the virtual trajectories to a “home” location in the rodent (Pfeiffer 100 

and Foster, 2013). We show here that such a model emerges if one complements the standard model 101 

for the representations by grid- and place cells by a simple self-supervised learning process for an 102 

inverse model, that learns during active locomotion to map small changes in grid cell activity to 103 

local movement commands which caused them. This inverse model can be implemented by a weight 104 

matrix W, see Fig. 1a. It is analogous to numerous kinds of inverse models that have previously been 105 

employed for modeling biological motor control (Wolpert and Kawato, 1998; Kawato, 1999). 106 

Learning of W during exploration of the 2D space (see Fig. 1c) can be implemented by a simple 107 

local plasticity rule for synaptic connections from grid cells to action-generating neurons. Due to the 108 

approximate linearity of the representation of the 2D space by grid cells and the linearity of W one 109 

can use this learnt map from state changes to action commands also for selecting a first action that 110 

moves into the direction of a distant goal s∗, see Fig. 1b and the underlying theory presented in 111 

(St¨ ockl et al., 2024). If this action selection is in addition subject to noise, a model for goal directed 112 

stochastic sampling of trajectories emerges. Specifically, the W matrix maps the difference between 113 

grid cell codes ( s∗ −ˆst) for a given goal and the current location onto a superposition ( a) of basic action 114 

commands that would cause, if executed, a movement step from the current location into the direction 115 

of the goal. If one replaces the grid cell representation of the next location that would result from this 116 

movement step by an estimate that is produced by the forward model, one can iterate this process, 117 

yielding an imagined sequence of steps in the direction of the goal. The variance of these imagined 118 

trajectories depends on the level of the noise that is superimposed on each action selection, see Fig. 119 

1d. The diversity of trajectories to the given goal that are generated in this way by the model is in 120 

the high noise regime qualitatively similar to the one shown in Fig. 4b of (Pfeiffer and Foster, 2013). 121

To sum up, we have shown that stochastic sampling from a standard model for a cognitive map 144 

of 2D space supports goal-directed imagination of trajectories from start to goal locations, capturing 145 

key qualitative patterns of rodent hippocampal replay in the same setting, including generalization 146 

to un-explored parts of the space, and flexible adjustment to novel contingencies. In the following 147 

sections we will examine whether sampling from cognitive maps can also reproduce goal-directed 148 

imagination and planning in non-spatial environments. 149 

## 2.2 Sampling from learnt cognitive maps for abstract concept spaces 150 

## enables generic problem solving through goal-directed imagination 151 

To allow navigation in (potentially) high dimensional conceptual spaces in order to solve abstract 152 

problems, see Fig. 2a for an illustration, we employ a generic model for learning cognitive maps, the 153 

Cognitive Map Learner (CML) from (St¨ ockl et al., 2024) that is based on the commonly accepted 154 

principle of predictive coding. As for the cognitive maps that are created through the rodent grid 155 

cell system, the key principle of the CML is to learn a forward model that relates current actions 156 

to the neural codes for the resulting states or observations. This is achieved in the CML model by 157 

learning embeddings Q and V of observations and actions into a common high-D space, see Fig. 2b. 158 

In biological terms, this high-D space models the space of neural activity patterns of neurons that 159 

encode places within the cognitive map. Hence this coding space has in the brain a dimension that 160 

is equal to the number of neurons in that area. However, a much smaller dimension – of around 161 

1000 – suffices for the tasks considered in this study. Similarly, it suffices to employ linear maps Q162 

and V that embed sensory inputs and efferent copies of action commands into this high-D coding 163 

space. Furthermore, simple local synaptic plasticity rules suffice for learning these embeddings in a 164 

self-supervised manner during exploration, see Fig. 2c. These local plasticity rules (see Equation 12 165 

and Equation 13 in Section 4) approximate gradient descent for learning state predictions according 166 

to (St¨ ockl et al., 2024). If nonlinear embeddings would be more useful, linear readouts from fixed 167 

nonlinear circuits, as proposed by (Maass et al., 2002), could be trained equally well. A detailed 168 

description of the CML algorithm can be found in Section 4.2 and Section D of the Supplement. 169 

Note that the CML is a deterministic model that interacts with the environment in an online 170 

manner, receiving after each action execution a resulting observation from the environment. In order 171 

to apply this model to the generation of imagined action sequences where no observations are received, 172 

we replace observations by predictions of resulting next states, see Fig. 2e. Specifically, after generating 173 

a virtual action command at, the model does not receive any observation ot+1 , and replaces it by an 174 

internally generated prediction ˆ st+1 = st + Va t. It continues from there to generate the next virtual 175 

action. Using this mechanism permits generating, for any given initial state st and any given goal 176 

state s∗, an imagined trajectory st, ˆ st+1 , ˆ st+2 , ... in the direction of the goal s∗, without receiving 177 

any observation. This becomes a probabilistic generative model if one adds noise in this imaginary 178

As first example for the capability of the GCML we show that it produces not just one, but a menu 185 

of possible solutions for a problem. We use here a formalization of problem solving that is widely 186 

adopted in AI and cognitive science: As task to find a short path from a start to a goal node in an 187 

abstract graph – whose nodes represent problem solving states and whose edges represent possible 188 

actions (Russell and Norvig, 2016; Newell et al., 1972; Hills et al., 2015; Geffner and Bonet, 2013). We 189 

consider a generic random graph with 32 nodes, see Fig. 3a. Whereas the CML generates according 190 

to (St¨ ockl et al., 2024) a single approximation to the shortest path problem, the GCML provides 191 

a selection of heuristic solutions (see Section D of the Supplement for algorithmic details). They 192 

take here the form of paths from start to goal whose length is close to minimal. Such a selection of 193 

possible solutions is useful if there are other criteria besides the length which make a particular path 194 

attractive. Mathematically, one can describe the output of the GCML as heuristic online solution 195 

to the well-known k-shortest path problem (Eppstein, 1998), where the objective is to find for some 196 

given k the k shortest paths from a given start to a given goal. As shown in Fig. 3c, most trajectories 197 

generated by the GCML in a low-noise condition (noise = 0.15) have the shortest possible length. 198 

Higher noise levels (noise = 0.25) yield a larger diversity of solutions, some of which are slightly longer 199 

paths (Fig. 3d). But their length is still clustered around the optimum. Remarkably is that they still 200 

reach the goal, in spite of the higher level of noise. Even if a bad move is initially selected, it is later 201 

compensated by moving on average in the direction of the goal. In other words, the GCML has a 202 

self-correcting homing-in heuristic to the given goal. 203 

Computational benefits of this capability, in comparison with other algorithms fir solving the k 204 

shortest path problem, are exhibited in Fig. 6. 205 

As illustration for a functional use of having diverse heuristic solutions to a problem we consider 206 

in Fig. 3e a case where specific rewards (blue) or losses (red) are subsequently assigned to the nodes 207 

of the graph. These could encode preferences or disadvantages of using specific nodes in a solution 208 

paths, such as avoiding on a trip a transfer at an airport where a thunderstorm is reported. We show 209 

in Fig. 3f that higher noise levels of the GCML, corresponding intuitively to more fantasy in problem 210 

solving, provide solution paths that collect a larger number of rewards, In this case we let the GCML 211 

generate for each noise level 40 trajectories, and select for each noise level the trajectory with the 212 

largest sum of rewards. 213 

The GCML provides a neural network model for problem solving that complements previously 214 

studied ones. With the Boltzmann Machine, or via neural sampling from a spiking neural network, one 215 

had already be able to solve problems in a less flexible manner, where all constraints and desiderata 216 

are programmed into the synaptic weights of the network (Buesing et al., 2011; Habenschuss et al., 217 

2013; Jonke et al., 2016). In contrast, the problem solving goal can be communicated to a GCML 218 

through synaptic input to the underlying neural network (see Fig. S2). 219 

## 2.3 Sampling from cognitive maps with compositional structure enables 220 

## strong generalization of problem solving 221 

Humans are able to imagine plans to achieve truly novel goals, such as wanting to sit together with a 222 

goat on the Schafberg mountain, once they receive a verbal (or otherwise symbolic, i.e., compositional) 223 

description of such goal. In terms of cognitive maps, such goals represent states that were never 224 

encountered before during exploration. Hence the cognitive map structure around them could not be 225 

shaped by direct experience, and strong generalization from similar local motifs in the state space is 226 

needed. We wondered whether the GCML is able to reproduce this strong generalization capability of 227 

the human brain. We considered a benchmark task for problem solving in a compositional domain that 228 

was recently used in a magnetoencephalography (MEG) study of compositional planning processes 229 

in the human brain (Schwartenbeck et al., 2023). Participants were asked to decide whether a given 230 

silhouette (like the one shown in black in Fig. 4a) could be decomposed using the given set of building 231 

blocks, as shown in Fig. 4b. This compositional task domain appears to be much easier than natural 232 

language processing, but it is actually NP-hard (Moore and Robson, 2001), even for silhouettes that 233 

are just composed of the first 4 building blocks shown in Fig. 4b. This implies that there is no known 234

Fig. 2: Architecture and learning process of the generative cognitive map learner (GCML). (a) Illustration of a non-spatial problem which brains are able to solve through goal-directed imagination: A plan can be imagined for reaching any given goal, such as getting a hammer. (b) The GCML employs the same cognitive map learning strategy as the CML model of (St¨ ockl et al., 2024): The cognitive map arises by learning embeddings of observations and (efferent copies of) actions into a common high-D state space so that the geometrical structure of the resulting cog-nitive map (embeddings) captures the relations between observations and actions. These embeddings 

Q and V can be chosen to be linear for the tasks that we consider, and can be learned by simple local rules for synaptic plasticity (c) The learning of the cognitive map, i.e., of the embeddings Q and V,is driven by a prediction learning goal: Predicting the embedding of the next observation (i.e., the next state) in terms of the current observation and action according to Equ. 11. (d) Simultaneously an inverse model in the form of a matrix W can be learnt that maps state differences onto actions which caused them. Note that if the learnt embedding V (the forward model) is linear and if Equ. 11 is satisfied, the existence of a linear inverse model is guaranteed. (e) To generate imagined trajectories toward a target state s∗, the estimated current state ˆ st is iteratively updated. However, importantly, no actions are carried out. Rather, predictions ˆ st+1 of resulting next observations (states) replace actual feedback from the environment. The next action is virtually selected for the next predicted state (bootstrapping). The difference between a goal state s∗ and the estimated current state ˆ st drives action selection through the learned matrix W like for real action selection, thereby enabling goal-directed imagination. 7

non-spatial domains, using an algorithmic form of goal-directed imagination. Of course, there are 303 

limitations to the performance of such a heuristic online method, which generates with low latency 304 

the first step of a proposed solution. More sophisticated search, reasoning, and internal evaluation 305 

methods can be programmed into offline algorithms to achieve better performance and handle more 306 

difficult tasks. As also indicated in Supplementary Information Section A, escaping local structures 307 

can require elevated noise levels, which may reduce stability. An interesting open question is to what 308

several cognitive maps on different levels of abstraction, that also include rules and memories of 310 

preceding successful solutions to similar problems, see (Yang and Maass, 2025) for a first step. 311

(inverse) model, which corresponds to assuming a fixed uncertainty during sampling—a formula-313 

tion commonly used in generative modeling (e.g., (Bishop et al., 1998)). Future work could explore 314 

sampling from a learned probability distribution over actions, states, and goals, which might better 315 

capture the true model uncertainty. Such an approach would connect more directly to the Bayesian 316 

framework and, more specifically, to probabilistic methods for inference and planning—such as plan-317

2022)—in which plans are inferred from learned probabilistic transition models. 319 

Altogether we have introduced the GCML as a new model for neural sampling that supports goal-320 

directed sampling. Hence it drastically enhances the application range of neural sampling in AI. In 321 

particular, it supports the drive towards more energy-efficient AI solutions for planning and problem 322 

solving. The local learning mechanism of the GCML makes this approach suitable for self-supervised 323 

on-chip learning during exploration. Furthermore, the (virtual) action selection mechanism of the 324 

GCML is suitable for in-memory computing with memristor crossbars, yielding a minimal delay for 325 

action selection that is independent of the size of the environment and the number of options for action 326 

selection. Hence, the GCML paves the way for creating edge devices that can employ goal-directed 327 

imagination, often referred to as fantasy, in order to find solutions to difficult and novel tasks. 328

## well as its relation to biology and other theoretical models. 474 

Cognitive maps for non-spatial task domains. 475 

Experimental data from neuroscience and cognitive science suggest that the brain—especially 476 

the human brain—employs cognitive maps not only for spatial navigation, but also for navigation 477 

in more abstract concept spaces, where spatial locations are replaced for example by images, or by 478 

combinations of learnt ranks of an item in different linear orders (Behrens et al., 2018; Bottini and 479 

Doeller, 2020; Tavares et al., 2015; Buzs´ aki and Moser, 2013; Iyer et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2025). We 480 

show here that the principles that enable according to the preceding section goal-directed imagination 481 

for 2D spatial cognitive maps generalize to cognitive maps for abstract concept spaces, and enable 482 

there generic problem solving (see Fig. 2a for an illustration of a non-spatial problem solving task). 483 

The role of the grid-cell system in supporting the generation of cognitive maps and conceptual 484 

navigation in abstract concept spaces is now well documented across a growing body of studies, 485 

including non-invasive neuroimaging experiments in humans (fMRI/MEG; e.g., (Constantinescu et al., 486 

2016; Park et al., 2021; Vigan` o et al., 2024)), fMRI studies in monkeys (Bongioanni et al., 2021), and 487 

intracranial recordings in monkeys (Veselic et al., 2025). However, the computational mechanisms 488 

that would allow grid-cell systems to scale to abstract spaces—particularly those that are not well 489 

captured by 2D Euclidean geometry and may require higher-dimensional representations—remain 490 

more debated (Dong and Fiete, 2024). 491 

We used distinct maps to navigate physical space and abstract problem spaces. However, it is 492 

important to note that our approach to “sampling from cognitive maps” is general and can be applied 493 

across both physical and abstract spaces, provided that the cognitive map is adapted to the task and 494 

its dimensionality. Spatial navigation and foraging occur in approximately Euclidean spaces, requiring 495 

simple 2D maps. Such low-dimensional maps can also potentially be used to address simple navigation 496 

and foraging tasks in two-dimensional abstract spaces, such as the one studied in (Constantinescu 497 

et al., 2016). 498 

However, 2D maps are insufficient for planning in non-planar random graphs and for our composi-499 

tional problem solving tasks. Hence, a system like the GCML, capable of learning in high-dimensional 500 

spaces, is useful. The use of distinct maps in our tasks is therefore not motivated by the difference 501 

between physical and abstract spaces, but by the dimensionality of the problems. 502 

Crucially, by highlighting the differences between learning maps of simpler (Euclidean, 2D) versus 503 

more complex (higher-dimensional) spaces, we are not ruling out the possibility that the grid cell 504 

system also generalizes to maps in higher dimensions required for challenging abstract tasks. For 505

“map fragmentation” (see also (George et al., 2021; Stachenfeld et al., 2017; Whittington et al., 2020) 508 

for other approaches). 509 

By providing a general approach to sampling from cognitive maps, our method is agnostic as to 510 

whether the maps are learned through the grid cell system (an idea that is increasingly empirically 511 

supported) or with the aid of other neural systems. 512

The GCML suggests a new model for neural sampling. 551 

On a more abstract algorithmic level the GCML provides an intriguing novel paradigm for using 552 

the inherent noise of biological and physical computing systems as a computational resource (Maass, 553 

2014). A new feature is that this noise enables goal-directed sampling, for a goal that is provided in 554 

the form of a synaptic input, rather than just sampling from a fixed probability distribution that has 555 

been programmed into the weights of synaptic connections. This endows the neural sampling method 556 

with a brain-like flexibility in adjusting to new goals or contingencies, and to solve new problems 557 

that were never encountered before. From a more general theoretical perspective our model for goal-558 

directed sampling is related to previous models for probabilistic inference in the brain for perception 559 

and action selection through sampling (Pouget et al., 2003; Fiser et al., 2010; Ujfalussy and Orb´ an, 560 

2022; Parr et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2006; Friston, 2010). But in contrast to this preceding work it 561 

provides a neural network paradigm for sampling from a marginal distribution, relative to problem 562 

specifications that are defined through the current synaptic input to the network. A theoretical basis 563 

for that had been provided in (Habenschuss et al., 2013). It was shown there that a stationary 564 

distribution exists for this sampling from a marginal distribution even if the synaptic connections 565 

of the network are not symmetric, which is always the case if one has synaptic connections between 566

GCMLs and RL. 600 

Sampling from a marginal distribution provides an alternative to RL for generating goal-directed 601 

behavior. Models based on RL often require methods from machine learning for training, such as 602 

BP and BPTT, whose biological viability is debated. Furthermore, except for methods based on 603 

successor representations, RL approaches require retraining for each new goal that is to be reached. 604 

The approach of (Stachenfeld et al., 2017) used the successor representation for RL in combination 605 

with a cognitive map. We are not aware of efforts to sample from this data structure. It is also an 606 

open problem whether this approach can be made applicable to tasks such as the compositional tasks 607 

that we consider, where planning needs to visit states that were not encountered during exploration. 608 

This also holds for Single-Goal-Conditioned-Contrastive RL (SGCRL), that was recently proposed 609 

(Liu et al., 2024; Bastankhah et al., 2025). But this approaches focuses, like the CML and GCML, 610 

on learning state representations that support fast action selection. In fact, actions are selected there 611 

also through a WTA operation applied to a dot product of vectors, one of which represents the goal. 612 

The focus of SGCRL is on producing efficient exploration strategies, however for a fixed goal. An open 613 

question is whether its method could also be used to enhance goal-invariant exploration in cognitive 614 

map based approaches. 615 

Apart from the versatility advantage of cognitive map based methods, there are also some recent 616 

experimental data which suggest that dopamine signals support learning of various types of predictions 617

predictions. Hence they might also support learning of cognitive maps by minimizing prediction errors 619 

for sensory inputs, which provides the basis for the CML and GCML model. Altogether, the diversity 620 

of data on dopamine signals suggests that the brain could employ not just one but a multitude of 621

GCMLs and other theoretical models 624 

There are various models for how the brain could learn cognitive maps, with a particular emphasis 625 

on the hippocampal–entorhinal system. Some approaches, such as the TEM (Whittington et al., 2020) 626 

and others (Chandra et al., 2025), emphasize a separation between learning the general structure of 627 

the environment (putatively via a grid code)—for example, the transitions between spatial locations, 628 

which generalize across different mazes—and learning the content of specific locations (putatively via 629 

place cells), which is maze-specific. Other models dispense with this distinction (George et al., 2021; 630 

Raju et al., 2024) but are still able to learn, offline, the transition structure of complex, non-Euclidean 631 

spaces. 632 

The GCML, in contrast, can learn cognitive maps in high-dimensional spaces through a simple 633 

online, predictive learning rule. A unique feature of the GCML (and its predecessor, the CML) is 634 

that the learned embeddings provide a sense of direction toward goals—a capability not explicitly 635 

addressed in the aforementioned methods or related approaches. 636 

Model Predictive Control (MPC) is another already existing framework for action selection based 637 

on a learned dynamics model. Compared with the GCML it has the advantage that it can handle also 638 

continuous state and action spaces. But apparently it lacks the capability of the GCML to choose 639 

goal-directed actions with short latency, that result from the embedding of states and actions into a 640 

high-D space. A nice challenge for future research will be to combine the best of both approaches in 641 

a hybrid model. 642 

## 4.4 Further details for the application of the GCML for goal-directed 643 

## imagination for generic problem solving (Section 2.2). 644 

The task setup was a random graph with 32 nodes, where the degree of each node was uniformly 645 

sampled between 2 and 5. Observations ot corresponded to nodes, and actions at represented travers-646 

ing edges in a specific direction. Hence each edge is modeled by two possible actions. Both ot and at647 

were encoded as unique one-hot vectors. The training dataset consisted of 200 randomly generated 648 

trajectories, each of length 32. During the planning process, GCML samples a set of trajectories from 649 

a given starting node to a target node. 650 

The mappings G transform the estimated high-dimensional state st into corresponding affordance 651 

factors. The affordance gating vector is a binary vector indicating available actions, where ones 652 

represent feasible actions and zeros indicate restricted actions. A linear transformation is sufficient in 653 

this setting, as the vectors st are approximately orthonormal. 654 

Exploration was regulated by adding a Gaussian noise vector ϵ—with each dimension sampled 655 

from N (0 , α ϵ)—to a normalized unit-length utility vector. The noise amplitude αϵ was set to a default 656 

value of 0.1. 657 

Initial values for model parameters were drawn from Gaussian distributions: Q ∼ N (0 , 1) and 658 

V, W, G ∼ N (0 , 0.1). Smaller initial values for V improved performance. Learning rates were set as 659 

ηq = 0 .1 and ηv = ηw = ηg = 0 .01. GCML demonstrates robustness to variations in learning rates, 660 

similar to the original CML, with stable training performance for ηg in the range [0 .001 , 0.05]. 661 

Each node was assigned a reward drawn from a uniform distribution U(−5, 5). 662 

To demonstrate that GCML provides a good approximation of the top-k shortest path problem 663 

(see Figs. reffig:fig3c-d), 40 trajectories were generated from a given starting node to a goal node 664 

under different noise levels in the GCML. The number of distinct trajectories for each possible tra-665 

jectory length (i.e., the number of edges in the path from the starting node to the goal node) was 666 

computed using a breadth-first search algorithm. For each trajectory length, the proportion of distinct 667 

trajectories generated by a given noise scale GCML relative to the total number of possible distinct 668 

trajectories serves as a measure of how well GCML approximates the top-k shortest path algorithm. 669

4.6.1 k shortest path problem 855 

As shown in the main text, GCML is an approximate k shortest path algorithm. We compare it with 856 

three k shortest path algorithm baselines: the classical K* (Aljazzar and Leue, 2011), mA* (Flerova 857 

et al., 2016), and a more advance bidirectional edge labeling A* algorithm (BELA*) (Linares L´ opez 858 

and Herman, 2024). None heuristic form of the algorithms is used, since nodes in our random graphs 859 

environment are equivalent. 860 

Details of K* algorithm. K* is an on-the-fly algorithm for enumerating the k shortest paths 861 

in nondecreasing cost order (Aljazzar and Leue, 2011). It first runs an A*-style search to obtain 862 

the shortest path and to incrementally reveal sidetrack edges (i.e., detours) relative to the current 863 

shortest path tree. These detours are organized into a path graph on which a Dijkstra-like procedure 864 

is executed to repeatedly extract the next best detour combination, reconstruct the corresponding full 865 

path, and output it. The two searches are interleaved: when the auxiliary search exhausts currently 866 

known detours, K* resumes A* to expand more of the original graph and updates the auxiliary 867 

structure, continuing until k solutions are produced. 868 

Details of mA* algorithm. mA* (Flerova et al., 2016) extends A* (Hart et al., 1968) from 869 

returning a single shortest path to enumerating the k shortest paths in nondecreasing path length. It 870 

maintains a priority queue of candidate nodes initialized with the start node and repeatedly selects 871 

the most promising node according to an evaluation that combines the accumulated path length with 872 

a heuristic estimate of the remaining distance to the goal (i.e., the remaining distance is fixed at zero 873

Comparison of working memory consumption. We define the working memory of the algo-920 

rithms as the transient memory required during online trajectory generation. The three search-based 921 

baselines impose a substantially heavier online memory burden than GCML, albeit for different 922 

reasons. K* must preserve structures for sidetrack management and path enumeration. mA* must 923 

maintain a dynamically growing search frontier, a record of explored states, and multiple predeces-924 

sor references to support the recovery of alternative shortest paths. BELA* also requires a record of 925 

labeled explored state and auxiliary structures called centroids for combining prefixes and suffixes of 926 

candidate paths. As the number of requested solutions increases, these intermediate representations 927 

of the baseline algorithms can accumulate. 928 

By contrast, GCML performs online generation by following a learned cognitive map prior and 929 

sampling directly toward the goal, so its transient memory is dominated by the current local decision 930 

context and the partial trajectory being produced, rather than by a global search over the graph. 931 

Consequently, GCML’s online working memory is typically much closer to a local, near-constant-932 

size inference buffer, whereas the baselines require graph-scale, search-dependent memory that grows 933 

with problem difficulty and the number of requested paths. In conclusion, GCML may incur a fixed 934 

static memory cost for storing model parameters and the learned cognitive map, whereas the working 935 

memory of the search baselines grows dynamically during online computation. 936 

4.6.2 Tiling problem 937 

We implemented two mature and widely used baselines on the same tiling environment setting used by 938 

our method (i.e., five BBs in training silhouettes and eight BBs in the test silhouettes): a reinforcement 939 

learning (RL) baseline, and a model predictive control (MPC) baseline. 940 

Details of RL algorithm. For RL algorithm, the dueling double deep Q-Network learning 941 

(D3QN) algorithm is adopted. The Q-function uses a dueling architecture (Wang et al., 2016) with 942 

Double-DQN target selection (Van Hasselt et al., 2016). The definition of observation and action 943 

keeps unchanged to GCML. When selecting actions, the action feasibility is enforced by an affordance 944 

mask as in GCML (invalid actions are masked before selection by affordance). We tuned the learning 945 

rate over {10 −3, 10 −4, 10 −5, 10 −6} and selected the value with the best validation performance. We 946 

also varied the depth of the encoder’s linear layers, the value and advantage heads, adopting the 947 

best-performing architecture. 948 

The network model consists of a convolutional encoder, and two dueling heads for value and advan-949 

tage estimation. The encoder has (i) a convolutional stack with channels 1 → 32 → 64 → 64, using 950 

3 × 3 kernels with stride 1 and padding 1, followed by ReLU activations, and (ii) a feedforward stack 951 

6400 → 1024 → 1024 → 512 with LayerNorm and GeLU activations, producing a 512-dimensional 952 

state representation s. On top of this representation, the value head is an multilayer perceptron 953 

(MLP) with layer size 512 → 512 → 256 → 128 → 1 that outputs V (s), and the advantage head is 954 

an MLP 512 → 512 → 256 → 128 → |A| that outputs A(s, a ); both heads use LayerNorm and GeLU 955 

activations. A denotes the set of all possible actions. 956 

We train the network with a dueling critic and experience replay. The Q-network is optimized 957 

with Adam (learning rate 10 −5) using mini-batches of 512 transitions sampled from a replay buffer of 958 

capacity 2 × 10 5. We use a discount factor γ = 0 .99. Training is run for 10,000 episodes. Exploration 959 

follows an ϵ-greedy policy with ϵ linearly annealed from 0 .30 to 0 .02 over 1,200 episodes. During eval-960 

uation, to generate different trajectories, we keep a stochasticity with ϵeval = 0 .3 (optimized among 961 

{ 0.05, 0 .1, 0 .15, ..., 0 .4}). With probability ϵeval , it performs policy-guided stochastic exploration: 962 

affordance-masked action scores from the trained Q-network are converted into a probability dis-963 

tribution via softmax, and a valid action is sampled from this distribution. The per-step reward is 964 

designed to reward the valid decisions: rt = 2 · Idone , where Idone indicates task completion. 965 

Details of MPC algorithm. To choose an action, MPC (Garcia et al., 1989) plans an optimal 966 

action sequence under a learned policy model, executes only the first action, and replans at the next 967 

step by discarding the remaining actions. In our implementation, MPC uses the cross-entropy method 968 

(CEM) (Kobilarov, 2012) as its policy-optimization (planning) procedure. CEM maintains a sampling 969 

distribution over action sequences and updates it iteratively over time. At each iteration, it draws 970 

candidate sequences, rolls them out through the learned dynamics to approximate the induced state 971 

trajectories and returns, and scores each candidate accordingly. A fixed number of the best-scoring 972 

sequences are then treated as elites, and their statistics are used to refit the distribution, yielding an 973 

updated action distribution for the current decision step. 974

In this section, we evaluate the ability of goal directed sampling from a grid-cell based cognitive 1280 

map to handle navigation tasks that require detours. As example we consider escape from U-shaped 1281 

obstacles. 1282 

As described in Section 4.1, the agent’s movement results from the combined effect of three 1283 

components: (1) attraction toward the goal, (2) stochasticity introduced through the noise term ϵ1284 

in action selection, and (3) a repulsive force exerted by obstacles. The sum of these components 1285 

determines the direction of each movement step. 1286 

Detour-based navigation, where the agent must temporarily move away from the goal, is enabled 1287 

by the latter two components: noise and repulsion. Using the same parameters as in Fig. 1f, we 1288 

generated the trajectories shown in Fig. S1a. When the U-shaped obstacle is shallow, the repulsive 1289 

force alone is sufficient to drive the agent around the obstacle; all paths consistently move downward 1290 

from the starting point. 1291 

However, this simple mechanism fails when the obstacle becomes deeper (Fig. S1b). In this case, 1292 

the extended lower walls exert upward repulsive forces, which—under the simple repulsion rule used 1293 

in Section 4.1, trap the agent within the U-shaped obstacle . 1294 

Increasing the noise level alleviates this issue (Fig. S1c). Higher stochasticity allows the agent to 1295 

occasionally escape the U-shaped region; once outside, the repulsive force becomes beneficial again, 1296 

pushing the agent away from the obstacle and toward the goal. Thus the noise level regulates to what 1297 

extent goal-directed planning can overcome obstacles by transiently moving away from the goal. 1298 

This simple planning method provides an alternative to the navigation strategy of (Bakermans 1299 

et al., 2025). Note that even more difficult obstacles can be handled with higher dimensional cognitive 1300 

maps as considered in Section 2.2. But there are certainly also limitations to the problem solving 1301 

capability of a simple heuristic online algorithm that is equipped with just a single cognitive map. For 1302 

example, to escape from an U-shaped obstacle one would need higher and higher levels of noise when 1303 

the U-shape becomes deeper. Then a hierarchical organization becomes more appropriate, where a 1304 

problem is divided on the top level into subproblems that each require different solution methods. An 1305
