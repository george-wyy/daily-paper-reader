Summary 23 

Inner (covert) speech, the silent production of language in one's mind, plays a 24 

central role in human cognition and is known to activate speech-related cortical 25 

regions while also requiring executive and attentional resources. However, the 26 

conventional focus on cortical similarity or dissimilarity between inner and overt 27 

speech leaves unaddressed the question of whether arousal systems sustain 28 

cortical language processing. Here, using pupillometry across three controlled 29 

counting experiments, we show a dissociation between inner and overt speech. 30 

Overt speech consistently led to pupil dilation across all conditions. Inner speech, 31 

by contrast, produced a strikingly different profile: pupil constriction during simple 32 

counting, no deviation from baseline during noun-phrase counting, and clear 33 

dilation only under a high cognitive load, but with dilation remaining significantly 34 

less than that for overt speech in every condition. Covert verbal production thus 35 

proceeds at a reduced arousal cost, yet this cost scales flexibly with task 36 

demands rather than being fixed. This pupillometric dissociation reveals that 37 

inner and overt speech occupy fundamentally distinct arousal states, even when 38 

their cortical substrates partially overlap. These findings open a non-invasive 39 

window for tracking inner speech physiology across development, individual 40 

differences, and clinical populations in whom inner speech is altered. 41

counting 43

Introduction 45 

There is an old saying that “the eyes speak as much as the mouth.” But do they 46 

speak the same language when we talk aloud versus when we talk silently to 47 

ourselves? 48 

Inner speech, the act of speaking silently in one’s head, is a key cognitive tool for 49 

human thought and self-regulation 1. Neuroimaging studies from the past two-50 

three decades hold that inner speech, also referred to as covert speech, partially 51 

shares the same neural substrate as overt speech, particularly in left-lateralized 52 

language networks (e.g., Broca area) 1–3 . This has led to a prevailing motor-53 

attenuation view positing that inner speech is essentially the same as overt 54 

speech, albeit with the final motor commands for articulation withheld 4. However, 55 

this focus on cortical similarity or dissimilarity between inner and overt speech 56 

leaves a critical dimension unaddressed: the arousal systems that sustain cortical 57 

language processing. The lateral prefrontal cortex depends on monoaminergic 58 

and cholinergic neuromodulation for its proper function 5, and these systems 59 

actively shape how cortical circuits process information—as shown in the auditory 60 

cortex, where arousal state determines the quality and reliability of sound-evoked 61 

neural responses 6,7 . A comparable dependence holds for language-related 62 

networks, where disruption of neuromodulatory input is associated with impaired 63 

language processing and poor recovery from aphasia 8. Yet whether inner and 64 

overt speech differentially engage arousal systems has not been directly 65 

examined. We therefore hypothesized that arousal systems may fundamentally 66 

underlie any cortical similarity or dissimilarity between the two speech modalities. 67 

Pupillometry provides a direct, non-invasive window into the arousal systems 68 

described above. Pupil diameter is a sensitive real-time index of arousal 69 

dynamics mainly linked to the locus coeruleus–noradrenergic and cholinergic 70 

systems 9,10 , predicting aspects of subcortical and cortical arousal that heart rate 71 

or electrodermal measures do not directly reflect 9–15 . It is also subject to top-down 72 

executive control 16 and reliably increases in response to cognitive effort, 73 

respiration, and physical activity 12,17–21 . Crucially, pupillometry requires no head-74 

mounted devices and remains robust during overt articulatory movements, 75 

making it uniquely suited for directly comparing inner and overt speech within a 76 

single paradigm (in contrast to other brain imaging methods, which are often 77 

susceptible to significant speech-related noise 1,22 ). Although pupillary responses 78 

during speech production have not yet been well studied, it has been suggested 79 

that overt vocalization elicits pupillary dilation 23,24 . Inner speech is known to 80 

recruit cortical processes that partly overlap with overt speech, including left-81 

lateralized language regions 1,3 . Moreover, inner speech may place demands on 82 

sustained attention and verbal working memory, particularly when phonological 83 

representations must be internally maintained and monitored 1. Some accounts 84 

further suggest that inner speech may also engage motor-inhibitory control to 85 

prevent overt articulation 4,25 . Additionally, motor imagery tasks have also been 86 

shown to elicit pupil dilation 26,27 , suggesting that pupil-linked arousal is engaged 87 

even when overt action is suppressed. On this basis, we hypothesized that pupil-88

In the present study, we addressed this question using a numerical counting 91 

paradigm. Counting provides a useful experimental model because it is a highly 92 

controlled verbal task with minimal affective variability, can be implemented in 93 

both inner and overt forms, and allows systematic manipulation of cognitive load 94 

while preserving the core structure of verbal production. It also makes it relatively 95 

feasible to experimentally verify, to some extent, whether inner speech is being 96 

engaged. Our recent work, for example, used numerical counting to show that 97 

preschool children (3-6 years old) are capable of inner speech to a measurable 98 

degree 28 . Although this paradigm does not capture the full range of naturally 99 

occurring inner speech, it reflects one form of inner speech that humans 100 

frequently use in everyday life 29 and thus provides a useful model for investigating 101 

its physiological characteristics. We therefore first tested whether inner speech 102 

engages pupil-linked arousal in a manner comparable to overt speech, using a 103 

simple numerical counting paradigm (Experiment 1). Contrary to our primary 104 

hypothesis, inner speech produced not dilation but constriction relative to 105 

baseline, while overt speech led to dilation as expected, raising the question of 106 

whether this dissociation is a robust feature of covert verbal production or specific 107 

to the demands of simple numerical counting. We next examined whether the 108 

dissociation persists when verbal content is enriched: in Experiment 2, 109 

participants counted using grammatically structured numeral-noun phrases, a 110 

manipulation that increases phonological and morphological complexity while 111 

preserving the counting structure. Finally, in Experiment 3, we asked whether the 112 

dissociation holds under a higher cognitive load by requiring step counting, 113 

thereby examining whether inner speech arousal scales with task demands. This 114 

stepwise approach allowed us to characterize the conditions under which pupil-115 

linked arousal during inner speech is attenuated relative to overt speech, and the 116 

extent to which that attenuation varies with verbal content and cognitive demands. 117

Sample Size Determination 136 

For Experiment 1, the sample size was determined using an a priori power 137 

analysis (G*Power) based on effect sizes reported in prior block-design 138 

pupillometry studies. We calculated that a minimum sample of 20 would be 139 

required to detect a large effect size (Cohen's dz = 0.8) 19,30,31 with 80% power at 140 

a Bonferroni-corrected α of 0.0167. To account for potential data attrition, we set 141 

our recruitment target at 25 participants. For the subsequent Experiments 2 and 142 

3, we conducted a new power analysis based on the main effect size observed 143 

in our own Experiment 1 (Cohen's d = 0.69, No-thought vs. Inner speech). This 144 

analysis indicated that a sample of 25 participants would be required to detect 145 

this effect with 80% power at the same Bonferroni-corrected α level. We therefore 146 

set our recruitment target at 30 participants for Experiments 2 and 3. 147 

Experimental Procedures 148 

Across all experiments, participants completed a block-designed task while 149 

maintaining their gaze on an on-screen instruction panel. Each block consisted 150 

of a 30-s rest period followed by a 45-s task period (Figure 1). During the rest 151 

period, the word “Rest” was displayed on the screen. The first 15 s of the rest 152 

period served as a washout interval, and the last 15 s were used as the baseline 153 

period for pupillometry analyses. During both rest and task periods, participants 154 

were instructed to keep looking at the screen and to minimize head movements. 155 

During task periods, participants were presented with a list of three instructions 156 

(conditions) and were prompted with an arrow indicating which instruction to 157 

follow: (1) No-thought (attempt to think of nothing), (2) Overt speech (count aloud), 158 

and (3) Inner speech (count silently in the mind). One set comprised one block 159 

for each of the three conditions, and each participant completed three sets (i.e., 160 

three blocks per condition) (Figure 1A). The order of conditions was randomized. 161 

Pupil diameter was recorded continuously throughout. No explicit instruction was 162 

given regarding counting pace; participants counted at their own natural tempo. 163 

Compliance was assessed by asking participants to report the final number 164 

reached during the overt speech and inner speech conditions. 165

Experiment 1: Simple numerical counting 183 

Participants counted upward from 1 using consecutive integers (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4…). 184 

Compliance questions were administered after each session. 185 

186 

Experiment 2: Numeral-noun phrase counting 187 

Participants counted in Japanese using grammatically structured numeral– 188 

classifier phrases that included the nominative particle ga (が) and category-189 

specific classifiers. Before each session, the experimenter gave instructions 190 

about the semantic category (cats, balls, or books), and participants generated 191 

sequential counting phrases accordingly. For example, the animal category 192 

required phrases such as neko ga ippiki , neko ga nihiki , neko ga sanbiki … (“one 193 

cat, two cats, three cats…”), the object category required booru ga ikko , booru ga 194 

niko … (“one ball, two balls…”), and the book category required hon ga issatsu ,195 

hon ga nisatsu … (“one book, two books…”). This manipulation introduced not 196 

only semantic variation but also Japanese-specific morpho-phonological 197 

alternations across numbers (e.g., gemination and voicing in forms such as 198 

ippiki /nihiki /sanbiki , ikko /niko , issatsu /nisatsu ), thereby increasing 199 

phonological/linguistic variability relative to simple numeral counting. The order 200 

of semantic categories across sessions was randomized and counterbalanced 201 

across participants. Compliance questions were administered after each block. 202 

Experiment 3: Step counting 203 

Participants performed step counting. Before each session, the experimenter 204 

explained the counting rule: counting upward from 1 in increments of 2, 3, or 4 205 

(e.g., 1, 3, 5, 7, 9…; 1, 4, 7, 10…; 1, 5, 9, 13…). The order of increment size 206 

across sessions was randomized and counterbalanced across participants. 207 

Compliance questions were administered after each block. 208 

Pupil recording and analysis 209 

Pupil diameter was continuously recorded using a screen-based eye tracker 210 

(Tobii Pro Spectrum, Tobii AB, Danderyd, Sweden) at 60 Hz and processed in 211 

Tobii Pro Lab (Tobii Pro Spectrum, Tobii AB, Danderyd, Sweden). Visual stimuli 212 

were presented on a 23.8-inch (60 cm) monitor (FlexScan EV2451, EIZO, Japan). 213 

A 9-point calibration was performed at the beginning of each session. Participants 214 

were seated approximately 70 cm from the stimulus display and were instructed 215 

to maintain fixation and minimize head movements. Following previous 216 

works 19,20,30 , only valid samples from the right eye were analyzed; missing or 217 

invalid samples (e.g., due to blinks or gaze loss) were not included. To minimize 218 

luminance-related confounds on pupil size, visual parameters were strictly 219 

matched across the three conditions. All screens were presented on a uniform 220 

gray background (RGB: 120, 120, 120), and all task-relevant symbols/letters 221 

were rendered in black (RGB: 0, 0, 0). The on-screen instructions were presented 222 

in Japanese using the Meiryo UI font at size 48 (Figure 1B). The three conditions 223 

differed only in the on-screen position of the instruction arrow, ensuring that 224 

luminance and the amount of visual information on screen remained constant 225 

across conditions. For each block, a 30-s rest period preceded the task. The first 226 

15 s of the rest period were treated as a washout interval, and the last 15 s were 227

Analytic plan 242 

To verify compliance with the inner-speech instructions, we computed 243 

Spearman's rho between overt and inner speech final counts across participants 244 

for each experiment; a strong positive correlation would indicate that individual 245 

counting tempo was preserved across conditions. Separately, a paired t-test was 246 

conducted to assess whether the mean final count differed systematically 247 

between the two conditions. 248 

For the primary pupillometry analysis, a one-way RM-ANOVA with condition (no-249 

thought, inner speech, overt speech) as a within-subject factor and Δpupil 250 

diameter as the dependent variable was used to test whether conditions differed 251 

overall. When a significant main effect was observed, pairwise comparisons were 252 

conducted using Bonferroni-corrected paired t-tests across three planned 253 

contrasts: no-thought vs. inner speech, inner speech vs. overt speech, and no-254 

thought vs. overt speech. One-sample t-tests against zero were additionally 255 

performed to determine whether each condition's Δpupil diameter significantly 256 

deviated from the pre-task baseline (the 15 seconds before task onset). 257 

Significance threshold was P < 0.05 throughout. All statistical analyses were 258 

performed using GraphPad Prism 9. The analytic approach was established in 259 

Experiment 1, formally preregistered for Experiment 2 on the Open Science 260 

Framework prior to data collection (https://osf.io/7efbv), and applied without 261 

modification in Experiment 3. 262

Discussion 333 

The results of the present study do not support the initial assumption that inner 334 

and overt speech produce equivalent pupillary responses. Across three 335 

experiments, overt speech consistently elicited pupil dilation, whereas inner 336 

speech invariably produced reduced responses relative to overt speech. Critically, 337 

the direction of the inner-speech pupillary response relative to baseline was not 338 

fixed: pupil constriction occurred during simple numerical counting (Experiment 339 

1), no significant deviation from baseline occurred during noun-phrase counting 340 

(Experiment 2), and clear dilation occurred during cognitively demanding step 341 

counting (Experiment 3). Together, these findings indicate that inner-speech-342 

linked pupil responses are robustly attenuated relative to overt speech, while their 343 

direction varies systematically with both the specific verbal content and the 344 

cognitive demands of the inner-speech task. 345 

A fundamental challenge in inner-speech research is verifying that participants 346 

are actually engaged in covert verbal production. The numerical counting 347 

paradigm offers an indirect but meaningful compliance check: participants who 348 

count rapidly during overt speech also tend to do so during inner speech. In 349 

comparing counting speed between inner- and overt-speech tasks, our task-350 

compliance results were consistent with this tendency ( rho > 0.85 across all three 351 

experiments). 352 

The most robust finding across all three experiments was the consistent pupil 353 

dilation elicited by overt speech, suggesting that the presence of vocalization is 354 

a critical determinant of pupil-linked arousal. Pupil diameter is a well-established 355 

index of cognitive effort and brain arousal state 11,17 , reflecting coordinated activity 356 

across the locus coeruleus–noradrenergic system 15,32 , the basal forebrain 357 

cholinergic system 10 , the orexin system 33 , and associated cortical networks 13,14 .358 

Although pupillometry has been applied to memory load 17 , lexical processing 34 ,359 

speech planning 34 , vocal reading 23,24 , musical imagery 35 , and physical activity 19 ,360 

direct comparisons of inner speech, overt speech, and instructed no-thought 361 

within a matched task structure are, to our knowledge, absent. The consistent 362 

overt-speech-induced pupil dilation observed here likely reflects the integrated 363 

arousal recruitment associated with motor execution, respiratory variation, 364 

sensorimotor feedback, and attention 15,18,24,36 , each of which is presumed to 365 

engage brainstem arousal systems and cortical networks 13,36 , potentially 366 

contributing to efficient speech production 5,8 . Inner speech, by contrast, did not 367 

approach overt-speech levels of pupil dilation in any condition, suggesting that 368 

covert verbal production can be executed without recruiting pupil-linked arousal 369 

systems and cortical networks to the same degree. 370 

Interestingly, inner speech was not uniformly associated with low pupil-linked 371 

arousal. Under a minimal cognitive load (Experiment 1), inner speech produced 372 

pupil constriction even relative to the no-thought condition, suggesting that highly 373 

routinised covert counting is executed in a markedly low-arousal state. As 374 

cognitive demand increased (Experiment 2), this constriction was abolished, and 375 

inner speech no longer differed from baseline. Under the highest load 376

Past neuroimaging studies have established that inner and overt speech share 381 

language-related networks and speech production processes 2,37 , and inner 382 

speech has recently been theorized as a form of inhibited overt speech 4. Our 383 

findings do not challenge this shared neural substrate; rather, they reveal that, at 384 

the level of pupil-linked arousal, inner speech operates in a distinct mode that is 385 

dissociated from the obligatory arousal accompanying voice production. Inner 386 

speech may therefore utilize overlapping neurocognitive mechanisms while 387 

constituting verbal processing at reduced physiological cost. This interpretation 388 

aligns with the observation that inner speech can proceed faster than overt 389 

speech 38 and with the Experiment 2 finding that participants counted to higher 390 

numbers during inner speech within the same time window, indicating more 391 

efficient progression. 392 

Given that pupillometry is non-invasive and contactless, these findings open new 393 

avenues for tracing the Vygotskian internalizations of overt into inner speech 394 

across development and for examining individual differences in inner speech 395 

physiology in naturalistic settings. The capacity of inner speech to sustain verbal 396 

processing at reduced arousal cost may be advantageous where efficient 397 

cognitive activity and mental calm must coexist—as in cognitive control 39 , sport 398 

performance 39 or routinised inner counting as a sleep aid 40 —yet may also enable 399 

maladaptive cognitive loops that contribute to rumination 41 . Conversely, the 400 

elevated arousal accompanying overt speech may carry functional value: Fawcett 401 

et al. 23 showed that the pupil dilation elicited by reading aloud correlates with the 402 

memory advantage for spoken over silent reading words, suggesting that overt 403 

speech leverages its greater arousal cost to strengthen encoding. Together, 404 

these asymmetric profiles point toward translational applications of pupillometry 405 

at the intersection of speech modality and subcortical and cortical arousal 406 

regulation 39 .407 

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, the inner speech induced here 408 

was task-evoked and numerically constrained; it does not generalize to 409 

spontaneous or dialogic inner speech, which represent distinct and richer forms 410 

of covert verbal experience 42,43 . Second, the no-thought condition reflects an 411 

instructed attempt to suppress thought rather than verified mental silence; mind-412 

wandering cannot be entirely eliminated, and this condition should be regarded 413 

as a tentative resting baseline 37 . Nevertheless, the replicability of the dissociation 414 

across three independent experiments, each employing a different counting 415 

paradigm, supports the robustness of the present findings as a foundational 416 

physiological account of inner versus overt speech. 417
