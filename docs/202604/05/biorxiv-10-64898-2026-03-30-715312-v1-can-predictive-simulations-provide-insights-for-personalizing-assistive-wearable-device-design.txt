# Abstract 

Optimizing assistive wearable devices is crucial for their efficacy and user adoption, yet state-of-the-art methods like Human-in-the-Loop Optimization (HILO) and biomechanical modeling face limitations. HILO is time-consuming and often restricted to optimizing control parameters, while inverse dynamics assumes invariant kinematics, which is unreliable for adaptive human-device interaction. Predictive simulation offers a powerful alternative, enabling computational exploration of design spaces. However, existing approaches often lack systematic optimization frameworks and rigorous validation against experimental data. To address this, we developed a Design Optimization Platform that integrates predictive simulations within a two-level optimization structure for personalizing assistive device design. This paper primarily validates the platform’s predictive simulations against a publicly available dataset of the passive Biarticular Thigh Exosuit (BATEX), assessing its reliability. Our findings show that the model can sufficiently predict the kinematics and major muscle activations, except for the pelvis tilt and some biarticular muscles. The key finding is that successful identification of personalized optimal BATEX stiffness parameters needs acceptable prediction of metabolic cost trends, not their precise values. Our analysis further reveals that the model’s accuracy in predicting Vasti muscle activation in the baseline condition is a significant indicator of its success in predicting metabolic cost trends. This demonstrates that accurate prediction of performance trends is more important for effective simulation-based design optimization than perfect biomechanical accuracy, advancing targeted and efficient assistive device development.

control problem, which assumes that the central nervous system optimizes a given cost 28 

function (e.g., metabolic cost) to achieve a desired movement (16); generated by 29 

controlling the muscles via trajectory optimization (17; 18) or closed-loop 30

design of assistive devices by computationally exploring the design space. For example, 32 

It has been utilized for evaluating the metabolic benefits of different multi- and 33 

single-joint assistance strategies for walking (21; 22). However, these studies lack a 34 

comprehensive verification of the simulated results against experimental data. Others 35 

have applied this method to design assistive devices for impaired users and validated 36 

their predictions against patient data. For instance, Waterval et. al (23) evaluated 37 

passive ankle-foot orthosis designs for patients with plantarflexion weakness, or 38 

Sreenivasa et. al (24) determined the optimal stiffness for a passive ankle exoskeleton 39 

for children with gait abnormalities. 40 

While some studies (25; 26) propose platforms for the design of human-exoskeleton 41 

systems using biomechanical models, a comprehensive and systematic framework for the 42 

design optimization of assistive wearable devices using predictive biomechanical models 43 

is still an evolving area of research. Many studies employing simulations utilize a 44 

brute-force approach, where a limited number of design options are individually 45 

simulated and the best case among them is chosen as the solution (27). This approach 46 

requires substantial computational resources as the number of design parameters and 47 

their ranges increase. To address this research gap, we previously introduced a Design 48 

Optimization Platform in (28). This platform employs a two-level optimization 49 

structure, using predictive simulation in its inner loop and an outer-loop optimizer to 50 

efficiently navigate high-dimensional design spaces. However, the primary limitation 51

behavior. Previous studies indicate that while simulations can reliably predict trends, 53 

they may significantly overestimate benefits like metabolic cost reductions or muscle 54

real-world device design. 57

# Methods 66 

## 0.1 Passive BATEX and experimental dataset 67 

The field of assistive wearable devices has seen a significant shift from traditional 68 

designs with rigid structures to lightweight, soft wearable devices (e.g., 69 

exosuits) ( 32 ; 33 ). We focus on the personalized design of the Biarticular Thigh Exosuit 70 

(BATEX), a novel passive exosuit designed to assist the hip and the knee joints through 71 

bio-inspired biarticular actuation (34). It provides assistance via two elastic elements 72 

per leg, acting in parallel to the Rectus Femoris (RF) and Hamstrings (HAM) muscles. 73 

A publicly available dataset from an experiment using the passive BATEX (detailed 74 

in (31)) is employed in this study. The BATEX’s passive elastic elements are perfectly 75 

defined design parameters that can be personalized using the Design Optimization 76 

Platform , and its real-world experimental dataset allows for a rigorous validation of the 77 

predictive simulations. Data from eight healthy male participants is used in this study 78 

(age: 27.9 ± 4.56 years; mass: 73 ± 7.12 kg; height: 1.8 ± 0.06 m), who walked on a 79 

treadmill at 1.3 m/s while wearing BATEX. The exosuit (weighing 2.2 kg) uses two 80 

elastic elements per leg. One spring runs parallel to the HAM and the other to the RF 81 

muscles (Fig 1). Nine distinct BATEX configurations and one condition without the 82 

exosuit (not considered in this study) are tested in the experiments. The configurations 83 

with the exosuit involve all combinations of three predefined springs with zero, low, and 84 

high stiffness levels (0, 650, and 1100 N/m, respectively). The publicly available, 85 

anonymized dataset of (31) was accessed for research purposes on 01.02.2025. The 86 

authors did not have access to any information that could identify individual 87 

participants during or after data collection. 88 

Biomechanical data are collected during the walking trials, including kinematics (23 89 

reflective markers; optical motion capture system), ground reaction forces (instrumented 90 

treadmill), muscle activity (surface EMG from six lower-limb muscles: Rectus Femoris 91 

(RF), Hamstrings (HAM), Vasti (VAS), Gluteus Maximus (GM), Soleus (SOL), and 92 

Gastrocnemius (GAS)), exosuit forces (force sensors), and metabolic energy 93 

consumption (indirect calorimetry). The processed dataset is directly used for the 94 

validation of predictive simulations in this study. 95

0.2.1 Baseline simulations 106 

A two-dimensional musculoskeletal model (H0918v3.hfd) with 9 degrees of freedom and 107 

18 Hill-type musculotendon units is used for all predictive simulations. To personalize 108 

model’s properties, it is scaled to each individual’s height, weight, and segment lengths 109 

based on scaling factors derived from the experimental dataset’s individualized 110 

OpenSim models. The mass of the BATEX hardware (2.2 kg) is distributed as point 111 

masses on the model segments (i.e., 1.2 kg to the pelvis, 0.4 kg to the femur, and 0.1 kg 112 

to the tibia of both legs) to account for the device’s inertia. 113 

The ”No Spring” (NS) condition, where participants wear the device with springs 114 

removed, is chosen as the baseline gait in this study. This choice ensures the comparison 115 

of the effects of different spring configurations. The goal of the baseline simulation is to 116 

personalize the model by optimizing a subject-specific neuromuscular controller that 117 

best reproduces the experimental gait. 118

0.2.2 Assisted simulations 139 

The other eight BATEX configurations (except the NS condition) are considered 140 

assisted cases. The same subject-specific musculoskeletal models of the baseline 141 

conditions (NS) are used for the simulation of the assisted conditions. The BATEX’s 142 

assistance is modeled by applying torques directly to the hip and knee joints. These 143 

torques are calculated at each time step based on the spring stiffness values, the current 144 

kinematics of the model, and constant lever arms for the springs around the hip and 145 

knee joints, which are calculated using motion capture data and force sensors as 146 

detailed in ( 31 ). Additionally, the rest lengths of the RF and HAM springs are set for a 147 

standing pose, where both the hip and knee joint angles are zero. A schematic overview 148 

of the BATEX device and its model are represented in Fig 1. 149 

The neuromuscular controller parameters derived from each participant’s baseline 150 

simulation are used as the initial guess for the assisted predictive simulations. This 151 

approach leverages the personalized gait and significantly reduces the optimization’s 152 

search space. The number of optimizable controller parameters is also reduced from 53 153 

to 32 by fixing parameters that are assumed to be invariant after the introduction of 154 

external assistance; namely, initial state offsets, stance and swing load thresholds, and 155 

the constant feedback offset gains. The cost function for the assisted simulations is 156 

identical to the baseline (Eq 1), but the weights of the experimental kinematic and 157 

muscle activation penalty terms ( JExp.Kinematics and JExp.M uscleActivation ) are 158 

adjusted. A higher weight is placed on matching the kinematics compared to muscle 159 

activations, reflecting the experimental observation that participants’ movement 160 

patterns do not change significantly when walking with the BATEX. 161

## 0.3 Using the Design Optimization Platform for individualized 205 

## design of BATEX 206 

Building on validated predictive simulation methods, the Design Optimization Platform 207 

is employed to find the optimal personalized spring stiffness for the BATEX device. The 208 

platform uses a two-level optimization structure (Fig 1), as detailed in our former 209 

study (28). An outer loop uses a Bayesian optimizer to find the optimal design 210 

parameters, and an inner loop utilizes predictive methods to simulate human-device 211 

movement. The same subject-specific musculoskeletal models and neuromuscular 212 

controllers from the assisted simulations are used in the platform. The specific kRF and 213 

kHAM stiffness values are varied by the outer-loop optimizer in each iteration. 214 

The cost function for optimization is the calculated metabolic cost of the assisted 215 

case, normalized by that of unassisted walking (NS), both from the same basic 216 

simulation model. This means that the cost function of the unassisted simulation model 217 

(NS) will be equal to 1. The outer loop starts with 10 random evaluations and 218 

terminates either when the cost function improvement is less than 0.05 (equal to 5% 219 

reduction of metabolic cost) over the last 15 iterations or after 100 total iterations. The 220 

cost function for the outer loop focuses on a single objective of minimizing the Cost of 221 

Transport (COT), which is the metabolic cost normalized by the distance traveled. This 222 

cost function is chosen since the main benefit of the BATEX experiments is to minimize 223 

the metabolic cost of gait for the users. For the unstable simulations where the model 224 

fails to complete the movement, a high cost value of 5 (equal to 500% increase in 225 

metabolic cost) is assigned to keep the optimizer away from these device configurations. 226 

To reduce the risk of converging to a local minimum in the complex design space, 227 

each subject-specific optimization problem is executed five times with different starting 228 

random seeds. The final optimal design solution for each participant is selected as the 229 

one with the lowest optimal cost function value among these five independent 230 

optimization runs. 231

# Discussion 334 

## 0.8 Simulation validation 335 

The analysis of baseline (NS) simulations reveals a strong overall agreement with 336 

experimental gait kinematics. The high accuracy in predicting hip, knee, and ankle joint 337 

angles is evidenced by high correlation coefficients and low NRMSE values. Conversely, 338 

the prediction of pelvis tilt is more challenging, showing significantly lower agreement 339 

with experimental data. This is likely due to the model’s simplification of the upper 340 

body as a single rigid segment, which limits its ability to capture the complex dynamics 341 

of the pelvis and trunk observed in human gait. Similar challenges have been reported 342 

in other predictive simulation studies (38). Furthermore, the predictive accuracy 343 

generally decreases from distal (ankle) to more proximal (pelvis) segments, suggesting 344 

that model inaccuracies propagate up the kinematic chain. Such discrepancies are also 345 

reported in other predictive simulation studies (23; 39; 40). 346 

Inter-subject variability is also observed in the predictive accuracy of muscle 347 

activations for baseline (NS) simulations. The model predicts the activation patterns of 348 

monoarticular and distal biarticular muscles like the VAS, SOL, and GAS sufficiently 349 

accurately for all participants. However, predictions of proximal biarticular muscles (RF 350 

and HAM) and the GM are more challenging and subject-dependent. This difficulty is 351 

consistent with findings in other studies (41; 42) and is likely linked to the complex, 352 

multi-joint roles of these muscles (RF and HAM) and their direct influence on the pelvis 353 

and trunk (GM). These discrepancies in muscle activation patterns are likely worsened 354 

by the model’s limitations in accurately capturing pelvis and trunk kinematics. 355 

Building on the baseline results, the assessment of the assisted gait conditions 356 

focuses on the model’s ability to predict changes in metabolic cost. The model 357 

successfully captures the general trends in metabolic cost changes across all participants 358 

and different spring stiffness configurations (with an average correlation coefficient of 359 

0.56). While the model often overestimates the absolute metabolic costs, its ability to 360 

correctly identify regions of higher and lower metabolic costs is acceptable for its utility 361 

in the Design Optimization Platform . 362 

Nonetheless, a significant inter-subject variability is observed in the predictions of 363

## 0.9 Design optimization 398 

The reliability of the optimal designs identified by the Design Optimization Platform is 399 

directly linked to the predictive accuracy of the underlying simulations. For participants 400 

with more accurate metabolic cost trend predictions (e.g., P.5), the platform’s results 401 

are more consistent with experimental trends. Conversely, for participants such as P.3, 402 

the optimal solutions often diverge significantly from experimentally observed optimal 403 

regions, highlighting a compromised ability to find reliable design solutions. It is 404 

important to note that the platform’s primary strength lies in identifying directions for 405 

improvement within the design space rather than in accurately predicting absolute 406 

metabolic savings. While the model tends to overestimate metabolic cost, its ability to 407 

accurately capture trends within the design space is crucial for the effective 408 

implementation of the Design Optimization Platform . 409 

For P.5, the platform performs acceptably as the design space perceived by the 410 

optimizer aligns well with the experimental data, correctly identifying a region of low 411 

metabolic cost at low kHAM and kRF stiffness values. The optimizer finds an optimal 412 

solution with a normalized metabolic cost of 0.91 after only 15 iterations. This practical 413 

and efficient optimization demonstrates the importance of having a robust underlying 414

## 0.10 Limitations 428 

A primary limitation of the Design Optimization Platform lies in its underlying 429 

predictive model. The use of a simplified two-dimensional musculoskeletal model with a 430 

limited number of degrees of freedom, a pre-defined neuromuscular controller, and a 431 

simplified representation of the device’s assistance as pure joint torques all contribute to 432 

inherent inaccuracies. These simplifications are likely responsible for the model’s 433 

inability to perfectly replicate complex gait, particularly in areas like pelvis tilt and 434 

biarticular muscle activations, as also reported in (23; 39; 40). The high variance in 435 

prediction accuracy across participants may be addressed by developing advanced 436 

neuromuscular models that benefit from larger data sets, covering a wide range of 437 

variability in participants and gait conditions. Additionally, the inner loop cost function 438 

that primarily focuses on metabolic cost, may not fully capture the multi-objective 439 

nature of human motor control including criteria such as stability and comfort. 440 

Another limitation is the model’s inability to accurately estimate the absolute 441 

magnitude of changes in metabolic cost. While the model successfully predicts the 442 

trends of metabolic cost within the design space, correctly identifying regions of higher 443 

and lower costs, it often overestimates absolute values. This is a common issue in 444 

predictive simulation studies (25; 29) and means that the specific amount of predicted 445 

metabolic savings should be interpreted with caution. The model’s strength is in 446 

guiding the selection of beneficial design zones rather than providing precise 447 

quantitative predictions. It is important to note that this limitation does not affect the 448 

quality of finding the optimal solution with the platform if the patterns of metabolic 449 

changes match those observed in experiments. 450 

# Conclusion and Outlook 451 

This paper addresses two primary aims. Firstly, we focus on validating the predictive 452 

simulations of human movement with the passive Biarticular Thigh Exosuit (BATEX) 453 

against experimental data, assessing their reliability for use within our Design 454 

Optimization Platform . Secondly, we demonstrate the platform’s utility in determining 455 

personalized optimal spring stiffness parameters for the BATEX. Our validations reveal 456 

that the predictive simulations can reliably capture crucial aspects of human-exosuit 457 

interaction. More specifically, the model demonstrates the critical ability to predict 458 

trends in metabolic cost across various device configurations. This capability is 459 

essential, as it allows the platform to identify beneficial regions within the design space 460 

even when the absolute magnitudes of metabolic benefits are not perfectly matched. 461 

Consequently, the Design Optimization Platform successfully identifies personalized 462

The key takeaway from this study is that, for efficient and reliable use of the 467

the context of metabolic cost reduction for assistive devices, effectively capturing the 470
