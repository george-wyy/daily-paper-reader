# Summary 11 

Gaze shifts are fundamental to visual exploration and processing, yet empirical knowledge is largely 12 

confined to a small number of model species, limiting our understanding of general principles of visual 13 

acquisition across animals. Here, we introduce a versatile, low -cost and open -source framework 14 

design for eye tracking in small freely -moving animals. Leveraging photogrammetry, 3D modeling, 15 

and 3D -printing, we produce headmounts adaptable to diverse head -eye geometries as we 16 

demonstrate for lizards, turtles, and mice. Detailed recordings in Pogona vitticeps, a laterally eyed 17 

ectotherm with a high -acuity retina, reveal a rich oculomotor repertoire with kinematics closely 18 

resembling the saccadic main sequence in primates. We found both movement -coupled large 19 

binocular saccades which exhibited a strong axial bias as well as small, isotropic, and monocular 20 

saccades suggesting a capacity for visual exploration of local details. Visual acquisition strategies 21 

were strongly tied to behavioral states with periods of active sampling (3 -5Hz saccades) separated 22 

by periods of ocular stillness lasting many seconds and suggesting divergent visual acquisition 23 

strategies for ectotherms. Further, active states were correlated with pupil constriction in contrast to 24 

mammals and similarly to birds. Our results demonstrate the utility of our experimental approach for 25 

understanding visual acquisition across terrestrial vertebrates. Such a multi -perspective view on 26 

natural visual acquisition across species will be essential for developing a generalized understanding 27 

of vision, its ecological functions, and its evolutionary trajectory. 28

# Introduction 1

Shifting gaze is fundamental to visual exploration and processing  [1,2] . Each gaze shift comprises 2

a decision to selectively acquire information from a specific region of the visual environment. In 3

foveated animals  [3] , this process is particularly precise and informative, offering a unique window 4

into the dynamic prioritization of goals across contexts  [4,5] . In most studies, gaze information is 5

acquired under head -restrained conditions  [6 –10] . This setting facilitates precise gaze measurements 6

in controlled conditions, yielding significant insights into visual processing  [11 –17] . However, under 7

natural conditions, visual exploration involves a dynamic coordination between eye, head and body 8

movements, which is disrupted under head fixation  [18 –20] . This discrepancy limits our 9

understanding of visual acquisition strategies. 10 

Over the last two decades, new head -mounted systems enabled eye tracking in head -free 11 

conditions for humans and primates  [21] . These technologies provided valuable insights into the 12 

integration of eye movements with broader behavioral patterns  [22] , such as describing a tight 13 

predictive coupling between gaze and gait in humans  [23] , or showing that hippocampal activity is 14 

highly tuned to 3D head orientations and eye movements in macaques  [24] . However, these systems 15 

are tailored to large heads and not readily compatible for use in smaller model species. Recently, 16 

innovative miniature video - and magnetic -based systems were developed, substantially expanding 17 

our access to eye measurements in freely moving small animals  [19,25 –34] . The first video 18 

implementation allowed oculography in freely -moving rats through bespoke camera and support 19 

modules  [25] , whereas later mouse -oriented systems further miniaturized the hardware and, in some 20 

cases, provided open -source solutions, improving accessibility  [26,27,30] . These were 21 

complemented by miniaturized magnet -sensor trackers that brought magnetic eye tracking into freely 22 

moving small animals without field -bound search coils  [28,29] . Still, these valuable systems were 23 

typically tailored for a particular species, and rely on non -flexible anchor positions, often tied to chronic 24 

electrophysiology or magnetic implants  [25 –28,30 –32,35,36] . Consequently, these did not offer an 25 

accessible route for repositioning the optical modules in species with markedly different eye -head 26 

geometries, such as broad -skulled, highly lateral -eyed lizards, or head -retracting turtles  [37] . This has 27 

constrained comparative analyses of visual acquisition across taxa, which is crucial for gaining an 28 

evolutionary understanding  [38,39] .29 

A particularly important stage in the evolution of visual acquisition is the transition to terrestriality. 30 

This transition was accompanied by a major expansion in the potential visual range. Correspondingly, 31 

vertebrates evolved larger eyes with higher acuity  [40,41] . Following the emergence of amniotes, 32 

vertebrates evolved a distinct neck and the atlas -axis bone complex which allowed flexible head 33 

movements dissociated from body movement, increased visual sampling range, and improved 34 

stabilization during self movement  [42] . By contrast, the available evidence from amphibians indicates 35 

minimal eye movements, and increased reliance on full body turns for visual orienting  [43] . However, 36 

the evolution of neural machinery for sampling complex terrestrial visual environments remains 37 

uncharted  [38,39,44] . Reptiles, the sister class to mammals within amniotes provide a critical 38 

comparative perspective on this question  [45,46] .39 

Multiple reptile species exhibit complex visual behaviors and possess steep -walled foveae and 40 

visual streaks associated with high spatial acuity  [47,48] . A limited number of studies in lizards 41 

examined visual behaviors. These focused on optokinetic reflexes and prey orientation, without 42 

recording eye -movement or performing visual sampling analyses  [49 –51] . The only quantitative eye -43

To bridge this gap, we developed a flexible system for measuring eye movements in freely moving 6

small animals. Inspired by the use of open -source components for miniaturized video acquisition of 7

the eyes  [26] , we developed an optical system and a personalization approach which allows 8

positioning the optic modules for diverse head -eye geometries in a variety of animals. Using 9

photogrammetry, we generated 3D -printed headmounts that can be adapted to specific species and 10 

subjects, ensuring a precise and stable fit while maintaining minimal interference with natural 11 

behavior. We first demonstrate the system’s versatility by acquiring eye video in the lizard  Pogona 12 

vitticeps , the turtle  Trachemys scripta elegans , and  Mus musculus . Next, focusing on  P. vitticeps , a 13 

foveated visually orienting, laterally eyed agamid lizard  [47,55] , we address key questions in visual 14 

acquisition. We ask whether agamid lizards exhibit goal -directed ocular saccades. We examine 15 

whether visual acquisition strategies in ectotherms differ from those in endotherms across long time 16 

scales. We probe the relation between pupil diameter and behavioral states. Studying these questions 17 

revealed a complex pattern of visual gaze shifts and lateralized behaviors, as well as state -dependent 18 

changes in visual acquisition and pupil diameter in  P. vitticeps .19 

# Results 20 

System design, fitting, and adaptability 21 

To monitor eye movements in freely behaving small animals, we developed a flexible, open -22 

source, low -cost, eye -tracking approach that is applicable across species (Methods, Table 1). A major 23 

challenge in implementing eye tracking systems across individuals and species is posed by the 24 

diversity in head -eye geometries. To address this, we designed personalized headmounts that are 25 

3D printed based on a 3D model of the animal’s head and eyes as scanned using photogrammetry 26 

(Fig. 1a, b). Importantly, this scan followed a surgical implantation of a single hexagonal screw anchor 27 

point for connecting the headmount. The model is used to define the desired camera -eye geometry 28 

in virtual space (via Fusion360), and produce a single -piece headmount to connect with the anchor, 29 

support cameras, and the infrared (IR) mirrors reflecting the eyes (Fig. 1b) (Methods). The headmount 30 

is 3D printed as a single piece (without the mirror holders which are printed separately with 31 

interlocking connectors). When a photogrammetry scan is not an option, the modules can be printed 32 

as individual interlocking pieces which form a modular solution kit, such that the fitting procedure can 33 

be performed directly on the animal (Fig. 1c) (Methods). To achieve sharp images under shorter 34 

working distances (1.5 –5cm) without background contamination, stock camera optics were replaced 35 

with manual focus lenses fitted with an IR -pass filter (Fig. 1d), and two IR LEDs were wired and glued 36 

to each camera module aimed at the mirrors  [26] . This provided stable illumination of the eye and 37 

synchronization verification via programmed power -off windows (Methods). The complete headmount 38 

with LEDs, wires, and cameras (2.13g each) weighed between 8 -10g depending on animal size. 39 

Before recording, headmounts were connected to the animal and counterbalanced by a pulley system 40 

holding the headmount and connecting cables. To assess the stability of the system, we quantified 41 

camera jitter (spatial cross -correlation peak shift between the first frame and other frames calculated 42 

on a stationary image segment) during free behavior. The typical measured jitter was small (0 –43 

100μm) (Fig. 1e) and could be corrected by subtracting the jitter amplitude. 44

To demonstrate the system’s versatility, we used it to successfully record eye movements in three 1

distinct model animals: bearded dragons ( Pogona vitticeps ), red -eared slider turtles ( Trachemys 2

scripta elegans ), and mice ( Mus musculus ). These species were chosen for their anatomical and 3

behavioral diversity (Fig. 1f):  P. vitticeps  lizards are typified by wide heads and lateralized eyes; turtles 4

posed a unique challenge due to their ability to retract and extend their heads into their shells; and 5

mice are small and abundant in research. To accommodate measurement in turtles, we incorporated 6

an extender between the anchor point and the headmount connector, allowing full range of head 7

movement by keeping the headmount outside the shell when the head is retracted  [56] . The longer 8

working distance between the eyes and cameras was compensated by the manual focus of the 9

modified lenses. Mice required a reduced anchor size and headmount design to account for their 10 

smaller size. To accommodate this, the hexagonal brass threaded spacer (0.5 grams) was replaced 11 

with a lighter plastic version, and the headmount design was scaled down to a total of under 5g. 12 

Eye movements repertoire in freely behaving  P. vitticeps 13 

The intricate musculature and neural circuitry controlling vertebrate eye movements enable 14 

diverse oculomotor behaviors that reflect visual acquisition strategies  [2] . Yet, limited measurements 15 

in freely moving conditions and across species hinder the derivation of general principles  [7] . To study 16 

the oculomotor behavior in  P. vitticeps  – a visually orienting agamid lizard with highly lateralized eyes 17 

– we acquired bilateral eye videos from freely exploring animals (n=5) placed in a behavioral arena 18 

(Video S1)  [57,58] . Eye videos were used to reconstruct egocentric gaze vectors (eye -in -head angles) 19 

from pupil annotations using a weak perspective model (Fig. 2a)  [59,25,60,36,35,33] , validated using 20 

a 3D Blender -based  [61]  simulation of the eye -camera system (Methods, Fig. S1). 21 

Our recordings revealed that freely behaving  P. vitticeps  express a diverse oculomotor repertoire, 22 

with movements varying in speed, conjugacy, and temporal density over the course of each session 23 

(Fig. 2b). Across animals, gaze vectors covered an average range of 35.0° ± 7.3° along the main 24 

movement axis (defined as the orientation of maximal bidirectional saccadic density) and 26.1° ± 6.4° 25 

along the perpendicular axis (Fig. S2). These spans were measured relative to each eye’s resting 26 

orientation, which varied modestly between recordings (σ main -axis =4.8°, σ perpendicular =2.9°). Within this 27 

workspace, gaze redirections often occurred as brief, discrete high -speed events, as next analyzed. 28 

Saccades are rapid eye movements that abruptly shift gaze, minimizing transit time while 29 

maximizing accuracy  [62,63] . To characterize the kinematics of saccadic eye movements in  P. 30 

vitticeps , we identified saccades using an angular speed threshold of 50°/s applied to gaze -angle 31 

traces. We detected multiple binocular configurations, including conjugate, divergent, convergent, 32 

disjunct (unequal inter -ocular dynamics), and monocular events (Fig. 2b). Saccades exhibited 33 

stereotyped speed profiles characterized by rapid acceleration to a single peak, followed by smooth 34 

deceleration back to baseline (Fig. 2c). The corresponding displacement showed a stereotyped 35 

sigmoid -shaped transition from the pre -movement baseline to a new gaze position, with most of the 36 

change occurring over a brief interval, and with a shallower tail as the movement approached its 37 

endpoint (Fig. 2d). Across animals, peak angular speed scaled systematically with saccade 38 

amplitude, forming a linear relationship across most of the measured range, with a tendency toward 39 

saturation at higher amplitudes (>20°) (Fig. 2e). 40 

In lateral -eyed rodents such as mice, eye movements during free behavior are tightly coupled 41 

with head -led gaze shifts, supporting faster transitions and providing image stabilization  [36] . In such 42 

an acquisition scheme, the two eyes rotate in temporal coordination, with no monocular saccades. 43

To support the interpretation that some saccades reflect active gaze redirection rather than purely 9

vestibulo -ocular stabilization, we asked whether they could be observed when the head is still. We 10 

recorded head kinematics using a head -mounted accelerometer and identified no -movement epochs 11 

(Methods). While most saccades occurred concomitant with head movement, a considerable fraction 12 

were detected during head -stationary periods (33.2 ± 9.4%, mean ± SEM; n = 15,613 events, 4 13 

animals). Further, monocular saccades constituted almost half of all head stationary events (48.9 ± 14 

8.8%), whereas head -led saccadic events were less frequently monocular (21.5 ± 5.5%). 15 

Correspondingly, there was a small but significant correlation between saccade amplitude and 16 

movement (Monte -Carlo shuffle (n = 10,000): Δ = 0.64° (n = 4), p = 1×10 ⁻⁴). Thus, while most gaze 17 

shifts occur during movement and play a role in gaze stabilization, a considerable fraction of eye -led 18 

monocular (and binocular) gaze shifts occur during stillness, suggesting a shift in visual attention. 19 

During free exploration, lizards frequently traversed the arena and produced repeated gaze shifts 20 

as they moved. Because directionality biases in eye movements often mirror ecological demands on 21 

visual sampling  [2] , we asked whether saccades exhibit structured directional organization. To test 22 

this, we expressed each event as a displacement vector in eye -centered coordinates and aligned all 23 

saccades to a common origin. We found that for concurrent saccades, relative endpoints clustered 24 

strongly along a single dominant axis, revealing a pronounced axial organization of gaze 25 

displacements. We note that these axes are tilted because cameras are aligned to the head -to -tail 26 

axis, while animals usually hold their heads at an inclined angle to this axis. We therefore rotated 27 

distributions to align to the main axis and revealed that this bias for concurrent saccades was 28 

consistent across animals (Fig. 2i, j). In contrast, monocular saccades exhibited a broader and more 29 

isotropic distribution of endpoints (Fig. 2h, j). In summary, we find a continuum of saccade profiles 30 

that shift between two separate modes of visual acquisition. During animal movement we find large, 31 

concurrent eye movements with a clear directional bias which can partially be explained by gaze 32 

stabilization. During stillness, when gaze stabilization is not active, we find smaller, isotropic eye 33 

movements that shift focus to small sub regions in the visual environment. 34 

Behavioral state affects ocular temporal dynamics and pupil dilation in  P. vitticeps 35 

In endotherms, spontaneous eye movements during wakefulness occur at a relatively stable rate 36 

of ~ 3 -4 Hz  [2,62] . As ectotherms, reptiles may employ a different temporal visual acquisition strategy 37 

shaped by their metabolic constraints and energy conservation demands  [64] . To investigate the 38 

temporal dynamics of visual acquisition, we monitored eye and head movements for extended time 39 

periods (2 -3 hours). We observed that eye movements shifted between a state with a limited number 40 

of eye movements (Fig. 3a) and a state with a high frequency of eye movements (Fig. 3b). To examine 41 

if these visual acquisition states coincided with animal movements, we segmented the behavioral 42 

recordings into Active states (during locomotion and head movements) and Quiet epochs (periods of 43 

stillness) using concurrent accelerometer measurements (Methods). Active states constituted 10.2% 44

The changes in behavioral state prompted us to examine the dynamics of pupil diameter. In 10 

mammals, pupil diameter is often binocularly coupled, and scales positively with arousal, providing 11 

an external index for cognitive effort and attention  [65 –68] . To discern inter -ocular pupil behavior in 12 

P. vitticeps , we computed left -vs -right pupil correlation values (Methods). Pupils showed clear positive 13 

correlations during 66.3 ± 7.9% of the time (Pearson r > 0.2) (Fig. 3c, S4). Whereas pupils were 14 

uncorrelated for  23.4 ± 3.5% of the time (−0.2 ≤ Pearson r ≤ 0.2) and anti -correlated for 10.3 ± 4.9% 15 

(Pearson r <  -0.2).  Despite this divergence and given the transitions in behavioral states (Fig. 3d -e), 16

state -dependent modulation, we compared pupil diameter distributions across behavioral states over 18 

long recording sessions (9.2 hours over 5 animals) (Fig. 3c, e). The long recording periods and 19 

uniformly illuminated arena were instrumental at averaging out illumination -specific effects. We found 20 

a significant difference (Monte Carlo shuffle, n=20000 p = 5.0e -05) between states: pupils were 21 

smaller during Active epochs and dilated during Quiet epochs (Fig. 3e). This difference was consistent 22 

over animals (Fig. 3f). Interestingly, it is inverse in directionality to the one found in mammals. 23 

# Discussion 24 

We developed a versatile and technically accessible approach for eye tracking in freely moving 25 

small animals. By combining 3D photogrammetry with personalized 3D -printed headmounts, we 26 

established a framework that is rapidly adaptable across species, as demonstrated across three 27 

species with distinct cranial morphologies (Figure 1). Focusing on  P. vitticeps , we found a rich 28 

oculomotor repertoire comprising gaze stabilizing saccades during movements with axial biases, and 29 

exploratory monocular and binocular saccades without movements (Fig. 2). Finally, we found that 30 

saccades dynamically changed throughout long recordings, exposing a bi -phasic visual acquisition 31 

strategy that was tied to behavioral state and pupil diameter fluctuations (Fig. 3). The implications of 32 

these results on our understanding of visual acquisition strategies across species are discussed 33 

below. 34 

Visual strategies across species depend on the degree of head motility, the position of eyes, and 35 

the morphology of retinas, and are reflected in saccade kinematics and oculomotor movement 36 

statistics  [69] . We found that eye movement spans are anisotropic with a clear bias to larger 37 

movements in one orientation (Fig. 2h -j). Comparable directional anisotropies have been reported in 38 

other amniotes, including primates, rodents, chameleons and birds  [29,53,70] . Such anisotropy likely 39 

reflects a combination of biomechanical constraints of the oculomotor plant  [71,72]  and the 40 

nonuniform distribution of behaviorally relevant visual targets across the sampled environments  [73] .41 

Another property with striking similarity to visually oriented amniotes is saccade kinematics  [2,62] .42 

Individual eye movements in  P. vitticeps  showed the characteristic rapid acceleration and 43 

deceleration phases that define vertebrate saccades  [38,74]  (Fig. 2c, d), and their peak speed scaled 44

Across taxa, saccades can serve different functional roles, ranging from compensatory quick 18 

phases that accompany head movements to voluntary shifts that reposition the eye toward 19 

behaviorally relevant targets  [2] . In many lateral -eyed mammals such as rodents and ungulates, 20 

saccades are reported primarily as quick phases supporting head -led gaze shifts and vestibular 21 

correction  [36,38] . Such animals rely predominantly on head rotations to redirect gaze with minor 22 

oculomotor span (~20° for mice  [36] ). This behavior fits the lack of a clear localized fovea or the 23 

existence of a visual streak  [81 –83] . By contrast,  P. vitticeps  frequently generated saccades in the 24 

absence of head movements, consistent with eye -led reorienting similarly to primates. These events 25 

likely reflect goal -directed ocular shifts, and are supported by evidence for a fovea in agamid lizards 26 

[47] . We note however, that foveated animals can perform goal -directed visual behaviors also using 27 

head movements. Avian reptiles can quickly direct gaze using their heads, with some species 28 

exhibiting only minimal eye movements (~3° for barn owls  [84,85]  and ~5° in chickadees  [86] ) while 29 

relying on head movements for both saccades and image stabilization during locomotion  [3,87,88] .30 

In contrast, the propensity for eye -only saccades in  P. vitticeps  is reminiscent of some acquisition 31 

strategies in foveated primates and head -orienting birds.  In primates, the existence of directed, time -32 

stamped saccades have been widely leveraged to study attention and decision -making  [5,89] . The 33 

presence of similar eye -led shifts in  P. vitticeps  suggests a tractable route for extending visual 34 

attention studies to reptiles, thus providing a comparative perspective that can shed light on the 35 

evolution of attention and corresponding neural processing  [90] .36 

In laterally eyed species, monocular saccades can be advantageous because portions of the 37 

visual field are accessible to only one eye, making unilateral shifts a direct and efficient means of 38 

sampling the most relevant features of each hemifield  [48,91] . However, this potential is not uniformly 39 

expressed across taxa: many lateral -eyed mammals and birds exhibit tightly yoked ocular rotations 40 

[29,36] . Against this backdrop, we observed flexible ocular coupling in  P. vitticeps  (Fig. 2a, f, g). 41 

Asymmetric and monocular saccades were common, forming a graded continuum of coupling 42 

strengths (Fig. 2f). This regime is consistent with proposals that monocular and conjugate saccades 43 

may arise from the same control architecture, with coupling strength being a tunable parameter  [91] .44 

In this view, monocular independence and binocular conjugacy represent endpoints of a shared 45 

control space, with coordination varying by context. Chameleons, for example, exhibit extreme ocular 46

The temporal structure of eye movements in  P. vitticeps  revealed a striking departure from 6

patterns described in endothermic vertebrates. Lizards often remained without ocular motion for 7

several tens of seconds, intervals far longer than the continuous high -frequency sampling typical of 8

endothermic vertebrates, including birds  [94] , rodents  [36,95] , and primates  [16,62,96,97] . In  P. 9

vitticeps , these prolonged quiet states alternated with relatively brief epochs of dense saccadic activity 10 

(active states), producing a bimodal distribution of inter -saccadic intervals (Fig. 3). This suggests that 11 

reptiles may alternate between two distinct visual acquisition modes which are presumably more 12 

energy efficient. This behavior aligns with the well -documented postural immobility in ectotherms  [98] ,13 

but the accompanying stillness of the eyes appears distinctive and indicates a state in which visual 14 

sampling demands differ markedly from those that drive active scanning  [99] . How the brain uses 15 

these quiet periods to process information remains to be studied  [100,101] .16 

In mammals, behavioral states are linked with brain states and pupil diameter across both sleep 17 

and wakefulness  [36,65 –68,95,102] . We found pupil diameter in  P. vitticeps  constricted during active, 18 

movement -rich states and dilated during quiet wakefulness. This polarity contrasts with the dilation -19 

with -arousal pattern characteristic of mammals  [66 –68] . A similar inversion has recently been 20 

documented in birds, where pupil constriction accompanies aroused wakefulness and REM -like sleep 21 

[103] . Because both reptiles and birds possess predominantly striated iris musculature  [104] , this 22 

cross -taxon correspondence suggests that rapid, behavior -linked pupil adjustments may reflect a 23 

broader sauropsid pattern rather than a feature unique to birds. Since the functional basis for pupillary 24 

state -dependence remains unclear, the existence of highly structured sleep state transitions in  P. 25 

vitticeps  [105]  could provide valuable clues for its function. 26 

Taken together, our findings highlight the value of comparative head -free eye tracking 27 

approaches. Recent technological advances provide unprecedented access to structural and 28 

functional properties of eyes and retinas on both the cellular and molecular level and across species 29 

[106 –109,69,110,111] . Our work complements these experimental advances by enabling precise 30 

quantification of ocular behavior in species that have been largely inaccessible using conventional 31 

eye -tracking techniques. Going forward, the compatibility of our method with electrophysiology and 32 

multi -camera tracking provides a practical route for extending our understanding of visual neural 33 

processing across taxa. Our results demonstrate how expanding eye -tracking to reptiles can facilitate 34 

the extraction of conserved acquisition strategies (e.g. the saccadic main sequence and axial biases 35

Eye tracking system 18 

Eye movements were recorded using a custom dual -camera IR eye -tracking system. Two miniature 19 

IR -sensitive cameras (NoIR Spy Camera for Raspberry Pi, Arducam OV5647 sensor) each controlled 20 

by an independent Raspberry Pi 4B. Cameras were aimed at the eyes via small (8x8mm) 940nm IR -21 

reflective mirrors connected to a personalized, single -piece 3D -printed headmount. The headmount 22 

was designed from a photogrammetry -derived 3D model of each animal to define precise camera -23 

eye geometry and ensure unobstructed visibility of both pupils. Mirrors were held in separately printed 24 

holders, allowing rapid replacement of this delicate component without modifying the rest of the 25 

headmount. Each camera was fitted with a QILENS 3.7mm manual -focus M7 lens to account for the 26 

short working distances (~ 1.8 -4 cm) and a custom -cut IR -pass filter (high -pass > 850nm). Illumination 27 

was provided by two 940 nm IR LEDs per camera, wired through a Cyclops LED driver for precise 28 

control and synchronization verification via predefined LED -off events (see Data Synchronization 29 

below).  To allow rapid, repeatable connection across sessions, the headmount attached to a 30 

chronically implanted hexagonal standoff (brass M3 (0.6g) for lizards and turtles, plastic M1 (0.15g) 31 

for mice), and the underside of each headmount has a matching hexagonal recess. This keyed 32 

interface constrained rotation and ensured that the headmount always returned to the same 33 

orientation relative to the eyes. Rarely, animals collided with the arena boundaries, breaking the 34 

mirror holders extending from the headmount. We therefore integrated generic holders that interlock 35 

to the headmount, restoring the predefined geometry upon replacement. 36

Video acquisition 4

Eye videos were acquired using miniature IR -sensitive board cameras (Arducam OV5647 “NoIR” 5

cm) while keeping the hardware outside the animal’s direct field of view. Camera configuration and 10

typically running at 640x480 px resolution and 60 frames -per -second. The software generated a 12 

digital frame -acquisition event for every captured frame, which was sent to the Open -Ephys system 13 

for temporal alignment across data streams. Illumination was provided by two 940 -nm IR LEDs per 14 

camera, powered by a Cyclops LED driver. The driver was programmed to briefly suspend LED power 15 

for 32ms at the end of each minute and transmitted a LED -ON event signal to an Open -Ephys 16 

acquisition board for offline synchronization between eye videos, accelerometer data and external 17 

behavioral recordings. Recording sessions (usually 3 -4 hours) were broken into 30 -minute blocks. 18

Video stability assessment 20 

Camera stability was quantified by tracking frame -to -frame shifts of a fixed region of interest (ROI) in 21 

the eye video. For each block and eye, a static ROI was manually selected on a stable feature of the 22 

image (headmount feature when available or stable mirror edges or prominent stationary scales in  P. 23 

vitticeps ). The ROI was extracted for all frames, and a normalized 2 -D cross -correlation was 24 

computed between the ROI of the current frame and that of the first frame in the sequence. The 25 

location of the correlation peak was taken as the best alignment between frames, and its 26 

displacement relative to the reference peak (frame 0) was recorded for each frame. Per -frame jitter 27 

was computed as the Euclidean distance of this displacement, which was subsequently converted to 28 

physical units using an individually calibrated pixel -to -millimeter scale derived from anatomical 29 

landmarks. These displacements were aggregated across stable periods to yield a per -camera jitter 30 

distribution. 31

Video analysis and eye angle reconstruction 8

Pupil positions were first estimated frame -by -frame using DeepLabCut, and frames with low -9

confidence detections were excluded. For each valid frame, a least -squares ellipse was fitted to the 10 

pupil boundary, yielding its center and major/minor axes. Angular eye position was then computed 11 

using a geometric method derived from Kerr et al. (2013), which infers azimuth (φ) and elevation (θ) 12 

from two measurable image quantities: the pupil -ellipse eccentricity (minor/major axis ratio) and the 13 

pupil -center displacement relative to the condition where the eye is pointing directly along the camera 14 

axis. In this framework, changes in ellipse eccentricity determine a depth -like scaling factor that 15 

relates pupil -center offsets in the image to rotations of the eye, and φ and θ are obtained by converting 16 

these normalized offsets into angular displacements using inverse -trigonometric relationships  [25] .17 

Because animals rarely look exactly at the camera, the “straight -ahead” reference position required 18 

for this computation was determined geometrically. The two IR LEDs mounted near each camera 19 

produce reliable reflections in the pupil. By annotating frames in which both reflections were visible, 20 

we identified the pupil location corresponding to the camera axis for each eye and used this as the 21 

zero -angle reference for subsequent projection. To quantify the accuracy of this reconstruction 22 

procedure under our optical geometry, we evaluated it using a Blender -generated synthetic dataset 23 

in which a 3D eye was rotated systematically through known azimuth -elevation combinations ( -60 to 24 

+60)  (Blender file available in the project repository). Applying the full ellipse -fitting and projection 25 

pipeline to the rendered images yielded angular errors that increased with eccentricity, ranging from 26 

~1 near the center to ~5 at the maximal eccentricities reached by  P. vitticeps  during behavior when 27 

taking camera tilt into account (Fig. S1). Before plotting, φ and θ traces were recentered from the 28 

camera -centric 0,0 alignment to anatomically comparable resting positions by subtracting their block -29 

wise medians. Pupil diameter (mm) was computed from the ellipse major axis using the same pixel 30 

calibration value used for camera jitter quantification. 31

Head Movement Detection and Behavioral state segmentation 33 

Where mentioned, head -movement activity was quantified from the three -axis accelerometer 34 

embedded in the Intan RHD2132 headstage, which was attached to the animal’s head via an 35 

implanted connector. Raw acceleration traces were processed using custom  MATLAB  code to 36 

identify movement events by first estimating the noise distribution from low -variance segments of the 37 

filtered signal (selected using kurtosis -based criteria) and then classifying timepoints as movement 38 

when the acceleration magnitude exceeds the noise baseline by several standard deviations. The 39 

resulting time -stamped movement events were imported into Python and used to annotate saccadic 40 

events (see below). Movement events were also converted into a continuous behavioral annotation 41 

by applying a rolling -window analysis (10 -s window, 1 -s step). For each window, the mean movement 42 

magnitude was computed, and windows were labeled as active when the mean exceeded an 43 

adaptively chosen threshold (user verified per recording) and quiet otherwise. Consecutive windows 44 

of matching labels were merged into contiguous behavioral epochs, used for all state -dependent 45 

analyses in the manuscript. 46

Eye -movement events were detected separately for each eye by computing frame -to -frame changes 1

in reconstructed eye angles (φ, θ) and identifying periods in which the instantaneous angular speed 2

exceeded a fixed threshold (50 deg/second). Consecutive suprathreshold frames were grouped into 3

candidate saccades. Because some events contained multiple direction changes, each candidate 4

event was subdivided whenever the instantaneous movement direction shifted by more than 90 deg, 5

and only segments with a minimum duration (2 frames) and a net angular displacement >0.5 deg 6

were retained. For each qualified saccade segment, onset and offset times and net angular 7

displacement were extracted. When head -movement information was available, each saccade was 8

labeled as “with head movement” if its duration overlapped any accelerometer -defined movement 9

event (as described above), and “without head movement” otherwise. Binocular classification was 10 

performed by matching left and right eye saccades based on their onset time. Pairs whose onsets 11 

differed by <34 ms were classified as synchronized binocular saccades, and all remaining events 12 

were treated as monocular. All subsequent analyses of saccade amplitude, direction, dynamics, and 13 

pupil behavior used these classified sets. For Fig. 2f, the ipsilateral peak for each event was taken 14 

from the detected saccade, while the contralateral peak is the maximal instantaneous speed within 15 

±51 ms of onset (irrespective of contralateral detection) . Because onset -based pairing can 16 

undercount binocular saccades when temporal drift occurs between eye videos within the 1 -minute 17 

verified epochs, we implemented an additional step to define a conservative set of verified monocular 18 

events. For each non -synchronized (candidate monocular) saccade detected in one eye (the 19 

“ipsilateral” eye), we examined the continuous ocular speed trace of the contralateral eye directly, 20 

irrespective of whether a contralateral saccade was detected by the segmentation algorithm. We 21 

computed the contralateral peak instantaneous angular speed within a symmetric temporal window 22 

around the ipsilateral onset (±51 ms, i.e., ±3 frames at 60 Hz). If the contralateral peak speed 23 

remained below the saccade threshold (0.8 deg/frame, ≈50 deg/s) throughout this window, the event 24 

was classified as verified. If contralateral peak speed exceeded threshold, the event was tagged as 25 

unverified. Events for which contralateral traces were unavailable in the verification window were 26

Pupil data analysis 22 

Pupil diameter (mm) was derived from the fitted ellipse parameters in each eye -tracking frame by 23 

converting the major axis from pixels using the per -eye pixel calibration described above. Frames 24 

with low DeepLabCut likelihood or unstable ellipse fits were excluded. To characterize state -25 

dependent changes in pupil size, pupil traces were aligned to behavioral annotations, and 26 

distributions were computed separately for active and quiet periods. For each animal, pupil diameters 27 

from both eyes were combined, clipped to remove extreme outliers, and z -scored relative to the 28 

animal’s pooled distribution. State -dependent effects were quantified as the difference between the 29 

active and quiet z -scored histograms (Active  - Quiet), allowing comparison across animals despite 30 

inter -individual differences in absolute pupil size. This reported, for each normalized diameter bin, 31 

whether that range is sampled more frequently during active or quiet behavior (Fig. 3f). To assess 32 

whether these differences exceeded chance, we performed a permutation test separately for each 33 

animal by randomly shuffling the active/quiet labels within each block and eye while keeping the pupil 34 

time series fixed. For each shuffled dataset, we recomputed the difference in median pupil diameter 35 

between quiet and active epochs and used the resulting null distribution to obtain a two -sided p -value 36 

for the observed median difference. Inter -eye pupil coordination was quantified from the z -scored 37
