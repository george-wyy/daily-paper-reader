## Abstract 

We present GlintMarkers , the first system to perform gaze-driven spatial perception using the inward-facing cameras on XR eyewear. Our key observation is that the cornea acts as a mirror that encodes both gaze direction and visual information about the environment in a small, low-contrast reflection. To extract spatial and semantic information from this reflection despite the camera’s limited pixel budget, we present a passive retroreflective marker design that concentrates reflected near-infrared light onto the cornea, produc-ing bright glint patterns. We develop a custom Perspective-n-Point (PnP) estimation framework adapted to corneal imaging and per-form orientation and distance estimation of tagged objects, as well as unique object identification.

## 1 Introduction 

“The eye embraces the beauty of the whole world.” - Leonardo da Vinci 

Gaze has become an important input primitive on AR headsets (e.g. Apple Vision Pro [ 3]) and XR glasses (Meta’s Orion [ 14 ]) that increasingly integrate eye-facing cameras to support gaze-driven user interfaces. Gaze-driven interactions with the physical world  

> arXiv:2604.12949v1 [cs.HC] 14 Apr 2026 UIST ’26, Nov 2–5, 2026, Detroit, MI, USA Lee et al.

have typically required the use of an additional outward facing camera to capture what the user is looking at. Here, we ask: Can we enable gaze-driven user interfaces with the physical world without an outward-facing camera? Instead, we use the eye as a mirror that compresses the wide-angle visual richness of the world into a reflection visible at the cornea. Our key observation is that images of the eye simultaneously provide dual visual capabilities: first the corneal reflection encodes spatial information that enables visual perception of the outside physical world (Fig. 1), second these same images also capture gaze, revealing user’s visual attention. Together, these two signals enable a new class of gaze-driven interactions with the physical environ-ment (Fig. 2): 

Intent-aware object control. By glancing at one light among several identical ones or a specific 3D printer in a makerspace, our system decodes the marker ID of the gazed object and routes commands to the correct target, resolving ambiguity that voice commands struggle with. 

Proximity-aware assistance. As a user approaches a gym machine, the system detects proximity, decodes its ID, and surfaces a tutorial without any explicit request. During furniture assembly, asking “Which is the right part?”, causes the system to leverage marker proximity and respond with the correct one. 

Spatial state inference. A quick glance at a cupboard reveals whether its door is open or closed based on the estimated pitch angle, and during furniture assembly the system can detect that a panel is being held at an incorrect orientation and alert the user before fastening. The key technical challenge is that the entire wide-angle scene is compressed onto the small surface of the cornea, and the inward-facing camera captures this reflection with a limited pixel budget, making it difficult to recover fine-grained visual detail. This makes conventional computer vision algorithms and object tagging de-signs for object detection and pose estimation poorly suited for corneal imaging, demanding a new approach to spatial sensing. To address these challenges, we make three key technical contribu-tions: 

Retroreflective markers for spatial sensing in the cornea. 

Conventional fiducial markers for spatial perception, such as QR codes and ArUco markers, require high visual contrast and de-liberate camera positioning making them unsuitable for reliable decoding through low-contrast corneal reflections on XR eyewear. We introduce a novel passive marker system consisting of minia-ture retroreflective patches that are designed to be decoded from corneal reflections. Retroreflective materials are unique as they are designed to concentrate the reflected optical energy back towards the source rather than spreading it in all directions, resulting in bright point reflections that manifest as sharp glints that stand out in the corneal reflections. Our markers both serve as fiducial markers to estimate orienta-tion and distance with respect to the eyewear and are able to encode a unique object ID. The markers require no power and can be used to mark objects of varying spatial scales ranging from desktop-sized devices such as coffee machines and laptops to larger appliances like refrigerators and cabinets. 

Smart glasses for dual sensing of corneal reflections and gaze. 

We build a lightweight, proof-of-concept eyewear prototype with a world-facing near-infrared LED to strobe the scene which causes retroreflective markers in the environment to be illuminated and produce glints in the corneal reflections. Our design includes an inward-facing, near-infrared camera facing the eye to measure corneal reflections and simultaneously track the user’s gaze. This design enables use even in dim indoor lighting environments. 

Real-time spatial reconstruction framework. We create a real-time geometric reconstruction framework to estimate orientation, and distance entirely from the 2D positions of glints visible in the corneal reflections using Perspective-n-Point (PnP) algorithms adapted to corneal reflections. We develop a one-time per-user cali-bration that accounts for individual differences in corneal curvature and eyewear fit. These spatial estimates, combined with gaze, aug-ment AI agents with the spatial context needed to proactively and intelligently engage with the user and their environment. 

## 2 Related work 

Corneal reflections. Corneal reflections have been studied as a source of indirect visual information about the environment. These reflections are difficult to decode, as they are low-contrast and reflect less than 1% of incident light [ 17 ], their convex geometry introduces distortions, and the texture of the iris contaminates the scene [ 25 ], all of which vary between users due to physiological differences [19]. Despite these challenges, prior works have developed techniques for a variety of visual understanding tasks using corneal reflection, including 3D scene reconstruction [ 2], super-resolution [ 18 ], lifel-ogging [ 12 ], object recognition [ 10 ], visual place recognition [ 20 ], and image registration [ 16 ]. They have also been used part of video-oculography (VOG) eye-tracking systems that estimate gaze di-rection using corneal reflections as geometric landmarks for gaze estimation [8, 27]. In contrast to these works, GlintMarkers differs in two ways. First, our work is focused on estimating two novel spatial primi-tives from corneal reflections, specifically 3-DoF orientation and distance estimation. This necessitates the design of a retroreflective marker scheme designed specifically for corneal reflections. Second, our system is designed to operate within the power and compute constraints of mobile AI glasses. 

Passive fiducial markers. Fiducial markers have been widely used for pose estimation and object identification. ARTag [ 11 ], April-Tag [ 21 , 26 ] and ArUco [ 9 ] are black-and-white 2D patterns that support 3D pose estimation and data encoding. These markers rely on high visual contrast and require a large surface area for reliable decoding at longer ranges which can be visually obtru-sive. As a result, they are challenging to decode from low-contrast, low-resolution corneal reflections imaged from power-constrained mobile cameras. Excitation-based markers have been developed to enable robust marker detection at longer ranges. InfraredTags [ 7 ], and Bright-Markers [ 6] use infrared-reflecting and fluorescent materials that are excited by an ambient illumination source, with reflected sig-nals captured by a camera. ## 3 GlintMarkers 

## 3.1 Smart glasses hardware design 

We built a proof-of-concept eyewear prototype consisting of an eye-facing camera and a near-infrared (NIR) illuminator directed toward the scene (Fig. 5). The prototype uses a 1 W, 850 nm NIR LED with a 120 ◦ viewing angle [ 13 ]. We selected 850 nm because commodity image sensors typically exhibit high sensitivity at this wavelength, while sensitivity decreases at longer NIR wavelengths. To capture corneal reflections, we mounted a 4K RGB camera [ 1 ]on the lower rim of a commodity eyeglass frame, oriented upward toward the eye. We removed the camera’s built-in IR-cut filter and replaced it with a visible-light blocking filter [ 15 ] so that the camera primarily captures NIR reflections. The camera records at 15 fps.

## 3.3 Spatial reconstruction framework 

We describe our spatial reconstruction framework in two phases (Fig. 8). First , marker detection localizes glints in the eye image, clusters them into candidate markers, and identifies which marker the user is attending to along with its ID. Second , PnP estimation recovers the marker’s 3D pose and distance from the camera. 

3.3.1 Marker detection. 

Pupil localization. We use DeepVOG [ 28 ], a lightweight convo-lutional neural network that outputs a probability map indicating which pixels belong to the pupil. We fit an ellipse to the largest connected component of this map. To handle blinks, we discard any frame where the maximum probability falls below 0.96, along with the two frames that follow (133 ms total). The pupil centroid serves two roles: it reduces the search space for subsequent glint detection, and we track it over time to infer gaze direction, which tells us which marker the user is attending to. 

Glint localization. We first run a Difference-of-Gaussians (DoG) blob detector [ 22 ] to coarsely identify up to 40 glint candidates per frame. 

Marker identification. As multiple markers can appear in a frame, we perform spatial clustering on the detected patches using the DBSCAN algorithm [ 23 ] with a distance threshold of 50 pixels to group the patches into clusters, each corresponding to one tag. We denote the marker being gazed at by the user as the one whose cluster centroid is closest to the pupil centroid. 

3.3.2 Pose Estimation and ID Decoding. Given the selected glint cluster, we must determine which marker in a known library the user is looking at and recover its orientation and distance. A natu-ral approach for grid-based markers would be to decode a binary pattern directly from the dot positions. However, this requires first establishing a precise grid alignment, which is fragile when one or two spurious glints are present in the cluster, and it ties the decoding method to a specific grid geometry. Instead, we adopt a geometry-based library matching approach: we attempt to fit a Perspective-n-Point (PnP) model to each candidate marker pat-tern in the library and accept the one with the lowest geometric error. This strategy is agnostic to the specific grid layout, natu-rally tolerates extra detections through cascading subset matching, and simultaneously yields the marker’s pose as a byproduct of the identification step. 

Glint center refinement. The DoG blob detector returns integer-pixel center estimates, but PnP accuracy is sensitive to image-point localization error. A naïve intensity-weighted centroid over a fixed window is unreliable when glints are closely spaced, because the tails of neighboring intensity profiles bleed into the window and bias the estimate. To isolate each glint, we compute a Voronoi partition seeded at all detected centers, so that each glint’s refine-ment window only includes pixels closer to it than to any neighbor. Canonical ordering. Before matching, we establish a canonical ordering of the detected glints so that they can be compared against library patterns. We identify the four corner glints via convex hull extraction. One corner, the anchor patch , is physically larger and produces a higher mean intensity (measured within a 4 px radius). We place the anchor first, the remaining corners in clockwise order, and interior dots sorted by their clockwise angular position rela-tive to the cluster centroid. Each library pattern defines its object coordinates in the same canonical order. 

PnP-Based Matching. Because all retroreflective patches lie on a single plane, we use the Infinitesimal Plane-based Pose Estima-tion (IPPE) method [ 5]. For each candidate library pattern with 𝑘 

dots, IPPE takes the 𝑘 canonically ordered image points and the corresponding object-frame coordinates, and outputs a rotation matrix, a translation vector t, and a reprojection RMSE. A match is accepted only if the RMSE falls below a threshold 𝜏 (default 2.5 px). When multiple candidates share the same dot count, we select the one with the lowest RMSE. The pose recovered from the accepted match directly identifies the marker and yields its orientation and distance. 

Reducing detection noise. The number of glints 𝑁 in a cluster may exceed the true marker’s patch count due to spurious reflec-tions. We first attempt to match library markers with exactly 𝑁 

patches using all 𝑁 detected glints. If no match falls below 𝜏 , we enumerate all subsets of size 𝑁 −1, re-apply the canonical ordering and matching for each subset, and accept the best match below 𝜏 .If this also fails, we continue to 𝑁 −2, 𝑁 −3, and so on, down to a minimum dot count. In practice, the cluster selection step already isolates the target marker’s glints, so the number of spurious detec-tions is typically small (0–2 extra dots), keeping the combinatorial cost manageable. 

Tracking markers across time. To avoid running the full de-tection and matching pipeline every frame, we enter a tracking mode after a successful identification with RMSE below 𝜏 . The glint positions are propagated to subsequent frames using Lucas-Kanade (KLT) optical flow [ 24 ]. We validate tracked points with a forward-backward consistency check: points are tracked forward then backward, and any point with a round-trip error exceeding 15 px is rejected. If all points pass, PnP is solved directly on the tracked positions without re-running detection or library matching. Tracking continues until any point is lost or the reprojection error exceeds the threshold, at which point the system falls back to full detection. 

3.3.3 From PnP to orientation and distance. A standard PnP solver recovers a full 6-DoF pose: 3-DoF rotation and a 3-DoF translation vector (𝑡 𝑥 , 𝑡 𝑦 , 𝑡 𝑧 ). In a conventional setting, the translation encodes the marker’s 3D position relative to the camera. In our setting, how-ever, the eye camera observes a virtual image of the marker formed by reflection on the convex corneal surface. The position of this virtual image depends not only on the marker’s true location but also on the eye’s position within the camera frame, which depends on each user. As a result, the individual translation components do not correspond to a stable 3D position in the scene. The norm

One-time user calibration. To account for per-user variations in eye geometry and eyewear fit, we perform a one-time calibration procedure to compute three per-user calibration parameters. The user fixates on a known marker at two or more known distances (e.g., 2 m and 3 m) with a frontal orientation for a few seconds each. Once computed, all three parameters persist across sessions for the same user and eyewear. 

Gaze Offset. The pupil center in the corneal reflection does not perfectly coincide with the true gaze direction due to individual dif-ferences in corneal geometry and glasses alignment. To correct for this, we project the marker center onto the undistorted eye image using the estimated PnP pose and compute the 2D offset (Δ𝑥, Δ𝑦 )

Pitch. The corneal surface introduces a nonlinear distortion in the reflected marker pattern that varies with gaze angle, particularly in the pitch (vertical) axis. This distortion causes a systematic bias in the estimated pitch angle that differs between upward and down-ward gaze. During calibration, we compute per-user correction constants for a dual-path pitch model determined from the known ground-truth pose at calibration. At runtime, we apply separate pitch offsets for positive and negative estimated pitch angles.

4.1.4 Discussion. These results show that GlintMarkers can esti-mate marker orientation and distance with practically useful ac-curacy. Roll, which depends primarily on the in-plane rotation of the dot pattern, is robust across all conditions ( ∼5°). Pitch, the axis most affected by the corneal convex-mirror distortion, benefits sub-stantially from calibration. Distance estimation achieves sub-20 cm MAE over a 2–4 m range after a one-time per-user calibration. The tag size versus distance trade-off provides guidance for marker design: a 36 cm tag maintains single-digit yaw error out to 4 m, while a 12 cm tag is suitable at shorter distances (2–3 m) but de-grades significantly at 4 m. The head-off condition confirms that the system remains functional when gaze and head direction are misaligned by 20 °.

4.2.1 Study design. Six distinct GlintMarkers were placed in front of the participant at 2 m distance with 40 cm gap from each other in a 2 × 3 grid. Each marker encodes a unique identity using a different retroreflective patch configuration on a shared 3 × 3 grid: three markers use 5 patches and three use 6 patches, assigned digits 1 through 6. Although only six markers were physically present, the system’s marker library contains all 32 geometrically distinct patterns achievable on the 3 × 3 grid. During identification, the cascading PnP matcher evaluates the detected glint cluster against all 32 library entries, making the classification task substantially harder than a closed six-class problem. Each experimental session consisted of 3 complete sweeps through all 6 markers, yielding 18 trials per participant. In each trial, the system announced a target digit via text-to-speech, and the par-ticipant fixated on the corresponding marker for 5 seconds while the eye camera captured frames. The trial sequence within each sweep was randomized. Only frames captured 3 seconds after digit onset (allowing time for the participant to shift gaze) were included in the analysis. A frame was classified as correct if the predicted marker ID matched the spoken ground-truth digit.

## 5 Discussion 

Curved surfaces. We tested whether GlintMarkers remain visible when attached to curved objects. Specifically, we placed markers on a curved chair back and on the side of an electric kettle. The user looked directly at the chair marker from 3 m and at the kettle marker from 2 m. As shown in Fig. 14, the marker pattern remained visible in the corneal reflection in both cases, suggesting that mod-erate surface curvature is compatible with our approach. However, stronger curvature can place individual patches at extreme angles relative to the incident NIR illumination and viewing direction, thereby reducing the amount of light retroreflected back to the eye camera. 

Dual-eye sensing. Our current system uses a single eye camera, but extending it to binocular sensing could further improve ro-bustness and accuracy. First, eyelashes often occlude part of the pupil region in one eye, which can hide a subset of glints or make them blurry; the other eye is unlikely to exhibit the same occlusion pattern, so combining both views can recover missing detections. Second, localized tear-film irregularities can blur portions of one corneal image as the eye dries between blinks, reducing glint local-ization accuracy. Third, spurious reflections from eyeglass lenses or nearby surfaces could be filtered through cross-validation: true retroreflective glints should appear in both eyes with geometrically consistent structure, whereas incidental artifacts are unlikely to do so. Fourth, the two eyes provide independent geometric mea-surements with slightly different viewing angles, which may help average out nonlinear distortions and reduce pose-estimation er-ror. Fifth, binocular sensing also yields two independent distance estimates that can be fused to improve precision. 

Laser dot projector. We investigated whether the structured-light dot projector found in commodity depth sensors (e.g., iPhone Li-DAR) could replace our dedicated NIR LED as the illumination source. In principle, the dense field of NIR dots emitted by such projectors could excite retroreflective patches without requiring a co-located LED on the eyewear. However, be-cause the projector emits a sparse, pseudo-random dot pattern rather than a uniform flood, many patches received no dot at any given instant. Even when a dot did land on a patch, the intensity difference between a dot striking a retroreflective patch and one striking the surrounding non-reflective surface was insufficient to reliably distinguish marker dots from background dots in the corneal reflection (Fig. 15). These two limitations, sparse spatial coverage and low on-patch versus off-patch contrast, suggest that structured-light projectors are not a practical substitute for flood illumination in our current design. A co-located NIR flood source remains preferable because it guarantees that every retroreflective patch is illuminated in every frame, producing the high-contrast glints that our detection pipeline relies on. 

Infrared filament. Our current markers use retroreflective patches attached to the object’s exterior surface. However, NIR-transmissive materials such as the 3D-printing filament used in InfraredTags [ 7 ]appear opaque in visible light but pass NIR radiation. Retroreflec-tive patches placed beneath such a shell would remain invisible to the naked eye yet still produce glints under NIR illumination, al-lowing manufacturers to embed GlintMarkers directly into product housings during fabrication rather than attaching them externally.
