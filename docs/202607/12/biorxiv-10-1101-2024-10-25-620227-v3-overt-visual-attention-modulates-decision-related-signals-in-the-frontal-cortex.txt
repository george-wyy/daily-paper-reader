Abstract 24 

When indicating a preference between two options, decision makers are thought to compare and 25 accumulate evidence in an attention-guided process. Little is known about this process’s neural 26 substrates or how visual attention affects the representations of accumulated evidence. We 27 conducted a simultaneous eye-tracking and fMRI experiment in which human subjects gradually 28 learned about the value of two food-lotteries. With this design we were able to extend decisions 29 over a prolonged time-course, manipulate the temporal onset of evidence, and therefore 30 dissociate sampled and accumulated evidence. We observed inconsistent correlations of both 31 sampled and accumulated evidence with activity in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), 32 the ventral striatum, and the intraparietal sulcus (IPS), and more consistent correlations of 33 accumulated evidence with activity in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC) and pre-34 supplementary motor area (pre-SMA). We also found that more gaze on an option increased its 35 choice probability and that gaze consistently amplified accumulated-value signals above and 36 beyond the non-gaze-modulated signals in the pre-SMA and partially in the dlPFC, providing 37 novel evidence that visual attention has lasting effects on decision variables and suggesting that 38 activity in the pre-SMA and dlPFC reflect gaze-weighted accumulated evidence. These results 39 shed new light on the neural mechanisms underlying gaze-driven decision processes. 40 41 42

Introduction 49 

Many decisions involve a process of value computation and comparison between options. 50 Imagine you are planning a vacation based on online travel-guides describing two locations. As 51 you research these options, you sample pieces of information that support one option or another 52 to varying degrees. For example, one destination might have cheaper flights while the other has 53 cheaper hotels. Additionally, some information may attract more of your attention, such as 54 pictures of waterfalls or ancient ruins. As you evaluate your options, you must integrate these 55 pieces of information and eventually decide when to stop and make a final decision. 56 Decisions like this are thought to rely on a bounded, evidence-accumulation process that 57 depends on factors such as the value of the sampled information and shifts in attention. 58 According to this framework, when two options are similar in value, evidence accumulates more 59 slowly towards the decision threshold, resulting in longer response times (RT) and more 60 opportunity for shifts in attention to influence the choice outcome. In contrast, when one option 61 is clearly superior, evidence accumulates more rapidly and the decision is made quickly with less 62 of a relation between gaze and choice. This choice process produces reliable, quantitative 63 patterns in choice, RT, and eye-tracking data (Ashby et al., 2016; Callaway et al., 2021; Gluth et 64 al., 2018; Krajbich et al., 2010; Smith & Krajbich, 2018). For instance, decisions with similar 65 values are more random (i.e., less predictable), tend to take more time (Konovalov & Krajbich, 66 2019), and can be experimentally manipulated by diverting attention towards one option more 67 than the other (Bhatnagar & Orquin, 2022; Gwinn et al., 2019; Pärnamets et al., 2015; Pleskac et 68 al., 2022; Tavares et al., 2017). SSMs vary in specific details, but generally share some core 73 features. When faced with a decision, people begin to sample information in favor of each 74 option. In many models, this information is evaluated and converted into relative evidence for 75 one option or the other. Relative evidence builds up over time until there is enough to commit to 76 an option (Busemeyer & Townsend, 1993; Ratcliff & Smith, 2004). 77 Most SSMs can be broken down into two key components: inputs and integrators 78 (Bogacz et al., 2006). Inputs encode the drift rate – the rate of evidence accumulation – and 79 integrators encode the decision variable – the amount of accumulated evidence. 80 Each option has its own input. In the context of value-based decisions, the input 81 represents the value of the currently considered piece of information. For a given option, the 82 average input value is generally assumed to be constant over the course of the decision but does 83 vary randomly from one instant to the next due to stochasticity in the sampling process (Shadlen 84 & Shohamy, 2016). 85 Integrators accumulate the stochastic sequences of sampled input values. Typically, each 86 option has its own integrator (Busemeyer & Townsend, 1993; Gold & Shadlen, 2007; Krajbich 87 & Rangel, 2011; Usher & McClelland, 2001; Wang, 2002) which accumulates the evidence from 88 that option’s input, but is also inhibited by the other options’ inputs or integrators. Thus, each 89 integrator represents the accumulated, relative evidence favoring a given option. The main reason is that decisions are typically very quick, with RTs shorter than the 97 time resolution of functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) (but see Gluth et al., 2012). 98 Evidence for the neural substrates of value-based SSMs have typically come as trial-level 99 measures correlating with model parameters (Hare et al., 2011; Rodriguez et al., 2015), scalp-100 level electric activity from electroencephalography (EEG) (Polanía et al., 2014), or a 101 combination of the two (Pisauro et al., 2017). Taken together, these studies have implicated a 102 fronto-parietal network underlying value-based decision-making, with more ventral/frontal 103 regions serving as inputs (for reviews see: Bartra et al., 2013; Clithero, 2018) and more 104 dorsal/parietal regions serving as integrators. 105 Despite their many strengths, these past value-based experiments have been limited by 106 their inability to determine whether purported integrator regions are accumulating evidence or 107 instead representing unchosen values (Boorman et al., 2011; Kolling et al., 2016; Wittmann et 108 al., 2016), decision conflict (Frömer et al., 2019; Kaanders et al., 2021; Shenhav et al., 2014, 109 2016; Vassena et al., 2020), or time on task (Grinband et al., 2011; Holroyd et al., 2018; 110 Mumford et al., 2024). In most experiments, these variables are highly correlated and difficult to 111 distinguish. Increasing the value of the worse option while holding the better option constant will 112 simultaneously increase the perceived conflict, increase the deliberation time, and slow the rate 113 of evidence accumulation. 114

6To distinguish between accumulated evidence and the other confounding explanations, 115 we sought a factor that modulates accumulated evidence within a decision, independent of time. 116 For this we turned to visual attention, measured with eye-tracking. Research on the attentional 117 drift diffusion model (aDDM) has argued that gaze amplifies value during the choice process 118 (Bhatnagar & Orquin, 2022; Krajbich et al., 2010; Sepulveda et al., 2020; Smith & Krajbich, 119 2019; Westbrook et al., 2020). This means that current gaze location should amplify value 120 signals in the input regions, and that the balance of gaze allocation over the course of the 121 decision should amplify accumulated evidence signals in the integrator regions. Both human and 122 non-human primate research has confirmed gaze effects on value inputs in the orbitofrontal 123 cortex (Lim et al., 2011; McGinty et al., 2016; Rich & Wallis, 2016; Hunt et al., 2018; but see 124 McGinty, 2019). However, it has yet to be shown that these gaze-modulated inputs are integrated 125 into accumulated decision values. Gaze modulated signals in purported integration regions would 126 provide critical evidence against the alternative explanations (i.e., conflict, time, or unchosen 127 value). 128 Here, we present the results of an fMRI experiment designed to provide evidence that 129 integrator regions accumulate gaze-weighted evidence. Our approach was to slow down the 130 decision process by gradually presenting choice-relevant information. Our task design allowed us 131 to extend the decision-making period to approximately a minute, while also allowing us to 132 dissociate the inputs’ sampled value (SV) signals from the integrators’ accumulated value (AV) 133 signals (Gwinn et al., 2019). The inputs represent the perceived value of stimuli currently on the 134 screen, while the integrators represent the values of previously presented stimuli within that 135 choice problem. Experiment description 146 

Our choice task builds on an extensive literature examining choices between familiar 147 snack foods. Instead of choosing between two food items, which typically only takes a few 148 seconds, we asked subjects to choose between two food lotteries. A lottery consisted of 3-6 149 different items, each with a different probability of being selected. Subjects did not know 150 anything else about the lotteries; they had to learn about them from experience (Hertwig et al., 151 2004). Specifically, subjects sampled a random draw from both lotteries every 4-8 seconds. They 152 continued to sample random draws until they were ready to stop and choose one of the two 153 lotteries (Fig. 1). Choosing a lottery led to a final random draw from that lottery, revealing the 154 actual food that the subject would receive if that trial was rewarded at the end of the experiment. 155 We placed no explicit limit on the number of draws subjects could sample within each 156 trial (i.e. pair of lotteries). To prevent subjects from spending the entire session on a single trial, 157 we gave them 45 minutes to make at least 60 choices. Subjects were informed that any unmade 158 choices would be randomly completed by the computer, and any trials beyond 60 would be 159 added to the list from which the rewarded trial would be drawn. To more directly test whether accumulated evidence signals were modulated by 354

location , and ∆SV × current gaze location .358 

We calculated accumulated dwell advantage as follows: For each sample t, accumulated 359 

dwell advantage is the cumulative difference in gaze allocation up to sample t-1, calculated as 360 

(total dwell left – total dwell right) / (total dwell left + total dwell right). This is a continuous 361 

measure from -1 (all previous gaze to right) to +1 (all previous gaze to left). 362 

We also included the interaction between accumulated dwell advantage and ∆AV (i.e., 363 

signed ac cumulated evidence) . This interaction term is positive when gaze is primarily to the left 364 

and left has more value or when gaze is primarily to the right and right has more value. This 365 

interaction term directly tests whether brain regions encoding accumulat ed evidence are 366 

modulated by the history of gaze allocation. This approach allows us to examine the interaction 367

calculation itself. 369

Discussion 478 

In this article we presented results from a simultaneous eye -tracking and fMRI study of 479 

value -based decisio n-making, using an expanded -judgment task where subjects sampled from ,480 24 

and then chose between food lotteries. Across each of our analyses, we consistently found that 481 

the pre -SMA represents accumulated value signals that are modulated by gaze . We also 482 

obse rved some evidence for gaze -weighted accumulated value signals in dlPFC, vmPFC, and the 483 

striatum, while activity in the IPS correlated only with gaze -free accumulated values. Activity 484 

correlat ing with sample value was weak and inconsistent, occurring prim arily in the vmPFC and 485 

(negatively) in the striatum , in contrast to prior findings in these regions (Lim et al., 2011; 486 

McGinty et al., 2016) .487 

These results provide novel evidence for the neural mechanisms underlying the SSM 488 

process , as exemplified by the DDM , which appears to govern many types of decisions 489 

(Busemeyer, 1985; Ratcliff, 1978) . The gaze modulation of accumulated value signals in the pre -490 

SMA and p erhaps other regions , provides critical evidence that these region s indeed represent 491 

accumulated evidence, as opposed to unchosen values (Boorman et al., 2011; Kolling et al., 492 

2016; Wittmann et al., 2016) , decision conflict (Frank et al., 2015; Frömer et a l., 2019; Kaanders 493 

et al., 2021; Shenhav et al., 2014, 2016; Vassena et al., 2020) , or time on task (Grinband et al., 494 

2011; Holroyd et al., 2018; Mumford et al., 2024) . While accumulated evidence is typically 495 

correlated with these other measures, we were a ble to dissociate them by taking advantage of the 496 

fact that accumulated evidence, but not the other measures, are modulated by gaze location. 497 

Value is amplified by gaze (Smith & Krajbich, 2019; Westbrook et al., 2020) , leading to stronger 498 

value signals in the brain when the decision maker is looking at the higher value option. 499 

Conflict -based models make qualitatively different predictions. Regions implementing 500 

conflict monitoring should show increased activity when options are similar in value, regardless 501 

of time. The conflict account predicts that BOLD activity should scale with inverse value 502 

difference: smaller |ΔV| → higher conflict → higher BOLD (Shenhav et al., 2014, 2016). In 503 25 

simple choice tasks, high conflict and high accumulated value are both associ ated with long 504 

response times (Pisauro et al., 2017) , leading to ambiguity about how to interpret purported 505 

neural correlates of accumulated value. In our task we avoid ed this ambiguity by analyzing the 506 

effect of accumulated value at each point in time, not just at the moment of decision. With this 507 

approach , conflict should be inversely correlated with accumulated value (as higher accumulated 508 

evidence indicates less similarity between options) . Moreov er, the conflict account makes no 509 

predictions about how BOLD activity should be modulated by gaze allocation for a given set of 510 

option values. 511 

A more serious concern is the potential confound with time -on -task BOLD activity. 512 

Accumulated value inevitably increases with time within a trial , leading to a correlation between 513 

the two variables (Grinband et al., 2011; Holroyd et al., 2018; Mumford et al., 2024) . This is 514 

where the gaze data were particularly important. Time -on -task regions should show no relatio n515 

with gaze allocation patterns . After accounting for non -gaze -weighted accumulated value, only 516 

accumulator regions , and not time -on -task regions , should show a relationship with gaze -517 

weighted accumulated value. The results of our analyses provide exactly such evidence : pre -518 

SMA activity , and to some extent striatal activity, was positively correlated with gaze -weighted 519 

accumulated value, even when accounting for previous gaze history and individual differences in 520 

attention discounting. 521 

Representation of unc hosen values (Boorman et al., 2011; Kolling et al., 2016; Wittmann 522 

et al., 2016) might explain some of the unexpected negative correlations that we sometimes 523 

observed between our value regressors and activity in the vmPFC, striatum, and IPS. One 524 

potential explanation for these negative results is the fact that when value difference is large, one 525 

of the two values is often very low. In other words, there is a negative correlation between |∆𝑉 |526 26 

and min {𝑉 (, 𝑉 @}. If some neurons in these regions represent the values of individual items or 527 

lotteries (Padoa -Schioppa & Assad, 2006; Rich & Wallis, 2016) , then we might expect to see 528 

some activity negatively correlated with |∆𝑉 |. This activity would align with a representation of 529 

the unchosen values. 530 

Our findings were made possible by considering the role that visual attention plays in the 531 

decision process. While SSMs capture choice behavior and RTs extremely well, most do not 532 

consider the effects of attention. Attention is thought to shift over the course of the decision, 533 

amplifying the attended inputs and/or inhibiting the non -attended inputs (Diederich, 1997; 534 

Johnson & Busemeyer, 2005; Roe et al., 2001) . These shifts in attention are reflected in eye -535 

movements (Hoffman & Subramaniam, 1995) , which affect choice outcomes (Fiedler et al., 536 

2013; Fiedler & Glöckner, 2015; Folke et al., 2016; Glaholt & Reingold, 2009; Gwinn et al., 537 

2019; Janiszewski et al., 2013; Jiang et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2012; Konovalov & Krajbich, 2016; 538 

Lopez -Persem et al., 2016; Orquin & Mueller Loose, 2013; Pärnamets et al., 2015; Polonio et al., 539 

2015; Russo & Leclerc, 1994; Sheng et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2012; Smith & Krajbich, 2018; 540 

Stewart et al., 2016; Tavares et al., 2017; Towal et al., 2013; Vaidya & Fellows, 2015; Vanunu et 541 

al. , 2021; Venkatraman et al., 2014; J. T. Wang et al., 2010) , and their effect on the choice 542 

process is captured by the attentional drift diffusion model (aDDM) and other related SSMs 543 

(Ashby et al., 2016; Fisher, 2021; Glickman et al., 2019; Jang et al., 202 1; Krajbich et al., 2010; 544 

Li & Ma, 2021; Smith & Krajbich, 2019; Teoh et al., 2020; Westbrook et al., 2020; Yang & 545 

Krajbich, 2023; Zilker & Pachur, 2023) .546 

Neural implementations of SSMs generally require at least two sets of neurons, one set to 547 

represent t he current information from the stimuli and a second set to integrate that information 548 

over time. In the current study, the information being used to make decisions was subjective 549 27 

value (i.e. , utility ). A large body of work has implicated the vmPFC and st riatum in representing 550 

value (Bartra et al., 2013; Boorman et al., 2009; Chib et al., 2009; Levy & Glimcher, 2011; 551 

Schoenbaum et al., 1998) . Our results partially confirm that the vmPFC represent s value 552 

information . The integrated value information, which is what ultimately determines the decision, 553 

is primarily encoded in the pre -SMA , though the vmPFC, striatum, and dlPFC may also be 554 

involved. The results in the vmPFC are consistent with work by some researchers, arguing that in 555 

addition to representing value, the vmPFC also represents the decision variable (Padoa -Schioppa 556 

& Assad, 2006; Strait et al., 2014) . The results in the dlPFC are also consistent with research in 557 

perceptual and value -based decision making, where r esearch has argu ed that the dlPFC is 558 

involved in accumulation and comparison processes (Busemeyer et al., 2019; Gluth et al., 2012; 559 

Heekeren et al., 2006; Hutcherson & Tusche, 2022) . Future work will need to further 560 

disentangle the role that these regions play in the choice process. 561 

By using eye -tracking, our study extends previous work connecting computational 562 

models to fMRI data. Our results partially align with Hare et al. ’s (2011) proposed neural model 563 

in which the vmPFC provides inputs to the pre -SM A and IPS , with the pre -SMA also 564 

exchanging information with the dlPFC . While the Hare model was based on dynamic causal 565 

modeling results, our task provides a more direct test of the proposed neural network .566 

Additionally, o ur eye -tracking data allow s us to identify additional features of the network. We567 

find that the pre -SMA and dlPFC are sensitive to gaze -weighted accumulated value , while the 568 

IPS is not . The reason for this distinction between the frontal regions and IPS is unclear, but it 569 

does sugge st that the pre -SMA is more likely to be the final decision -making region, consistent 570 

with some recent studies (Juechems et al., 2017; Pisauro et al., 2017; Rodriguez et al., 2015; 571 

Rouault et al., 2019 ; Aquino et al., 2023 ). Of course, the recruitment of t he pre -SMA may be 572 28 

because our subjects made their decisions with a button press , which is supported by the 573 

correlations between accumulated value and motor cortex activity . Had our study required eye -574 

movements to indicate a cho ice, we may very well have ob served integrator activity in other 575 

regions such as the frontal eye fields or posterior parietal cortex (O’Connell et al., 2018) .576 

However, the activity in dlPFC may be independent of response modality, consistent with 577 

findings in perceptual decision making (Heekeren et al., 2006) .578 

A major advantage of this study is its use of a task designed to slow down the decision 579 

process and force sequential integra tion of information. Such expanded judgment tasks have 580 

been used to study SSM assumptions in perceptual decision -making, more recently in 581 

combination with neural recordings , but mostly with electrophysiology in rats and monkeys 582 

(Brunton et al., 2013; Cisek et al., 2009; Tsetsos et al., 2012; T. Yang & Shadlen, 2007) .583 

On the other hand, one concern with longer decision times is that decision -makers might 584 

either under -weight (i.e., forget), or put too much weight on, early information. Our analysis of 585 

subjec ts’ temporal weighting functions did reveal a primacy effect, where the first sample in 586 

each trial was overweighted, as well as a recency effect, where the last samples in each trial were 587 

also overweighted. Nonetheless, u sing a temporal weighting function with these primacy and 588 

recency effects did not substantially change the conclusions from our fMRI analysis . While these 589 

analyses did reveal weak support for accumulator dynamics in the vmPFC , these results should 590 

be interpreted with caution because adding recency effects into the accumulated -value signal 591 

increased the correlation between | ∆SV | and | ∆AV | from 0.12 to 0.21. Moreover, the recency 592 

effect is surely overestimated due to well -known statistical artifact s (Mullett & Stewart, 2016) .593 

In short, because decision -maker s tend to terminate the decision process after strong pieces of 594 

evidence, and because a strong piece of evidence will tend to have a large noise component, 595 29 

information that appears at the end of a trial will appear to have a stronger influence on choice s596 

than it should. 597 

Another potential limitation of our study was the number of participants. While our 598 

sam ple size of 2 0 subjects is modest by current neuroimaging standards, the within -subject 599 

statistical power from our extended decision paradigm (~380 observations per subject), 600 

combined with hypothesis -driven ROI analyses and multiple comparisons correction, provides 601 

confidence in our core findings. Nevertheless, replication with larger samples would be valuable, 602 

particularly for more fully characterizing null effects and marginal findings. 603 

Taken together, we separate d sampled input values from the overall d ecision value, or 604 

accumulated value, and found a network of brain regions that are involved in an aDDM -like 605 

choice process. This process involves passing sampled input values to an integrator which 606 

responds to not only the values themselves, but also to th e gaze -modulated values. These results 607 

indicate that gaze effects on value representations are not epiphenomenal ; rather , they reflect 608 

how gaze is incorporated into the decision process, affecting how we perceive the value of each 609 

option over the course of the entire decision. 610

Temporal -weighting -function model fits 683 

We estimated model parameters using an iterative maximum a posterior i (MAP) 684 

approach (Huys et al., 2011; Wittmann et al., 2020) . This method improves upon maximum 685 

likelihood estimation (MLE) by simultaneously estimating parameters at both the subject - and 686 

group -level. This hierarchical procedure constrains subject -level parameters and reduc es the 687 

influence of outlier data. 688 

Group -level parameters were initialized with uninformativ e Gaussian prior s with mean of 689 

0.1 and variance of 100. For all models, 𝜂 was held constant at 1. During the expectation step, 690 

we estimated model parameters ( 𝜀 3, 𝜀 5, 𝛽 ) for each participant using MLE and calculat ed the log -691 

likelihood of their choices given the model parameters. During the maximization step, we 692 

calculated the maximum posterior probability based on the observed choices and prior group -693 34 

level parameters, and then updated the group -level parameters to generate posterior parameter 694 

estimates. These posterior parameter estimates were then used as the priors in subsequent steps 695 

in this procedure. We iteratively repeated the expectation and maximization steps until 696 

convergence of the posterior likelihood summed over group -level parameters exceeded a change 697 

of le ss than 0.0001 from the previous iteration (for a maximum of 800 iterations). During this 698 

procedure, bounded free parameters were transformed from Gaussian space to native model 699 

space using link functions (e.g., sigmoidal function for 𝜀 3, 𝜀 5) to ensure accurate estimation near 700 

the bounds. 701 

We assessed parameter recovery of this model on simulated choices using best -fitting 702 

model parameters. We then refit the simulated choices using the same MAP process described 703 

above. We found strong Pearson’s r correla tions between the generated and estimated parameter 704 

values ( r > .8; Fig. 8), though both parameters were systematically over -estimated. 705
