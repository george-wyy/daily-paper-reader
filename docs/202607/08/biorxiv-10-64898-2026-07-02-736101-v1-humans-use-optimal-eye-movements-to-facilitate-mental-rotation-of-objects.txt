Abstract 1

The ability to mentally rotate objects is a fundamental feature of human cognition, and 2

humans can use this ability to make choices about objects based on their geometry. However, 3

remarkably little is known about how such choices are reached, and what sort of visual information 4

might facilitate them. We devised an experiment where participants had to mentally simulate an 5

object’s rotation to choose which of two objects was better for a subsequent task, based on its shape 6

alone. We also tracked their gaze while they made their choice, to see which visual information 7

they were using to facilitate this mental simulation. We found that participants were consistently 8

able to choose the most suitable object for the task, and, remarkably, the visual information they 9

sampled was directly linked to their choices. Put simply, participants made better choices when 10 

they looked at more informative regions of the objects, and participants who sampled regions that 11 

were better for facilitating mental simulation made better choices overall. These findings reveal a 12 

direct link between fixations, simulation, and decision-making, suggesting that to perform any fine-13 

grained mental simulation people need to direct their gaze at specific, informative points of an 14 

object to simulate its two-dimensional proximal image displacement. 15

Main text 18 

Humans have a remarkable ability to mentally rotate objects in their mind 1,2 , and can use 19 

this ability to make choices about objects – whether a young child deciding which shape to slot 20 

through a hole, or an adult playing “mental Tetris” to decide how to pack possessions into a small 21 

car when moving house. Such decisions require a careful mental simulation of the visual 22 

transformations each object would undergo when rotated, and then a choice of which object might 23 

be best to fulfill the current task. Despite making such seemingly trivial choices daily, it is unclear 24 

what sort of visual information is required to facilitate a dynamic mental simulation of a static 25 

physical object. Humans use eye movements to gather fine-grained visual information to make 26 

choices and decisions 3–6 , but it is unknown whether they direct their gaze to specific parts of an 27 

object to simulate its visual transformation. 28 

We hypothesised that when faced with a task that requires simulating a static object’s 29 

rotation to make a decision – such as deciding which of two objects have a more suitable geometry 30 

for a task – participants would simulate the fine-grained visual displacement that would occur if 31 

the objects were rotated 7. Moreover, to facilitate this mental simulation they should look at the 32 

areas of the objects that would provide the most information about this visual displacement 4,8 . We 33 

devised a task where participants were presented with the most, and least discriminable viewpoints 34 

of a single object – that is, the viewpoints that would change appearance the most or least if the 35 

object were to be rotated slightly (Figure 1A, left). They then had to choose which viewpoint they 36 

would prefer to use in a subsequent discrimination task (Figure 1A, right). Crucially this 37 

discrimination task was easier if they chose the most discriminable viewpoint rather than the least 38 

discriminable viewpoint. We predicted that to determine which viewpoint would be easier for the 39 

discrimination phase of the experiment, participants would have to simulate how much 40 

displacement would occur if each viewpoint rotated. We measured eye movements to explore 41 

whether they preferentially sampled the areas on the object that would undergo the largest visual 42 

displacement. This allowed us to test for a link between participants’ choices, fixations, and the 43 

quality of the information gathered for the mental simulation. 44 

Participants were presented with two viewpoints of a block-sequence object: the most and 45 

least discriminable viewpoints for that object, determined using a simple optical-flow model that 46 

measures the 2D displacement that would occur as an object rotates from one viewpoint to the 47 

next 7. Participants were allowed to free-view these objects for an unlimited time, and were 48 

instructed to choose which viewpoint they wanted to use for a subsequent discrimination task. In 49 

this discrimination task, the chosen viewpoint was displayed, along with a ‘discrimination 50 

viewpoint’, which was the chosen viewpoint rotated in 3D by 2° either clockwise or anticlockwise 51 

around the vertical axis. Participants had to indicate the direction of the rotation. Crucially, this 52 

discrimination task was very difficult if the hard viewpoint was chosen, and easier if the easy 53 

viewpoint was chosen. 54 

We conducted two versions of this experiment: 1) a lab-based study to measure 55 

participants’ eye movements during the task, and 2) an online study to assess generalisability to a 56 

larger group of participants. First we wanted to see whether participants were able to decide which 57 

was the better object and choose it for the discrimination task. Across both experiments, 58 

performance was on average better when participants performed the discrimination task on the easy 59 

compared to the hard viewpoint (Figure 1B, right, Wilcoxon Z = 1699, p <0.0001; BF 10 = 2634, 60 

and so participants who tended to choose the easier viewpoint were more likely to be correct in the 61 

discrimination task (r = 0.57, t(61) = 5.37, p <0.0001). Participants’ decisions also reflected this 62 

performance benefit, as they were more likely to choose the easier viewpoint: 42/64 participants 63

We next wanted to investigate whether participants sampled information from regions that 66 

were the most informative about an object’s displacement to facilitate the mental simulation of 67 

object rotation. We quantified informative regions using an optical flow model, which can provide 68 

a spatial map of which regions of an object would undergo the most displacement if the object 69 

rotated. For each fixation, we calculated the maximum amount of optical flow information that fell 70 

within a 1-degree radius window around the fixation location (Figure 1C, see Supp Materials for 71 

details). We compared actual information uptake with randomly shuffled fixation and object pairs 72 

from 1000 bootstrapped samples, and used an ROC analysis to determine whether participants 73 

sampled more informative regions than would be expected by chance (AUC > 0.5). If participants 74 

require fine-grained information about specific regions of the object to perform a mental simulation 75 

in order to make a choice, we expect that participants fixate more informative regions of the object. 76 

Overall, participants sampled more informative regions than predicted by chance (AUC for 77 

information gathered across the whole trial was >0.5 for all participants). They also sampled more 78 

highly informative areas of chosen objects than they did in non-chosen objects (t(19) = -6.87, 79 

p<0.0001), suggesting a link between information gathering and choices. Participants also gathered 80 

better information in trials where they chose the easy object (Figure 1C, bottom middle; t(19) = -81 

6.87, p<0.0001), and individual participants who were better at the task overall (who had a higher 82 

proportion of trials where they chose the easy object) gathered better information from the easy 83 

objects than people who were not so good at the task (Figure 1C, bottom right; in a simple linear 84 

model there was a main effect of task difficulty: F(1,36) = 16.78, p<0.00023, and an interaction 85 

between object difficulty and proportion of trials in which the participant chose the easy object: 86 

F(1,36) = 16.96, p = 0.00021). 87 

Our results suggest that people can make decisions about which object is best for a given 88 

task based on a mental simulation of the object’s 2D transformation. To make this decision, they 89 

fixate on object regions where a rotation would lead to particularly large shifts in the image. 90 

Participants whose fixation strategies target these more informative regions tend to perform better 91 

at this task. This provides evidence that humans use eye movements either to gather information in 92 

preparation for mental simulation, or while evaluating the outcomes of the simulation (or both), 93 

and that these processes directly affect their choices. This speaks to an emerging body of evidence 94 

that humans may use eye movements to simulate an object’s motion 9,10 or predict future changes 95 

in a scene 11 , but goes beyond this to show that visual information aids the simulation of a change, 96 

such as a rotation, that a static object might undergo. It also shows a direct link between fixation, 97 

simulation, and decision-making, suggesting that to perform this fine-grained mental simulation 98 

people need to direct their gaze at specific, informative points of the object to simulate their two-99 

dimensional proximal image displacement, rather than imagining the gist of the object’s movement 100 

as a whole 7,12 . In planning sequences of saccades to gather information for simulation, this suggests 101 

a local rather than global information maximisation approach 4,8 . Most importantly however, it 102 

shows that our mental models and internal representations and simulations are driven by the 103 

proximal information that is gathered using carefully targeted eye movements. 104
