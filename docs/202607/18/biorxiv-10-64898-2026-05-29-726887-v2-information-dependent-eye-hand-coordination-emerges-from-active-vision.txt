Abstract In daily activities, humans rely on visual information to plan hand movements, 9

making the extraction of task-relevant information through eye gaze a key aspect of motor 10 

control. Behavioral studies have revealed characteristic saccade-pursuit patterns, likely governed 11 

by shared neural circuits, which enable an efficient reduction of task-related uncertainty. 12 

However, a unifying computational principle explaining the emergence of these patterns in 13 

continuous tasks such as reading or driving is still lacking. Here we propose a dual stochastic 14 

model predictive control formulation of active vision, in which eye movements are continuously 15 

controlled to minimize task-relevant uncertainty and build an internal model used for hand 16 

movement planning. Through experiments manipulating the amount, density, and difficulty of 17 

future visual information, we show how eye movement patterns adapt to the information context 18 

while maintaining an invariant extraction horizon. A saccade-pursuit pattern naturally emerges 19 

from the model, which accurately predicts both eye and hand movement features observed in 20 

experiments. These results provide a principled framework for understanding the continuous 21 

regulation of human eye movements and open new perspectives for applications in robotic 22 

assistance and active perception. 23 24 

## Introduction 25 

Daily activities such as assembling a device, catching a fly, or driving in traffic rely on seamless coor-26 

dination between our eyes, which gather information, and our hands, which execute the task. The 27 

principles underlying hand movements control have been extensively studied Milner (1992 ); Uno 28 

et al. (1989 ); Harris and Wolpert (1998 ); Franklin et al. (2007 ); Wang et al. (2016 ); Berret et al. (2024 ), 29 

providing strong evidence that motion is planned over a future horizon to counteract disturbances 30 

and uncertainty. In contrast, our understanding of the principles governing eye movement con-31 

trol, and its coordination with hand movements, remains limited. Behavioral studies suggest that 32 

gaze is directed to support decision-making and motor planning. For instance, during interception, 33 

gaze anticipates the predicted bounce point Land and McLeod (2000 ); Arthur et al. (2024 ); Trevi 34 

and Márquez (2025 ), and when viewing tools, it is preferably drawn to its functional regions (e.g., 35 

the hammer head) rather than to manipulative parts (e.g., the handle) Belardinelli et al. (2016 ); 36 

Pilacinski et al. (2021 ). In continuous tracking tasks, efficient information gathering is essential for 37 

planning over a future horizon Bashford et al. (2022 ), as illustrated by experienced drivers who 38 

consistently look farther ahead of their vehicle than novices ( Figure 1 A) Xu et al. (2022 ). In the 39 

present paper, we propose that such behaviors emerge from an active vision principle, whereby 40

This principle must consider the characteristics of the eyes as sensors. In humans (as well as 43 

haplorhine primates, birds of prey, and some reptiles and fish), visual acuity peaks at the fovea 44 

and declines towards the periphery Curcio et al. (1987 ); Stewart et al. (2020 ). Although the eyes 45 

have low inertia Robinson (1964 ), their precise control by three antagonist muscle pairs entails non-46 

negligible effort and is often coordinated with head and body movements Guitton and Volle (1987 ). 47 

Together with neurophysiological constraints Schütz and Stewart (2025 ), this leads to characteristic 48 

patterns of saccades and pursuit (or fixation) Robinson (1964 ); Ko et al. (2010 ); Danion and Flana-49 

gan (2018 ); Mathew et al. (2019 ). A computational account of active vision should naturally give 50 

rise to these patterns. 51 

Existing models typically treat saccadic and pursuit movements separately, often relying on pre-52 

defined targets or velocity profiles. Approaches include linear feedback control Robinson (1973 ); 53 

Krauzlis and Lisberger (1989 ), minimum variance Harris and Wolpert (1998 ), optimal control Zhou 54 

et al. (2018 ), and stochastic optimal control Varsha et al. (2021 ); Vasudevan et al. (2023 ). In parallel, 55 

probabilistic models of visual attention emphasize goal dependence Ballard and Hayhoe (2009 ), 56 

prior knowledge Henderson (2017 ), context Eckstein (2017 ), and visual saliency Walter and Bex 57 

(2022 ), predicting sequences of fixation targets rather than modeling eye movements. These ap-58 

proaches include dynamics Bayesian networks Ballard and Hayhoe (2009 ); Hayhoe and Ballard 59 

(2014 ), Markov decision processes Ma et al. (2022 ); Chen et al. (2021 ), stochastic optimal con-60 

trol Vasilyev (2019 ),information gain maximization Renninger et al. (2005 ); Najemnik and Geisler 61 

(2005 ); Paeye et al. (2016 ); Hoppe and Rothkopf (2019 ), and intrinsic reward formulations Xu-Wilson 62 

et al. (2009 ); Rothkirch et al. (2013 ). As a result, they typically capture either saccadic behavior or 63 

discrete fixation sequences. 64 

Attempts to jointly model saccades and pursuit often rely on switching mechanisms based on 65 

eye-target error De Brouwer et al. (2002 ) or its variability Coutinho et al. (2021 ), effectively treat-66 

ing them as separate processes. However, neuroscience evidence indicates that the underlying 67 

premotor pathways and oculomotor subsystems are largely shared Liston and Krauzlis (2003 ); 68 

de Xivry and Lefèvre (2007 ); Goettker and Gegenfurtner (2021 ). A unified stochastic optimal feed-69 

back controller has been proposed to account for saccade–pursuit coordination Crevecoeur and 70 

Kording (2017 ), but it primarily captures motor aspects and does not address the key role of the 71 

eyes as information-gathering sensors. Moreover, humans can adust motion plan online in re-72 

sponse to changing visual input Van Gisbergen et al. (1987 ); Doré et al. (2025 ), suggesting prospec-73 

tive, horizon-based control rather than purely reactive strategies. 74 

Efficient eye–hand coordination is not innate but develops through sensorimotor experience, 75 

as the relationships between visual input, body state, and motor output are learned during in-76 

fancy and refined through childhood von Hofsten (1989 ); Corbetta and Snapp-Childs (2009 ). Here 77 

we propose a model of coordinated eye and hand movement in adults based on an active vision 78 

principle. The model generates saccade-pursuit patterns from a single continuous optimization 79 

process, incorporating key neurophysiological constraints and an internal task representation to 80 

enable efficient information acquisition and action. 81 

To evaluate the model, we recorded eye and hand movements in 17 participants performing a 82 

trajectory-tracking task in which future target motion was visible over a finite horizon, allowing par-83 

ticipants to extract visual information about future desired hand positions. Previous work with a 84 

similar task has shown that people plan in a MPC-like fashion Hafs et al. (2026 ), and that their move-85 

ment variability decreases and planning horizon increases over practice Bashford et al. (2018 ). We 86 

systematically manipulated three properties of available information: (i) temporal horizon (how 87 

far into the future information is available for motion planning), (ii) spatial density (how much in-88 

formation is contained per unit space), and (iii) the effort required for accurate tracking, to assess 89 

their influence on gaze strategies and performance. 90

Active vision for eye-hand coordination 92 

We developed an active eye-hand control strategy according to which eye movements are viewed 93 

as selecting gaze positions to acquire task-relevant information through minimizing task-relevant 94 

uncertainty. We modeled the continuous coordination of eye and hand movements as a dual 95

## Discussion 223 

In many hand–eye coordination tasks—such as hitting a ball Land and McLeod (2000 ) or assem-224 

bling a device Capponi et al. (2024 )—humans actively direct gaze toward task-relevant features to 225 

acquire information necessary for accurate control. Through a computational implementation of 226 

this active vision principle, our model explains how humans control eye movements to reduce task-227 

relevant uncertainty, and use the resulting memorized information to guide hand movements. We 228 

assume that the CNS coordinates gaze to acquire informative observations while controlling the 229 

body to accomplish the task, balancing performance with the oculomotor effort for information 230 

gathering. Visual sensitivity is modeled through eccentricity-dependent noise, reflecting reduced 231 

acuity away from the fovea. Combined with internal models of the motor plant and environment, 232 

this allows prediction of future states and sensory inputs, enabling the hand to act on predicted 233 

targets despite sensory delays. 234 

A key result of our simulations is that the active vision principle naturally generates saccade – 235 

pursuit behavior. While such patterns are well documented in tracking tasks Danion and Flanagan 236 

(2018 ); Coudiere and Danion (2024 ), previous models typically relied on separate controllers for 237 

saccades and pursuit Schütz and Stewart (2025 ); Souto and Kerzel (2021 ). Here, no explicit switch-238 

ing mechanism was imposed; instead, both behaviors emerge from a single objective. Intuitively, 239 

this arises from a trade-off between integrating visual information over time and maintaining the 240 

target within a high-acuity region. Smooth pursuit allows temporal integration of sensory informa-241 

tion when retinal motion is small, improving state estimation. In general, as a target drifts away 242 

from the fovea due to motion and delays, uncertainty increases because of eccentricity-dependent 243 

noise, making corrective saccades necessary to re-center the gaze. This interplay between infor-244 

mation accumulation and degradation provides a mechanistic explanation for the emergence of 245 

saccade–pursuit alternation. 246 

At the sensory and motor levels, we deliberately used simplified models to limit free parameters. 247 

For the eye, we assumed a spherical oculomotor plant with noiseless motor commands and a 248 

foveated noise profile, neglecting known asymmetries between vertical and horizontal movements 249 

Rottach et al. (1996 ). For the hand, wrist dynamics were approximated by a third-order linear 250 

model Gaveau et al. (2021 ), rather than a full neuromusculoskeletal representation Quesada et al. 251 

(2026 ). Consequently, the model does not capture impedance modulation mechanisms Burdet 252 

et al. (2013 ), which are known to respond to uncertainty and disturbances Franklin et al. (2008 ); 253 

Berret et al. (2024 ). However, given the absence of external perturbations and the use of constant 254 

additive motor noise, these simplifications were sufficient for capturing the dominant behaviors of 255 

interest—namely, eye movement strategies and their interaction with hand control across a broad 256 

range of information conditions. 257 

Our model accurately predicted eye and hand movement patterns under systematic variations 258 

of the time horizon of available information, its spatial density, and the difficulty of information 259 

acquisition. Participants consistently maintained gaze approximately 200 ms ahead of the hand, 260 

consistent with compensation for sensory delays. This temporal lead was robust, producing pre-261 

dictable shifts in gaze position with scrolling velocity, without degrading motor performance. When 262 

less than 400 ms of future information was available, gaze patterns became constrained and shifted 263 

towards the hand, leading to a marked deterioration in performance. Overall, the model repro-264 

duced (i) the main trends across nine experimental conditions (36 predictions) for eye movement 265 

metrics, in particular the remarkable stability of the time horizon, and (ii) captured the evolution of 266

Interestingly, both model and participants exhibited a “filtering effect.” With increasing informa-274 

tion density, gaze did not strictly track high-frequency trajectory components (peaks), but instead 275

information gathering difficulty did not produce this filtering. Instead, gaze amplitude matched 279 

trajectory peaks, but with increased minimum distance, reflecting degraded estimation accuracy 280 

rather than selective attention. 281 

Beyond gaze control, this paper introduced a principled formulation of hand movement based 282 

on performance optimization and energetic cost, in line with optimal feedback control models 283 

Todorov (2005 ); Česonis and Franklin (2022 ); Guigon (2023 ); De Comite et al. (2023 ). However, in 284 

contrast to standard formulations, hand planning relies on predicted future task states, yielding 285 

a feedforward component embedded within a stochastic, receding-horizon (i.e., MPC) framework. 286 

This unified formulation captures the predictive and adaptive nature of human movement, allow-287 

ing replanning as new observations become available Takagi et al. (2025 ). 288 

Our formulation is conceptually related to active inference Jacques (2023 ), where behavior min-289 

imizes prediction error based on hierarchical neural structures Rao and Ballard (1999 ); Friston 290 

(2018 ). However, rather than relying on variational free energy minimization, we adopted an MPC 291 

framework, which provides a direct and flexible formulation for motion planning and naturally 292 

accommodates reward or vigor modulation Ahmed and A. (2020 ). 293 

The proposed active vision framework is general and can be extended to a wide range of sen-294 

sorimotor tasks. More broadly, it could be applied beyond vision to other forms of active sensing, 295 

such as exploratory touch Lederman and Klatzky (1993 ); Ryan et al. (2021 ), head movements for 296 

auditory localization Gessa et al. (2022 ); Carlini et al. (2024 ), or whole-body actions to estimate 297 

object properties and external forces Maurer et al. (2006 ). These behaviors share a common prin-298 

ciple: organisms actively move their sensors to optimize information acquisition for task execution. 299 

Extending the present framework to such modalities may help uncover unified computational prin-300 

ciples governing the coordination of perception and action. 301 

Finally, the framework offers promising technical applications. First, it could be used to predict 302 

human intention from gaze patterns, as it generates realistic eye movements grounded in opti-303 

mal control principles. This could support various human–robot interaction applications, such as 304 

shared driving or robot-assisted surgery. Second, the underlying principle of active information 305 

acquisition could be transferred to artificial systems with simple adaptations of the sensorimotor 306 

parameters. Robots, drones, and autonomous agents often need to actively explore environments 307 

to gather task-relevant information Amigoni and Caglioti (2010 ); Ashour et al. (2020 ). Similarly, 308 

in haptics, robotic manipulators equipped with dense tactile sensors could exploit active sensing 309 

strategies to efficiently infer object properties Zito et al. (2013 ); Seminara et al. (2019 ); Dutta et al. 310 

(2026 ). In both cases, the integration of perception and action through principled information-311 

seeking control may significantly enhance performance and robustness. 312

## Methods 313 

Experiment 314 

The trajectory tracking experiment protocol was approved by the Science, Engineering & Technol-315 

ogy Research Ethics Committee of Imperial College London, UK (SETREC No. 21IC6578). 17 partic-316 

ipants, aged 23-42 years old (9 males, 8 females) without known sensorimotor impairment were 317 

informed about the experiment and signed a consent form prior to starting it. Their anthropomor-318 

phic data were: height 170 .3 ± 10 .7 cm, weight 66 .8 ± 29 .2 kg and hand length 17 .8 ± 5 .2 cm. Each 319 

participant sat in front of a monitor ( Figure 2 A), with their right arm attached to a wrist robotic 320 

interface. 321 

The HRX robotic interface (Human RobotiX, UK) was used to record wrist flexion/extension move-322 

ments. The wrist joint rotation was measured with a resolution of 3.5⋅10 −6 rad and affinely mapped 323 

to the horizontal movement of a cursor on the screen (motion capped at 30 ◦), calibrated around a 324 

comfortable neutral hand posture. Data were recorded at 120 Hz. 325 

Muscle electromyography (EMG) from the flexor carpi radialis and extensor carpi radialis longus 326 

was recorded at 2 kHz using the Wavex MiniX system (Cometa Systems). For maximum voluntary 327 

contraction (MVC) recording, the wrist interface was locked in a neutral position and participants 328 

were instructed to flex or extend with their maximal force for 5 s. The raw EMG signals were: (i) 329 

band-pass filtered between 50 Hz and 400 Hz, (ii) centered and rectified, (iii) low-pass Butterworth 330 

filtered with a cut-off frequency of 5 Hz, and (iv) normalized to [0,1], by dividing by the maximum 331 

post-processed activity recorded during MVC. The total activation is computed as the sum of the 332 

normalized flexor and extensor activities. 333 

Eye gaze was recorded at 120 Hz using the Tobii Pro Fusion system (Tobii), following a standard 334 

five-point calibration during which participants fixed the screen center, then its four corners. The 335 

collected data were pre-processed to ensure its quality. Trials with more than 15% loss rate were 336 

discarded. Two participants (out of 17) with more than 9 invalid trials (out of 38) were excluded 337 

from the dataset. For the remaining trials, intermittent data due to blinking, large gaze deviation, or 338 

posture was handled as follows: small gaps ( < 83 ms) were linearly interpolated, while larger gaps 339 

were marked as invalid. Data segments with high local velocity standard deviation (computed over 340 

a 400 ms sliding window) velocity standard deviation were classified as jittery and removed. Pro-341 

cessed gaze data were converted from screen coordinate to eye movement angles. Saccades and 342 

smooth pursuit events were identified via peak detection on the velocity profile, using thresholds 343 

of minimum amplitude 100 ◦∕𝑠 , prominence 70 ◦∕𝑠 and distance 125 ms. The peak cut-off was set to 344 

50% of peak height for experiment data and 100 Hz simulated data, and 30% for 1000 Hz simulated 345 

data. 346 

We analyze eye gaze as follows. As the future target trajectory is shown to the participants on a 347 

rolling basis on the monitor, the gaze vertical position ( V-pos ) indicates the range of their planning 348 

horizon. The gaze always lead ahead the hand movement, and this range can be evaluated by 349 

dividing the gaze V-pos by the scrolling velocity ( lead time ). Combined with the gaze horizontal 350 

position ( H-pos ), the gaze trajectory can be projected onto the horizontal position-time plane ( P-pos )351 

for comparison with the reference trajectory and the hand trajectory. The gaze does not always 352 

land on the target trajectory. Such deviation are studied in terms of the horizontal gaze-target 353 

distance ( H-dist ) and the minimal gaze-target distance on the screen ( Dist ). An illustration of these 354 

metrics are shown in Figure 6 .355

## Gaze filtering effect with frequency 667 

As observed both in model prediction and experiment, the eye gaze do not directly land on the trajectory; it tends to more frequently land on the inside of a curve, resulting in what is similar to a ’low-pass filtering’ effect. At lower frequencies, the gaze more closely follows the shape of the target trajectory while at higher frequencies it omits the high frequency components, as shown in Appendix 1—figure 1 .

## Sensitivity analysis 680 

In the main text of the manuscript, we presented the simulation result with the one selected hyperparameter set. Here we preform a sensitivity analysis, in which each hyperparameter is perturbed around its optimal value. The eye planning horizon and replan rate is assumed to model the model performance on a linear scale while the others on an exponen-tial scale. The linear parameters are perturbed within ±20% of their optimal value, with a 2% step size. The exponential parameters are perturbed by [1∕1 .5, 1∕1 .48 , ..., 1∕1 .02 , 1, 1.02 , ..., 1.48 , 1.5] × original value.
