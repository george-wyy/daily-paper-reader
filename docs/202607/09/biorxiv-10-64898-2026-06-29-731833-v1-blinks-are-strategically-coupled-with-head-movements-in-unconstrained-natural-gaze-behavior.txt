# Abstract 12 

Blinks are a ubiquitous yet largely unnoticed aspect of human vision, despite causing frequent 13 

interruptions of visual input that can amount to up to 10% of waking time. By leveraging a large 14 

dataset of unconstrained gaze behavior during two natural tasks , we found a novel behavioral 15 

strategy to limit the impact of blinks : blinks were strategically coupled with head movements ,16 

which minimizes information loss due to unreliable visual input during head movements. 17 

Specifically, b link probability increas ed with higher head velocit ies and showed strong temporal 18 

modulation relative to head movement onset : Blink probability was reduced before head 19 

movement initiation and then peaked during the head movement. The strength of th is coupling 20 

was tailored to the individual needs of participant s, with participants with higher baseline blink 21 

showing a stronger synchronization . This indicates that blinks are a part of an individually 22 

coordinated strategy when orchestrating eye and head movements during unconstrained 23 

natural behavior .24

Introduction 25 

Although we blink every few seconds 1,2 , we are rarely aware of it. This is notable , as every 26 

time we blink, we lose visual input , and given both t he frequency and duration of blinking, it 27 

has been estimated that individuals can spend up to 10% of their waking time with their eyes 28 

closed 3. Researchers have long been fasci nated by the question of why we do not notice 29 

these constant blackouts in our visual input 4–6. The critical factor seems to be an internal 30 

suppression mechanism that reduces visual sensitivity 7,8 , which is s imilar to ideas for how we 31 

deal with the consequences of our own eye movements 9,10 . However, the question remains :32 

why are we not more affected by the constant loss of visual input in our everyday behavior? 33 

Blink s are necessary to keep the cornea moisturized 11,12 , which helps to improve the optical 34 

quality of the images 13 . Thus, we cannot simply avoid blink ing. However, there is growing 35 

evidence that blinks might also have other functions. Blinks might not be as detrimental to 36 

visual perception as previously thought, as visual performance after blinks can even be 37 

boosted (Ang & Maus, 2020), and this seems to be related to the visual transients on the retina 38 

caused by blinks 15 . In addition, the timing of blinks also provides evidence for mechanisms 39 

that go beyond passive moisturization . We blink more frequently than would be necessary for 40 

moisturizatio n 13,16 , and w hen we blink is also not random : The timing of blinks is actively 41 

shaped by task demands 17 : Hoppe and colleagues 18 showed that subjects rapidly learn how 42 

to time blinks to m atch the statistics of events they are required to detect. The timing of blinks43 

can also be adapted to perform ing subsequent actions 19 –21 . In addition, when investigating 44 

isolated eye movements, previous studies found that blinks are more likely to occur during 45 

larger gaze shifts 22 –24 . Furthermore, recent work suggests that blinks can be related to 46 

cognitive processes, for example, to disengage from information 25 , for example , when finishing 47 

a sentence or turning a page 26,27 . Thus, within the constraints of specific tasks, blinks seem to 48 

be generally timed to minimize the risk of critical information loss. 49 

However, how do we deal w ith the need to blink in less constrained, natural behavior 28 ? In 50 

typical vision experiments, participants perform a structured task with individual short trials, 51 

which determine when visual information is task -relevant. In addition, such experiments often 52 

also constrain movements by fixing the head, which is in stark contrast to the coordination of 53 

eye and hea d as a hallmark of natural gaze behavior 29 . In the present study , we leverage a 54 

large -scale data set of unconstrained eye and head movements where participants perform 55 

two continuous natural tasks to reveal a strategy for blink ing beha vior that plausibly helps with 56 

the problem of minimizing information loss , namely, that blinks are coupled with head 57 

movements. This coupling shows a natural synchronization of blinks with longer times of less 58 

reliable visual input resulting from faster head movements . This strategy suggests that during 59 

unconstrained natural behavior, when to blink is part of an individua lly coordinated plan that 60 

orchestrates eye and head movements. 61

Blinks are coupled with large gaze shifts. When looking at the raw data (Figure 1B), we 89 

noticed a striking pattern: blinks often coincided with large gaze shifts. This also became 90 

evident when looking at summary statistics: the average gaze shift across blinks was much 91 

larger than the average gaze shifts caused by eye or head movements (see Figure 1C), 92 

sometimes even by several orders of magnitude. This indicates that blinks were frequently 93 

coupled with particularly large gaze shifts. To test for this coupling , we estimated an expected 94 

gaze shift distribution if blinks would occur at random times (Methods for more Details ). When 95 

comparing the random with the actual distribution of gaze shifts (Figure 1D), you can already 96 

see that while the random distribution leads to mostly small gaze shifts, the distribution of the 97 

actual blinks has two peaks: one that follows the random distribution, but also a clear second 98 

peak , which is centered on large gaze shifts. Based on the random distribution, we could also 99 

directly compare the expected avera ge gaze shift during blinks based on a random distribution 100 

with the observed mean gaze shift for each participant and each task (Figure 1 E). The 101 

difference was extreme : Every subject in both tasks showed a higher actual mean gaze shift 102 

than what would have been expected from the random distribution (t(80)=4 1.425 , p<.001, 103 

d=4. 603 for Blocks, and t(80)= 35 .199 , p<.001, d=3. 911 ). It is i mportant to note here that w hile 104 

gaze -evoked blinks for larger gaze shifts have been described for isolated movements 22 –24 ,105 

these results suggest that in unconstrained , sequential behavior, many blinks seem to be 106 

strategically coupled with very large gaze shifts .107 

The occurrence of blinks is tuned to head velocity. After demonstrating this overall link 108 

between blinks and large gaze shifts , we tested whether we could establish a relationship 109 

between the characteristics of gaze shift s and the probability of blinking. Large gaze shifts in 110 

unconstrained behavior are typically combined shifts of eye and head (see Figure 1B ), which 111 

means that the head is still moving during blinks . Since , by defin ition, we do no t have access 112 

to eye movements during blinks , we focused our analysis on head movement s. We 113 

hypothesized that high head velocities could be related to blinks . Fast head movements lead 114 

to large field visual motion on the retina , which makes t he visual input less reliable and 115 

potentially challenges visual stability. To test this , we looked at all detected head movements 116 

(in total more than 109 k). For each participant and task, w e sorted the head movements by 117 

their peak velocity and then binned them in groups of 5% and computed the proportion of 118 

movements that contained a blink. Across observers and tasks, we found a lawful relationship 119 

between head velocity and the occurrence of a blink : the higher the head velocity, the higher 120 

the probability of blinking . Interestingly, this relationship was well described by a cumulative 121 

Gaussian, akin to a typical psychometric function (see Figure 3A for an example observer). 122 

This reveals that for large gaze shifts, there is a gradual and continuous scaling of the 123 

probability of blinking based on head velocity. This demonstrates a lawful relationship in the 124

Discussion 191 

By leveraging a large data set of unconstrained eye and head movement s, we revealed a192 

natural ly occurring strategic relationship between blinks and head movements. Blink frequency 193 

was tuned to head velocity and showed a strong temporal modulation with respect to head 194 

movement onset. Overall, the use of this strategy was also related to individual differences, 195 

with people with a higher blink rate showing a stronger coupling of blinks and head movements. 196 

Th ese findings parsimoniously relate previous ideas regarding the active timing of blinks to 197 

minimize information loss and potential benefits of blinks for visual perceptio n and show that 198 

during unconstrained natural behavior, blinks are integrated in the coordination of eye and 199 

head movements .200 

Natural Blinking is temporally tuned to minimize information loss . While it is clear that we 201 

need to blink to moisturize the cornea 11 –13 , when we blink is strategically adapted to minimize 202 

information loss 18,19 . Our r ecording s of natural blink ing behavior r evealed two novel behavioral 203 

strategies consistent with this idea. 204 

First, blinks are strategically shifted to co -occur with head movements (see Figure 3A). This 205 

effec t go es beyond previous research that demonstrated gaze -evoked blinking for single large 206 

gaze shifts 22 –24 , as it shows an active use of a strategy that ‘hides’ blinks at moments of 207 

unreliable vi su al input during head movements . Th e strategic shift of blinks into faster head 208 

movements is complementary to other results that show that there are special kinds of torsional 209 

eye movements that are only performed while blinking 20 . To demonstrate the strategic 210 

rel ationship between head movements and blinks, we observed that blinking is not only 211 

temporally tuned to head movement onset, but also shaped by the upcoming head movement 212 

characteristics (Figure 2A). The coupling of blinking and head movements is tuned to head 213 

velocity, where especially fast movements lead to greater retinal motion. Fast head velocities 214 

(see Fig 2A) translate to similarly fast movements on the retina, as even when the eyes 215 

counterrotate to maintain fixation during fast head movements (the so -called vestibulo -ocular 216 

reflex, see 30,31 ), the peripheral visual input is still moving at velocities similar to head speed. 217 

Such high velocities , especially for the long durations of head movements, make the analysis 218 

of visual input difficult 32,33 . The strategic link between the planning of head movements and 219 

blinks is also evident in results that show that for the same gaze shift amplitudes, blinks are 220 

more frequent when the head is involved 23 and that blinking activity is also registered when 221 

participants perform memory -guided head shifts while the eyes are closed 24 . Thus, since 222

good strategy, as it minimizes the loss of reliable visual information. 224 

Second , we found a reduction of blinks before the execution of eye and head movements 225 

(Figure 3A). This shows that while accumulating visual information to plan the next movements, 226 

blink s are reduced to avoid interference with the ongoing visual target selection and to foster 227 

movement preparat ion . Such a reduction in blink rate mimics recent work that indicates that 228 

the reduction of small fixational eye movements (called “ microsaccadic inhibition ”) can serve 229 

as a proxy for the expectation of both sensory events 34 –36 , and self -planned movements 37 . In 230 

addition, such temporal shifts of potentially interfering corrective saccades are not only present 231 

during fixation, but have also been reported when intercepting moving targets 38 . Our results 232 

go beyond these previous findings by showing that such an inhibition of potentially interfering 233 

behavior (microsaccades or blinks), also generalizes to complex, sequential, and self -paced 234 

natural tasks. Thus, strategically adapting our behavior to minimize potential information loss 235 

and be mo st prepared for processing visual information at critical moments in time is a general 236 

mechanism that translates from artificial laboratory tasks to natural behavior. 237 

Natural blinking can benefit perception. The use of large head movements to shift gaze has 238 

a drastic effect . While eye movement s can only change what can be fixate d in an otherwise 239 

constant visual scene , head movements bring new parts of the world into the visual field . This 240 

makes the potential boost of visual processing after blink s especially beneficial 14 . The critical 241 

aspect for this benefit seems to be the visual transients produced by the blink 15 , and we 242 

observed that the blinks tend to be executed more towards the beginning of the head 243 

movem ent (see Figure 3A) , allowing undisturbed processing of the visual information at the 244 

end of the gaze shift. This idea also fits well with the specific enhancement of low spatial 245 

frequencies 15 . This helps particularly with coarse -to -fine analysis 39 , which is especially critical 246 

when needing to process novel visual input .247 

In addition, the coupling of blinks and head movements c ould also serve a further purpose ,248 

namely, perceiving a stable world despite chang es in retinal input resulting from self -motion 40 .249 

Work on visual sensitivity during eye movements has shown that a critical factor in not 250 

perceiving motion blur caused by our own eye movements is the masking of the blurred retinal 251 

image by the stable image present before and after the eye movement 41 . However, while such 252 

a passive masking mechanism works for short eye movements , it is not feasible for longer 253 

head movements (typical saccade durations are 50 -100 ms; the average head movement 254 

duration in our study is 550 ms) . Motion blur in the middle of head movements would still be 255 

visible, so actively suppressing it by blinking might actually benefit the perception of a stable 256 

world. This fits well to the observation of higher blink frequenc ies when eye movements are 257 

performed over background s that produce more blur 22 . While it is important to note that 258

Individuals strategically orchestrate blinks, eye and head movements to their 272 

advantage . Under natural circumstances, not every blink is related to a head movement (see 273 

the first peak of the gaze shift distribution in Figure 1C). Sometimes we have to blink to 274 

moisturize our eyes , even when no head movement is planned . However, our results 275 

demonstrate that people strategically plan and coordinate blinks, as well as eye and head 276 

movements together. 277 

One particularly interesting aspect is the difference in the beginning of the reduction in blink 278 

rate before eye and head movements. This shows that blinks were i nhibited in relation to the 279 

planning of the upcoming movement, and thus can be considered to reflect the planning 280 

horizon before a movement. The planning horizon was significantly longer for head movements 281 

(see Figure 3C), which makes sense as eye movement s are considered to have relatively 282 

minimal costs due to their short duration and limited required effort 45 . In contrast, head 283 

movements have longer latencies 46 , take much more time (see Figure 3A), and due to the 284 

higher weight and inertia, are much more costly than eye movements 45,47 . The differences in 285 

the planning horizon are also visible between the different tasks: the planning horizon for head 286 

movements is s ignificantly longer in the block than in the paint task (Blocks: M= -531.27 ms, 287 

SD=138.21 ms; Paint: M= -384.57 ms, SD=124.95 ms, t(78) = 9.50 3, p <.001; see Figure 3C). 288 

This is directly related to the different roles that head movements have in these two ta sks: in 289 

the painting task, head movements are mostly used to switch between the original painting 290 

and the piece of paper. In comparison, during the block task, participants frequently use head 291 

movements to scan the table to find fitting blocks. This more s equential, planned behavior is 292 

then reflected in a longer planning horizon for the block task. 293

The strategic coordination of blinks and head movements was also strengthened by the fact 294 

that it was used differently by our participants. There is converging evidence that individual 295 

differences can reflect an individual optimization of behavior: Participants prefer to look at 296 

different positions in faces, which is related to their own optimal point to discriminate them 48 ,297 

can provid e insights into idiosyncrasies in sensorimotor processing 49 –51 , or reflect a tuning of 298 

motor behavior towards one’s own cost functions 52,53 . In line with these ideas, w e observed 299 

that the more participants needed to blink , the stronger their coupling of blinks and head 300 

movements. This was true for the tuning to head velocity (Figure 2B), as well as the temporal 301 

modulation of blink rate (Figure 3B). In other words , the more often participants had to blink, 302 

the more likely it was that they embedded these blinks during head movements . This tuning is 303 

a good strategy as it helps them to address multiple problems with a single solution : they can 304 

moisturize their eyes by blinking, while at the same time minimizing the los s of critical visual 305 

information due to unreliable visual input , and potential ly even getting benefit s for visual 306 

perception. 307 

Overall, our results highlight and emphasize the strategic control of blinks during unconstrained 308 

behavior in complex, sequential, and self -paced natural tasks . We show, in addition, that 309 

naturally occurring blinks and head movements are coupled , with blinks more likely during 310 

larger head movements . Individuals strategically orchestrate their behavior and include blink 311 

timing in movement planning . This pattern of behavior minimizes the loss of critical visual 312 

information, can benefit perceptual stability, and is tuned to the individual needs of a 313 

participant. 314

Preprocessing of behavioral data . The whole pipeline to label and extract the behavioral 339 

data is publicly available at: https://gitlab.com/AlexGoettker/labe lmobiledata . To compute gaze 340 

shifts in real -world coordinates, we started by using the recording of the azimuth and elevation 341 

of the eye position (as eye in head position) and the IMU data to compute the gaze and head 342 

positions in world coordinates. For th at, we followed the procedure available from  PupilLabs .343 

In short, we upsample d the IMU data from 110 Hz to 200 Hz, which is available for the eye 344 

position signal. We then used the quaternion values of the IMU to project them into a common 345 

reference frame. This procedure gives us the head azimuth and elevation, and the gaze 346 

azimuth a nd elevation (which is now eye + head). We then used a second -order Savitzky -347 

Golay filter with a window size of 10 samples (50 ms) to filter these position traces. From the 348 

position trace s, we compute gaze and head velocity by computing the distance betwee n two 349 

consecutive samples on the sphere and multiplying it by the sampling frequency , which we 350 

then filtered with a second -order Butterworth filter with a cutoff frequency of 15 Hz. 351 

Blink Detection. We used the default blink detector by  PupilLabs , and visually validated parts 352 

of the blink detection by checking whether, during fast head movements, the eye camera really 353 

shows a blink. Note here that while blinks are interpolated by default in the initial output data, 354 

we set the gaze position and velocity vectors during the blinks (+/ - 5 samples or 25 ms around 355 

the blinks) to NaN. 356

1000 ms (or 200 samples). Wit hin this window, the threshold for slow eye movements was set 360

calculated, and then samples that were above the mean + 2.5 std of the velocity were excluded 362 

from this computation until there was no change in the mean estimate. The threshold for this 363 

window was then set to the final mean value + 2.5. std. Then the sliding window was moved 364 

by 10 ms (2 samples) and the procedure was repeated. This went through the whole r ecording, 365
