## Optimizing MR-based gaze-decoding for eyes-closed eye-tracking in fMRI 1

Sina M. Kling 1, Uriel Lascombes 1, Matthias Nau 2, Guillaume S. Masson 1, Martin Szinte 1

21. Institut de Neurosciences de la Timone, CNRS, Aix-Marseille Université, Marseille, France 32. Institute for Brain and Behaviour Amsterdam, Department of Experimental and Applied 4Psychology, Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam, Amsterdam, The Netherlands 56

Abstract 7Eye movements provide valuable insights into human cognition and are a critical variable in 8numerous functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) studies. Yet, when the eyes are closed, 9camera-based eye-tracking is unavailable, making studies of eyes-closed states challenging. Here, 10 we address this gap through MR-based gaze decoding with DeepMReye, a deep learning framework 11 for camera-free reconstruction of gaze behavior from the MR-signal of the eyes. We first show that 12 fine-tuning DeepMReye using visuomotor calibration data acquired when the eyes were open 13 significantly improves gaze decoding, and that this fine-tuning does not require simultaneous 14 camera-based data. We next assessed whether model performance could be further improved by 15 incorporating data acquired while participants gazed at known positions with both eyes open and 16 closed. Notably, while DeepMReye was originally trained exclusively on eyes-open data, the network 17 successfully generalized eyes-closed periods, with performance improving significantly through fine-18 tuning on the eyes-closed data. These findings demonstrate that reliable gaze monitoring during 19 eyes-closed periods is feasible, enabling a more effective integration of eye-tracking in fMRI 20 research and, consequently, advancing our understanding of human cognition. 21 22

Introduction 26 Eye movements offer a dynamic window into perceptual and cognitive processes. As essential 27 components of visual behavior, they not only indicate where we direct our gaze, but also reflect how 28 we allocate attention, process information, and make decisions (McCarley & Kramer, 2007). 29 Accordingly, eye movements have been linked to language comprehension and memory (Hannula 30 et al., 2010; Mézière et al., 2023; Ryan & Shen, 2020), attention and decision-making (Blair et al., 31 n.d.; Fiedler & Glöckner, 2012; Gidlöf et al., 2013; Tatler, 2007), and mental imagery (Benedek et 32 al., 2017; Laeng et al., 2014). Eye movements are informative well beyond visual processing, 33 extending even to sleep, where rapid eye movements during REM reflect internal cognitive activity 34 in the absence of any external stimulus (Senzai & Scanziani, 2022). 35 In functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) research, eye movement monitoring is 36 essential. Tracking gaze ensures participants attend to stimuli as intended, helping to reduce 37 confounds from unintended viewing behavior. Even subtle gaze shifts can substantially alter 38

2activation patterns in visual cortex, particularly in paradigms relying on retinotopic mapping or precise 1stimulus presentation (Laconte et al., 2007; Lee et al., 2015). Access to accurate gaze data is thus 2vital for controlling attention, verifying task compliance, and improving the interpretability of fMRI 3findings. Traditional MR-compatible eye-tracking systems typically rely on infrared video to track 4corneal reflections and pupil position. However, in addition to their high cost, these camera-based 5systems face practical limitations in MRI environments, including constrained setup geometry, 6interference from scanner hardware, signal dropout, and susceptibility to motion artifacts. Most 7critically, they require participants' eyes to remain open, making them unusable in contexts where 8eyes are closed or obscured such as during sleep, eyes-closed mind wandering and other resting 9state paradigms. To overcome these constraints, recent efforts have explored decoding gaze directly 10 from eye-voxel signals. Early approaches demonstrated that eye movements could be inferred from 11 the MR signal of the eyes itself (Beauchamp, 2003; Tregellas et al., 2002), with subsequent work 12 extending to gaze decoding under naturalistic viewing conditions (Nau et al., 2025; Son et al., 2020). 13 DeepMReye, an established framework for eye voxel-based gaze decoding, uses convolutional 14 neural networks to reconstruct gaze from MRI signals of the eyeballs and surrounding tissues (Frey 15 et al., 2021). This camera-less approach performs robustly across diverse viewing tasks and has the 16 unique ability to be applied retrospectively to datasets lacking camera-based eye-tracking data. 17 However, DeepMReye has been validated mostly under eyes-open conditions, with only 18 limited evidence of successful performance under eyes-closed conditions (Frey et al., 2021). 19 However, many fMRI paradigms include periods when participants' eyes are closed, such as resting-20 state scans, sleep studies, visual-imagery tasks, and clinical protocols (Bianciardi et al., 2009; 21 Fulford et al., 2018). This creates a significant gap, as eye movement dynamics may differ 22 substantially between eyes-open and eyes-closed states (Allik et al., 1981; Collewijn et al., 1985), 23 and eyelids closure can influence eyeball position and motion (Kirchner et al., 2022). The present 24 study addresses this gap with two primary aims. First, we determined how DeepMReye can be 25 further optimized for specific experimental set-ups, as our local one, by systematically fine-tuning 26 the network to local scanner environments and task-specific viewing dynamics. Second, we develop 27 a novel paradigm using auditory-guided eye movements to fine-tune the model without visual input, 28 further called visual/eyes state tasks. Our results demonstrate that fine-tuning with task-specific 29 eyes-open data significantly improves decoding performance of DeepMReye during “guided 30 fixation”, “guided pursuit” and “free viewing” parts. Furthermore, we demonstrate that fine-tuning with 31 eyes-closed data substantially improves decoding accuracy during eyes-closed states. This 32 advancement enables gaze monitoring in previously inaccessible fMRI contexts and opens new 33 possibilities for retrospective analysis of large existing fMRI data resources. By extending MR-based 34 gaze decoding beyond its current limitations, this work provides researchers with a powerful tool for 35 understanding eye movements across the full spectrum of conscious states. 36 37

Decoding gaze when the eyes are closed 23 

Having demonstrated that DeepMReye can be fine-tuned using eyes-open visuomotor calibration 24 tasks, we investigated decoding gaze position when the eyes are closed. To this end, we developed 25 a set of novel fMRI tasks, the visual/eyes state tasks, in which participants performed guided eye 26 movements tracing one of four triangle shapes, each oriented in a different cardinal direction (up, 27 down, left, right). Each corner of the triangle was paired with a distinct auditory cue, allowing 28 participants to associate temporal auditory information with spatial positions (Figure 1C). 29 The visual/eyes state tasks comprised of four parts that systematically varied the visual input (vision, 30 no vision) and eyes-state (eyes-open, eyes-blink, eyes-closed): (1) “ Vision with eyes-open ”, in which 31 participants viewed a screen and followed auditory cues with open eyes, (2) “Vision and eyes-blink”, 32 where participants blinked during each fixation point guided by auditory cues, (3) “No vision and 33 eyes-open”, in which no stimulus was shown but the eyes remain open, and lastly (4) “No vision and 34 eyes-closed”, in which participants replicated the same sequences with their eyes closed, guided 35 solely by auditory cues. Importantly, this design enabled the fine-tuning of DeepMReye on eyes-36 closed data, going beyond previous reports (Frey et al., 2021; Nau et al., 2025). Moreover, it allowed 37

Discussion 17 

Eye-tracking in an MRI scanner is technically demanding, and many laboratories lack the 18 equipment to do it at all. Here, we asked whether a short visuomotor calibration paradigm with a set 19 of classical oculomotor tasks can be used to fine-tune DeepMReye and adapt it to appartus that 20 were not part of the original data used for pretraining. Indeed, we found that fine-tuning reliably 21 improved decoding across a wider-than-standard visual field (18 dva-square), and that this 22 improvement reflected genuine model adaptation rather than simple output rescaling. Going beyond 23 the abilities of camera-based eye-tracking, we then examined eyes-closed conditions. Notably, we 24 show that complex, structured eye movements performed with closed eyes can be decoded from 25 fMRI data. Using an auditory-guided paradigm, we generated eyes-closed training data and showed 26 that including it substantially improved decoding performance. This opens a window into paradigms 27 where eyes must remain closed, such as sleep, resting-state, and visual imagery tasks, as well as 28 tasks performed with populations for whom camera-based eye-tracking has never been a realistic 29 option (patient, children). Finally, we also show that DeepMReye can differentiate open and closed 30 eyelids with fine-tuned models outperforming the pre-trained baseline. 31

Further considerations for decoding eyes-closed eye-movements 31 

The ability to decode gaze during eyes-closed periods opens a window into oculomotor behaviors 32 that have been difficult to study non-invasively. Promising applications include sleep neuroimaging, 33 where REM eye movements could be tracked as markers of cognitive processing (Senzai & 34 Scanziani, 2022, Nau et al., 2025); eyes-closed resting-state and mental imagery paradigms, where 35 eye movements reflect internally directed cognition even without visual input, and clinical populations 36 for whom camera-based eye-tracking or sustained eyes-open fixation is not feasible. Yet several 37 important considerations shape how these results should be interpreted. 38

11 First, it is known that eyes-closed oculomotor behavior is qualitatively different from eyes-open one. 1When the eyelids close, it physically deforms the eyeball, altering its shape and therefore the MR 2signal geometry that DeepMReye relies upon (Brodoehl et al., 2016). Beyond the mechanical effect 3of the eyelids, the BOLD signal itself may differ between eyes-open and eyes-closed states (Marx et 4al., 2004). Additionally, eyes-closed eye movements have distinct features: Bell's phenomenon (a 5reflexive upward rotation of the eyes upon closure), reduced saccade frequency, and highly 6idiosyncratic individual movement strategies (Haladjian et al., 2015; Shaikh et al., 2010). These 7differences mean that a model trained primarily on eyes-open data may impose a specific structure 8onto decoded eyes-closed trajectories, rather than faithfully capturing the underlying movements, an 9open question that future work should systematically address. 10 At the same time, these same signal differences make eyelid-state itself decodable: the original 11 DeepMReye model was already shown to classify eyelids open versus closed from fMRI without any 12 fine-tuning (Frey et al., 2021), suggesting eyelid-state information is implicitly encoded in the models’ 13 input data. Building on this, we show that fine-tuning further improves decoding, and initializing from 14 gaze-position-fine-tuned weights is sufficient. Future work could further improve eyelid-state 15 decoding by exploring architectures that explicitly leverage this entanglement of eyelid information 16 in the eyeballs signal. 17 Second, voluntarily executing precise eye movements with eyes closed is inherently difficult (Allik et 18 al., 1981). Participants then rely on non-visual information (auditory or proprioceptive) or on internal 19 models of spatial locations (visuospatial memory, visual imagery) than visual cues to guide 20 movements that are normally visually anchored (Allik et al., 1981; Balslev et al., 2011). Moreover, 21 proprioceptive inputs are distorted due to closed eyelids and there is lack of visual feedback resulting 22 in ocular trajectories with substantially greater spatial variability and fixation errors. Yet it has been 23 reported, that even in darkness or with eyes closed, participants are able to report the direction of 24 an eye movement (Skavenski et al., 1972) and produce simple geometric shapes with their eye 25 movements (Allik et al., 1981). Still, this sets a practical ceiling on decoding accuracy that is 26 independent of model quality. Without visual feedback, the eye movements themselves are 27 inherently more variable: fixations are less stable, trajectories deviate more from their intended 28 paths, and spatial errors accumulate across a sequence. A perfect decoder would faithfully recover 29 these movements, but the recovered trajectories would still look imprecise, because the underlying 30 movements are imprecise. As we evaluated the models’ performance implicitly through spatial 31 accuracy, the remaining performance gap between eyes-open and eyes-closed results therefore 32 likely reflects this greater biological variability in the movements being decoded, rather than a failure 33 of the model itself. 34

Conclusion 11 

Together, these results mark a meaningful step toward making fMRI-based gaze decoding broadly 12 accessible, even for studies of eyes-closed states. By implementing practical visuomotor calibration 13 tasks and fine-tuning procedure, and enabling structured gaze monitoring during eyes-closed states, 14 this work expands the range of experimental contexts in which DeepMReye can serve as a practical 15 tool. The ability to retrospectively apply these methods to existing datasets acquired without camera-16 based eye-tracking further underscores their wide applicability. Particularly for free-viewing 17 paradigms and clinical populations, the advances demonstrated here lay the groundwork for a new 18 generation of fMRI studies in which eye movements are routinely monitored regardless of whether 19 participants' eyes are open or closed. 20 21

Visual/eyes state tasks 19 

These tasks were composed of four consecutive parts manipulating both availability of visual 20 information and eyes- or eyelid-state: “vision and eyes-open”, “vision and eyes-blink”, “no vision and 21 eyes-open”, “no vision and eyes-closed”. Statistical analysis 16 

Statistical significance was assessed using non-parametric permutation tests across participants. 17 For each comparison, we randomly shuffled participant labels 10,000 times to generate a null 18 distribution of the test statistic under the assumption that participants could be interchanged without 19 affecting the outcome.
