Abstract. Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have demon-strated remarkable video reasoning capabilities across diverse tasks. How-ever, their ability to understand human intent at a fine-grained level in egocentric videos remains largely unexplored. Existing benchmarks focus primarily on episode-level intent reasoning, overlooking the finer granularity of step-level intent understanding. Yet applications such as intelligent assistants, robotic imitation learning, and augmented reality guidance require understanding not only what a person is doing at each step, but also why and what comes next, in order to provide timely and context-aware support. To this end, we introduce EgoIntent , a step-level intent understanding benchmark for egocentric videos. It comprises 

3,014 steps spanning 15 diverse indoor and outdoor daily-life scenarios, and evaluates models on three complementary dimensions: local intent 

(What), global intent (Why), and next-step plan (Next). Crucially, each clip is truncated immediately before the key outcome of the queried step ( e.g ., contact or grasp) occurs and contains no frames from subse-quent steps, preventing future-frame leakage and enabling a clean eval-uation of anticipatory step understanding and next-step planning. We evaluate 15 MLLMs, including both state-of-the-art closed-source and open-source models. Even the best-performing model achieves an aver-age score of only 33.31 across the three intent dimensions, underscoring that step-level intent understanding in egocentric videos remains a highly challenging problem that calls for further investigation. 

1 Introduction 

With the rapid advancement of Multimodal Large Language Models [1, 9, 15, 16, 22, 37, 39, 40], building agents that can perceive their surroundings and act autonomously is gradually becoming a reality rather than a distant vision. An ideal AI assistant should not merely respond passively to explicit user instruc-tions [21], but should also be capable of proactively inferring user intent [25,36], namely, understanding a user’s current goal and anticipating subsequent needs by observing behavior [7,18], thereby providing timely assistance without requir-ing explicit requests. Achieving this capability requires understanding human intent from continuous visual observations. However, it remains unclear whether current MLLMs possess such an ability.

Egocentric videos provide a natural testbed for this problem [17,34,44]. Their unique first-person perspective unifies human perception, actions, and scene con-text within a shared coordinate frame, offering rich cues for understanding hu-man behavior and intent. Recently, several benchmarks have begun to explore intent-related reasoning in egocentric settings [3, 23, 25, 33, 45]. For example, EgoGazeVQA [25] focuses on gaze-grounded intent question answering at the scene- or clip-level, while WAGIBench [33] studies episode-level goal inference in wearable assistant scenarios. However, in real-world procedural activities, hu-man intent often evolves dynamically with each action step [31]. Coarse-grained reasoning at the episode or clip level is therefore insufficient to capture such fine-grained intent transitions [24]. Moreover, existing works typically define intent EgoIntent 3

only in specific scenarios and at relatively coarse granularity, leaving step-level intent understanding in procedural activities largely underexplored. In this paper, we introduce EgoIntent, a benchmark for step-level intent un-derstanding in egocentric videos. Building upon the existing step-level temporal annotations in Ego4D, we further annotate 3,014 steps from 15 daily life sce-narios with three complementary dimensions: local intent, which refers to what is being done at the current step; global intent, which refers to the higher level purpose behind this step; and next-step plan, which refers to the most likely subsequent step. These annotations enable a systematic evaluation of the fine-grained intent understanding of MLLMs at the step-level. In addition, to prevent models from exploiting future visual information, we temporally truncate each queried step before its key outcome occurs, as illustrated in Fig.2. As a result, the model can observe only the ongoing process of the current step, without access to subsequent frames, thereby avoiding future frame leakage and enabling a more reliable evaluation of predictive reasoning and next-step planning. We conduct a systematic evaluation of 15 representative MLLMs. As shown in Fig. 1, the results indicate that even the best-performing model achieves an average score of only 33.31 across the three dimensions, suggesting that step-level intent understanding in egocentric videos remains highly challenging and far from being solved for current models. In summary, our main contributions are as follows: 

– We present EgoIntent, a benchmark for step-level intent understanding in egocentric videos, covering three complementary dimensions: local intent, global intent, and next-step plan. 

– We design a temporal truncation mechanism that truncates each queried step before its key outcome occurs and removes frames from subsequent steps, thereby preventing future frame leakage and enabling a more reliable evaluation of predictive reasoning. 

– We systematically evaluate 15 representative MLLMs, revealing their sub-stantial limitations in step-level intent understanding and providing fine-grained analyses across dimensions and scenarios, which offer valuable in-sights for future research. 

2 Related Work 

Egocentric video datasets and benchmarks. Egocentric video understand-ing has been advanced by a series of datasets and benchmarks [30]. Ego4D [12] provides a large-scale benchmark for first-person perception, covering episodic memory, hand-object interactions, and future prediction. Ego-Exo4D [13] ex-tends this line with synchronized egocentric and exocentric videos for multiview understanding of skilled activities. EPIC-KITCHENS [10] provides fine-grained action annotations in kitchen scenarios, while HD-EPIC [26] further enriches egocentric videos with more detailed multimodal annotations. With the rise of MLLMs, recent benchmarks have shifted toward higher-level reasoning over ego-centric videos. EgoSchema [23] evaluates very long-form video understanding 4 Y. Pan et al. 

through multiple-choice QA over three-minute clips. EgoThink [6] and VidEgo-Think [5] study egocentric reasoning in static and video settings, respectively. MM-Ego [41] introduces EgoMemoria to evaluate memorization of fine-grained visual details across videos of varying lengths. EgoPlan-Bench [3] evaluates next-action planning based on task progress, current observations, and task goals. While these benchmarks have advanced egocentric video understanding across multiple dimensions, they primarily focus on understanding what is hap-pening, remembering visual details, or deciding what to do next. For a proactive AI assistant, however, recognizing observable behavior alone is insufficient; it must also understand why an action is being performed. Existing benchmarks rarely evaluate this explicit intent understanding, especially at the step-level in procedural activities. 

Intent reasoning benchmarks. Intent reasoning aims to infer the underlying purpose behind observed behavior and is a key capability for moving AI assis-tants from passive response to proactive support [18]. In the third-person video domain, IntentQA [19] builds an intent-oriented VideoQA benchmark on top of NExT-QA [38] by selecting causal and temporal question types, but it does not explicitly decompose intent into step-level local intent, global intent, and next-step planning. Recently, several works have explored intent-related reasoning in egocentric settings. EgoGazeVQA [25] studies gaze-guided egocentric video QA with spatial, temporal, and causal intent questions, but relies on gaze signals as additional input. WAGIBench [33] evaluates multimodal episode-level goal in-ference for assistive wearable agents using video, audio, digital, and longitudinal context, rather than step-level latent intent inference from visual observations alone. However, for a truly proactive AI assistant, intent understanding must op-erate at a finer granularity. In everyday procedural activities, intent evolves dynamically with each step, and the same action may serve different goals de-pending on context [28,29]. Coarse-grained reasoning at the episode or clip level cannot capture such transitions. Moreover, intent is inherently multi-layered: it encompasses what is being done (local intent), why it is being done (global in-tent), and what is likely to be done next (next-step plan). Existing works do not jointly cover all three aspects at the step-level. To fill this gap, we introduce EgoIntent, a step-level benchmark for multi-dimensional intent understanding in egocentric videos.

3.2 Task Definition 

Given a temporally truncated egocentric video clip corresponding to a queried step, EgoIntent evaluates whether a model can infer three dimensions of step-level intent from the current visual context alone, before the key outcome of that step becomes visually explicit. Local intent refers to the actor’s immediate goal within the current step, answering what the actor is trying to accomplish now. Global intent captures the higher-level functional role of the current step in the overall event, answering why this step is needed at this point in the procedure. Next-step plan denotes the action that is most likely to begin after the current step is completed, answering what is most likely to happen next. These three dimensions respectively characterize the actor’s immediate objective, higher-level procedural function, and likely action progression. During annotation and benchmark use, we impose a unified set of bound-ary constraints on these three dimensions. Local intent must correspond to an immediate goal that can be directly achieved within the current step, rather than a simple restatement of the surface action. Global intent must describe the functional role of the step in the complete procedure, rather than repeating the local goal or directly describing the next action. Next-step plan must be tempo-rally subsequent to the current step and refer to the action most likely to begin after the current step ends. These constraints are designed to reduce semantic overlap across dimensions as much as possible. The distinctions among the three dimensions are summarized in Table 1.

Annotation Procedure. For each step, we first take the original narration text from Ego4D as an initial reference for local intent, and then manually revise it according to the video content so that it is transformed from a surface-level action description into an intent expression that better reflects the actor’s immediate goal. We then use the context of the current step within the complete event to generate candidate descriptions for global intent and next-step plan . However, the complete event context is used only to propose candidate interpretations and does not directly determine whether a final label is acceptable. The final retained label must be reasonably supported by evidence visible before truncation, rather than relying on information that appears only after truncation. Among these annotations, Table 2 presents several representative examples of local intent revision. As shown, the original narration-like labels are often closer to surface descriptions of visible actions, whereas the revised local intents place greater emphasis on the actor’s immediate goal in the current step. This revision process helps distinguish step-level intent from literal action description. Temporal Truncation Strategy. To strictly constrain the task to intent in-ference before the key outcome becomes visible, we apply pre-outcome temporal truncation to the video clip corresponding to each queried step. The goal is to retain sufficient observational context while removing information that would di-rectly reveal the outcome of the current step, thereby preventing the model from inferring the current intent from an already completed result. Specifically, we use the narration timestamp_sec provided by GoalStep as the initial temporal an-chor, and then perform manual inspection and adjustment when necessary, such that the final retained clip always ends before the key outcome of the current step becomes visually explicit. Fig. 5 illustrates this pre-outcome truncation mechanism. Under this design, the model cannot rely on the completed result state to infer the current in-tent, and must instead perform predictive understanding based on pre-outcome action tendencies, scene context, hand-object interaction cues, and procedural structure. Because the truncated clip also excludes frames from subsequent steps, this setting further prevents direct leakage from future-step context. Statistically, among all 3,014 steps, 2,253 directly use the original times-tamp_sec as the final truncation point, while the remaining 761 are manually revised because the original timestamps deviate from the boundary at which the key outcome becomes visible. For the revised samples, the median absolute adjustment is 0.11 seconds, and the mean absolute adjustment is 0.23 seconds. In addition, during a boundary review on a randomly sampled set of 973 steps, 957 were judged to satisfy the requirement that the key outcome has not yet be-come clearly visible. From the review results, the three dimensions show overall good annotation consistency, although their difficulty levels are not identical. The most common source of confusion is the boundary between local intent and next-step plan ,whereas disagreements on global intent mainly arise from whether the level of abstraction is appropriate. Most labels revised after re-checking are not caused by incorrect judgment of video facts, but rather by refined boundary control among the three dimensions or by tightening labels to reduce potential future leakage. This ambiguity is especially pronounced for preparatory or transitional steps, where an immediate goal can be easily conflated with an upcoming ac-tion. We also explicitly calibrate the abstrac-tion level of global intent to capture the step’s functional role in the procedure without collapsing into action paraphrases. This suggests that the main anno-tation challenge in EgoIntent does not lie in recognizing observable video facts themselves, but in controlling the boundaries of open-ended step-level intent descriptions and preventing future information leakage.

During evaluation, we do not provide the task name, event name, human annotations, or any other external metadata, thereby ensuring that the evaluation setting remains fully aligned with the goal of the benchmark. To ensure fair comparison, we adopt a unified prompting protocol for all models and require each model to generate three fixed fields: local intent ,

global intent , and next-step plan . All open-source models are run locally in offline mode, with video inputs sampled at 6 FPS. For closed-source models, their native video API interfaces typically use a default sparse sampling rate of 1 FPS, which may miss substantial dynamic details such as action transitions and hand-object interactions, making fine-grained step-level judgment more difficult. To mitigate this discrepancy, we do not directly feed videos into closed-source models. Instead, we uniformly sample image sequences from each queried clip at 6 FPS and provide them as multi-image inputs rather than video inputs. This design better aligns the density of visible temporal information across open-source and closed-source models, making the comparison more equitable. Since EgoIntent is an open-ended generation task, model outputs may differ from reference answers in surface form while remaining semantically correct. We therefore do not use exact string matching. Instead, we adopt a Qwen3-based semantic consistency evaluation protocol. Specifically, we use Qwen3 [40] as a unified evaluator to compare the generated local intent , global intent ,and next-step plan with the corresponding reference answers, and to score semantic consistency for each dimension separately. 4.2 Main Result 

We first compare the overall performance of 15 MLLMs on EgoIntent, and fur-ther conduct a fine-grained analysis of model capability across the three di-mensions of local intent, global intent, and next-step plan, as well as under the indoor/outdoor split. The overall results are shown in Fig. 1, while detailed quantitative results are reported in Table 3. Overall, existing MLLMs still achieve limited performance on EgoIntent, indi-cating that step-level intent understanding in egocentric procedural activities re-mains a challenging problem. Among the closed-source models, Qwen3.5-plus [27] achieves the best overall performance with a score of 33.31, followed by Gemini-3-flash-preview [11] with 32.17. Among the open-source models, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B [27] performs best, reaching 31.68 overall. Despite these differences, even the strongest model attains only relatively modest scores and does not yet demonstrate stable and reliable intent inference, suggesting a substantial gap between current models and the level of proactive intent understanding required for real-world deployment. Across the different intent dimensions, model performance is notably unbal-anced. In general, models are better at capturing the higher-level functional role of the current step within the overall procedure, while they are more prone to errors in identifying the immediate goal of the current step and predicting the action that is most likely to follow next. For example, the best-performing model, Qwen3.5-plus [27], achieves 36.80 on global intent, compared with 32.06 on lo-cal intent and 31.06 on next-step plan; a similar trend is also observed for the best open-source model, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B [27], which scores 34.05, 30.94, and 30.06, respectively. This suggests that, compared with relatively coarse-grained functional reasoning over procedures, fine-grained recognition of current intent and forward-looking next-step planning impose greater demands on ex-isting models. The challenge is further amplified by our deliberate temporal truncation setting: because the video is cut before the key outcome becomes visible, models cannot rely on explicit result cues and must instead infer intent solely from limited pre-outcome visual context. In addition, Table 3 shows that although there are measurable performance differences across models, no model clearly and consistently dominates across all metrics or robustly solves the task. In other words, while stronger general-purpose MLLMs tend to perform better overall, their advantage remains limited and does not indicate that step-level intent understanding has been adequately addressed by current approaches. Taken together, these results further validate the effectiveness of EgoIntent as a benchmark: it not only discriminates between models of different capability levels, but also reveals the shared weaknesses of existing methods in fine-grained intent inference. 14 Y. 4.3 Scenario-Level Analysis 

To further examine model behavior across different procedural scenarios, we present a scenario-level performance heatmap in Fig.7. We observe that model performance is not uniform across scenarios, suggesting that the challenges posed by EgoIntent do not stem from a single factor. Instead, difficulty varies substan-tially with the environment type, manipulated objects, action organization pat-terns, and the complexity of the procedural context. In some scenarios, models can leverage relatively explicit environmental and object cues to make plausi-ble inferences. In others, however, the relationship between the local action and its functional goal is more implicit, or the subsequent action is more uncertain, leading to a clear drop in performance. Notably, even the better-performing models do not maintain a consistent ad-vantage across all scenarios. This suggests that current MLLMs still rely heavily on salient cues in specific scenarios for step-level intent understanding, rather than exhibiting stable and transferable procedural intent reasoning across sce-narios. In other words, while current models can perform reasonably well in familiar scenarios or those with richer observable cues, they remain prone to failure in scenarios that require stronger context integration, higher-level func-tional abstraction, and more accurate next-step prediction. These findings fur-ther indicate that EgoIntent not only measures overall model capability, but also serves as a diagnostic benchmark for revealing the specific limitations of different models in scenario generalization. 

5 Conclusion 

We introduced EgoIntent , a step-level benchmark for egocentric procedural videos that evaluates three complementary dimensions of intent: local intent 

(What), global intent (Why), and next-step plan (Next). To enable a clean as-sessment of anticipatory understanding, each queried clip is temporally truncated before the step’s key outcome and excludes all future frames to avoid leakage. Ex-periments on 15 representative MLLMs show that performance remains limited (best average score: 33.31), highlighting substantial gaps in fine-grained intent inference and next-step prediction.
