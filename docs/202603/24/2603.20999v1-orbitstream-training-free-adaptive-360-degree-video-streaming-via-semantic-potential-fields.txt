## Abstract 

Adaptive 360 ° video streaming for teleoperation faces dual challenges: viewport prediction under uncertain gaze pat-terns and bitrate adaptation over volatile wireless channels. While data-driven and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) methods achieve high Quality of Experience (QoE), their "black-box" nature and reliance on training data can limit deployment in safety-critical systems. To address this, we propose OrbitStream, a training-free framework that com-bines semantic scene understanding with robust control the-ory. We formulate viewport prediction as a Gravitational Viewport Prediction (GVP) problem, where semantic objects generate potential fields that attract user gaze. Furthermore, we employ a Saturation-Based Proportional-Derivative (PD) Controller for buffer regulation. On object-rich teleoperation traces, OrbitStream achieves a 94.7% zero-shot viewport pre-diction accuracy without user-specific profiling, approach-ing trajectory-extrapolation baselines ( ∼98.5%). Across 3,600 Monte Carlo simulations on diverse network traces, Orbit-Stream yields a mean QoE of 2.71. It ranks second among 12 evaluated algorithms, close to the top-performing BOLA-E (2.80) while outperforming FastMPC (1.84). The system ex-hibits an average decision latency of 1.01 ms with minimal rebuffering events. By providing competitive QoE with inter-pretability and zero training overhead, OrbitStream demon-strates that physics-based control, combined with semantic modeling, offers a practical solution for 360 ° streaming in teleoperation.

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Adaptive Bitrate Streaming 

Traditional adaptive bitrate (ABR) algorithms tackle quality adaptation through varying strategies [ 33 , 34 ]. Rule-based methods , including buffer-based [ 10 , 32 ] and rate-based ap-proaches [ 22 , 38 ], prioritize simplicity. Buffer-based methods dictate bitrate via buffer occupancy, guaranteeing stability but suffering from sluggish responsiveness to sudden chan-nel improvements. Optimization-based approaches , such as Model Predictive Control (MPC) [ 42 , 43 ], frame streaming as a finite-horizon utility maximization problem. Despite their mathematical rigor, they heavily depend on accurate throughput predictions, risking dramatic quality degradation when forecasts inexplicably fail. 

Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) methods, notably Pensieve [ 14 ] and Fugu [ 40 ], attain high performance bench-marks by learning ABR policies from expansive network traces. Nevertheless, their "black-box" nature and depen-dency on pre-training can present challenges in environ-ments that require transparent and predictable behavior. 

## 2.2 Viewport Prediction in 360 ° Video 

The spherical geometry inherent to 360 ° video demands effi-cient spatial delivery. Tile-based streaming , exemplified by FLARE [ 25 ] and Pano [ 8], curtails bandwidth by priori-tizing the user’s field of view, though they frequently rely on deterministic trajectory extrapolation [ 12 , 46 ]. Recent optimizations have explored robust frameworks handling field-of-view uncertainty explicitly via mathematical relax-ation (e.g., 360-ROBUST), as well as multi-trajectory pre-dictions (e.g., MFVP) for lightweight viewport modeling. 

Semantic-aware predictors , such as those exploiting ob-ject saliency [ 20 , 21 , 35 , 41 ] or multi-agent DRL frameworks, typically necessitate aggregate viewing statistics or complex deep learning architectures. This can make them difficult to deploy in zero-shot teleoperation where prior user data may be absent. 

Distinction of OrbitStream. OrbitStream formulates viewport prediction as a continuous physics-inspired simu-lation. Treating semantic objects as gravitational attractors provides anticipation without requiring crowd statistics or DRL training. It provides an interpretable framework aligned with the needs of autonomous teleoperation.

## 6 Implementation 

The OrbitStream framework is implemented as a modular control loop (Algorithm 1) executing once per video chunk. The architecture decouples perception tasks from control logic to support low-latency operation. 

## 6.1 Perception-Control Decoupling 

The framework operates in two concurrent stages. Percep-tion runs on an edge-co-located server, executing object detection on the incoming 360 ° stream to extract bounding boxes and class labels (incurring ∼12.8 ms latency per frame on an NVIDIA T4). Control executes locally at the Opera-tor Terminal, using object metadata to update the semantic potential field, simulate gaze dynamics, and compute the PD rate allocation.

## 7.4 Result 3: Real-Time Feasibility 

Minimizing feedback latency is an essential requirement for effective robotic control. Benchmarking computational overhead (Table 3) demonstrates that rule-based pipelines execute nearly instantaneously ( ∼ 3 𝜇 s), while numerical optimization routines like MPC incur slightly elevated, but still very low, costs ( ∼ 13 –19 𝜇 s). ## 7.6 Result 5: Ablation on Core Predictor Variables 

To evaluate the configuration logic, we analyzed the sys-tem’s sensitivity to imperfect object detection and varied hyperparameters. 

Detection Errors: Introducing synthetic false-negative object masking decreases the mean hit ratio from 94.7% down to 82.2% when 30% of scene hazards are missed. However, QoE degradation is partially mitigated by the Boltzmann distribution’s peripheral spread mapping. 

Semantic Mass Hierarchy: Flattening the semantic mass assignments ( 𝑀 ℓ = 1 for all objects) instead of using priority-based weighting diluted the focal attractor, reducing the overall hit ratio by 4.2% and increasing viewport penalties during split-second hazards. 

Distribution Tuning: Modulating the attention temper-ature 𝛽 proved sensitive. Elevating 𝛽 → 1.5 sharpens spa-tial allocation, improving QoE on stationary targets but in-creasing viewport penalties by 41% during erratic subject movements. Similarly, omitting the regularizer ( 𝛿 = 0) led to numerical singularities at close proximity, causing prediction lags.

## 8 Conclusion 

We proposed OrbitStream , an adaptive streaming frame-work tailored for 360 ° teleoperation. By formulating viewport prediction as test-particle dynamics within semantic poten-tial fields, alongside a PD rate controller, we provide a con-trol logic alternative to deep learning paradigms. Empirical evaluations demonstrate OrbitStream ’s capability to de-liver competitive QoE (2.71, secondary to BOLA-E) alongside buffer stability ( 𝜎 𝐵 = 0.42 s) and semantic-aware viewport prediction (94.7% accuracy). By showing that interpretable, physics-based control can operate within viable soft real-time margins (1.01 ms) while maintaining competitive quality and stability, OrbitStream provides a practical methodology for teleoperation systems where analyzability is beneficial. 

## 8.1 Limitations & Future Work 

A primary limitation of the proposed approach is its marked computational overhead relative to conventional rule-based or MPC algorithms. As noted in the evaluation, comput-ing the potential fields and evaluating stochastic differential equations elevates control latency to roughly 1.01 ms . While this is generally acceptable for 30 -fps rendering loops, it poses a direct bottleneck for deployment on highly resource-constrained micro-controllers that require microsecond-level efficiencies. Furthermore, our framework relies heavily on the accu-racy of upstream object detection. Because it operates strictly downstream of the vision models, any detection failures—caused by occlusion, poor lighting, or environmental noise—will in-herently cascade into the control loop and degrade viewport prediction accuracy. Lastly, assuming a static semantic mass hierarchy restricts flexibility; it prevents the system from autonomously adapting to specialized, user-specific teleoper-ation behaviors that might prioritize different scene elements over time. Future efforts will explore: (1) tuning semantic weights from operator corrections, (2) stress-testing the architecture against severely occluded detector pipelines, and (3) migrat-ing the stability constraints into sampled-data equivalents to tighten computational boundaries.
